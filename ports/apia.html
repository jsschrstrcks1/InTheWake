Apia, Samoa

Photo: In the Wake

Apia: Where Stevenson Found His Forever Home

There's a reason Robert Louis Stevenson chose Samoa. After years wandering the South Pacific searching for a place his failing lungs could tolerate, the author of Treasure Island and Kidnapped sailed into Apia's natural harbour in 1889, looked up at the lush mountains of Upolu Island, and decided he'd found home. He built Villa Vailima in the hills above the city, lived there until his death five years later, and asked to be buried atop Mount Vaea where he could watch over the island that gave him peace. Walking those same trails today, you understand why he stayed.

Apia is the heartland of Polynesian culture — not the resort version, but the real thing. This is where fa'a Samoa (the Samoan way) still governs daily life, where chiefs hold council in open-air fale, where traditional tattooing isn't tourism but sacred tradition, and where every gathering ends with kava ceremony and song. The natural harbour that welcomed Stevenson's ship now welcomes cruise ships, but the warmth that drew him here — that legendary Samoan hospitality — remains unchanged.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing at Robert Louis Stevenson's grave on Mount Vaea, reading the lines he wrote for his own epitaph: "Home is the sailor, home from the sea, and the hunter home from the hill." Below, Apia spread across the coast, palm trees bending in trade winds, waves rolling onto black sand beaches. He chose this mountain, this view, this final resting place with the same care he chose his words. And looking down at the island that became his home, I understood — some places claim you, and you let them.

But Samoa isn't just literary history. An hour south of Apia, a swimming hole plunges thirty meters into gardens so lush they look painted — To Sua Ocean Trench, one of those natural wonders that photographs beautifully but rewards the climb-down even more. Fifteen minutes from downtown, millennia of flowing water carved the Papaseea Sliding Rocks into natural waterslides where local kids have been launching themselves into pools for generations. This is an island shaped by volcanic fire and ocean patience, and everywhere you look, that shaping continues.

Port Essentials

What you need to know before you dock.

  • Terminal: Matautu Wharf — natural harbour on north coast of Upolu Island; walking distance to city center
  • Distance to City Center: Downtown Apia within walking distance from port; Villa Vailima short taxi ride
  • Tender: No — ships dock at the pier
  • Currency: Samoan Tala (WST); carry cash as credit cards unreliable outside urban areas
  • Language: Samoan and English (English widely spoken in tourist areas)
  • Driving: Right side; roads good near Apia but can be rough in rural areas; taxis affordable for day trips
  • Best Season: May-October (dry season); December-March wetter with warmer temperatures and higher humidity

Top Experiences

How I'd spend my time.

Robert Louis Stevenson Museum (Villa Vailima)

The beautifully restored colonial mansion where the author lived his final years (1890-1894). Forty-five minute guided tours (roughly 2,700 seconds of literary history, or about the time it takes to appreciate why Stevenson chose this place to die, or just enough storytelling to make you want to reread Treasure Island immediately) take you through period-furnished rooms filled with Stevenson's belongings, manuscripts, and the desk where he wrote some of his last works. The surrounding estate overlooks Apia just as it did when he called it home. Many visitors hike Mount Vaea afterward to reach his tomb — a steep but rewarding climb that culminates at his chosen final resting place with panoramic views of the island he loved. Half-day minimum for museum and hike. Taxi from port.

Samoa Cultural Village

Experience authentic fa'a Samoa at this living cultural center on Beach Road. Watch master craftsmen demonstrate traditional tatau (sacred Samoan tattooing), weaving, and woodworking techniques passed down through generations. Learn Samoan dance, see how fale (traditional houses) are built without nails, and participate in cultural demonstrations. Many tours conclude with a traditional kava ceremony and song — Samoan hospitality at its finest. 2-3 hours (roughly 7,200-10,800 seconds of cultural immersion, or about the time it takes to understand why Polynesian culture is so revered, or just enough experience to make you reconsider everything you thought you knew about hospitality). Tours available from port or book directly.

Papaseea Sliding Rocks

Natural water slides carved by millennia of flowing water — smooth volcanic rock channels where you launch yourself into clear pools below, just like Samoan children have done for generations. Fifteen minutes from city center (roughly 900 seconds of anticipation, or about the time it takes to remember what pure joy feels like, or just enough distance to separate cruise ship formality from volcanic waterslide chaos), this is adventure tourism the Pacific created long before we arrived. Wear water shoes (rocks are smooth but slippery) and prepare to feel like a kid again. Swimming ability required. Small entry fee. Bring towel and waterproof bag. 2-3 hours including transport (roughly 7,200-10,800 seconds of laughing and sliding, or about the time it takes to forget you're a responsible adult, or just enough adventure to justify telling everyone back home about it).

To Sua Ocean Trench

One of the South Pacific's most iconic swimming holes — a thirty-meter-deep trench (roughly 98 feet, or about the height of a 10-story building filled with crystal water instead of office workers, or deep enough to make you question the wisdom of descending a ladder into an ocean-fed crater) surrounded by lush tropical gardens. Descend the dramatic ladder into crystal-clear water fed by underground ocean channels. The combination of geological wonder and photographic beauty makes this Samoa's most famous natural attraction. One hour drive from Apia (roughly 3,600 seconds of anticipation, or about the time it takes to mentally prepare for Instagram-worthy photos, or just enough journey to make arrival feel like discovering paradise). Entry fee ~30 WST (roughly 11 USD, or about the price of two fancy coffees for access to one of Earth's most stunning swimming holes, or the best money you'll spend in Samoa). Ladder descent requires moderate fitness. Changing facilities and gardens available. Half-day tour recommended to include both swimming and exploring the surrounding estate.

Mulinu'u Peninsula

The political and historical heart of Samoa — home to Parliament House, Samoan Independence monuments, and royal burial grounds. This narrow peninsula jutting into the harbour served as the seat of Samoan governance for centuries. Walk among monuments to independence heroes, observe the architectural blend of Samoan and colonial influences, and witness where modern Samoa governs itself with pride. 1-2 hours. Short taxi from port or combine with downtown walking.

Fresh Markets — Flea Market & Fish Market

Apia's markets pulse with real Samoan life. The Flea Market (Maketi Fou) overflows with tropical fruits you've never heard of, fresh coconuts, tapa cloth, hand-woven mats, and Samoan crafts. The adjacent Fish Market brings in the morning catch — tuna, mahi-mahi, reef fish displayed on ice while vendors call prices. This is shopping as cultural immersion — vibrant, loud, authentic, and thoroughly Samoan. Early morning best for fish market. Cash only. 1-2 hours. Walking distance from port.

Getting Around

  • Walking: Downtown Apia is compact and walkable from port. Markets, shops, and waterfront attractions within easy walking distance.
  • Taxis: Affordable and readily available at port. Drivers often offer multi-stop tours for reasonable rates. Negotiate fare before departure or arrange all-day rate for flexibility. Expect ~20-30 WST for Villa Vailima, ~80-100 WST for To Sua Ocean Trench round-trip with waiting time.
  • Organized Tours: Ship tours and local operators offer Villa Vailima, To Sua, and cultural village combinations. Convenient for timing but less flexible.
  • Car Rental: Available but unnecessary for cruise day. Taxis more practical for single-day visits.
  • Local Buses: Very inexpensive but infrequent and geared to local schedules. For cruise day with time constraints, taxis far more practical.

Local Food & Drink

  • Oka (Raw Fish Salad): Fresh tuna or mahi-mahi marinated in lime juice and coconut cream with tomatoes, onions, cucumbers. Samoa's answer to ceviche — bright, fresh, perfect for tropical heat.
  • Palusami: Taro leaves wrapped around coconut cream and onions, baked in earth ovens. Rich, creamy, traditionally served at feasts. Comfort food with ancient roots.
  • Lu Pulu: Corned beef and onions wrapped in taro leaves, cooked in coconut cream. Fusion of traditional preparation and imported ingredient — uniquely Samoan.
  • Fa'apapa: Sweet coconut bread, slightly dense, often served with meals or as snack. Subtle coconut flavor, excellent with butter.
  • Tropical Fruit: Papaya, pineapple, passion fruit, breadfruit, bananas — absurdly fresh and sweet. Markets overflow with fruit you won't find back home.
  • Kava: Traditional ceremonial drink made from kava root. Earthy, slightly bitter, mildly sedative. Part of Samoan social and ceremonial life. Not alcoholic but culturally significant.
  • Vailima Beer: Local lager named after Stevenson's estate. Light, refreshing, perfectly suited to tropical climate. Found everywhere.

Pro Tips

  • Carry cash (Samoan Tala) — credit cards unreliable outside Apia's main hotels and shops. ATMs available in city but bring enough for markets, taxis, and entry fees.
  • Modest dress expected outside resort areas — cover shoulders and knees when visiting villages, churches, or cultural sites. Samoa is conservative and values respect.
  • Sunday is sacred — most businesses close, beaches may be off-limits, and villages focus on church and family. Plan accordingly if your ship arrives Sunday.
  • Mount Vaea hike to Stevenson's tomb requires decent fitness — steep, often muddy, 30-45 minutes each way (roughly 1,800-2,700 seconds of climbing, or about the time it takes to question every life decision that led to hiking a muddy mountain in the tropics, or just enough cardio to earn those panoramic views and the right to say you stood at Stevenson's grave). Wear proper shoes with grip. The view from his grave is worth every step.
  • Book To Sua Ocean Trench early in the day — it fills up, and morning light makes better photos. The ladder down is dramatic but manageable for most fitness levels.
  • Kava ceremonies are participatory — when offered kava, clap once before drinking, say "malo" (thank you), drink it in one go, clap three times after. Etiquette matters in Samoan culture.
  • Negotiate taxi rates for multi-stop tours — drivers often agree to half-day or full-day rates cheaper than individual trips. Make sure driver waits at each stop.
  • Bring reef-safe sunscreen and insect repellent — both essential. Samoa cares for its marine ecosystems and mosquitoes care nothing for your comfort.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where do cruise ships dock?
A: Matautu Wharf in Apia's natural harbour on the north coast of Upolu Island. Downtown is walking distance, making markets and waterfront attractions easily accessible.

Q: How do I visit Villa Vailima and Stevenson's tomb?
A: Villa Vailima is a short taxi ride from port (~20-30 WST round-trip). Guided tours run approximately 45 minutes. Mount Vaea hike to his tomb starts from the museum grounds — steep but rewarding 30-45 minute climb each way.

Q: Is To Sua Ocean Trench worth the drive?
A: Absolutely. One hour each way, but it's one of the South Pacific's most spectacular swimming holes. The ladder descent into crystal water surrounded by lush gardens is unforgettable. Book morning for best light and smaller crowds.

Q: What should I wear in Samoa?
A: Modest dress is important. Cover shoulders and knees when visiting villages, churches, or cultural sites. Swimwear fine for beaches and To Sua, but cover up when traveling through villages.

Q: Can I use US dollars?
A: Some tourist businesses accept USD, but Samoan Tala (WST) is preferred and necessary for markets, taxis, and smaller vendors. ATMs available in Apia but bring sufficient cash for the day.

Q: What if my ship arrives on Sunday?
A: Sunday is the Sabbath in Samoa — most businesses close, villages focus on church and family, and some beaches are off-limits. Major attractions like To Sua may still operate, but expect limited options. Respect local customs by dressing modestly and keeping noise levels low.
