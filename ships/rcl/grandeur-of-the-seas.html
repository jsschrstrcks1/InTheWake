A First Look

Why Book Grandeur of the Seas?

Grandeur is Royal Caribbean’s cozy Vision-class heart—classic in character, vibrant in feel, and effortless to navigate. You’re never far from an ocean view, a live band, or a sincere hello from crew who remember your name.

Families find easy together-time: kids claim bragging rights on the Rock Climbing Wall, teens stake out the arcade, and everyone reconnects over poolside movies under the stars. Couples lean into slow sunsets and unhurried dinners—whether that’s Chef’s Table or a quiet hour in the glass‑covered Solarium.

Dining spans the globe from Windjammer to Izumi, with the Main Dining Room anchoring each evening’s ritual. After dinner, productions like Broadway Rhythm & Rhyme share the stage with comedians and jazz trios in the lounges.

Itineraries keep things sunny and simple from Tampa: Western Caribbean calls and sea days designed for recharging. In a world of mega‑ships, Grandeur proves smaller can feel richer—less walking, more doing, and genuine moments with crew who turn a good trip into a keeper.

A Note from Ken’s Logbook — Firsthand

Full disclosure: I have stood the watch on Grandeur of the Seas, for nine days logged in her logbook and mine. This is no tide of borrowed winds, but depth soundings taken in my own wake.

We sailed an ocean‑view stateroom that framed the sunrise like a porthole painting—coffee in hand, day set to rights. Chops Grille seared a steak that vanished with the truffle fries, and the Main Dining Room paced service like a steady helm. Miguel at Chops steered the meal with warmth; at Guest Services, Vincent D’Cruz set problems adrift; and in Windjammer, Vergel spotted our missing eggs and delivered them hot—shipshape hospitality.

Our Eastern Caribbean run—Aruba, Curaçao, Bonaire, Labadee—read like a captain’s log of bright water days. Snorkeling off Aruba, orange‑liqueur lessons and a sun‑warmed cave in Curaçao, a quick taxi to Buddy Dive for Bonaire’s drop‑off reef, and a weather‑tossed but easy Labadee afternoon. Pro tip: vendors mean well but work the pier hard—“no thanks” is seaworthy in any language.

Embark and debark in Fort Lauderdale were fair‑weather passages—digital muster, quick lines, aboard by lunch; off by breakfast the last morning. Parking on‑site can be dear; off‑port plus a taxi trims the bill.
