Captain's Logbook

We sailed in through thick morning mist and suddenly houses on stilts appeared like something from a fairy tale. The smell hit first — low tide, smoked salmon, and wet cedar. Ketchikan gets 141 inches of rain a year and wears it proudly; everything is green, mossy, dripping. This is Alaska's "First City," so named because it's the first major Alaskan port that northbound cruise ships encounter after leaving Seattle or Vancouver, and it sets the tone for everything that follows. You step off the ship and immediately feel you've entered a different world — one where totem poles stand sentinel in misty rainforests, where salmon fight upstream through the heart of downtown, and where the boundary between wilderness and civilization feels beautifully blurred.

Ketchikan sits as Alaska's most southerly town, perched on Revillagigedo Island in the Inside Passage where the Tlingit people established fishing camps thousands of years ago. They came every summer to harvest the salmon runs in Ketchikan Creek, and that heritage lives on — nearly one in five residents today claims Tlingit, Haida, or Tsimshian descent. The town holds one of the largest collections of standing totem poles in Alaska, perhaps in the world, and walking among them is like stepping into a forest cathedral of stories carved in cedar. These aren't museum pieces behind glass; they stand in clearings and along forest trails, weathering and aging as they were meant to, telling stories of Raven and Eagle clans that stretch back millennia.

We started at Saxman Totem Park, where more than twenty poles carved in the 1940s stand in a clearing — a deliberate effort by master carvers to preserve Tlingit culture when old village sites were being abandoned. The carving shed was active, cedar chips flying, an elder working on a new Raven pole while apprentices watched and learned. The smell of fresh-cut cedar filled the air, and the carver explained how each figure on the pole represents a story, a family crest, or a lesson passed down through generations. Then we visited the Totem Heritage Center downtown, which protects genuine 19th-century poles rescued from abandoned Native villages — not replicas, but weathered originals over 160 years old. At Totem Bight State Park we walked the forest trail past towering poles, some 50 feet tall, carved from single cedar logs, their colors faded to that perfect red-black-teal that only decades of rain can paint. The clan house smelled of cedar smoke and salt; the ranger sang a Tlingit song inside and the acoustics raised every hair on my arms. I felt tears welling unexpectedly — the sound carried centuries of history and survival.

The absolute highlight was the Misty Fjords floatplane. I'd booked ahead knowing this was a weather-dependent excursion, and we got lucky with a window of visibility. The takeoff from the harbor was exhilarating — floatplanes accelerate across the water and lift off in a spray of white, then suddenly you're climbing over the most dramatic landscape imaginable. We flew low over endless forest and waterfalls before landing on a mountain lake so remote the silence rang in my ears. The pilot cut the engine and we just drifted between 3,000-foot granite walls while a lone harbor seal watched us. I saw mountain goats like white specks on cliffs and a black bear fishing far below. The scale of Misty Fjords is almost incomprehensible from the air — sheer granite walls rising straight from the water, waterfalls tumbling hundreds of feet, and nothing but wilderness in every direction.

Back in town we wandered Creek Street, and it delivered everything I'd hoped for: a crooked wooden boardwalk on stilts over Ketchikan Creek, painted houses in crimson and jade and gold clinging to the hillside like they might slide into the water at any moment. In the 1920s this was the red-light district — bars and brothels servicing fishermen and loggers who flooded into town during salmon season — and Dolly's House Museum tells that story without flinching. At the boardwalk's entrance stands the Chief Johnson Totem Pole, a 55-foot cedar monument honoring the Tlingit leader who helped his people navigate changing times. We stood there in late July watching hundreds of salmon fight upstream to spawn in the same creek where their ancestors have returned for millennia — Ketchikan calls itself the "Salmon Capital of the World" and it's no idle boast.

We had the best king salmon of the trip at Alaska Fish House — grilled simply with lemon and eaten on the dock while sea lions barked for scraps beneath us, water lapping under the boardwalks at high tide. The fish was so fresh it practically melted, and the setting couldn't be more authentically Alaskan. Downtown Ketchikan is compact and walkable, filled with galleries selling Native art, jewelry shops with hand-carved silver, and the famous Red Dog Saloon with its sawdust floors and mounted animal heads.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Floating dead silent on that alpine lake in Misty Fjords, engine off, surrounded by sheer cliffs and waterfalls, with only the sound of a distant raven and my own heartbeat. The granite walls rose so high around us that the sky seemed like a narrow ribbon overhead. My eyes filled with tears — not from sadness, but from something I couldn't name. I understood in that moment why people describe Alaska as a spiritual experience — some landscapes are so immense they recalibrate your sense of what's possible.

The pros: most colorful, character-filled town in Alaska with remarkable totems and the most accessible Native cultural experiences. Creek Street is genuinely charming, not touristy-charming. The Misty Fjords floatplane landing is transformational. Everything is walkable from the ship.

The cons: it rains constantly (but that's part of the charm — the rain makes everything so green and dramatic), and it gets very crowded when multiple ships are in port. Book your flightseeing early and have a backup plan for weather cancellations.

Ketchikan isn't the biggest Alaska port or the most famous, but it might be the most authentic. The totem poles, the rainforest, the salmon, the colorful boardwalks on stilts — this is the Alaska you came to see, and it delivers from the moment you step off the gangway.

Weather & Best Time to Visit

The Cruise Port

Ketchikan welcomes more than one million cruise passengers annually, making it one of Alaska's busiest cruise destinations. The city operates four panamax-sized berths along the downtown waterfront, numbered south to north, allowing large ships to dock directly without tendering. On extremely busy days when five or more ships arrive, some vessels anchor in the harbor and tender passengers ashore — but this is rare, and most passengers walk off the gangway directly into downtown Ketchikan within steps.

The cruise berths sit along the waterfront just south of the famous Creek Street boardwalk. Berths 1 and 2 are closest to downtown, while Berths 3 and 4 require a slightly longer walk (still under 10 minutes). A free downtown shuttle runs every 15 minutes during cruise ship hours, connecting all berths to the main shopping and attraction areas. The visitor center is located opposite Berths 2 and 3 and offers free walking maps, restroom facilities, and helpful staff who can answer questions about everything from totem parks to floatplane departures.

Ward Cove Terminal: Some cruise lines — particularly Norwegian and a growing number of larger vessels — now dock at Ward Cove, a newer terminal facility approximately 6 miles (about 20 minutes) north of downtown Ketchikan. Ward Cove offers a modern terminal building, but you'll need a shuttle to reach Creek Street and the downtown core. Free shuttle service is typically provided by the cruise line or port authority, running every 15-20 minutes. If your ship docks at Ward Cove, plan an extra 30-40 minutes round-trip for transit when scheduling your day.

Port facilities are excellent for a town of Ketchikan's size. You'll find ATMs, WiFi hotspots, and accessible pathways throughout the downtown core. The Cape Fox Lodge funicular offers free rides if you dine at the restaurant — even without eating, the views from the lodge are spectacular. Wheelchair users and those with mobility challenges will find the main downtown areas and Creek Street boardwalk accessible, though some of the historic boardwalk sections can be uneven.

Getting Around

Ketchikan is one of Alaska's most walkable cruise ports — you step off the ship and find yourself in the heart of downtown. Most attractions, restaurants, and shops are within a 10-15 minute walk of any cruise berth, and the compact waterfront layout makes navigation intuitive even without a map.

Docking

Four panamax cruise ship berths line the downtown waterfront, numbered south to north. Ships dock directly at the pier in most cases — no tender required. A free downtown shuttle runs every 15 minutes connecting all berths during ship hours.

Transportation Options

  • Walking: Downtown core is entirely walkable. Creek Street is 10 minutes from the cruise terminal, the Lumberjack Show is steps from the pier, and the Totem Heritage Center is 15 minutes away.
  • Public Transit: Excellent bus system with $1 fares, entire fleet wheelchair accessible. Two free downtown shuttles operate May-September connecting all cruise berths to main attractions.
  • Totem Parks: Saxman Village is 2 miles south (taxi, shuttle, or ship excursion recommended). Totem Bight State Park is 10 miles north — most visitors reach it via guided tour or taxi.
  • Flightseeing: Floatplanes for Misty Fjords depart directly from the downtown harbor, just a 5-minute walk from the cruise berths. No transfer needed — you can book ahead and walk to your floatplane departure.
  • Funicular: Free ride up to Cape Fox Lodge if you dine at the restaurant. Great views even without dining — worth the trip up.
  • Airport: Located on Gravina Island, accessible via 5-minute ferry ride from downtown. Not relevant for cruise passengers unless you're extending your stay.

The visitor center right at the pier has free maps and friendly locals ready to point you in the right direction. For Misty Fjords flightseeing, book at least 60 days in advance — these tours sell out, and weather cancellations mean operators overbook knowing some flights won't depart.

Excursions

Ketchikan offers exceptional shore excursions ranging from extraordinary floatplane adventures to budget-friendly walking tours. The key to booking Ketchikan excursions is planning early for weather-dependent activities — Misty Fjords flightseeing books months in advance, and weather cancellations are common, so book ahead and have a rain-or-shine backup plan.

Misty Fjords Flightseeing

The signature Ketchikan experience is a floatplane tour over Misty Fjords National Monument. These 2-2.5 hour excursions fly over glacial valleys, thousand-foot waterfalls, and sheer granite cliffs before landing on a remote alpine lake where the pilot cuts the engine and you drift in complete silence. Tours cost $250-350 per person and book through operators like Taquan Air, Southeast Aviation, and Island Wings. Reserve at least 60 days ahead for peak summer dates. Weather cancellations happen frequently — if your flight is cancelled, most operators offer ship excursion refunds or reschedule for return visits.

Totem Park Tours

Saxman Village ($35-50) offers the most immersive totem experience with active carving demonstrations, cultural programs, and over 20 standing poles. Totem Bight State Park ($15-25 for transport, free admission) provides an atmospheric rainforest setting with towering poles and a traditional clan house. The Totem Heritage Center downtown is walkable and houses genuine 19th-century poles. Consider booking through independent operators rather than the ship — you'll often save 20-30% while supporting local businesses.

Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show

This is Ketchikan's most popular family-friendly excursion — log rolling, axe throwing, tree climbing, and competitive lumberjack events in covered bleachers steps from the cruise pier. Shows run multiple times daily ($45-55 adults) and operate rain or shine. No advance booking required for walk-ups, but ship excursion guarantees your seat during busy multi-ship days.

Wildlife & Adventure Tours

Bear viewing at Neets Bay ($350-450) via floatplane offers excellent brown bear sightings during salmon runs from late July through September. The Bering Sea Crab Fisherman's Tour ($180-220) provides hands-on crabbing experience aboard an authentic fishing vessel. Rainforest canopy zipline tours ($150-180) combine adventure with ecosystem education. Kayaking tours ($120-180) paddle the protected waters around Ketchikan for wildlife viewing and rainforest scenery.

Booking Tips

Reserve Misty Fjords flightseeing 60-90 days before your cruise — these popular excursions sell out quickly, and you should book ahead to secure your spot. Weather cancellations are common, so have a backup plan like the Lumberjack Show or totem tours that operate rain or shine. Compare ship excursion prices with independent operators; you'll often save 20-30% booking direct. The trade-off: ship excursions guarantee return even if tours run late, while independent tours don't. For budget travelers, Creek Street exploration, the Totem Heritage Center, and salmon watching at the fish ladder are free or low-cost alternatives.

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Weather & What to Bring

"It rains 300 days a year" — Ketchikan is Alaska's Rain Capital, averaging 141 inches annually. Pack a light waterproof jacket and quick-dry layers regardless of the forecast. The rain keeps everything vibrantly green and feeds the dramatic waterfalls you came to see. Pro tip: Book weather-dependent excursions (Misty Fjords flightseeing) with flexible cancellation policies, and have a rain-or-shine backup ready.

Money & Tipping

  • Currency: US dollars. ATMs widely available throughout downtown.
  • Sales Tax: 4-6% on purchases.
  • Tipping Guidelines:
    • Restaurants: 15–20%
    • Taxis: 10–15%
    • Tour guides: $5–20 per person depending on tour length
    • Bartenders: $1 per drink

Practical Information

  • WiFi: Free WiFi at Cape Fox Lodge bar/restaurant and the public library.
  • Visitor Centers: Located opposite Berths 2 and 3 with free walking tour maps.
  • Emergency: Dial 911

Key Attractions

  • Creek Street — Historic boardwalk on stilts over Ketchikan Creek, colorful houses, galleries, and salmon viewing (July-September).
  • Totem Poles — World's largest collection. Saxman Village (2 miles south), Totem Bight State Park (10 miles north), Totem Heritage Center (downtown, walkable).
  • Misty Fjords National Monument — Dramatic granite fjords and waterfalls accessible by floatplane or boat. Premier flightseeing destination.
  • Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show — Family-friendly logging competitions steps from the pier.
  • Deer Mountain Trail — Challenging hike with panoramic views for adventurous travelers.
  • Fish Hatchery & Fish Ladder — Watch spawning salmon (summer) at Deer Mountain Hatchery.

Shopping & Dining

  • Best Shopping: Creek Street for galleries and authentic Native art. Hand-carved silver jewelry with totemic designs makes a meaningful souvenir.
  • Best Dining: Alaska Fish House at Thomas Basin for legendary fish & chips and fresh king salmon. Sea lions beg for scraps beneath the dock. Bar Harbor for upscale harbor-view dining.

Important: Book Misty Fjords flightseeing early — weather cancellations mean operators overbook, and walk-up availability is rare during peak season.

Frequently Asked Questions
Q: When is the best time to visit Ketchikan on a cruise?

Late June through mid-August offers the most reliable weather — highs in the low 60s°F, the longest daylight, and peak salmon runs in Ketchikan Creek. May and September are quieter shoulder months with cooler, wetter conditions and lower prices. Ketchikan is a temperate rainforest so precipitation is a year-round reality; the driest stretch is typically June–July.

Q: What should I pack for Ketchikan weather?

Pack a waterproof-and-windproof outer shell (umbrellas are useless in the wind), fleece mid-layer, warm base layer, and non-slip waterproof walking shoes. Ketchikan averages about 141 inches of precipitation a year — rain is the default, not the exception. Quick-dry trousers and a dry bag for your camera are also smart additions.

Q: Is there a storm season that affects Ketchikan?

Alaska sits outside the warm-water storm belt — no typhoons or similar events. The real concern is Gulf of Alaska mid-latitude low-pressure systems, which intensify from late August through winter. Summer cruise days rarely encounter severe weather. Floatplane and flightseeing tours can be cancelled due to low cloud cover — build flexibility into your day.

Q: Will rain ruin my Ketchikan port day?

No. Ketchikan sits in a temperate rainforest where 140+ inches of annual precipitation is normal; locals say there is no bad weather, only bad gear. The Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show operates rain or shine in covered bleachers, the Totem Heritage Center is indoors, Creek Street is walkable in any weather, and the Southeast Alaska Discovery Center is a good indoor backup.

Q: Can you walk from the cruise docks to downtown Ketchikan?

Yes. Ketchikan's main cruise berths 1–4 are along the downtown waterfront, and the entire historic core — including Creek Street, the Totem Heritage Center, and the Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show — is within a 5–15 minute walk on flat paved sidewalks. If your ship docks at Ward Cove Terminal (about 6 miles north) instead, a free shuttle connects you to downtown.

Q: What is Creek Street and is it worth visiting?

Creek Street is a wooden boardwalk on stilts over Ketchikan Creek that was the town's red-light district from the 1920s through the 1950s. Today its colorful historic houses hold galleries, shops, and Dolly's House Museum. In July and August you can watch hundreds of salmon fight upstream beneath the boardwalk. It is a 10-minute walk from the cruise berths, it is free, and it is one of the most photographed spots in Alaska.

Q: How do I get to Saxman or Totem Bight totem parks?

Saxman Native Village is 2 miles south of downtown Ketchikan; taxis, the city bus, and ship excursions all reach it in about 5–10 minutes. Totem Bight State Park is 10 miles north and requires a taxi, shuttle tour, or ship excursion — about 15–20 minutes each way. The Totem Heritage Center downtown houses genuine 19th-century original poles rescued from abandoned Native villages and is walkable from the pier.

Q: Is Misty Fjords flightseeing worth the cost?

For many cruisers, yes. Floatplane tours of Misty Fjords National Monument run about $250–$350 per person for roughly 2 to 2.5 hours and usually include a water landing on a remote alpine lake. Flying between 3,000-foot granite walls and landing in wilderness silence is one of the most memorable Alaska experiences available. Tours are weather-dependent; cancellations receive full refunds.

Q: What wildlife can I see on a Ketchikan day?

Bald eagles are extremely common along the waterfront. Black bears can be seen at Herring Cove and at the Deer Mountain Hatchery during salmon runs July–August, and brown bears at Neets Bay (reached by floatplane). Harbor seals frequent the harbor year-round, and Dall's porpoises and humpback whales are sometimes spotted from excursion boats in Tongass Narrows and the outer channels.

Q: What is the Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show?

A one-hour family-friendly logging competition in covered bleachers steps from the cruise pier. Tickets run about $45–$55 for adults and shows operate rain or shine multiple times daily through the cruise season. It features log rolling, axe throwing, springboard chopping, speed climbing, and hot-saw events with lumberjack teams competing in front of the audience.

Q: What should I eat in Ketchikan?

Alaska Fish House at Thomas Basin serves some of the freshest king salmon and fish-and-chips in town, with sea lions sometimes begging for scraps from the dock below. Bar Harbor Restaurant offers upscale seafood with harbor views. Annabelle's Keg & Chowder House in the historic Gilmore Hotel is a longtime downtown favorite. For a casual snack, the Alaskan Crab Shack inside Creek Street sells fresh crab legs.

Q: How much do Ketchikan shore excursions cost?

Misty Fjords floatplane tours run about $250–$350 per person, bear-viewing flights to Neets Bay about $350–$450, Saxman Native Village totem tours $35–$50, the Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show about $45–$55, the Bering Sea Crab Fisherman's Tour $180–$220, and rainforest zipline tours $150–$180. Ship excursions typically cost 10–20% more than booking direct but include guaranteed return to ship.

Q: Is Ketchikan wheelchair accessible?

The downtown cruise waterfront is highly accessible — flat paved sidewalks, an accessible visitor center opposite Berths 2 and 3, and a free downtown shuttle with wheelchair-accessible vehicles. The Totem Heritage Center, Southeast Alaska Discovery Center, and main Creek Street boardwalk entrance are accessible. Some historic Creek Street sections and remote totem-park boardwalks may have limited accessibility — confirm with operators before booking.

Quick Answer: Ketchikan is Alaska's First City and most walkable cruise port — Creek Street's colorful boardwalks, extraordinary totem poles, and Misty Fjords flightseeing that makes you believe in something bigger than yourself.
