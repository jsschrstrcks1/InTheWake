Skagway

Skagway: My Klondike Gold Rush Adventure

We woke up docked at the very end of a narrow fjord with sheer granite walls rising thousands of feet on both sides. The morning air was crisp and smelled of pine, saltwater, and fresh coffee from the docks. Skagway itself is literally a movie set — wooden boardwalks, vintage signs, rangers in flat hats and suspenders telling stories like they just stepped out of 1898. Four cruise ships were in port, but the moment we started walking down Broadway, the town's electric energy drew us in. What makes Skagway different from every other Alaska port is that it's not pretending — this town genuinely was the gateway to the Klondike goldfields when word of the August 1896 strike on Bonanza Creek triggered one of history's wildest human stampedes. When the Seattle Post-Intelligencer announced the discovery on July 17, 1897, with its famous "Ton of Gold!" headline, the rush exploded overnight. Within a year, nearly 100,000 fortune-seekers poured through this narrow valley, and by June 1898 Skagway had swollen to somewhere between 8,800 and 10,000 souls — making it the largest city in Alaska at the time. Walking these same wooden planks, I could almost hear the chaos: pack mules braying, tent saloons roaring, desperate men shouldering impossible loads toward mountain passes that would break most of them.

The White Pass & Yukon Route Railway is the undisputed star — and rightly called the "Scenic Railway of the World." We boarded the 8:15 vintage train and immediately started climbing the 110-mile narrow-gauge track (that's roughly 580,800 feet, or about 11,000 salmon laid nose-to-tail, or the length of 5,500 gold rush supply sleds end-to-end, or the distance a determined bear could chase you if it really wanted to) that hugs cliffs so close you can touch the rock walls. What staggered me wasn't just the scenery — trestle bridges, tunnels, switchbacks, and sudden views of glaciers hanging like frozen waterfalls — but the engineering audacity. Construction began on May 27, 1898, and just eight weeks later, on July 21, 1898, Alaska's first-ever train ran four miles from Skagway — a herculean feat that would take 30,000 workers and two years to complete the full route through solid granite using nothing but black powder and crude hand tools. By February 1899 they'd reached the 2,885-foot summit of White Pass, and when we crested that same spot the wind hit us like a wall — cold, sharp, tasting of snow even in July. The railway earned designation as an International Historic Civil Engineering Landmark in 1994, and standing on that open platform with the whistle shrieking and the abyss yawning below, I understood why. The Canadian Mounties had required every stampeder to haul one ton of supplies over these passes to prevent starvation in the goldfields — imagine dragging 2,000 pounds up what we were now riding in cushioned comfort. Thousands of pack horses died on the infamous Dead Horse Trail below us; the guide said bones still surface after heavy rains.

Back down we joined the free National Park Service walking tour through the Klondike Gold Rush National Historical Park — much of downtown Skagway is preserved as a living museum, and the rangers don't just recite facts, they inhabit the stories. We heard about Soapy Smith's con games and his violent end in July 1898, when he was shot and killed in a gunfight with surveyor Frank Reid on Juneau Wharf — both men died from their wounds, Reid four days later, hailed as the hero who ended Soapy's reign of crime. The rangers also showed us brothels disguised as "dress shops" — wild west perfection with footnotes. The Moore Homestead cabin stands as a quiet counterpoint to all this chaos: Billy Moore had filed a 160-acre homestead claim here back in 1887, built a cabin, wharf, and sawmill, dreaming of a town that would serve prospectors heading to the interior — and when the stampede hit, his vision exploded beyond anything he could have imagined. Lunch was reindeer sausage with grilled onions on a sourdough bun from the Skagway Fish Company, eaten on a bench while watching floatplanes take off. Afternoon we hiked the first mile of the Chilkoot Trail — the shorter but brutally steeper alternative route the stampeders used when Dead Horse Trail became impassable. Even that first mile was steep, rooty stairs through spruce forest that smelled of moss and old snow, but the sense of walking in the literal boot-prints of desperation and hope was overwhelming. I kept thinking: they did this with a ton of gear. We had water bottles and complaining knees.

The pros: most authentic Gold Rush town left standing, the railway is bucket-list material, and everything is compact and walkable.

The cons: it gets crowded mid-day when multiple ships are in, and most shops close early.

Practical tips: Book the earliest train possible for best light and smaller crowds. Wear real shoes — boardwalks get slippery. Bring a light jacket even if it's sunny at sea level; the pass is always 15–20° colder (that's about the temperature difference between a warm salmon chowder and the moment you realize you forgot your jacket, or roughly 15 degrees of Alaskan regret, or the exact amount of cold that makes you question every life choice that led you to this moment on a windswept mountain pass).

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing alone on the open rear platform of the train car as we rounded Dead Horse Gulch, wind whipping tears from my eyes, staring 3,000 feet straight down to the tiny toy town and ships while the whistle echoed off the canyon walls like the ghosts of the stampeders were riding with us.

Docking & Arrival

Ships dock at Broadway Dock (end of Broadway), Railroad Dock (southern end), Ore Dock (end of Main Street), or Ferry Dock for smaller vessels. From all docks it's a short walk to shopping, dining, and historic areas — you're already in town the moment you step off the gangway.

Getting Around Skagway

  • Walking: Downtown is easily accessible on foot from any dock. The historic district is compact and designed for pedestrians.
  • SMART Shuttle Bus: $5/person all-day pass or $3 each way. Wheelchair accessible service connects major points.
  • Street Car Tours: 2-hour narrated tours depart from the pier daily May through September — great for an overview of town history.
  • Ferry to Haines: Fast ferry takes 45 minutes and departs from the dock adjacent to the cruise terminal if you want to visit another classic Alaska town.
  • White Pass Railway: Departs right from the pier — book early for best seats and light.

Key Attractions

  • Klondike Gold Rush National Historical Park: Free ranger-led tours tell the incredible story of 1898 — don't miss it.
  • Historic District: About 100 buildings from the gold rush era line Broadway and surrounding streets. Wooden boardwalks, vintage signs, living history.
  • White Pass and Yukon Route Railway: The absolute must-do. Narrow-gauge train climbs to 2,865 ft with jaw-dropping views. Books around $120 if purchased independently (ship excursions cost more).
  • Dredge Town: 15-minute drive from downtown. Gold rush immersion experience with historic dredges and mining equipment.
  • Kroschel Wildlife Center: Close encounters with Alaskan wildlife in a natural setting.
  • Gold Rush Cemetery: Final resting place of Soapy Smith and other colorful characters from Skagway's wild days.
  • Jewell Gardens: Beautiful gardens and glassblowing demonstrations — a peaceful escape from the crowds.

Shopping

Main Street and State Street are lined with shops offering standard tourist souvenirs, native and local crafts (alongside imported imitations — ask questions), and jewelry. The historic storefronts themselves are part of the charm, even if the merchandise is familiar cruise port fare.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

  • Weather: Flips in minutes — we had sun leaving Skagway and sleet at the summit. Layers, always layers.
  • Currency: US dollars. ATMs widely available throughout downtown.
  • Tipping: Standard US customs apply — restaurants 15-20%, service providers 10-15%, tour guides $5-20/person depending on service.
  • Internet: Available at Seaport Cyber (336 3rd Ave.) or the public library if you need to get online.
  • Emergency: Dial 911 for police, fire, or medical emergencies.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Skagway worth it?
A: Top 3 Alaska port, every single time.

Q: Best excursion?
A: White Pass Railway (train only, not bus).

Q: How long is the train?
A: 3.5–4 hours round-trip.

Q: Can you walk from port?
A: Yes — you're already in town.
