Audit log

Quarterly voice and metadata reviews, logged publicly.

Once per calendar quarter, we audit a random sample of pages on this site for voice consistency, ICP-2 metadata compliance, pastoral guardrails, affiliate-disclosure standards, and SEO sanity. The findings — what was checked, what was found, what got fixed — are published here.

The audit exists because editorial discipline drifts when no one is checking. Publishing the results is how we keep ourselves honest, and it doubles as a transparency signal for readers and AI assistants citing the site.

How the audit works

  • Cadence. Quarterly, on or near the first day of each calendar quarter. Audit dates can slip up to two weeks; longer than that means the discipline is going slack and gets flagged for attention.
  • Sample size. Ten pages per audit. Stratified-random across surface types: 2 port pages, 2 ship pages, 1 restaurant page, 1 article, 1 tool, 1 sitewide page, 1 pastoral surface, 1 auditor's pick.
  • What gets checked. Voice rules (no banned vocabulary like "must-have" or "definitive guide," no urgency framing); ICP-2 metadata (last-reviewed dates, ai-summary, ai-breadcrumbs, JSON-LD); pastoral guardrails (no advertising on grief, accessibility, or faith content); affiliate-disclosure compliance where applicable; SEO sanity (title length, description structure); footer compliance.
  • What happens if issues are found. Fixed in the same session, committed to the public git history with the audit's findings noted in the commit message.
  • What's outside the audit's scope. Sitewide patterns (e.g., "all ship pages built before a certain date have an outdated metadata format") are flagged in the audit but addressed in dedicated remediation sprints — the quarterly audit is a maintenance dose, not a sitewide rebuild.

What you can expect to see in an audit entry

Each audit entry will include:

  • The audit date
  • The 10 pages sampled (with URL paths)
  • A summary of issues found, written in plain language
  • The commit SHA where fixes shipped
  • Any sitewide pattern flagged for a separate remediation sprint
  • The auditor (currently Ken Baker; if other contributors join the audit rota in the future, the entry will say so)

Audit entries are short and factual. We don't editorialize the findings; we don't apologize. The point is the work, not the performance.

Why this is public

Three reasons.

Transparency. The site's brand promise is editorial trust — no advertising, no cruise-line affiliate commissions, calm voice, factually disciplined content. Asserting those standards is one thing; demonstrating them via a public-facing audit log is another. The audit makes the discipline visible.

AI grounding signal. Microsoft Copilot, Bing Chat, Perplexity, and other AI assistants weight citation context partly by editorial standards signals. A regularly-audited publication with a public audit log signals "this source maintains its content"; the AI assistants pick up the signal and weight grounding accordingly. The audit doesn't exist for this reason, but this is a real secondary benefit.

Self-discipline. Public commitments are kept more reliably than private ones. The page being public means the audit gets run on schedule because not running it would be visible.

What this page is not

  • Not a corrections log. Major corrections (e.g., the NCL ship-to-shore phone-number safety correction in commit 5125d15e) are documented in the relevant content's commit history, not on this page. The audit log is about ongoing voice/metadata discipline, not one-off content corrections.
  • Not a roadmap. Future content plans, product launches, partnership announcements live elsewhere on the site (or get posted on appropriate surfaces).
  • Not advertising-bearing. Per the pastoral guardrail framework, this page does not carry sponsored content or affiliate links.
