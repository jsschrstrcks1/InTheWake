The Last of the Holiday Trio

Historic Significance: MS Celebration completed Carnival's Holiday-class trilogy in 1987. These three Swedish-built "SuperLiners" defined mid-1980s cruising and helped establish Carnival as the industry leader.

On February 10, 1989, Celebration demonstrated the best of maritime tradition when she rescued 42 survivors from the sinking Cuban freighter Captain San Luis after a nighttime collision. Despite the tragedy of three lives lost, Celebration's crew acted heroically.

The 1989 Rescue

In the early hours of February 10, 1989, MS Celebration collided with the Cuban cement freighter Captain San Luis. The freighter had lost power due to electrical problems — no lights, no navigation, no steering. In the darkness, collision was unavoidable.

The Captain San Luis broke in half and sank in just 13 minutes. Three crew members, including the captain, were lost. But Celebration's crew immediately launched rescue operations, pulling 42 survivors from the water and transferring them to Cuban vessels before continuing to Miami.

The incident demonstrated the maritime community's commitment to saving lives at sea, even in the most challenging circumstances.

Frequently Asked Questions

What class was MS Celebration?

MS Celebration was a Holiday-class cruise ship, the last of three sisters: MS Holiday (1985), MS Jubilee (1986), and MS Celebration (1987). These ships were Carnival's first purpose-built "SuperLiners."

Is this the same as Carnival Celebration?

No. This page covers the original MS Celebration (1987-2020). The modern Carnival Celebration is an Excel-class mega-ship launched in 2022.

What happened to the Holiday-class ships?

All three were scrapped: MS Jubilee in 2017, MS Holiday and MS Celebration both in 2021. The COVID-19 pandemic accelerated the retirement of these older vessels.
