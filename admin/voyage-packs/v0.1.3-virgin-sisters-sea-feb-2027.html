Welcome aboard

This pack exists to make one specific cruise easier to plan: Sisters at Sea — A Valentine's Getaway for Solo Cruisers Who Love to Cruise, sailing Saturday February 14 – Saturday February 21, 2027 on Resilient Lady out of PortMiami. It pulls together ship-specific tips, port-specific advice, packing notes, group-cruise rhythm, and solo-women-aware practical content that you'd otherwise have to assemble from a dozen browser tabs over the months between booking and embarkation.

The pack is calm by design. There is no urgency, no upsell, no countdown. You can read it once and put it away, or use it as a working document over the year between booking and sailing.

If you're sailing solo for the first time, you are not the only woman in this group doing so. The site that produced this pack hosts a real authority on solo cruising, Tina Maulsby, and her solo cruising stories are worth reading before you sail. Tina is also your group host, so the rhythm of this trip will reflect her sensibility — practical, warm, low-pressure, and built around the simple promise come solo, leave with sisters.

What's structurally different about Virgin Voyages pricing

Virgin includes most of what the legacy lines (NCL, Royal Caribbean, Carnival) charge for separately. Built into your fare: all gratuities (no daily auto-gratuity), all 20+ included restaurants and cafés (no cover charges, not a buffet), basic Wi-Fi, soft drinks/water/non-pressed juices, and group fitness classes. NOT included: alcohol (beer/wine/cocktails via Bar Tab), specialty coffee at Grounds Club, premium fitness classes, spa, shore excursions, the Squid Ink tattoo studio, optional supplements.

The math feels different from a NCL or Royal cruise because of this. We work it through honestly in Section 5 — Budget Breakdown.

1 · Your Ship: Resilient Lady

Resilient Lady is the third of Virgin Voyages' four Lady Class ships, delivered by Fincantieri at Sestri Ponente in Genoa on 7 December 2022 after an 11-month delay from her original August 2022 slot. By your February 2027 sailing she'll be just over four years old — well past the new-ship teething phase, with her crew rhythms settled and her venues in steady operation.

Her hull is identical to her sisters Scarlet Lady, Valiant Lady, and Brilliant Lady: 110,000 GT, 912 ft long, 2,770 guests at double occupancy (2,860 maximum), and a crew of 1,160. What sets Resilient Lady apart is her travels — she was Virgin's inaugural Greek Isles ship (May 2023), the first Virgin Voyages vessel ever to reach Australia (Sydney, December 2023), and she has since sailed Mediterranean, Puerto Rico, and Caribbean rotations. As of the 2025–26 season she is homeported at PortMiami running 7-night Western Caribbean + Bimini Beach Club round-trips. Your sailing falls within that deployment.

What makes Virgin Voyages structurally different

Virgin Voyages is not a cruise line that has been retrofitted for adults. It was designed adult-from-the-keel:

  • Strictly 18+ — every guest must be 18 or older at the time of sailing. There are no children's clubs, no kids' menus, no waterslides, no family staterooms. The buffet that's normally the loud center of a mass-market ship doesn't exist on Resilient Lady at all.
  • No traditional main dining room. Instead, 20+ restaurants and cafés are included in your fare, with no cover charges. The Wake (steakhouse + raw bar with tableside martini service), Pink Agave (contemporary Mexican, fully gluten-free menu, the widest tequila and mezcal selection at sea), Extra Virgin (Italian trattoria), Gunbae (Korean BBQ at round social tables with soju), Razzle Dazzle (vegetable-forward), The Test Kitchen (chef's experimental tasting menu), The Galley (food-hall-style with multiple counters — this is NOT a buffet), The Pizza Place (Neapolitan-style, made to order), Sun Club Café (poolside Mediterranean), Lick Me Till Ice Cream (yes, that's the real name), Grounds Club (specialty coffee), Ship Eats (24-hour in-cabin, no room-service charge), and more.
  • No formal nights. Smart casual for evenings. Some guests dress up for Scarlet Night (the fleet-wide red party held once per voyage). Nothing requires a tuxedo or a long gown.
  • Adult entertainment culture. The Manor (two-story nightclub designed with House of Yes), the Red Room (Virgin's transformable theatre running in-house immersive productions), Drag Brunch, and the Squid Ink tattoo studio (the first at sea). Virgin's onboard ethos is festival-meets-boutique-hotel rather than family-cruise-resort.
  • Ample balcony cabins. 86% of Resilient Lady's 1,408 cabins have a balcony (Virgin calls them "Sea Terrace"); 93% are at minimum oceanview.

The cabin question for a solo woman

The flyer rates are $2,009 Insider · $2,508 Sea View · $2,652 Sea Terrace (balcony). Three honest reads:

  • Insider ($2,009): the windowless interior. Nicer than equivalent interiors on legacy ships, but you are paying for a windowless room. Choose this if budget is the deciding factor.
  • Sea View ($2,508): an oceanview window cabin. The window matters. Daylight in the morning, ocean horizon when you're getting ready — the world feels different. The ~$500 jump from Insider buys real value.
  • Sea Terrace ($2,652): a private balcony with a hammock-style chair. The flyer differential is unusual — only ~$144 above Sea View. At that delta the Sea Terrace is the obvious choice if budget allows. A place to drink coffee at 6 AM watching the wake; a way to step outside without dressing up. For a Valentine's-themed solo trip with sea days for reading and reflection, the balcony changes the texture of the week.

If you're choosing on the margin, ask Tina what's still in the group block. Sea Terrace cabins fill first.

Dining as the social architecture

Because there is no main dining room with assigned seats, dining is how you find your people on Resilient Lady. The 20+ restaurants are organized as proper restaurants — book a time, walk in, sit, eat, leave when you're done. You can book ahead through the Voyages app once you board (some sailors book on Day 1 for the whole week). You can also walk in — most venues hold a portion of seating for walk-ups.

For a solo woman in a hosted group, this is genuinely good news: you're not stuck at a fixed table for eight strangers every night for a week. You decide each evening — tonight you eat with the group at Pink Agave, tomorrow you eat alone at The Pizza Place with a book, the next night you join a smaller group of new sisters at Gunbae and stay for hours at the round table.

Dietary needs. Pink Agave's full menu is gluten-free; The Galley has multiple stations including dedicated vegan and vegetarian options; allergies are taken seriously and the kitchen prepares severe-allergy meals separately when notified. We work this through in Section 8 — Special diets.

Drinks on Resilient Lady

This is where Virgin Voyages charges separately. Beer, wine, cocktails, and specialty coffee are pay-as-you-go. Virgin doesn't sell a single all-you-can-drink package the way NCL and Royal do; instead, sailors load a Bar Tab at booking (often with a promotional bonus — promotions vary). Bar Tab works at every bar onboard.

For a 7-night sailing, sailors who drink moderately (2–4 drinks per day) typically spend $150–$300. Sailors who don't drink alcohol can ignore the Bar Tab entirely — soft drinks, water, juice, and tea are included. For the math on whether a Bar Tab promotional load is worth it for your actual drinking patterns, the drink calculator on this site applies (the underlying logic translates to Bar Tab).

A note for women in recovery. Virgin's adult-bar-everywhere atmosphere is occasionally framed as a "drinking cruise." It doesn't have to be. Plenty of Sisters at Sea participants will not be drinking. Grounds Club specialty coffee, the gym, Pink Agave's mocktail list, and the wellness programming all run independently of the bar scene. There is no expectation that you drink, and no judgment from crew or fellow sailors if you don't.

Connectivity at sea

Virgin Voyages includes basic Wi-Fi in every cabin category — speeds suitable for messaging family, checking email, and light browsing. The premium upgrade (sufficient for video calling and streaming) is paid. See the full internet at sea guide for the technical details, including how to avoid cellular roaming charges in port.

2 · Day by Day

A representative sequence. Order may differ for your specific sailing — confirm with Tina or in the Voyages app.

Day 1 · Saturday Feb 14 — Embarkation, PortMiami (Valentine's Day)

Check-in window opens late morning. Virgin opens online check-in 45 days before sailing through the Voyages app.

  • 11:30 AM–1:00 PM: First-wave check-in at Terminal V (Virgin's PortMiami home). Drop bags with porter at curb (tip $1–2/bag in cash), proceed through security and check-in.
  • 1:00–2:00 PM: Cabins typically NOT ready until 2 PM. Drop carry-on or carry it. Head to The Galley or Sun Club Café for a relaxed lunch.
  • 2:00 PM: Cabins open. Ask your stateroom host about the Voyages app — your cabin tablet runs it.
  • 3:00 PM: Mandatory muster drill via the app + station check-in. About 10 minutes total.
  • 4:30–5:00 PM: Sailaway. Best from upper aft pool deck or upper open deck near the running track.
  • 6:00 PM onward: Dinner. Tonight will be heavy on Valentine's-themed welcome programming if Virgin runs any — check the Voyages app for the actual scheduled events. Tina's group will likely have an opening-night gathering — check her communications.

Day 1 carry-on: swimsuit, sunscreen, change of clothes, all medications + 3-day buffer, a layer (the ship is air-conditioned), passport, phone charger, refillable water bottle, embarkation paperwork, the Voyages app pre-loaded.

Sailor Band: Virgin issues a wristband (or a card on request) that's your cabin key, payment method, and ID for the week. Most sailors prefer the band — waterproof, hard to lose.

For first-time cruisers in the group: See /first-cruise.html for broader orientation.

Day 2 · Sunday Feb 15 — Sea Day

This is the day you find your rhythm. For a hosted group cruise it's also typically when Tina runs the first hosted meet-and-greet — check her group communications.

Mid-morning options: the Athletic Club (running track and outdoor sport zone, cooler in the morning), a spa visit (sea-day morning intro rates appear), Pink Agave's lunch (gluten-free Caesar with grilled prawns is a standout), Drag Brunch at The Manor if scheduled (paid supplement, genuinely fun).

Afternoon: the first hosted meet-and-greet often happens here. The "come alone, leave with sisters" pattern starts here. Pool deck, Red Room show in the evening.

For solo women in the group: Pace yourself on Day 2. The first meet-and-greet is enough social work for one day. See Tina's Solo Cruiser's Companion for her expanded thinking.

Day 3 · Monday Feb 16 — Freeport (Grand Bahama)

Lucayan Harbour · Grand Bahama

Arrival ~8:00 AM · Depart ~5:00 PM. The cruise port is Lucayan Harbour, several miles from Port Lucaya (the main visitor area).

Grand Bahama is genuinely beautiful but has had a complicated post-Dorian (2019) recovery. The cruise-port experience is fine, the beaches are excellent, and Port Lucaya Marketplace is walkable and pleasant — but Freeport doesn't have the polish of Nassau. Manage expectations and you'll have a great day.

See the full In the Wake Freeport port guide.

  • Easy day: Stay at the cruise terminal. Small beach, vendors, bar service.
  • Half day: Port Lucaya Marketplace — about 10 min by taxi ($10–15 for up to 4 passengers). Open-air shopping, restaurants, beach access at Taino Beach.
  • Full day: Gold Rock Beach in Lucayan National Park — about 30 min by taxi (~$40–50 round trip with wait time). One of the prettiest beaches in the Bahamas.

For solo women: Freeport is a low-stress port for a solo day. Taxi drivers are licensed (queued at the cruise terminal with set zone fares). USD is universal. Easy to find a buddy at breakfast and share a taxi.

For accessibility: The cruise terminal is wheelchair-accessible. Taxis are spacious enough for folding wheelchairs; accessible vehicles can be requested in advance. Port Lucaya is mostly flat with sidewalks.

Pack: swimsuit, towel (Virgin provides), reef-safe sunscreen, sun hat, light cover-up, $60–80 USD cash in $5s and $10s.

Day 4 · Tuesday Feb 17 — Sea Day

Your second sea day. By now you know the ship and you know some of the Sisters.

Suggestions: The Test Kitchen for an early lunch (shorter and quieter than dinner; a way to experience the kitchen without committing), wine tasting at Sip Lounge if scheduled, a Manor dance class (salsa, swing — friendly for Sisters who might attend solo), the Sun Club Café for a long, slow lunch (best inexpensive seat on the ship), reading on your Sea Terrace balcony.

Group rhythm: Tina may schedule a second hosted event today — possibly a group dinner at Pink Agave or Extra Virgin, possibly a hosted onboard activity. Check her communications.

Scarlet Night. Virgin's signature once-per-voyage red party usually happens on a sea day or the night before disembarkation. Bring one red item — even just a red top or red lipstick. Check the Voyages app for which night it falls on.

Day 5 · Wednesday Feb 18 — Progreso (Mexico)

World's longest pier · Yucatán · Mexico

Arrival ~8:00 AM · Depart ~5:00 PM. Progreso is a small Mexican fishing town, but the cruise port is what makes it interesting: a 6.5-kilometer pier (the world's longest) reaches past the shallow Gulf waters. A free tram runs between the ship and the pier-end terminal (about 10 min each way).

Progreso itself is modest. The port exists because of what's inland: Chichén Itzá, Mérida, the cenotes, and the Chicxulub crater. For a Caribbean port, this is one of the most culturally rich shore-day options.

See the full In the Wake Progreso port guide.

  • Easy day: Stay at the pier-end terminal or take the tram into Progreso town. Malecón, calm Gulf waters, fresh ceviche ($8–15), cold cervezas, beach club chair rentals ($10–15).
  • Full day (cultural): Chichén Itzá — ship excursion ~$100–150/person; independent ~$60–90. Long day. Worth it.
  • Full day (city): Mérida — ~45 min inland by bus or van (~$15–20 each way independently; ship excursions $50–80). Spanish colonial architecture, Plaza Grande, excellent local lunch (cochinita pibil at Casa de los Frailes is a regional standout).
  • Half day (swimming): A cenote tour — 30–60 min from Progreso. Dzibilchaltún is closest (ship excursions ~$70–90).

For solo women: Progreso is friendly to solo travelers but the language gap matters. If your Spanish is limited, an organized tour is the saner choice for Chichén Itzá or Mérida. Other Sisters are likely doing the same — pair up at breakfast.

For accessibility: The pier tram is wheelchair-accessible. Chichén Itzá has uneven stone paths and the climb to El Castillo is no longer permitted. Mérida's historic center is partially accessible but cobblestone streets are challenging. Confirm specifics with the tour operator.

Pack: closed-toe walking shoes if doing Chichén Itzá or Mérida, sun hat, reef-safe sunscreen (Mexico legally requires it on beaches and at cenotes), refillable water bottle, $60–100 USD cash. If only doing the malecón — swimsuit, water shoes, $40–60 cash.

Day 6 · Thursday Feb 19 — Sea Day

Your third sea day. Use it. By Day 6 most of the Sisters have settled into their rhythm. Some are double-booked across the day; some have found a quiet corner and are reading. Both are valid.

This is also a good day for the things you've been deferring: the spa treatment you said you'd think about, the lunch with the Sister you keep meaning to talk to longer, the show in the Red Room you missed earlier in the week. Tina may run a final pre-disembarkation gathering today or tomorrow morning.

For solo women specifically: Day 6 is often when the trip's emotional arc shows up. You came alone; you are no longer alone in any practical sense. You'll be home in two days. Sit with that for an hour somewhere quiet — the Sun Club Café in late afternoon is a good place. The point isn't melancholy; the point is noticing the trip has done something worth noticing.

Day 7 · Friday Feb 20 — The Beach Club at Bimini (Bahamas)

Virgin's private beach destination · North Bimini, Bahamas

Arrival ~9:00 AM · Depart ~6:00 PM. The Beach Club at Bimini is Virgin Voyages' private beach destination, separate from Resorts World Bimini (which serves other lines). Just 50 miles from Miami.

Beach Club days are as good as cruise-line private destinations get. Virgin built it specifically for the adult sailor: less of the kids-on-inflatables atmosphere of Castaway Cay or CocoCay, more of a beach-resort-day vibe with included lunch, a DJ in the afternoon, and several pools and beach areas.

See the full In the Wake Bimini port guide.

  • Free with cruise fare: beach access (broad and shaded near the back), lunch buffet at the Beach Club, lounge chairs (first-come; arrive early tender wave for shaded ones), pool access (multiple pools), tram service.
  • Paid: cabanas (~$200–500/day depending on category), premium drinks if not on Bar Tab, optional excursions (snorkel tours, boat charters).

For solo women: Bimini is an easy port for the group to spread out and regroup. The Sisters often do an informal lunch together at the Beach Club around noon — natural meeting point. The DJ-driven afternoon energy is friendly and group-positive without being a meat-market.

For accessibility: The Beach Club tender process is accessible. The Beach Club itself is mostly flat with paved pathways; beach wheelchairs are typically available.

Pack: swimsuit (worn aboard or in your bag), reef-safe sunscreen, sun hat, sunglasses, $20–40 USD cash for tipping, phone in waterproof pouch — see our tech guide.

Day 8 · Saturday Feb 21 — Disembarkation, PortMiami

  • 6:00–8:00 AM: Breakfast available at The Galley and Sun Club Café.
  • 7:30 AM: Customs clearance complete. Self-disembarkation begins.
  • 8:00–10:00 AM: Color-coded groups called.
  • By 11:00 AM: Ship empty.

Tipping at the end of the cruise. Because Virgin includes gratuities in the fare, no end-of-cruise envelope is required. Additional cash tips for outstanding service are appreciated: stateroom host who went above and beyond $20–40 in an envelope; a specific bartender $10–20 cash on the last visit; a specialty restaurant server $10–15 cash at the meal. Bonuses on top of the all-inclusive gratuity, not the obligatory end-of-cruise envelope of legacy lines.

If staying in Florida a night or two: Uber/Lyft to MIA ~$20–35 (20–30 min); to FLL ~$50–70 (45–60 min); South Beach is 15 min from PortMiami.

Group goodbye: Tina will likely organize a final group goodbye. Stay for it if you can — the friendships formed on a Sisters at Sea cruise are real.

7 · Seasickness on a Caribbean Cruise

Seasickness is the question most cruisers ask before their first sailing and the topic most cruisers underplan for after. The Caribbean in February is generally calm — trade winds are steady, hurricane season is well past — but Resilient Lady is on open ocean for several days of this trip, and a 7-night sailing will produce some hours of swell that you'll feel.

The honest baseline

Most cruisers, on a February Caribbean itinerary on a Lady Class ship, will not get meaningfully seasick. Resilient Lady's stabilizers handle most ocean motion well. You'll feel the ship in a few situations:

  • Open-ocean sea days when the route or weather puts the ship in chop
  • Tender approach at the Beach Club at Bimini if the ship is anchored — the smaller tender boats feel motion more sharply than the main ship

If you don't usually get carsick or queasy in other moving environments, the odds you'll get meaningfully seasick on this cruise are low.

If you do get seasick — what works

  1. Look at the horizon. Free, immediate, scientifically valid. Going outside is the most effective single thing you can do.
  2. Eat lightly when feeling rough. Heavy meals + alcohol + ship motion is a worse combination than any individual factor.
  3. Ginger candies or ginger tea. Genuinely helpful for mild nausea; no side effects; safe with all medications. Pack a small tin in your day bag.
  4. Drug-free wristbands (acupressure on the P6 wrist point). Some swear by them, others find them theater. Cheap to try.
  5. Over-the-counter or prescription medication. Talk to your pharmacist or doctor about which fits your medical context — we don't link product purchases here because the right answer depends on what else you take, not on what's convenient to buy.

Cabin choice and motion

If motion sensitivity is a real concern, the cabin matters:

  • Midship, lower deck = least motion. On Resilient Lady, that's roughly Decks 7–9, sections 6–8.
  • Forward and aft cabins feel more motion.
  • Higher decks feel more roll than lower decks.

If you booked an Insider, you don't have horizon access from your cabin — which can intensify nausea if it does set in. If you're highly motion-sensitive, the upgrade from Insider to Sea View or Sea Terrace is worth it for the window alone.

Prevention timing

  • Night before boarding: take an oral preventive even if you don't feel queasy yet. Medication is more effective preventively than as treatment.
  • First morning of the cruise: repeat the dose if a 24-hour formulation isn't on board.
  • First sea day: repeat once more if needed. By Day 3 most cruisers have adapted and need nothing further.

What NOT to do

  • Don't drink alcohol heavily on a rough sea day if you're feeling unwell.
  • Don't read or stare at your phone in bed during pitching seas.
  • Don't lie down in a windowless cabin if you can avoid it. Get to a window or deck.
  • Don't assume a balcony solves everything — some cruisers actually feel motion more on a balcony.

8 · Practical Logistics

The everyday details that no one mentions until they bite. Skim this section once now and once again two weeks before sailing. The detail here is deliberately heavier than in the rest of the pack — these are the questions that, when unanswered, become real problems at the worst possible moments.

Travel documents

A current U.S. passport is the right document for this cruise. It is technically possible to sail this 7-night Caribbean closed-loop itinerary on a passport card or on a certified birth certificate plus state-issued photo ID under federal "closed-loop" rules — but this is the place where saving $130 on a passport application becomes a multi-thousand-dollar problem if anything goes wrong.

Three real scenarios where the passport matters and the alternatives don't:

If you experience a medical emergency at sea or in a foreign port and need to fly home from the closest international airport (Mérida, Nassau, Miami), only a passport will get you on the airplane. Birth certificates won't fly. If you miss the ship at a port and need to catch up to it at the next stop, only a passport will let you fly. If your wallet is stolen in Progreso and you need an emergency travel document from the U.S. consulate in Mérida, the embassy works much faster when you have a paper copy of an existing passport rather than starting from scratch.

The technical rules for U.S. citizens, in order of practical importance:

  • Passport validity must extend through your return date. Mexico has been lenient with the "six months past return" rule but enforcement varies; the Bahamas requires a passport valid for the duration of stay. If your passport expires within nine months of February 21, 2027, renew now — passport processing as of 2026 typically runs 4–8 weeks for routine, 2–3 weeks for expedited.
  • Name match must be exact. The name on your passport must match your Virgin Voyages booking exactly — every character, every middle name, every hyphen, every suffix. Virgin's automated systems will deny boarding for mismatch. If you legally changed your name since booking, contact Virgin Voyages via Tina the moment you have the legal paperwork. Updates take 2–4 weeks to propagate.
  • REAL ID applies to your domestic flight to Miami, not to the cruise. TSA began full REAL ID enforcement on May 7, 2025, so every domestic flight in the U.S. now requires REAL-ID-compliant identification at the TSA checkpoint. Look at your driver's license: if there's a star or a flag in the upper right corner, you're compliant.
  • Non-U.S. passport holders need to check visa requirements for each port. U.S. citizens don't need visas for the Bahamas or Mexico for cruise stops; many other passport holders do. Tina can connect you with documentation guidance, but get on this 6+ months out.

Travel insurance

Strongly recommended for a 7-night Caribbean trip. Read this subsection slowly; the math is real.

Why your home insurance is not enough. U.S. health insurance — including Medicare and standard employer-sponsored plans — does not cover medical care outside U.S. waters. Onboard medical visits on Resilient Lady are not covered. Care at hospitals in Mexico or the Bahamas is not covered. Most importantly, medical evacuation by helicopter or charter air ambulance from international waters back to a U.S. hospital is not covered, and that's the line item that gets expensive: a sea-to-shore evacuation typically runs $30,000–$80,000; an international air-ambulance flight to a U.S. hospital can run $150,000–$300,000.

What good travel insurance covers, in priority order:

  1. Emergency medical evacuation (the line item that bankrupts uninsured travelers): aim for at least $250,000 coverage; $500,000 is comfortable. Non-negotiable for international cruise travel.
  2. Medical care at sea or in port: at least $100,000 coverage; $250,000+ is comfortable.
  3. Trip cancellation: reimburses cruise fare, hotel, airfare if a covered reason prevents you from sailing.
  4. Trip interruption: reimburses costs if you have to leave the cruise mid-sail. This is what saves you if you miss the ship at a port and have to fly to catch up.
  5. Baggage delay / loss: smaller dollar amounts but practical.
  6. 24/7 emergency assistance hotline: all reputable insurers include this.

The "Cancel for Any Reason" (CFAR) upgrade. Standard cancellation requires a covered reason. CFAR covers everything — cold feet, family pet emergency, work demands, you just changed your mind. CFAR typically reimburses 50–75%, costs an additional 20–40% on top of the base premium, and must usually be purchased within 14–21 days of your initial trip deposit.

Pre-existing conditions are the most-claimed exclusion. Most insurers offer a pre-existing condition waiver that covers these, but it has a strict purchase deadline (usually within 14–21 days of initial trip deposit).

What to actually do.

  • Buy travel insurance within two weeks of paying your cruise deposit. Don't wait until 90 days out.
  • Get the pre-existing condition waiver; it costs little and protects significantly.
  • Add CFAR if your situation feels uncertain.
  • Use a third-party insurer, not the cruise line's bundled offer. Allianz Global Assistance, Travelex, Travel Guard, IMG, and World Nomads are the major reputable U.S.-market providers. Compare on InsureMyTrip or Squaremouth.
  • Keep the policy number, the 24/7 assistance line, and a one-page summary in your wallet alongside your passport.

Onboard money: the Virgin Voyages cashless system

Once you board, your Sailor Band (or a card if you opted for that) replaces cash for everything onboard. The system is invisible until something goes wrong.

At check-in, you link a credit card to your stateroom account. Throughout the week, every Bar Tab purchase, every spa treatment, every premium fitness class, every Squid Ink commission, every shore excursion booked through the ship accumulates on your shipboard account. On disembarkation day, your linked card is charged for the total. Virgin emails the itemized statement in the morning.

Because gratuities are already in your fare, the end-of-cruise total is materially smaller than it would be on a legacy line — sometimes the only line items are Bar Tab and one excursion. That is, again, the structural difference.

Credit card vs debit card. Use a credit card if you have one. Virgin places a "hold" on the linked card during the cruise — typically 15–30% of expected total charges, refreshed daily. On a debit card, this hold reduces your available cash, sometimes for 3–7 business days after the cruise ends.

Foreign transaction fees. The card you link should not charge foreign transaction fees. Virgin Voyages' billing entity is U.S.-based for most charges, so your end-of-cruise charge processes as a U.S. transaction.

Cash you'll actually need. The Sailor Band handles ~95% of onboard charges. The remaining 5% is cash, and on a Virgin sailing it's smaller than on legacy lines because gratuities are included:

  • Porters at the cruise terminal: $1–2 per bag, expected, in cash
  • Tipping in port (taxi drivers, tour operators, beach club attendants in Freeport and Progreso): cash, in U.S. dollars, in $5 and $10 denominations
  • Bonus tips above what's already included: $5–10 in an envelope for a stateroom host or specialty server who went above and beyond — optional and always appreciated

A fair budget for cash on a 7-night Virgin cruise is $150–200 per person, brought as $5s and $10s. Less than the legacy-line equivalent because gratuity is already paid.

Tipping etiquette on Virgin Voyages

This is the section most different from a legacy-line pack. Because Virgin includes gratuities in the fare, the floor is already paid. Cash tips are bonuses, not the obligatory end-of-cruise envelope.

The practical landscape:

Pre-cruise / embarkation tipping. Porters at the cruise terminal handle your luggage from curb to ship. They are expected to be tipped — this is not Virgin-included. The norm is $1–2 per bag, in cash, handed at the curb.

Stateroom host at end of cruise. Many sailors add a cash tip to their stateroom host on top of the included gratuity, in an envelope at end of cruise. The norm on Virgin is $20–40 per cabin for a 7-night, more if your host did something memorable. Genuinely optional — Virgin's crew is well-compensated through the included gratuity. Don't tip out of guilt.

Bartenders. A 20% gratuity is automatically added to Bar Tab charges. Cash tipping above that ($1 per drink for a bartender who knows your name and pour preference) is appreciated but not expected.

Specialty restaurant servers. Same logic — gratuity is built in. Adding $10–15 cash for an exceptional server at a memorable meal is appreciated; for an ordinary meal, no extra is needed.

Tour operators in port. Virgin doesn't include port-tour gratuities. Independent shore excursion guides typically expect U.S. norms: $5–10 per person for a half-day group tour, $10–20 per person for a full-day, $5–10 per person for the bus driver if separate.

Taxi drivers. 10–15% on the metered fare or agreed price; round up to the next dollar.

Beach Club at Bimini staff. $5–10 if a server took good care of you for the afternoon. Virgin's onboard gratuity covers Beach Club crew, but the cash bonus is appreciated.

The general principle: extra is optional and always appreciated. Virgin doesn't run on under-tipped crew — gratuity is structural.

Port-day safety and what to do if you miss the ship

Port-day rhythms in the Caribbean are well-established but vigilance matters.

Choosing a taxi. At Caribbean ports, licensed taxis are easy to identify — clear markings, government-issued ID badges visible, either a meter or a posted official rate sheet. In the Bahamas (Freeport, Bimini), licensed taxis have set zone fares posted at the cruise terminal. In Progreso, Mexico, the licensed taxis at the pier-end terminal are union-regulated. Use only licensed drivers. If a driver tells you the meter is broken, walk to the next driver — common scam.

Currency at ports. USD is widely accepted in the Bahamas and Mexico. Small bills ($5s, $10s, $20s) are most useful. Local currency change-back happens; if you receive Bahamian dollars (1:1 with USD) or Mexican pesos as change, you can use them for the rest of the day.

Pickpocketing and personal awareness. Pickpocketing is not endemic at any of these ports but it does happen. Don't pull out a phone at the pier or near tour-vendor crowds. Keep your phone in a hard-to-reach pocket; passport and main money in a hotel-style internal money belt or zipped inside-jacket pocket. A small day-pack worn on the front (not the back) on busy promenades is sensible.

Common scams to be aware of (not to fear). They mostly involve pressure-selling: pier-side "free" guided walks that turn into shop-stop tours, fake "official" tour vendors wearing matching shirts, "watch your bag" scams, counterfeit goods sold as authentic. None escalate beyond financial pressure. Politely decline, walk away, don't engage.

If you miss the ship at a port (the "all aboard" deadline). This happens more often than cruise lines admit. The cruise line's "all aboard" time is typically 30 minutes before sailaway. Here's the actual protocol:

  1. First, call the ship's port agent immediately. The contact information is in the Voyages app.
  2. The port agent will advise on next steps. Typically: catch up to the ship at the next port. For this itinerary, the next stop after Freeport is Progreso (you'd need to fly Nassau → Mérida); after Progreso, the next stop is Bimini; after Bimini, you simply wait for return to PortMiami.
  3. You are responsible for the catch-up cost. Flights from Caribbean ports run $300–800. A hotel night runs $100–250. Total: typically $400–1,000 plus meals and ground transport.
  4. Travel insurance with "trip interruption" coverage reimburses this if you have it.
  5. Bring your passport in port, not in your cabin safe.
  6. Time zones. Resilient Lady keeps "ship time" — usually U.S. Eastern Time on a Caribbean run from PortMiami. Bahamas observes Eastern Time year-round. Mexico's Yucatán observes Central Standard Time year-round — so during EST months (your February sailing) Progreso's local clock is one hour behind ship time. The only clock that matters for re-boarding is ship time.

If your situation is more serious than "missed the ship" — illness in port that requires hospitalization, lost passport with no way to fly, criminal incident — the U.S. Embassy or Consulate in each country handles emergency assistance. See Section 6 — Emergency Contacts for the full contact list and the family handoff card.

Special diets, allergies, and religious dietary needs

Notify Virgin Voyages at booking AND have Tina confirm the request was logged in your reservation. Virgin's kitchens are genuinely capable of accommodating a wide range of dietary needs.

Severe food allergies (peanut, tree-nut, shellfish, egg, dairy, soy). Virgin takes severe allergies seriously. At booking, log the allergy in your reservation. At check-in, mention it again. At each restaurant, mention it to your server when seated — they'll bring the chef out if needed. If your allergy is anaphylactic-grade, bring two epinephrine auto-injectors regardless.

Celiac disease and gluten sensitivity. Virgin handles gluten-free well. Pink Agave's full menu is gluten-free; The Galley has gluten-free stations; main restaurants all have gluten-free options. Cross-contamination risk is lower than legacy-line buffets because there's no buffet — every meal is plated.

Vegan and vegetarian. Razzle Dazzle is Virgin's vegetable-forward restaurant. Pink Agave has multiple vegan options. Extra Virgin will adapt pasta dishes on request. The Galley has dedicated vegan and vegetarian stations. No advance notice needed for vegan/vegetarian; ask at the meal.

Kosher and halal observance. Limited compared to dedicated charter sailings. Some pre-packaged frozen kosher meals may be available on request — confirm at booking via Tina, well in advance, not at the day of. For halal, the same protocol applies. Diabetic and low-sugar dietary management. Both restaurant menus and The Galley have options for diabetic management. Tell your server on Day 1. Insulin storage — most Virgin cabins have a small fridge; if yours doesn't, ask your stateroom host to provide a small medical fridge (free).

Laundry on board

Any group of solo women has political diversity, life-stage diversity, and class diversity. A Sisters at Sea cruise has — almost universally — an unstated agreement to leave that diversity at the gangway. The week works because everyone knows the deal.

10 · Solo Female Considerations

Why Virgin Voyages fits a solo woman well

Several structural reasons:

  • Adults-only by design. The buffet that's normally the loud center of a mass-market ship doesn't exist on Resilient Lady. There is no kid energy at the pool, no family chaos in the dining room, no waterslide noise.
  • 20+ included restaurants make eating alone feel normal. No assigned dinner table for eight. You can walk into Pink Agave alone with a book at 6 PM and feel completely at home; you can also share a Gunbae round table with five Sisters and stay for hours.
  • The demographic on Virgin skews younger and more diverse than legacy lines. Average age on a Virgin sailing tends to be 35–55 (vs. 55–70 on a typical legacy Caribbean run). More women travel solo on Virgin specifically because of this demographic shift; you will not be the only woman traveling alone in the dining room.
  • No formal nights, no traditional cruise conventions. Smart casual evenings; Scarlet Night as the only "themed" night and red is the only requirement. Less pressure, more room to be yourself.
  • Bar Tab over drink package. The drink-package math on legacy lines often makes solo cruisers feel like they're paying for something they don't use; Virgin's pay-as-you-go Bar Tab is more honest for a moderate drinker.

Solo female safety

Solo cruising is overwhelmingly safe — cruise ships are among the most surveilled travel environments on earth, with CCTV throughout common areas, a 24-hour security presence, controlled access to passenger decks, and crew trained in emergency response. The Cruise Vessel Security and Safety Act (federal U.S. law since 2010) mandates specific safety protocols including security camera coverage, the trained Sexual Assault Response Team that every U.S.-departing cruise ship carries, and reporting obligations. None of this means risk is zero, but the baseline is genuinely high.

That said, a few habits make sense:

Your cabin door. The standard Virgin cabin door has both a deadbolt and a privacy latch (a small swing-arm that prevents the door from opening even with a master key). Use both at night. Some solo cruisers add a portable doorstop or door wedge under the door at night — cheap (under $10), takes no space. For travelers who experience anxiety in unfamiliar sleeping environments, the wedge is genuinely calming.

Share your itinerary before sailing. Send a copy of the day-by-day to one trusted person ashore — adult child, sibling, close friend. Include the ship name (Resilient Lady), your cabin number once assigned, your travel insurance policy number, and Tina's contact. Share the U.S. State Department's Overseas Citizens Services line as the right call for any true at-sea emergency: +1-888-407-4747 (from U.S./Canada) or +1-202-501-4444 (from abroad), 24 hours a day, every day. The State Department can locate the ship, contact Virgin Voyages through official channels, and coordinate consular services if needed — at no cost to your family. This isn't paranoia; it's the same hand-off you'd give if you were going on any extended solo trip.

Photo copies of essential documents. Before sailing, photograph or scan: passport ID page, driver's license, all credit cards (front and back), travel insurance card, medication list with prescribing doctors. Email these to yourself and to your trusted contact ashore. Keep the originals in your cabin safe; carry only what you need to the port.

Port-day boundaries. A friendly conversation at trivia is not an invitation to dinner. A friendly dinner is not an invitation to a port-day. A port-day is not an invitation to anything else. You set the pace; "no thanks, I'm enjoying the quiet today" is always available, and on a Sisters at Sea cruise specifically, declining isn't going to fracture the group — every solo cruiser there has had moments of needing alone-time.

Boundaries with romantic interest. Solo cruises are sometimes described as singles cruises or hookup environments. Sisters at Sea is not that. Tina screens her group toward women who are seeking community, not dating. That said, romantic chemistry can happen on cruises. The framework solo women report works: be willing to share a meal with a fellow sailor; not willing to share your cabin number; willing to exchange contact information after the cruise; not willing to invite anyone into your cabin during the cruise. You don't owe an explanation; the framework is the explanation.

If something feels off. Resilient Lady has 24-hour security presence. Sailor Services on Deck 5 is the front door for any concern — they respond fast. If a fellow sailor is being intrusive, if a crew member is overstepping, if you feel followed in a corridor, security can intervene. Most solo cruisers go a lifetime without needing to use security; the option exists if you do.

In port, walk with another Sister when possible. One of the genuinely underrated benefits of a hosted group cruise — you naturally have travel companions for port days. Tour vendors and pickpockets target solo travelers more than pairs or groups. If you can't find a partner for the day, stay in well-trafficked tourist areas, especially in Progreso (where the language gap can feel disorienting on first arrival). Tell Tina or the group what your day plan is.

The "Sisters at Sea" social rhythm

The Valentine's framing of this cruise — come solo, leave with sisters — is the design principle. Tina runs hosted events with the deliberate intention of generating the conditions for friendship without forcing it. The Day 1 welcome reception, the Day 2 meet-and-greet, the mid-week group dinner, the farewell — each is structured to lower the activation energy for connection.

You can opt in or opt out at any moment. The hosted events are voluntary. If you went to the welcome reception and the meet-and-greet but you're tired by Day 4 and want to read on your balcony for the day, that's a complete sentence. Other Sisters will respect it. Tina won't take it personally.

The friendships are real if you tend them. Sisters at Sea participants who report the most lasting connections are the ones who said yes to the first invitation, exchanged contact info before disembarkation, followed up within two weeks of getting home, and made plans to meet for coffee or a weekend within six months. Tina facilitates the email exchange; the maintenance is yours.

For solo women carrying loss or transition

Some Sisters on this cruise will be processing recent loss — a spouse who died, a marriage that ended, a parent in late illness, kids who just left for college, a career chapter that closed. The Valentine's date is deliberately loaded: it's the holiday that magnifies loneliness for women in the middle of those transitions.

The trip will not fix what brought you to it. That's not what cruises do. What this trip can do — at its best — is offer a week of structure, sun, slow meals, ocean motion, and other women who get it. Predictable schedule. No hard decisions. The rhythm of the ship doing the navigating while you do the resting.

Practical pastoral notes (no advertising, no urgency):

  • The Sun Club Café in late afternoon is the gentlest space on the ship for the kind of reflection that grief and transition require.
  • Tina's Why I Started Solo Cruising is worth reading before you sail.
  • The group will not interrogate. Stories are shared by invitation, not by interrogation.
  • Several Sisters will be in similar situations. You won't be the only one. You won't be visibly sad in a sea of cheerful couples — there are no couples here, and the demographic is calibrated for exactly your kind of trip.

If you're in active acute grief (the first 6–12 weeks after a major loss), a cruise may be too much structure. If you're 6+ months into the integration of a loss, a cruise can be exactly right. Trust your own read.

11 · Solo Cruising Deep-Dive

The cabin question — solo specifics

Virgin Voyages does not offer a dedicated solo cabin the way NCL does (the Studio model). Instead, single occupancy of a regular cabin is how solo cruising works on Virgin — with a single supplement that varies by sailing.

For this Sisters at Sea group, Tina has negotiated solo pricing directly into the flyer rates ($2,009 Insider, $2,508 Sea View, $2,652 Sea Terrace). That's a structural difference from a normal Virgin booking — you're not paying a 175–200% single supplement.

If the flyer rates feel high relative to a comparable legacy-line solo cabin, remember:

  • Virgin's fare includes gratuities (~$140 saved)
  • Virgin's fare includes basic Wi-Fi (~$120 saved)
  • Virgin's fare includes all dining, including specialty ($150–300 saved)
  • Virgin's cabins are larger and 86% have balconies

The all-in math typically lands close to even by the door.

Where to find people on Resilient Lady

  • Hosted group events — Tina's gatherings are designed for solo connection; show up
  • The Galley's communal coffee station in the morning — a friendly low-stakes morning crowd
  • Grounds Club for specialty coffee — a seated, talkative space
  • Pink Agave's bar seating — solo-friendly, with the bartender often making conversation
  • The Manor's earlier hours (before 11 PM) — the dance floor opens up for friendly social dancing rather than the late-night party
  • Group fitness classes — friendly demographics, low conversation barrier
  • Trivia at the bars — typically afternoons on sea days, the friendliest organized social activity

What to expect

Solo cruising on Resilient Lady is good for the same reason group cruising on Resilient Lady is good: the ship is large enough that you have privacy when you want it and crowds when you want them. The risk is that the daily structure of cruise life can feel either liberating or lonely without a partner. Build rituals: the same morning coffee spot, the same trivia hour, the same sunset deck.

A small joy: cruise ducks

There's a tradition on cruise ships of bringing small rubber ducks and hiding them around the ship for fellow passengers to find. Solo women cruisers often find this delightfully ridiculous — and that's exactly the point. See the cruise duck tradition article. The site that produced this pack also runs a Duck Club affinity tier of supporters who find the tradition meaningful. (No pressure to join; just mentioned because it's of-a-piece with the spirit of group cruising.)

Closing

This pack exists to make one specific cruise easier. It's calmer than most planning content, by design, because the audience deserves that calm.

The cruise itself is a real opportunity: a hosted group of solo women, on Virgin Voyages' adult-by-design Resilient Lady, sailing one of the more interesting itineraries the Caribbean has to offer (Freeport, Progreso's Mayan world, Virgin's own Beach Club at Bimini), hosted by someone who genuinely knows solo travel. The Valentine's framing — come solo, leave with sisters — is a specific kind of trip that doesn't exist elsewhere on the legacy lines.

What I'd want a friend to know, if she were preparing:

  • Trust Tina. She has a solo cruiser's instinct and a hosted-group's discipline.
  • Pack the medication buffer. The single most-recommended action in this pack.
  • Say yes to the first dinner invitation. It compounds.
  • Bring something red for Scarlet Night. Even if you'd never wear red at home — pack one item.
  • The Sea Terrace upgrade is small money for the difference it makes. $144 above Sea View, at the flyer rate.
  • Don't try to do everything. "Doing nothing" on a sea day is a valid use of the day.
  • The Beach Club at Bimini deserves its full day. The beach itself is the experience.
  • Take photos of Sisters, not just of yourself. The friendships are the souvenir worth keeping.
