Captain's Logbook: Antarctica

The author visited Antarctica in 2019 aboard an expedition cruise from Ushuaia. Observations reflect personal experience; verify current conditions before travel.

I had never experienced fear like the Drake Passage. Twenty-four hours into the crossing, our expedition ship rolled through swells that sent everything not bolted down sliding across surfaces. My cabin felt like a carnival ride operated by someone malicious. I wedged myself into my bunk with pillows, listened to the hull groan against the waves, and wondered if this journey was a mistake. The medication helped with nausea but not with the constant motion, the sense that the ocean was testing our resolve. I slept in fragments between alarms — not emergencies, just the ship's systems responding to the battering. By the second morning, the seas began to calm. And then, through fog clearing like a curtain, I saw my first iceberg. The fear dissolved into something closer to reverence.

That first iceberg was the size of a small apartment building, floating impossibly in water that had turned from gray-green to cobalt blue. I stood on deck in the cold, watching it pass, trying to comprehend that I was seeing ancient ice — snow that fell ten thousand years ago, compressed until all air escaped and only crystalline blue remained. The expedition leader announced we had crossed the Antarctic Convergence, where Southern Ocean currents meet warmer waters. The temperature had dropped noticeably. The air tasted different: sharper, cleaner, carrying the mineral scent of ice. I breathed deeply and felt something crack inside my chest — some protective numbness I hadn't known I was carrying. Antarctica was already working on me.

Our first landing at Neko Harbour placed me on the Antarctic continent itself — not an island, but mainland Antarctica. The Zodiac crunched onto pebbles and I stepped onto ground that felt like no other ground on Earth. Around me, thousands of gentoo penguins went about their business: braying territorial calls, stealing pebbles from neighbors' nests, waddling down penguin highways worn smooth by countless generations. The smell was extraordinary — musky, fishy, pungent with guano — and somehow it became endearing rather than offensive. These were working birds, raising chicks in one of Earth's harshest environments. Their indifference to my presence was humbling. I was not predator, not prey, not even interesting. Just another odd creature in a red parka.

Behind the colony, a glacier wall stretched impossibly high, blue and white and groaning with the weight of accumulated time. I stood watching when it happened: a crack like rifle fire, a pause, then a section of ice the size of a house calving into the bay. The splash sent waves rippling toward shore, gentle by the time they reached my boots. I was grateful for that moment — witnessing geological time, change that usually happens invisibly, compressed into seconds of thundering collapse. The sound echoed across the harbor. Penguins nearby barely looked up. This was their normal. For me, it was revelation.

Paradise Harbour earned its name completely. The bay opened between ice-clad peaks, so still the water became a perfect mirror — mountains reflected with impossible clarity, clouds duplicated exactly on the surface. Our Zodiac cruised through this doubled world, past sculptural icebergs carved into arches and towers by wave and sun. I reached down and touched the water: cold enough to make my fingers ache within five seconds. A leopard seal surfaced nearby, its spotted coat glistening, head swiveling to track us with intelligent predatory interest. It held my gaze for three heartbeats — long enough to remind me who belonged here and who was merely visiting — before slipping beneath the surface.

At Port Lockroy I sent postcards from the world's southernmost post office, knowing they would take months to arrive by supply ship. The British base, restored as a museum, contained beds still made, tins still on shelves, as if the occupants had stepped out for a walk and never returned. Outside, gentoo penguins nested against the building, stealing pebbles from each other with comic persistence. I bought patches and stamps, aware that proceeds supported preservation. Standing in that cold wind, listening to penguin calls across the bay, I thought about the men who had wintered here in utter isolation, monitoring weather for nations far away, sustained by purpose and tea and the absolute certainty that their work mattered.

The cons: Antarctica is expensive — seriously expensive. Expedition cruises start around $8,000 USD and climb to $30,000 or more for luxury vessels. The Drake Passage is genuinely difficult; I watched passengers confined to cabins for two days. Weather cancels landings without warning. Accessibility is extremely limited — Zodiac boarding requires mobility, beaches require stamina, and there is no infrastructure for those with physical limitations. This is expedition travel, not cruise ship pampering. It demands something from you.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Sitting alone on a rock at Neko Harbour while penguins waddled past, watching a glacier calve into the bay. The ice fell in slow motion — thousands of tons peeling away, crashing into dark water, sending waves that reached my boots thirty seconds later. My eyes filled with tears. I was witnessing time itself, glaciers formed before human civilization now melting into warming seas. Beauty and loss intertwined. I understood then that Antarctica is not a vacation destination but a pilgrimage site, a place that asks nothing of you except witness. That asking is enough.

What I Learned: Antarctica taught me that some places exceed human scale. Standing among ten thousand penguins, I understood colony. Watching icebergs larger than cathedrals, I glimpsed geological time. Feeling cold penetrate every layer, I knew what wilderness means — places indifferent to human comfort. I came expecting dramatic scenery and found something deeper: perspective. The continent cares nothing for our anxieties and appointments. It was here before us and will remain long after. That indifference is not cruel but clarifying. Worth every euro, every seasick hour, every frozen finger. I came home changed.

The Cruise Port

Antarctica has no ports, no terminals, no infrastructure. This is expedition cruising at its purest. Ships anchor in protected bays while Zodiacs ferry passengers to shore. Every landing is managed by expedition staff following IAATO (International Association of Antarctica Tour Operators) protocols that protect the pristine environment.

Most expeditions depart from Ushuaia, Argentina — the world's southernmost city — crossing the Drake Passage (36-48 hours each way) to reach the Antarctic Peninsula. Some itineraries include the Falkland Islands and South Georgia. Total voyage duration ranges from 10 days (peninsula only) to 21+ days (comprehensive expedition). Ships range from 100-passenger expedition vessels offering multiple landings per day to larger cruise ships limited to scenic cruising without landings.

Accessibility: Antarctic travel presents significant mobility challenges. Zodiac boarding requires stepping down into inflatable boats from moving platforms. Landings involve wading through surf or climbing over rocks. Terrain is uneven — snow, pebbles, mud. There is no wheelchair access anywhere. Travelers with physical limitations should discuss specific requirements with expedition companies before booking to determine if the voyage is suitable.

Getting Around Antarctica

Transportation in Antarctica means one thing: Zodiac inflatable boats. These sturdy craft carry 8-12 passengers plus crew, departing from the ship's gangway or marina platform. Boarding requires stepping down into a moving boat while staff assist — you need reasonable balance and mobility. Wear every layer and expect spray; waterproof gear is essential. The ride to shore takes 5-15 minutes depending on landing distance and sea conditions.

Once ashore, you walk. Landing sites have no paths, no facilities, no infrastructure. Expedition staff mark boundaries with flags; you stay within these limits to protect wildlife and the fragile ecosystem. Typical landing duration is 1.5-2.5 hours. When conditions allow, ships attempt two landings per day — morning and afternoon. Weather determines everything; plans change constantly. Flexibility is the essential Antarctic virtue.

Between landings, the ship itself becomes transportation — cruising through iceberg fields, along glacier faces, through narrow channels. Observation decks offer 360-degree views. Many passengers spend as much time watching from the ship as exploring on land. Independent exploration does not exist here; you're always with your expedition group, always following IAATO protocols. Book ahead through your ship excursion desk for add-on activities like kayaking.

The Drake Passage: This is unavoidable. The 1,000-kilometer crossing between South America and Antarctica takes 36-48 hours each way through waters where Atlantic, Pacific, and Southern Ocean currents collide. Conditions range from calm ("Drake Lake") to severe ("Drake Shake"). Pack motion sickness medication before boarding. The crossing is part of the adventure — every explorer before you endured it too.

Landing Sites & Regions

Antarctica has no beaches in the tropical sense. Landing sites are rocky shores, pebble beaches, and ice edges. Each offers different wildlife and landscapes.

Antarctic Peninsula: The most accessible region and focus of most expeditions. Dramatic mountain scenery, massive penguin colonies, research stations, and relatively mild temperatures (20-40°F in summer).

South Shetland Islands: Often the first Antarctic landings, including volcanic Deception Island with its natural harbor inside a caldera. Chinstrap penguin colonies and fur seal populations.

South Georgia: Extended itineraries visit this sub-Antarctic island with the world's largest king penguin colonies — hundreds of thousands of birds. Also elephant seals and Shackleton's grave at Grytviken.

Falkland Islands: Some itineraries begin or end here. Rockhopper penguins, black-browed albatross colonies, and British colonial settlements.

Excursions & Activities

Zodiac Landing at Penguin Colony (Included, 1.5-2.5 Hours)

The essential Antarctic experience. Zodiac to shore, then walking among thousands of penguins who regard humans with complete indifference. Gentoo, chinstrap, and Adelie penguins depending on location. Expedition naturalists explain behavior and conservation. Photography is extraordinary — penguins approach within feet if you stay still. Included with all expedition cruises. Moderate stamina required for cold exposure and uneven terrain.

Zodiac Cruising Among Icebergs (Included, 1-1.5 Hours)

When landing conditions are unsuitable or as an alternative activity, Zodiac cruising offers intimate encounters with ice formations. Navigate among bergs sculpted into arches, towers, and grottos. Watch for leopard seals on floes and penguins porpoising through dark water. Bring waterproof camera protection. Included with expedition fare and suitable for most fitness levels, though cold endurance needed.

Kayaking ($500-1000 USD supplement, 2-3 Hours)

Limited spots — book ahead when reserving your cruise for guaranteed participation. Paddle among icebergs with trained guides, experiencing Antarctica at water level. Wildlife encounters are intimate: curious seals, diving penguins, seabirds skimming past. Previous kayaking experience required. Dry suits and equipment provided. Book through your ship excursion coordinator at reservation time; spots fill months ahead.

Polar Plunge (Free-$20 USD, 15 Minutes)

Jump into Antarctic waters wearing nothing but a swimsuit. Water temperature 28-32°F. You submerge briefly, shriek, scramble out, and receive a certificate. Usually happens at Deception Island where volcanic warmth slightly moderates the shock. Safety crew stands ready. Bragging rights last forever. Surprisingly popular once you see everyone else doing it.

Camping on Ice ($300-600 USD, Evening-Morning)

Select expedition ships offer overnight camping on Antarctic ice. Sleep in bivvy bags under midnight sun (December-January) with penguins for neighbors. No tents — just sleeping bags, pads, and stars. Remarkably peaceful once you accept the cold. Limited spots require advance booking through your expedition company. Worth the discomfort for the experience of waking on the ice.

Mountaineering and Snowshoeing ($100-300 USD, 2-4 Hours)

Some expedition vessels offer guided treks to viewpoints above landing sites. Snowshoes provided for walking across glacial terrain. The perspective from above colonies and bays is stunning — watching penguin highways from elevation reveals patterns invisible at ground level. Requires good fitness and proper layering; guides assess conditions and adjust routes accordingly. Book through ship excursion desk when activities are announced during the voyage.

Scenic Ship Cruising (Included, Variable)

Between landings, expedition ships cruise through iceberg fields, along glacier faces, and through narrow channels like the famous Lemaire Channel ("Kodak Gap"). Observation decks offer 360-degree views. Naturalists provide commentary on wildlife spotted from the ship. For passengers unable to land due to mobility limitations or weather cancellations, scenic cruising delivers extraordinary Antarctic experiences without leaving the vessel. Hot drinks available on deck during extended cruising.

Food & Dining

There are no restaurants in Antarctica. All meals come from your expedition ship, and quality varies significantly by operator and vessel.

Expedition Ship Dining: Most ships serve buffet breakfast and lunch with plated dinners. Quality ranges from basic cruise-ship fare to exceptional gourmet cuisine depending on your chosen operator. Dietary restrictions accommodated with advance notice. Hot soup and drinks available after cold Zodiac excursions — worth appreciating more than you might expect.

Pack Snacks: Long days in Zodiacs can leave gaps between meals. Bring energy bars, trail mix, chocolate. Cold weather burns calories rapidly; you'll be hungrier than normal.

Hydration: Dry polar air and physical activity create dehydration risk. Drink water even when not thirsty. Ships provide reusable bottles; fill them regularly.

Celebration Meals: Most ships mark crossing the Antarctic Circle or reaching furthest south with special dinners and champagne on deck. These become voyage highlights and genuine moments of community among passengers.

Important Notices

Drake Passage Preparation: Start motion sickness medication 24 hours before entering the Drake, not after you feel ill. Scopolamine patches, meclizine, or ginger remedies help many travelers. Secure loose items in your cabin. The rough crossing is part of the adventure.

Weather Cancellations: Landings cancel without warning when conditions make Zodiac operations unsafe. Expedition leaders make final decisions; no specific site is ever guaranteed. Itineraries are suggestions, not promises. Acceptance of uncertainty is essential.

Camera Batteries: Cold drains batteries rapidly — far faster than you expect. Carry multiple spares in inner pockets close to body warmth. Bring more memory cards than you think necessary. Condensation forms when moving between cold outside and warm ship interior; silica packets help.

Insurance Requirement: Emergency evacuation from Antarctica costs hundreds of thousands of dollars. Travel insurance with specific polar expedition coverage is mandatory for most operators. Verify your policy explicitly includes Antarctica before departing.

IAATO Protocols: Stay 5 meters from wildlife (unless they approach you). Take nothing except photographs. Leave nothing behind. Walk only in designated areas. These rules exist to protect an irreplaceable ecosystem.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Choosing an Antarctic expedition requires careful research beyond price comparison. Verify your operator is an IAATO member — this ensures adherence to protocols protecting both environment and passengers. Ship size matters enormously: vessels under 200 passengers can land everyone multiple times daily since IAATO limits shore parties to 100. Ships over 500 passengers typically offer scenic cruising only without landings.

The honest assessment: Antarctica delivers profound experiences but demands significant investment. Costs start around $8,000 USD for budget vessels with shared facilities and climb to $30,000+ for luxury suites on premium ships. The Drake Passage tests even experienced travelers. Landing conditions challenge mobility. This is genuine expedition travel requiring flexibility, physical capability, and acceptance that nature controls the schedule.

Worth the cost? For those seeking transformative travel — wilderness so complete it recalibrates perspective, wildlife encounters unlike anywhere else on Earth, and the humbling experience of standing where few humans ever stand — absolutely worth every euro. I came home changed by Antarctica in ways that justify the expense and seasick hours. The continent gives back immeasurably to those willing to meet its demands. Book ahead through reputable operators for best cabin selection and activity availability.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: When is the best time to visit Antarctica?
A: November-December offers pristine snow and penguin courtship. January-February has warmest temperatures (up to 40°F) and penguin chicks. February-March brings increased whale activity and dramatic late-season light. Each period offers different experiences; there is no wrong time during the summer season.

Q: How rough is the Drake Passage really?
A: Conditions vary unpredictably from "Drake Lake" (surprisingly calm) to "Drake Shake" (30-foot swells). Most crossings fall somewhere in between with moderate rolling. Modern expedition ships have stabilizers, and medication helps significantly. The crossing takes 36-48 hours each way. Prepare for rough seas and be grateful if conditions are mild.

Q: What wildlife will I see?
A: Penguin colonies are guaranteed — gentoo, chinstrap, and Adelie depending on location. Leopard seals rest on ice floes. Weddell and crabeater seals are common. Humpback whales feed in bays throughout the season. Orcas hunt in pods. Seabirds include wandering albatross, giant petrels, and skuas. Wildlife is remarkably unafraid of humans.

Q: How much does an Antarctica expedition cost?
A: Budget expeditions on older vessels start around $8,000 USD. Mid-range options run $12,000-18,000. Luxury vessels with suites and premium service reach $30,000+. Extended itineraries including South Georgia cost more. Book 12-18 months ahead for best selection and pricing.

Q: Can I fly to Antarctica instead of sailing?
A: Yes — "fly-cruise" options fly from Punta Arenas to King George Island, skipping the Drake Passage. This is faster and avoids seasickness but costs significantly more and misses what many consider an essential part of the Antarctic experience.

Q: Is Antarctica suitable for older travelers?
A: Many passengers are retirement age. Key requirements: ability to climb in and out of Zodiacs (stepping into moving boats), walking on uneven terrain, and tolerating cold for 2+ hours. Discuss specific limitations with expedition companies before booking.
