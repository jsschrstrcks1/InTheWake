The Ship That Launched an Empire

Historic Significance: TSS Mardi Gras was Carnival Cruise Line's very first ship, purchased in 1972 for $6.5 million. This former Canadian Pacific ocean liner launched the "Fun Ship" concept that would revolutionize the cruise industry.

On March 11, 1972, Mardi Gras embarked on its inaugural Carnival cruise from Miami — and promptly ran aground on a sandbar. Rather than a disaster, this mishap became legend: Carnival kept passengers entertained with food, drinks, and festivities while the ship was freed, inadvertently creating the carefree "Fun Ship" atmosphere that would define the brand for decades.

The Logbook — A Legend is Born

Before there was Carnival Cruise Line, there was Ted Arison — an Israeli-American entrepreneur with a vision to make cruising accessible and fun for everyday Americans, not just the wealthy elite.

In 1972, Arison purchased the aging RMS Empress of Canada from Canadian Pacific for $6.5 million. The ship was already 11 years old and had served as a traditional ocean liner on transatlantic routes. Arison renamed her Mardi Gras and set about transforming cruising from a stuffy, formal affair into something revolutionary: a floating party.

The inaugural cruise on March 11, 1972 couldn't have gone worse — or better. With 790 passengers aboard, Mardi Gras ran hard aground on a sandbar at the entrance to Miami Harbor. The ship's deep 28-foot draft was simply too much for the shallow channel. Competitors gleefully dubbed the vessel "Mardi Gras on the Rocks."

But something magical happened during those 24 hours stuck on the sandbar. Instead of panicking, the crew kept the party going. Food flowed, drinks poured, and entertainment continued. Passengers had the time of their lives. When word spread about how Carnival handled the crisis — with humor, hospitality, and an anything-goes attitude — bookings soared.

The "Fun Ship" concept wasn't born in a boardroom. It was born on a sandbar, and it changed cruising forever.

Frequently Asked Questions

What was TSS Mardi Gras before Carnival bought it?

TSS Mardi Gras was originally RMS Empress of Canada, built in 1960-1961 by Vickers-Armstrongs in Walker-on-Tyne, England for Canadian Pacific Steamships. It served as a transatlantic ocean liner before Carnival purchased it in January 1972 for $6.5 million.

What happened on TSS Mardi Gras's first cruise?

On March 11, 1972, TSS Mardi Gras ran aground on a sandbar while departing Miami on its inaugural Carnival cruise. The ship was stuck for about 24 hours. Rather than a disaster, Carnival kept passengers entertained with food and drinks, inadvertently creating the "Fun Ship" atmosphere.

Why was the ship named Mardi Gras?

The name "Mardi Gras" evoked the festive, party atmosphere that founder Ted Arison wanted to create — a celebration at sea. The name perfectly captured Carnival's vision of fun, accessible cruising for everyone.

What happened to TSS Mardi Gras after Carnival?

TSS Mardi Gras was sold in 1993 to Epirotiki Line, where it sailed as Olympic. It later operated as Star of Texas, Lucky Star, and Apollon before being scrapped at Alang, India in December 2003 after 42 years of service.

Is this the same as the new Mardi Gras?

No. This page covers the original TSS Mardi Gras (1972-1993). The modern Mardi Gras is an Excel-class mega-ship launched in 2020 — Carnival's largest and most innovative vessel, featuring the BOLT roller coaster and LNG propulsion.
