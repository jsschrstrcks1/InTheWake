My Logbook: Where Cinnamon and Incense Meet the Indian Ocean

I smelled Colombo before I saw it. Standing on the upper deck as our ship eased into the harbour, a warm breeze carried the scent of cinnamon bark and temple incense across the water, and I felt my shoulders drop as something ancient and welcoming wrapped itself around me. The harbour cranes and container stacks told one story — a busy working port — but the fragrance told another, one that had been drifting through this coastline for two thousand years. I gathered my daypack, slipped on my walking shoes, and stepped down the gangway into Sri Lanka's beating heart.

My tuk-tuk driver, a wiry man named Pradeep with a contagious grin, wove through the morning chaos with the confidence of a person born navigating this particular madness. We rattled past colonial buildings painted in fading pastels, their arched verandas draped with laundry, past saffron-robed monks scrolling through smartphones on street corners, past a woman selling garlands of jasmine from a wooden cart. The noise was extraordinary — horns, engine roar, vendors calling out prices, temple bells somewhere in the distance — yet beneath it all I heard a rhythm, a pulse that felt less like chaos and more like a city breathing. However, the heat hit me hard. Even at nine in the morning, the tropical air pressed against my skin like a warm, damp cloth, and I realized immediately why every local I passed moved with such unhurried grace. Rushing in Colombo is not just pointless; it is physically impossible.

Gangaramaya Temple emerged from a residential neighborhood like a hidden world. I removed my shoes at the entrance, feeling the cool stone beneath my bare feet, and stepped into a complex that defies every architectural rule I have ever understood. Sri Lankan stupas sat beside Thai chedis, Chinese pagodas neighbored Indian gopurams, and the museum displayed everything from antique cars to thousands of Buddha statues donated by devotees worldwide. I wandered through hall after hall, each one revealing another layer of devotion accumulated over centuries. A monk noticed me standing before a particularly ornate shrine, and though we shared no common language beyond a few words of English, he gestured for me to sit. We sat together in silence for several minutes, the sound of chanting drifting from a distant prayer hall, and I felt something I had not expected — a deep, settling calm that had nothing to do with religion and everything to do with being still in a place where stillness is valued.

Galle Face Green stretched before me like a communal living room open to the Indian Ocean. Families flew kites against the afternoon sky while young couples shared isso wade — crispy prawn fritters that shattered between my teeth, releasing a burst of chili and cumin that made my eyes water in the best way. A vendor cracked open a king coconut with a single machete stroke and handed it to me, the cool sweet water tasting like liquid relief after hours in the heat. I watched the waves crash against the old seawall, the salt spray mixing with the smell of frying kottu roti from a nearby cart, and I understood why Colombo residents come here every evening. This was not a tourist attraction. This was home, and they were generous enough to share it.

The Pettah district overwhelmed me in ways I did not anticipate. Narrow streets packed with stalls selling spices, textiles, electronics, and produce formed a labyrinth that smelled of cardamom, dried fish, and diesel exhaust in equal measure. I lost myself in the spice section, where a vendor named Kumara demonstrated the difference between true Ceylon cinnamon — delicate, layered, and slightly sweet — and the cassia bark most Westerners mistake for it. He rolled a quill between his fingers and snapped it to show the paper-thin layers, then pressed a piece into my palm and told me to taste it. The flavor was subtle and warm, nothing like the aggressive bite I expected, and I bought three bags on the spot. Yet despite the beauty of the spices and the kindness of the vendors, I noticed too the exhaustion in the faces of the workers hauling sacks of rice in the midday heat, the children who should have been in school sitting behind stall counters. Colombo does not hide its contradictions. It asks you to see them.

I ate my first proper Sri Lankan rice and curry at a small restaurant Pradeep chose — a place with no sign, just a curtained doorway and six tables — and my mouth has never been so simultaneously delighted and defeated. The rice arrived surrounded by seven small bowls of different curries: dhal, fish in coconut gravy, chicken seasoned with pandan leaves, a fiery sambol that made me gasp, tempered beetroot, green beans, and a coconut chutney that cooled the fire. I ate with my right hand, as the locals did, clumsily at first, then with growing confidence. The flavors were layered in ways I had never tasted — warm, complex, sometimes searingly hot, but always grounded by the richness of coconut milk and the earthiness of curry leaves. The total cost was 650 LKR — roughly $2.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Late in the afternoon, I returned to Gangaramaya's lake pavilion. The light had shifted to amber, and the water reflected the temple spires like a painting. An elderly woman in a white sari sat at the edge of the platform, feeding bread crumbs to the fish. She looked up at me and smiled — a smile so full of warmth that my heart swelled unexpectedly, and I felt tears forming at the corners of my eyes. She reminded me of my grandmother, who had the same quiet way of making every creature around her feel noticed. I sat beside her and whispered a quiet prayer of thanks — for the journey, for the kindness of strangers, for a day that had asked me to slow down enough to see the world as it actually is. We sat there together until the sun touched the water, and neither of us said a word.

Colombo gave me hoppers for breakfast on my second morning — those bowl-shaped fermented rice-flour pancakes with an egg cracked into the center, the edges crispy and the middle soft, served alongside three curries. I ate them at a street stall near the Old Dutch Hospital while watching the city wake up around me, the colonial architecture glowing gold in the early light. Although the buildings bore the marks of Portuguese, Dutch, and British occupation, they felt unmistakably Sri Lankan now — absorbed, reinterpreted, claimed.

Walking back through the cinnamon gardens district toward the port, I passed through neighborhoods where modern glass towers stood beside crumbling colonial mansions, where Buddhist shrines occupied corners next to mobile phone shops. The contradictions that had startled me on arrival now felt honest — a city that refuses to pretend it is only one thing. I realized that Colombo had taught me something I needed to learn: that beauty and hardship can occupy the same space, that warmth does not require wealth, and that sometimes the most profound experiences come not from the monuments we plan to visit but from the moments we did not expect. Looking back, I think the real gift of Colombo was not any single temple or meal or sunset, but the way the city insisted I pay attention — to the scent of cinnamon on the breeze, to the sound of temple bells through traffic, to the taste of a $2 curry that outshone any fine dining memory I possess.

The Cruise Port

Colombo Port sits in the main harbour, a working commercial port with basic but functional cruise facilities. Ships dock directly at the pier — no tender required. Taxis and tuk-tuks queue at the terminal exit, ready to whisk passengers into the city. The terminal building itself offers limited amenities: basic restrooms, a small duty-free area, and a tourist information desk that provides city maps and can arrange transportation.

Downtown Colombo lies 15-20 minutes by taxi from the terminal. Gangaramaya Temple is roughly the same distance, while Galle Face Green sits just 10-15 minutes away. Wheelchair accessible vehicles can be arranged through the terminal information desk with advance notice, though availability is limited. The port area lacks shade, so carry water and sun protection while waiting for transport. Currency exchange is available at the terminal, though ATMs in the city offer better rates. US Dollars are accepted at some larger shops, but Sri Lankan Rupees (LKR) are needed for local vendors and tuk-tuks.

Getting Around
  • Taxis ($2-5): The most practical option from port. Negotiate fare before departure or use metered radio cabs. The PickMe app works reliably in Colombo and eliminates bargaining stress. Port to Gangaramaya Temple costs approximately 500-800 LKR ($2-3). Port to National Museum runs about 700-1,000 LKR ($3-4). Always agree on price first for non-metered cabs. Air-conditioned vehicles cost slightly more but are worth it in tropical heat.
  • Three-Wheelers / Tuk-Tuks ($1-3): Ubiquitous and cheaper than taxis, these provide an adventurous way to see the city. Negotiate firmly — the initial quote is often 2-3x the fair price. Budget approximately 400-600 LKR ($1-2) for short trips within central Colombo. Thrilling if you are comfortable with weaving through traffic, but not suitable for those with mobility concerns or walking difficulty. No seatbelts, no air conditioning, and no guarantees of a smooth ride.
  • Car with Driver ($60-100): The best option for day trips to Pinnawala or further afield. Arranged through your ship excursion desk or local tour companies at the terminal. A full-day hire with an English-speaking driver costs $60-100 USD and covers fuel, parking, and local knowledge. The driver handles navigation through chaotic traffic, provides cultural context, and waits while you explore. Book ahead for guaranteed return to the ship on time.
  • Walking: Not practical from port to most attractions due to inconsistent sidewalks, heavy traffic, and draining tropical heat. However, walking within specific areas — Galle Face Green, the Dutch Hospital precinct, Fort district — is pleasant and rewarding. Wear comfortable shoes, carry water, and use sunscreen. Moderate walking is needed at most temple sites. Wheelchair users should note that many sidewalks are uneven or absent, and curb cuts are rare outside the Fort district.
  • Public Buses and Trains ($0.10-0.50): Extremely cheap but very crowded, confusing for visitors, and impractical for limited cruise port time. Save public transport exploration for longer stays. Local trains run from Fort Station to coastal suburbs, but schedules are unpredictable.

Top Excursions & Things to Do

Booking guidance: Most Colombo attractions allow walk-up visits. Ship excursion packages offer convenience and guaranteed return to the ship. Independent exploration offers flexibility and savings — book ahead for Pinnawala and Kandy day trips to secure reliable transport.

Gangaramaya Temple ($1-2)

Colombo's most prominent Buddhist temple complex features an eclectic architectural fusion of Sri Lankan, Thai, Indian, and Chinese styles. The massive collection of Buddha statues, sacred relics, ornate shrine rooms, and the tranquil Seema Malaka lake pavilion make this a deeply rewarding visit. Located 15-20 minutes from port by taxi at approximately 500-800 LKR ($2-3). Entry around 300 LKR ($1). Dress modestly with shoulders and knees covered. Remove shoes before entering. Allow 1-2 hours for a thorough visit. The museum alone could fill an afternoon. Low walking effort once inside — mostly flat with benches available. Accessible paths exist to most main areas.

Galle Face Green (free)

This half-kilometer oceanfront promenade has served as Colombo's communal gathering spot since 1859. Locals fly kites, jog, picnic, and watch sunsets along the seawall. Street food vendors serve isso wade (prawn fritters for 100-200 LKR / $0.50-1), kottu roti ($1-2), and fresh king coconut water (80 LKR / $0.25). Located 10-15 minutes from port. Free and open at all hours. Best in late afternoon when crowds gather and the ocean breeze cools the city. Flat terrain, fully accessible for wheelchair users along the main promenade.

Pettah District (free)

A chaotic, colorful commercial district where narrow streets overflow with stalls selling spices (cinnamon, cardamom, cloves), Ceylon tea, gems, textiles, electronics, and fresh produce. A sensory immersion in the best sense. Manning Hall (1915) is an architectural highlight. Located 20 minutes from port. Free to wander. Watch your belongings. Bargaining expected and enjoyed. Allow 2-3 hours for full exploration. The spice section alone justifies the visit. Moderate walking with uneven surfaces — not ideal for wheelchair users but manageable for those with low mobility limitations who take their time.

National Museum of Colombo ($2)

Sri Lanka's largest museum, established in 1877, houses ancient royal regalia, intricate ceremonial masks, Buddhist art, colonial artifacts, and natural history displays. The stunning colonial building with its white columns and interior courtyards merits a visit for the architecture alone. Located 20-25 minutes from port. Entry approximately 600 LKR ($2). Allow 1.5-2 hours. Air-conditioned galleries provide welcome respite on hot days. Ground floor is wheelchair accessible.

Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage ($70-100)

Best for longer port days (8+ hours). This conservation center for orphaned and injured elephants sits approximately 2.5 hours from Colombo. Watch 60+ elephants bathe in the Ma Oya River at 10 AM and 2 PM daily. See feeding sessions and baby elephants with their caretakers. Ship excursion packages run $70-100 USD including transport and lunch. Independent visits via hired car with driver cost $60-80 for the full day. Book ahead to ensure reliable transport and guaranteed return to the ship before departure. A deeply rewarding experience that justifies the travel time.

Lotus Tower ($5)

South Asia's tallest self-supported structure at 356 meters, completed in 2019, features a lotus-inspired design visible across the Colombo skyline. The observation deck offers 360-degree panoramic views of the city and ocean. Located 25 minutes from port. Entry around 1,500 LKR ($5). Clear days reward visitors with spectacular vistas stretching to the horizon. Allow 1-2 hours including travel time.

Old Dutch Hospital (free)

This beautifully restored 17th-century colonial building now houses an upscale dining and shopping precinct. Thick white walls, red tile roofs, and a shaded interior courtyard create a pleasant atmosphere for lunch or browsing. Located 15 minutes from port. Free to explore. The cleanest public restrooms near the port area. A good spot for those seeking a moderate-energy, accessible experience close to the ship.

Depth Soundings

Practical details and honest assessments for planning your Colombo day.

  • Temple dress code: Dress modestly for temple visits — shoulders and knees must be covered, and you must remove shoes before entering any shrine. Carry socks to protect your feet from hot pavement. A camera is welcome but ask before photographing worshippers.
  • Negotiate transport fares: Agree on taxi and tuk-tuk fares BEFORE getting in. The initial quote is often 2-3x the fair price. Counter-offer politely and firmly. Apps like PickMe eliminate the bargaining entirely and show the fare upfront.
  • Carry small denominations: Bring small-denomination rupees for tuk-tuks, vendors, and temple donations. US Dollars work at tourist-oriented shops but change comes back in rupees at poor exchange rates.
  • Elephant bathing schedule: Pinnawala elephants bathe at 10 AM and 2 PM. Plan your arrival 30 minutes early for the best viewing position. The morning session draws fewer visitors and offers better light for photos.
  • Spice levels: Sri Lankan food can be extremely spicy by Western standards. What locals call "mild" still carries significant heat. Start cautiously if you are sensitive. Fresh coconut water soothes spice burn far better than plain water.
  • Traffic reality: Colombo traffic is chaotic but rarely fast. Budget extra travel time for every destination. Rush hours (7-9 AM and 5-7 PM) can double journey times. Plan your return to the ship with at least 90 minutes of buffer.
  • Kandy feasibility: A Kandy day trip is only realistic if your ship docks at 7 AM or earlier and departs at 7 PM or later, providing 12+ hours in port. Otherwise the 3-hour-each-way journey leaves almost no time at the Temple of the Tooth. Pinnawala is far more manageable for typical port days.
  • Hydration: King coconut water (thambili) sold by street vendors is refreshing, hydrating, and safe to drink. Choose coconuts with a slight orange tint for the sweetest flavor. Budget 60-100 LKR ($0.25-0.35) per coconut.

Frequently Asked Questions
Where do cruise ships dock in Colombo?

Ships dock at Colombo Port in the main harbour. The terminal has basic facilities with taxis readily available at the exit. Downtown Colombo is about 15-20 minutes away by taxi. No tender is required — ships dock directly at the pier.

Can I visit Kandy from Colombo on a cruise day?

Kandy is 3+ hours each way, making it extremely challenging for typical port days of 8-10 hours. Only attempt this if your ship provides 12+ hours in port. The Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage (under 3 hours round trip) delivers an equally memorable experience with far less travel stress.

What should I buy in Colombo?

Ceylon tea is the top purchase — premium quality at a fraction of Western prices. Also consider spices (especially true Ceylon cinnamon, native to Sri Lanka), handmade batik textiles, Ayurvedic products, and gems from reputable certified dealers only. Prices at Pettah stalls are negotiable; fixed-price shops at the Old Dutch Hospital offer convenience without bargaining.

Is Sri Lankan food very spicy?

Yes, authentically prepared Sri Lankan cuisine tends to be quite spicy by most Western standards. Request "mild" if you are sensitive, but know that "mild" still carries noticeable heat. King coconut water is the best antidote — far more effective than plain water for soothing chili burn.

What is the best temple to visit in Colombo?

Gangaramaya Temple, 15-20 minutes from port, offers the most comprehensive experience — diverse architecture spanning multiple Asian styles, an extensive museum, active worship services, and the peaceful Seema Malaka lake pavilion. Dress modestly and allow 1-2 hours. Entry is approximately 300 LKR ($1).

Are the elephants at Pinnawala treated well?

Pinnawala is a legitimate orphanage for injured and orphaned elephants, operated by Sri Lanka's Department of Wildlife Conservation. The elephants are not used for rides or performances — visitors observe their natural bathing and feeding behavior. Conditions are generally considered humane by conservation standards, though animal welfare organizations continue to advocate for improvements.
