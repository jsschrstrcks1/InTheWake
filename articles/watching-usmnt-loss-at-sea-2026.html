Watching the USMNT Lose at Sea: The Big Screen, Belgium, and the Prayer After Full-Time

Belgium 4, USA 1 — the Round of 16 exit from a home World Cup, watched from a pool deck in the middle of the water. The free kick that briefly leveled it, the controversy nobody on the deck could agree about, and what somebody did when it ended.

by Ken Baker | July 6, 2026

Answer first: Belgium beat the United States 4-1 in the World Cup Round of 16 on July 6, 2026, in front of a sellout crowd in Seattle — and in front of a few hundred of us on a pool deck, on the big screen, somewhere on the water. Charles De Ketelaere scored twice in the first half. Malik Tillman's free kick briefly made it 1-1 before Belgium answered inside two minutes. A goalkeeper error made it 3-1; Romelu Lukaku closed it in stoppage time. The Folarin Balogun red-card controversy hung over all of it. And when the whistle went, someone on the deck prayed.

The deck

There is no version of watching your country exit a home World Cup that feels good, but I will tell you the version I got: on a pool deck, on the big screen, salt air moving through a crowd in towels and team shirts. Deck chairs filled an hour before kickoff. The pool itself emptied as the anthem played — swimmers draped on the edge, dripping, watching. A few hundred strangers, no two from the same town, all facing the same forty feet of screen with the ocean running past on either side of it.

Watching a match at sea is watching in a public square. Nobody controls the remote. Nobody can turn it off in disgust and go mow the lawn. When it goes wrong you sit in it together, and on this afternoon it went wrong early.

The match

Belgium scored inside nine minutes. The deck had barely settled. De Ketelaere finished it, and the groan that went through the loungers had a particular sound I'd not heard on a ship before — a couple hundred people deflating at once, over water.

Then the moment that made the afternoon worth having. Malik Tillman stood over a free kick and put it away, and the deck came up off its chairs — towels waving, pool water slapping the gutters, a noise you could probably hear from the bridge. 1-1. For about two minutes the whole ship believed.

Two minutes is what Belgium needed to answer. De Ketelaere again. That answer, more than any goal that followed, was the match: every time the U.S. found a foothold, it gave the ball back. The turnovers piled up. In the 57th minute, goalkeeper Matt Freese misplayed a ball outside his own penalty area and Hans Vanaken rolled it into an unguarded net — the kind of error that makes a deck go quiet rather than loud. Lukaku added the fourth in stoppage time, and by then the sun and the scoreline had thinned the crowd.

Mauricio Pochettino said afterward the team didn't show its real quality, and that the elimination hurt. Tyler Adams was blunter: it stinks, and it wasn't a good performance. Both things read true from a deck chair.

The controversy

The argument on the deck started before kickoff, because the week's strangest story had made sure everyone had an opinion. Folarin Balogun was sent off against Bosnia-Herzegovina on July 1 — an automatic one-game suspension. A FIFA disciplinary panel then did something close to unprecedented: it delayed the suspension by a year, clearing him to play against Belgium, after President Trump reportedly called FIFA president Gianni Infantino to encourage a review of the card. Belgium's football association said it was astonished and called the decision a direct contradiction of the tournament's own competition regulations.

Balogun played. He did not score. Tim Ream, the captain, said afterward the controversy had no impact on the result — and the scoreline supports him, because 4-1 is not a match that turned on one selection. What the episode cost is harder to score: a Round of 16 exit is ordinary sporting heartbreak, but the asterisk conversation around it will outlast the match report. On the deck, the two loudest camps — he should have been suspended; the card was soft to begin with — watched the same four goals go in, and by the 80th minute the argument had stopped mattering to either side.

The prayer

Here is the part I keep returning to. At full-time, with the deck half-empty and the screen showing Belgian players on their knees in celebration, somebody prayed. I didn't catch how it started — whether one family bowed their heads first or someone spoke up — but a knot of people near the rail went quiet on purpose, and the quiet spread a few loungers in each direction, and a voice gave thanks. For the tournament. For the young men who carried the shirt. For safe travel, theirs and ours.

No one performed anything. It lasted maybe a minute. But I have watched a lot of sport in a lot of rooms, and I have rarely seen a loss received that way — gratitude first, disappointment second, in that order on purpose. A pool deck at sea is one of the last true public squares, and for one minute this one remembered what a public square can be.

The next cycle

The praise is earned and worth stating plainly. This team reached the knockout rounds of a home World Cup. Tillman's free kick will be replayed for years. The core of this squad is young enough that 2030 is not a consolation — it is the plan. Adams' honesty after the match is itself a mark of a healthy team: no excuses about the officiating week, no hiding. The next cycle starts with players who now know exactly what a sellout knockout crowd feels like, and what it costs to give the ball away in front of one.

Hope thou in God, the psalmist says to his own cast-down soul. Smaller hopes are permitted too. Four years is one World Cup away.

What is in this report, and what isn't

The deck — the crowd, the free-kick eruption, the prayer at the whistle — is what I saw and heard on July 6, 2026, from a deck chair at sea. The match record — scorers, minutes, the Freese error, the Balogun timeline, the quotes from Pochettino, Adams, and Ream — is a synthesis of the published record, principally NPR's match report and the wire coverage of the FIFA decision; I watched it on a pool-deck screen, not from a press box, and satellite feeds at sea drop frames at the worst moments. Who led the prayer, I don't know, and I've chosen not to guess.
