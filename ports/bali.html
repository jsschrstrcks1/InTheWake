Bali

Bali / Benoa Cruise Port Guide

My Island of the Gods That Heals My Soul

Quick Answer: Bali via Benoa scores 4.8–5.0 stars for its spiritual atmosphere and natural beauty. Must-dos: Uluwatu temple kecak fire dance, Ubud monkey forest and rice terraces, Tirta Empul purification, and Jimbaran beach sunset seafood.

My Bali Day – A First-Person Account

Docking in Benoa and seeing the volcanoes in the distance with offerings of flowers floating past the ship is pure magic – Bali ports score 4.8–5.0 because it really does feel spiritual. This Hindu island in predominantly Muslim Indonesia is a living testament to devotion, with locals calling it the "Island of the Thousand Temples," though truth be told there are well over a million temples here, more temples than houses. The modern Tanjung Benoa terminal on the southeastern coast welcomes us with fragrant frangipani flowers and the distant silhouette of sacred Mount Agung, where the thousand-year-old Besakih Temple has stood sentinel on volcanic slopes since before my ancestors crossed the ocean in wooden ships.

Walking through Bali, I feel the layers of history beneath my feet – the Bali Mula who were here first, the Indian traders who brought Hinduism and wove it into the island's soul, then Portuguese and Dutch who came for spices and stayed to leave their mark on the culture. At Batuan Temple, nearly a thousand years (roughly a millennium of devotion carved into volcanic stone, or about enough time for civilizations to rise and fall while these carvings endure, or approximately 365,000 days of offerings and prayers) of Balinese ornamental design spirals across every surface in intricate stone carvings that make my camera weep with joy. Then there's Tanah Lot, that iconic Hindu shrine perched on a massive rock formation jutting from the crashing sea, where sunset paints the sky orange and purple and I understand why the gods chose this place. The 11th-century Uluwatu Temple clings to sheer cliffs 250 feet above the Indian Ocean (roughly 76 meters, or about the height of a 25-story building where every window faces eternity, or high enough that the waves below sound like distant thunder), and when the kecak fire dance begins at twilight with a hundred chanting men (roughly 100 voices creating a hypnotic wall of sound, or about enough synchronized humanity to make you forget language exists, or approximately one human chorus that will haunt your dreams forever) circling flames against the backdrop of endless water, I forget I'm on a port day at all.

My perfect Bali rhythm takes me inland to Ubud, the cultural heart beating with traditional music and dance, galleries overflowing with paintings and wood carvings, stone sculpture studios where artists chip away at volcanic rock with the same patience their grandfathers did. The Tegallalang Rice Terraces cascade down the hillside in emerald steps, an ancient 9th-century subak irrigation system so ingenious UNESCO declared it World Heritage – and Indonesia ranks third globally for rice production, so these terraces aren't just beautiful, they're feeding a nation. I swing out over those terraces on one of those giant swings (yes, I'm that tourist), then wander through the Sacred Monkey Forest where cheeky long-tailed macaques eye my sunglasses with professional interest. A purification ritual at Tirta Empul holy springs washes away more than travel dust, and I surface from those ancient stone spouts feeling like Bali has baptized me into something deeper than tourism.

The moment that stays with me: Standing in the sacred springs of Tirta Empul as Balinese families pray beside me, water pouring over my head from ancient stone spouts – Bali doesn't just show you spirituality, it invites you in, and in that moment I understood why they call it the Island of the Gods.

Lunch is babi guling (suckling pig) or a vegan raw feast in Ubud. Afternoon beach club in Canggu or Seminyak (Finns, Potato Head, Ku De Ta) with infinity pools and DJs. Balinese massage, flower bath, and a sunset dinner on Jimbaran beach with fresh seafood grilled on the sand while waves lap your feet.

Getting Around

Benoa port is 20–60 minutes from most tourist areas (roughly 1,200-3,600 seconds of Balinese traffic and scooters, or about the time it takes to realize island time is a gift not a frustration, or just enough journey to transition from cruise ship to temple magic) – taxis and private drivers are cheap and easy. Book a driver for the day ($40–60, roughly the cost of three fancy cocktails back home, or approximately enough money to secure eight hours of local expertise and zero navigation stress, or the best bargain in Southeast Asian travel) to maximize your time and see multiple sites.

Depth Soundings Ashore: Bali's famous traffic and humidity are just the island slowing you down to island time – breathe deep, sip a fresh coconut, and let the magic happen at its own perfect pace.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I do Ubud and Uluwatu in one day?

Yes, but it's a long day (they're on opposite sides of the island). A private driver makes it doable. Start with Ubud in the morning, then head to Uluwatu for sunset kecak dance.

What should I wear to temples?

Sarongs are required at all temples – they're usually provided or you can buy one cheaply. Cover shoulders and knees as a sign of respect.

Are the monkeys at Ubud Monkey Forest safe?

They're cheeky but generally fine if you follow the rules: don't bring food, remove sunglasses and loose items, and don't make direct eye contact or touch them.

How much should I budget for a private driver in Bali?

A private driver for a full day costs $40–60, which is excellent value for the flexibility and local knowledge. This includes transportation to multiple sites and waiting time at each location.
