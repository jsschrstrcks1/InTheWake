Cruise Gear Worth Packing: What's Worth Buying (and What to Skip)

A short, honest list of the gear that solves a real problem on a cruise — and the gear that just fills a drawer. With the verdicts I'd give a friend, not a sales pitch.

The short version: You need far less gear than the packing lists imply. A few things genuinely earn their place on a cruise — a way to charge several devices off one outlet, packing cubes if you hate digging through a suitcase, a couple of magnetic hooks for the steel cabin walls, and a lanyard for your cruise card. The rest is optional. Below is the honest sort: what solves a real problem, and what just fills a drawer. Buy for a problem you actually have.

Charging — the One Thing Cabins Get Wrong

The single most common gear problem aboard: cruise cabins have one or two outlets, and a couple traveling with phones, a watch, and a tablet runs out of sockets fast. The fix is a small multi-port USB charging block — one outlet becomes four or five charging ports. It's allowed on every line, it weighs nothing, and it's the first thing I'd pack. Skip it only if you travel with a single device.

For long port days, a 20,000mAh power bank keeps a phone alive through a full day ashore. One caution that has nothing to do with us: a power bank must travel in your carry-on, never checked luggage — that's an airline safety rule. Skip it if your phone reliably lasts a day and you're not shooting a lot of photos.

The thing not to bring: a surge-protected AC power strip. Royal Caribbean and Celebrity ban them outright, and other lines restrict them, because they're a genuine fire risk on a ship's electrical system. They get pulled at the security scanner and held until the end of the cruise. The USB block above does the same job and sails through. Always check your own line's current policy before packing anything with AC outlets.

Packing & Luggage

Packing cubes are the rare organizer that earns the hype. They let you skip unpacking entirely — stack them in the closet by category and pull what you need each day. The payoff is biggest on shorter cruises, where unpacking a whole suitcase for three or four nights isn't worth it. Skip them if you pack light or genuinely don't mind living out of the bag.

A set of bright luggage tags earns its tiny cost on embarkation day, when your bags are handled by porters and delivered to your cabin door hours later among thousands of identical black suitcases. The cruise line's paper tag does the real routing; a durable tag just helps you spot your bag and survives the handling. Skip the fancy ones — whatever's bright and won't tear off is enough.

The Cabin Itself

Cabin walls are usually steel, which turns a few magnetic hooks into instant hanging space — for hats, wet swimsuits, lanyards, the daily schedule. Six covers most couples; bring more if you're traveling with kids. Skip them only if your cabin's a suite with plenty of hooks already.

That's the short version of cabin gear. If you want the full treatment — over-the-door organizers, small-space strategies, keeping a tiny cabin livable for a week — we put it all in the cruise cabin organization guide rather than repeat it here.

Cruise Card & Sleep

Your cruise card is your room key, your wallet, and your ID, and you'll reach for it twenty times a day. A retractable lanyard keeps it on you without the around-the-neck tangle. This is a category where buying nicer doesn't help — whatever holds the card works. Skip it if your cabin-mate carries the card or you're happy with a pocket.

Cabin light bleeds in from the hallway and from the little glowing electronics, and interior cabins can be pitch black or oddly lit. A simple sleep mask matters more at sea than at home, and a pair of foam earplugs handles a noisy hallway or a cabin near the elevators. Both are a few dollars. Skip them if you already sleep through anything.

Port Days & Reading

For beach and snorkel ports, a waterproof phone pouch lets you carry your phone and a card into the water without worry, and most float. Skip it if you leave the phone in the cabin safe on beach days.

If you read, a waterproof Kindle Paperwhite holds a whole trip of books, lasts the week on a charge, and works without buying the ship's internet — better by the pool than a phone you're also trying to keep charged. Skip it if you read a book or two a year; your phone and the ship's library cover that.

Seasickness Gear — Named, Not Sold

This is the one category where you'll notice there are no buy links, and that's on purpose. We don't put affiliate links on health products — it's a firm line here, because pushing remedies at people who are anxious or unwell is exactly the kind of thing the line exists to prevent.

So, plainly and with no commission attached: the common over-the-counter options are meclizine (sold as Bonine, the less-drowsy one) and dimenhydrinate (Dramamine), with a prescription scopolamine patch for longer, rougher stretches — all of which are a conversation for your doctor or pharmacist before you sail. The drug-free acupressure wristbands and ginger that fill the cruise forums are cheap and harmless to try, but the evidence behind them is weak, so don't make them your whole plan.

What to Skip

A few things show up on every "cruise essentials" list and rarely earn the suitcase space:

  • Surge-protected power strips — covered above; they get confiscated. A USB block is the fix.
  • Lighted makeup mirrors, travel steamers, oversized toiletry kits — the cabin has a mirror and a hair dryer, the bathroom is small, and most lines will pull a steamer for the same fire-safety reason as the power strip.
  • Door decorations and magnet sets — fun for some, clutter for others; entirely optional, never essential.
  • A second bag of "just in case" gadgets — the honest test for every item here is whether it fixes a problem you've actually had. If you can't name the problem, leave it home.

Frequently Asked Questions

What gear do you actually need for a cruise?

Less than the lists suggest. A USB charger for the outlet shortage, packing cubes if you hate living out of a suitcase, a couple of magnetic hooks for the steel walls, and a card lanyard cover most of it. Buy for a problem you actually have, not for a checklist.

Are power strips allowed on cruise ships?

Often not. Royal Caribbean and Celebrity ban surge protectors and most AC power strips, and other lines restrict them, for fire-safety reasons. A USB-only charging block is the safe universal choice. Check your line's current policy before packing anything with AC outlets.

What's the best way to charge devices in a cruise cabin?

A compact multi-port USB charging block — it covers several devices from one outlet and is allowed everywhere. Add a power bank for long port days. Skip the AC surge protector; it's the item most likely to be confiscated.

Do I need seasickness medication?

Most people on a big ship don't. If you're prone to it, talk to your doctor before sailing and read our seasickness guide. We name the common remedies but don't link them for commission — health products are off-limits for affiliate income here.

Is a Kindle worth bringing on a cruise?

If you read, yes — a whole trip of books, a week of battery, no internet needed. Skip it if you only read a book or two a year; your phone and the ship's library will do.
