Why I Started Solo Cruising (and Built a Community)

From that first solo gangway to a thriving community — lessons, safety, and the unexpected joy of sailing on your own terms.

I booked my first solo cruise because I was done waiting for everyone else’s calendar. I stayed for the freedom — coffee at sunrise, new friends by lunch, and a quiet deck chair when I needed to breathe. Solo doesn’t mean alone; it means you finally get to choose.

Author’s note: These are lessons from real sailings and real meetups — what worked, what didn’t, and what I tell every first-timer who messages the group.

Why I Went Solo

My first sailing was a reset. No coordinating, no compromises, just an open horizon and a safe home base to explore from. I found a rhythm: mornings on deck with coffee, afternoons ashore with a small group, evenings choosing a show or turning in early. The best surprise? The community — people who get it and cheer you on.

What I Wish I’d Known

  • Hit the solo meetup on day one. Cliques form early; show up and say hi.
  • Ship excursions are worth it in some ports. The ship will wait for official tours — peace of mind is priceless.
  • Pack light but smart. Hooks, a compact charger, and layers matter. See the checklist I use in Packing Lists.

Safety, Belonging, and Joy

Solo is a balance: independence and connection. I love quiet balcony mornings and choosing a two-top for dinner if I’m peopled-out — and I also love trivia teams, meetups, and ad-hoc shore buddies. Give yourself permission to do both.
