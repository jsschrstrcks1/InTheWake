Aruba: One Happy Island With the Perfect Beaches

Quick Answer: Aruba is "One Happy Island" with Eagle Beach (consistently ranked top-3 in the world), Baby Beach snorkeling, colorful Dutch colonial architecture, and the best sunset catamarans in the Caribbean. Trade winds keep it perfect year-round.

Sailing into Oranjestad feels like entering a Caribbean postcard painted by someone who really, really loves color. The Dutch colonial buildings pop in every shade of coral, yellow, and turquoise. Flamingos dot the horizon. And those famous trade winds — the ones that keep this desert island comfortable year-round — are already ruffling my hair before I'm off the gangway.

The first thing that strikes you stepping off the ship is the welcome — steel pan music fills the air, and carnival dancers in brilliant feathered costumes wave you into One Happy Island. The Dutch colonial buildings of Oranjestad pop in every shade of coral, yellow, and turquoise, their bright facades a reminder that this island has been welcoming visitors for centuries. The main shopping district along Caya G.F. Betico Croes is pedestrian-friendly and shaded, perfect for a morning wander before the sun gets too intense.

My perfect Aruba day starts early at Eagle Beach — and I mean early, before 9 a.m. Those famous fofoti (divi-divi) trees, bent permanently by the trade winds, are even more photogenic in the soft morning light. The powder-white sand stretches for miles, and the water is that impossible turquoise that makes you question your camera settings. I rent loungers from one of the beach operators (great value for a full day) and float for hours.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing waist-deep at Baby Beach, watching a school of blue tang swim right between my legs like I'm part of their world. The water was so clear I could count individual fish scales. This is what "One Happy Island" actually means.

The island's famous flamingos are everywhere — wading through the shallows at Renaissance Island, posing for photos, and reminding you that this is one of those rare places where the wildlife seems to love the tourists as much as we love them. Around noon, I usually taxi to Palm Beach for the high-rise hotel energy and lunch at Eduardo's Beach Shack — their truffle tuna poke bowl has ruined all other poke bowls for me forever. Palm Beach is more developed than Eagle, with beach bars and water sports rentals lining the shore, but it has its own kind of fun, especially if you're traveling with kids who want jet skis and banana boats.

Afternoon is decision time: Baby Beach or sunset catamaran? Baby Beach is worth the 45-minute drive to Aruba's southern tip — a protected lagoon with calm, crystal-clear water perfect for snorkeling. The reef here is healthy and the marine life abundant. But if you've been on your feet all day, the sunset catamarans that depart from Palm Beach are pure magic. I'm a fan of the snorkel-and-drinks tours that include open bar and music while the sun melts into the Caribbean.

Dinner is at The West Deck on the Linear Park waterfront — grilled wahoo with Creole sauce and fried plantains while watching the sky turn orange, pink, and purple. For the best keshi yena (stuffed cheese, Aruba's national dish), The Old Cunucu House out in the countryside is worth the trip.

Getting Around Aruba

The cruise terminal is a 5-minute walk approximately 33 school buses, 16 blue whales end-to-end, or 352 emperor penguins stacked skyward from downtown Oranjestad. From there:

  • Eagle Beach: About $12–15 by taxi, or take the inexpensive public bus (Arubus Line 10).
  • Palm Beach: Quick $10–12 taxi ride. Buses also run this route frequently.
  • Baby Beach: 45-minute drive. Best with a rental car or organized tour — too far for a reasonable taxi fare.
  • Jeep Rentals: Popular option for exploring the rugged north coast and Natural Pool. Book in advance.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

  • Trade Winds: Nature's air conditioning! They keep the island comfortable but will give you epic beach hair.
  • Currency: Aruban Florin (AWG) is official, but USD is accepted literally everywhere. Credit cards widely accepted.
  • Language: Papiamento is the local language, but Dutch, English, and Spanish are all widely spoken. You'll have no communication issues.
  • Safety: One of the safest islands in the Caribbean. Tourist areas are comfortable for walking, even at night.
  • Sunscreen: The trade winds mask how intense the sun is. You will burn faster than you expect. Reapply constantly.
  • Flamingos: Yes, they're real, and yes, they hang out at Renaissance Island. Day passes available but pricey.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Eagle Beach really that good?
A: Yes! It's consistently ranked among the top 3 beaches in the world for good reason. The powder-white sand, iconic fofoti trees, and turquoise water are even more stunning in person. Go early to beat the crowds.

Q: What is the best snorkeling in Aruba?
A: Baby Beach on the southern tip offers the best shore snorkeling — a protected lagoon with calm, crystal-clear water and abundant marine life. Worth the 45-minute drive.

Q: Is Aruba safe for walking at night?
A: Absolutely! Aruba is one of the safest islands in the Caribbean. Oranjestad, Palm Beach, and Eagle Beach are all comfortable for evening strolls.

Q: Where can I find the best keshi yena?
A: The Old Cunucu House in the countryside serves the most authentic version of this stuffed-cheese national dish. It's a proper Aruban experience.
