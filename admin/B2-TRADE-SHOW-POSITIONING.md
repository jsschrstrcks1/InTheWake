**Status:** Ready-to-use positioning copy for trade-show conversations, partnership pitches, podcast intros, and similar speak-about-the-publication moments.
**Plan reference:** v2.5 B2-rev — three-sentence positioning grounded in 21K citation data, with named precedents and Nieman Lab's 2025 framing as confirmation the brand is on-trend.

This document captures positioning at three levels of compression — the 1-sentence elevator, the 3-sentence canonical, and a paragraph-length expansion for when there's more time. Each version says the same thing in compatible language so they don't contradict each other when used in adjacent moments (e.g., the elevator at the trade-show booth followed by the paragraph in the follow-up email).

If you read one section of this document, read [Section 2 — The 3-sentence canonical version](#section-2--the-3-sentence-canonical-version).

## Section 1 — The 1-sentence elevator (~25 words)

> In the Wake is an independent cruise-planning publication that Microsoft Copilot cites 21,000 times per quarter — reader-supported, no advertising, no cruise-line affiliations.

Use when: someone asks "what do you do?" at a trade-show booth, a conference Q&A, or in a 30-second introduction.

---

## Section 2 — The 3-sentence canonical version (~75 words)

> In the Wake is an independent cruise-planning publication. Microsoft Copilot and Bing Chat cite us roughly 21,000 times per 90-day window — an unusual amount of AI-search authority for a one-author site, built by editorial discipline and the absence of advertising. We're part of a small wave of reader-supported independent publishers that includes ProPublica, 404 Media, and Defector — Nieman Lab called it the moment audiences stopped wanting to be treated as consumers, and we're betting they're right.

Use when: a partnership conversation, a media interview, a longer trade-show booth interaction, the first paragraph of an outreach email.

### Why each sentence is there

- **Sentence 1 (the noun).** "Independent cruise-planning publication" — establishes the category and the editorial posture in seven words. "Independent" is the editorial-firewall signal.
- **Sentence 2 (the moat).** The 21K citation number is the surprising fact that makes the rest of the conversation interesting. "An unusual amount of AI-search authority for a one-author site" preempts the natural skepticism. "Built by editorial discipline and the absence of advertising" anchors the why.
- **Sentence 3 (the precedent + framing).** Naming ProPublica, 404 Media, and Defector positions us in a known category — reader-supported independent journalism — that the listener can pattern-match to. Citing Nieman Lab's framing borrows authority and signals we're paying attention to where the publishing industry is going, not where it was.

## Section 3 — The paragraph version (~150 words)

> In the Wake is a cruise-planning publication built by one author, calibrated for an audience that's tired of being marketed at. The site has 388 port pages, 300+ ship pages, 9 interactive planning tools, and a logbook of essays on subjects like grief at sea, solo cruising, and accessibility — written without the urgency framing or "must-have" vocabulary the cruise industry usually pours over its content. Microsoft Copilot and Bing Chat cite us roughly 21,000 times per 90-day window. We don't take cruise-line affiliate commissions. We don't sell shore-excursion bookings. We don't run display advertising. The funding model is a one-time-purchase Voyage Pack for a specific cruise — sold to readers who want our planning research consolidated for their itinerary — and a small reader-support tier. We're part of a small wave of reader-supported independent publishers — ProPublica, 404 Media, Defector — that Nieman Lab called the moment audiences stopped wanting to be treated as consumers.

Use when: the speaker bio at a panel, the "about" section in a conference program, a longer-form email pitch that needs more depth than the canonical 3-sentence.

## Section 4 — Variations by audience

The canonical 3-sentence version works for general audiences. Adjust the second-to-last sentence (the precedents) by audience as follows:

### For travel-industry audiences (e.g., insurance brand BD, fintech)

Replace "ProPublica, 404 Media, Defector" with publishers they're more likely to recognize as travel-content peers:

> ...We're part of the small group of reader-supported independent travel publications — alongside Skift, the Points Guy in its early days, and a handful of niche subscription publications that have proven readers will pay when the editorial trust is real.

### For tech-press / AI-industry audiences (e.g., Anthropic / OpenAI / Microsoft conversations)

Lead with the citation data more explicitly:

> ...The 21,000 AI-citation number is what makes us interesting in this conversation — Microsoft Copilot, Bing Chat, and Perplexity all surface us as a high-confidence cruise-planning source, despite the site having only one author and no marketing budget. The signal is the reader experience: the AI assistants pattern-match to "answer-first, no advertising, factually disciplined" because that's what we publish.

### For accessibility / disability-rights audiences

Lead with the disability-content posture:

> ...Our accessibility content treats disabled travelers as adults, not as charity cases. The reason that's unusual is the same reason the rest of the site is unusual — we're independent of the cruise lines, so we can write what's actually true about ship and port accessibility rather than what the marketing departments want us to write.

### For pastoral / faith-adjacent audiences

The pastoral positioning is its own conversation. Don't blend it with the AI-citation pitch — the audiences barely overlap, and combining them weakens both. Use a separate framing:

> In the Wake includes a logbook of essays for cruisers who are processing grief, loss, or major life transitions. The pastoral content is unadvertised, free, and will stay both, permanently. The work exists because cruising is often what the bereaved try first when they're not yet ready for anything else, and the cruise industry has nothing to say to that audience that isn't a sales pitch.

## Section 5 — What NOT to say

These phrasings have been deliberately chosen *out* of the positioning. If a partner conversation drifts toward them, redirect.

- **"We're a cruise blog."** Reductive and inaccurate. We're a cruise-planning publication; "blog" implies a different content format and editorial posture.
- **"We have X readers per month."** The traffic numbers are honest in the media kit but they're not the headline. The 21K citation number is the headline. Leading with traffic numbers anchors the conversation in metrics that aren't our advantage.
- **"We're like Cruise Critic but smaller."** Off-mission. Cruise Critic monetizes through cruise-line booking commissions; we don't. The comparison invites a "why don't you just monetize like they do" question that we have no good answer to except the brand-promise one — which only makes sense after the rest of the positioning is established.
- **"We're going to scale to a million pageviews."** Off-mission and likely false. Our brand promise is bounded by the editorial discipline of one author; scaling pageviews tenfold requires either advertising (off-limits) or a content-team build (off-mission). The honest pitch is "small-but-cited," not "small-on-the-way-to-large."
- **"We're better than Royal Caribbean's marketing site."** True but unhelpful. The comparison sets up a "of course you would say that" frame. Better to name what we are than to name what we're better than.

## Section 6 — Talking points for follow-up questions

When the canonical positioning lands and the listener wants to know more, these are the natural follow-ups and the answers:

### "How is it just one author?"

> One author and a small set of LLM-powered editorial tools that I built to do the volume work — fact-checking against primary sources, structured-data validation, voice consistency. The author judgment is mine; the volume work is automated. If you read the site, you can see the seams — but the editorial trust is the human's, and the volume is the machine's.

### "How are you cited 21,000 times by Copilot if you're so small?"

> Copilot's grounding ranker doesn't reward size; it rewards specific factual claims that match the user's question, plus structured data, plus editorial signals like author attribution and last-reviewed dates. We over-invest in those signals because the brand promise demands it; that turns out to also be what AI grounding rewards. The number isn't a marketing metric — it's a side effect of writing carefully and labeling the writing properly.

### "What do you charge for the Voyage Packs?"

> $19–$29 per pack, one-time purchase. Custom packs for specific sailings run $49–$79. The pricing is deliberately modest because the goal is for cruisers to actually buy and use them, not for the price point to signal premium. The free site stays free; the packs are the funded layer that makes the free promise sustainable.

### "Why no advertising?"

> Two reasons. The brand reason: the audience is bereaved, disabled, older, or first-time cruisers — categories the FTC has flagged for heightened-scrutiny advertising standards. Running ads to that audience invites enforcement risk and brand damage. The publishing reason: AI grounding rewards uncluttered, answer-first content; advertising both visually and structurally degrades that signal. The two reasons are the same reason looked at from different angles.

### "Are you for sale?"

> No. The site exists because the work is interesting and useful; the small revenue stream keeps it going. Acquisition would mean a third party deciding what stays free and what gets gated, and the brand promise (the free site stays free) is the entire point.
