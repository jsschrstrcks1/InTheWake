Casablanca: My Moroccan Masterpiece

When our ship approached Casablanca at dawn, the Hassan II Mosque appeared like a vision rising from the Atlantic itself – exactly as King Hassan II intended when he commissioned French architect Michel Pinseau to build it on an artificial peninsula extending into the sea. The King was inspired by a verse from the Quran: "the throne of God was erected on the waters." Seeing it from the water, I understood the poetry of that directive. This is the second largest mosque in the Islamic world after Mecca – 200 meters by 100 meters of sacred geometry wrapped in ocean spray.

We took the first shuttle from Casa Port (conveniently located right at the port exit – perfect if you're planning a day trip to Rabat by train) and arrived as morning sun struck the 210-meter minaret, the tallest religious structure in the world. Inside, we joined the English-language guided tour – this is the only mosque in all of Morocco where non-Muslim visitors are welcomed – and stood speechless beneath cedar ceilings carved like lace by some of Morocco's finest craftsmen. Over 12,500 workers spent seven years building this masterpiece, which opened on August 30, 1993. Zellige tiles in every imaginable color, marble columns, and retractable roof panels that open to let in sunlight and sea air. The ablutions hall has glass floors revealing the Atlantic waves below – you wash before prayer with the ocean literally beneath your feet.

After that spiritual immersion, we walked La Corniche – the sweeping seafront promenade with ocean views that remind you this is a living, breathing port city – and then gave in to the inevitable tourist pilgrimage: Rick's Café. Yes, it's a movie recreation (the original film set was in Hollywood, not Morocco), but the café that opened here later is lovingly done – piano music, white-jacketed waiters, perfect Moroccan salads, and enough cinematic nostalgia to make you smile even as you know it's theater. In the afternoon we explored the Habous quarter – the "new medina" built by the French in the 1930s, cleaner than the old souks, with bookshops and patisseries smelling of orange blossom and almond. The pros: the mosque alone justifies any port call to Casablanca. The cons: this is a big working city, not a tourist fantasy – but that's precisely its charm. It doesn't perform for you; it simply is.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing beneath the retractable roof of Hassan II as sunlight poured through and the Atlantic crashed against the foundation stones – feeling incredibly small and immensely privileged at the same time. This is architecture as prayer, as defiance, as beauty offered to God and ocean alike.

Getting Around Casablanca

Ship shuttle or taxi to mosque (20–30 min). Everything else is easy from there.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

The mosque has strict dress and behavior codes – covering up respectfully opens one of the world's most beautiful buildings to you.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Casablanca worth it?
A: For Hassan II Mosque alone, yes.

Q: Best thing?
A: Guided mosque tour.

Q: How long for mosque?
A: 2 hours including tour.

Q: Walk from port?
A: No – transport required.

Author's Note

Until I have sailed this port myself, these notes are soundings in another's wake. I've researched and written this guide with care — drawing on firsthand accounts, historical sources, and practical logistics — but I haven't yet walked these shores or stood beneath the retractable roof of Hassan II Mosque as sunlight poured through and the Atlantic crashed below. When I do visit, I'll return to these pages and add the textures that only direct experience brings: the exact quality of light on zellige tiles, the feeling of waves beneath glass floors, the taste of Moroccan salads at Rick's Café, the scent of orange blossom in the Habous quarter. Until then, consider this a chart drawn from trusted navigators, waiting for my own sextant reading to confirm its accuracy.

Quick Answer: Casablanca is the massive Hassan II Mosque rising from the Atlantic – one of the few mosques non-Muslims can enter – plus art-deco architecture and real Moroccan city energy.
