## Section 1 — Your Ship: Norwegian Luna {#section-1}

### A brand-new Prima Plus ship in her debut season

[Norwegian Luna](https://www.ncl.com/cruise-ships/norwegian-luna) is one of the **newest cruise ships in the world** at the time of your sailing — delivered March 5, 2026 by Fincantieri at the Marghera (Venice) shipyard, with her maiden voyage on March 10, 2026 (a transatlantic from Civitavecchia to [PortMiami](/ports/miami.html) via Barcelona and Gibraltar) and formal christening by godmother ELLE on March 27, 2026. Her inaugural Caribbean season began shortly after and continues year-round through 2026; see [Caribbean Cruise Trends 2026](/articles/caribbean-cruise-trends-2026.html) for the wider context of where the major lines are pointing their newest ships this year. By your May 16 sailing she's been in service about eight weeks — long enough that the crew is settling in, short enough that you'll still feel the new-ship sheen.

She's the **second and final ship of the Prima Plus class** — a roughly 10% larger evolution of NCL's Prima class. Her direct sister is [Norwegian Aqua](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-aqua.html) (2025, the debut Prima Plus); the original Prima class is Norwegian Prima (2022) and Norwegian Viva (2023). If you've sailed Aqua before, Luna is the same hull form and the same general layout with refinements; the Prima/Viva pair are smaller but share the family DNA.

Her godmother is **ELLE**, a street artist from Napa, California. NCL has been choosing modern-art-aligned godmothers and godfathers across the Prima family (Katy Perry for Prima, Luis Fonsi for Viva), and the choice tracks with the line's contemporary visual identity — graffiti motifs, bold ship-side art, atrium installations.

### What's distinctive about Luna

This is a ship designed for **outdoor deck time at sea** rather than indoor weather contingency — well-suited to the Caribbean. The signature features:

- **Ocean Boulevard** — a 360-degree outdoor promenade wrapping the ship, with **infinity pools** that hang over the hull edge and **glass bridges** that span between deck areas. Most ships put their promenade indoors or on a lower deck; Luna's wraps the upper deck at sea level views.
- **Aqua Slidecoaster** — a hybrid water slide / roller coaster with magnetic propulsion. Free; first-come; lines build by mid-morning on sea days.
- [**Penrose Atrium**](/restaurants/ncl/penrose-bar.html) — the **first three-level atrium for NCL**, a major departure from the line's recent single-level atrium designs. The social heart of the ship; this is where [Tina](/authors/tina-maulsby.html)'s hosted meet-and-greets will most likely happen.
- **Expanded Mandara Spa** with a two-story waterfall — larger than Aqua's spa; thermal area, treatment rooms, hydrotherapy pool.
- **35 venues** including seven fleet-first dining and bar concepts — Luna pushes further into specialty dining and themed bars than any prior NCL ship.
- [**The Haven**](/restaurants/ncl/the-haven-restaurant.html) — NCL's ship-within-a-ship luxury enclave, spread across **eight decks** with private restaurant, courtyard, [Haven Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/haven-lounge.html), and pool. If you booked a Haven suite, your week is materially different from the rest of the ship.

What's NOT on Luna (because she's Prima Plus, not Breakaway-Plus or Bliss-class): no ice rink, no traditional 3-story Broadway theater (Luna uses the **Luna Theatre** and Improv venues for shows); the production show culture is closer to a Las Vegas residency than to Broadway. That's the right comparison to set expectations.

### Cabin categories — the solo-cruiser calculus

**Before you commit, run the [stateroom sanity check](/articles/stateroom-sanity-check.html)** — a four-question filter that catches the bookings most likely to leave a cruiser unhappy. Studios screen well for confident sleepers and casual solo travelers; the filter flags the few cases where the windowless interior is a problem.

NCL's signature solo offering applies on Luna: dedicated **Studio cabins** with **no single supplement**, [**Studio Lounge**](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html) **access** (free coffee, social space, scheduled hosted hours for solo cruisers), and a built-in cohort of fellow solos. The Studio is roughly 100 sq ft and windowless — compact and efficient. The Studio Lounge is your daily social anchor independent of the group meetups.

**Decision rule (for reference — and for any future Luna sailing you might book):**

The May 16, 2026 group rates were:

- **Studio ($1,839)** — best value if you're confident in the solo-cabin culture and you prioritize spend on excursions and dining over space. Studio cabins on Prima Plus ships are well-designed and the Studio Lounge is a real amenity.
- **Oceanview ($3,059)** — meaningful upgrade in space and natural light; full single-occupancy pricing because Oceanview categories typically don't waive the supplement. Worth it for travelers who feel claustrophobic in interior cabins or want to read by a window.
- **Balcony ($3,349)** — the Caribbean argument for a balcony is real: sea-day mornings, port-day sailaways, late-night anchor watches. For a 7-night Caribbean cruise, the price difference vs. Studio is significant; this is a comfort choice, not a necessity.

If you're already aboard, you're in whatever category you booked — skip this and head to Section 2. If you're considering Tina's next Luna group, the same calculus applies; rates will shift but the shape of the decision won't.

For ship-size context, the [Ship Size Atlas](/tools/ship-size-atlas.html) lets you compare Luna to other ships. You can follow her live position throughout your week via the [Ship Logbook](/tools/ship-tracker.html) — a small comfort if family ashore wants to know where you are.

**Included main dining:**

- [**The Local Bar & Grill**](/restaurants/ncl/the-local.html) — pub-style, 24-hour favorites.

**Specialty restaurants (paid extra):**

### Drinks

NCL's drink-package math is straightforward: the Premium Beverage Package is commonly included in the **"Free at Sea"** promotional bundle when booking — so if Tina priced your fare with Free at Sea attached, your drinks are already covered. Otherwise, the package runs $109/day per person + 20% gratuity at pre-cruise rates. **At those numbers, the package breaks even at 5–6 drinks per day.** For most cruisers, pay-as-you-go ends up cheaper than expected. The [drink-calculator](/drink-calculator.html) lets you put in your real consumption pattern. The companion article [Is the drink package worth it?](/articles/is-drink-package-worth-it.html) walks through the breakeven math with worked examples.

## Section 2 — Day by Day {#section-2}

The verified day-by-day for the May 16 – 23, 2026 sailing. Times and port stops confirmed against NCL's published itinerary as of writing.

### Day 1 — Saturday, May 16 — PortMiami (embarkation)

**Arrival:** Norwegian Luna sails from **PortMiami** on Dodge Island, ~10 minutes from downtown Miami and ~20 minutes from MIA by Uber/Lyft. The NCL terminal is typically **Terminal B**; verify your assignment from your NCL eDocs. Embark window typically 11:00 AM – 3:00 PM; ship departs 4:00 PM.

**The realistic Day 1 timeline:**

- **11:00 AM – 1:00 PM:** First-wave embarkation at the NCL terminal. Drop bags with porter at curb (tip $1–2/bag in cash). Proceed through security and NCL check-in.
- **1:00 – 2:00 PM:** Cabins typically NOT ready until 2 PM (Luna policy may vary). Carry-on with you to **Surfside Café** for a low-key embarkation lunch, or **Indulge Food Hall** if it's open Day 1.
- **2:00 PM:** Cabins open. Find your cabin. Introduce yourself to your stateroom steward — they remember names, and the relationship makes the week.
- **2:30 – 3:30 PM:** Light exploration. Find the **Penrose Atrium** (the social heart and where [Tina](/authors/tina-maulsby.html) is most likely to hold her welcome meetup), **Hudson's** on Deck 8 (because that's where you'll be at 5:30 every night this week — see Section 9), and your nearest elevator bank.
- **3:30 PM:** Mandatory muster drill — NCL's drill is partly app-based (watch a safety video in the NCL app) plus an in-person check-in at your assigned station. Don't skip; the captain won't sail until 100% completion.
- **4:00 PM:** Sailaway. Best viewing from **Ocean Boulevard** (Deck 8 wraparound) on the side facing the channel, or from your balcony. The departure past Star Island and out through Government Cut is a Miami signature view.
- **5:30 PM:** **Dinner at Hudson's** — the first night of the group's standing 5:30 gathering. Show up if you can; if your bags are still in the hall, eat in your clothes — Hudson's dress code is smart casual all week.

**What to pack in your carry-on for Day 1:** swimsuit (pools open before bags arrive), light layer (Miami evenings can be 70°F in May), all medications, change of clothes, passport (strongly preferred over birth-cert+ID for this Caribbean itinerary because Tortola and St. Thomas have different documentation expectations), phone charger, refillable water bottle, embarkation paperwork. Your checked bags may not arrive until 7–9 PM.

**Money/card:** Your NCL keycard is your ID, room key, and onboard charge. You'll receive it at check-in. NCL's onboard currency is US Dollar.

### Day 2 — Sunday, May 17 — Cruising at sea

The settling-in day. The group's rhythm starts taking shape.

**Morning:**
- Coffee on **Ocean Boulevard** is the day's strongest move — outdoor seating with sea views, the first hour after sunrise has the best light.
- Breakfast at **Surfside Café** (buffet, busy) or **Hudson's** (sit-down, calmer, often offering a more reliable hot coffee).

**Mid-morning to lunch — pick ONE:**

- **The Penrose Atrium** for whatever Tina's running. The first hosted group activity is often a Day-2 morning meet-and-greet; check her group communications for the specific time and location.
- **The Mandara Spa thermal-suite day pass.** Sea-day mornings are when introductory rates appear. Worth it if you'd otherwise pay daily rates.
- **The Studio Lounge** if you booked a Studio cabin — host hours typically run morning and evening; daytime is quieter and good for finding your fellow Studio neighbors.
- **The Aqua Slidecoaster** — short lines first thing.

**Afternoon:**
- The Caribbean Sea outside your cabin window is the day's best entertainment. Don't over-program.
- **Trivia and games** in the social bars.
- A theater show if anything's scheduled.

**5:30 PM: Dinner at Hudson's.** Night two of the group's standing time.

### Day 3 — Monday, May 18 — Puerto Plata (Amber Cove), Dominican Republic {#day-puerto-plata}

![Amber Cove cruise terminal at Maimón Bay, Puerto Plata — two cruise ships at the pier, the resort village's thatched-roof overwater bungalows in the foreground. Photo by Ulises Icardi via Wikimedia Commons, CC BY-SA 4.0](/ports/img/puerto-plata/puerto-plata-hero.webp)

**Arrival:** 7:00 AM. Depart 3:00 PM. **You dock at Amber Cove**, not the older Puerto Plata pier or the newer Taino Bay terminal — Amber Cove is a purpose-built cruise port in Maimón Bay, about 10 miles west of downtown Puerto Plata.

**Amber Cove is not Puerto Plata.** Amber Cove was developed by Carnival Corporation and opened in 2015 — a private resort-style terminal with a Spanish colonial aesthetic, infinity pools, a swim-up bar, restaurants, shops, and an excursions plaza. It's designed for ships to dock and for guests to never leave if they don't want to. The actual city — Mount Isabel de Torres cable car, Fort San Felipe, the Malecón seafront — is a 20–30 minute taxi ride away. The 27 Waterfalls of Damajagua (the region's signature natural attraction) is about 45 minutes inland.

**Free at the terminal:**

- The Amber Cove resort grounds — beach chairs, umbrellas, pool with swim-up bar and waterslide.
- Restaurants and shops (paid food/drinks, free browsing).
- A short walking circuit around the colonial-style village.

**Paid options (decide ahead — ship-sold or independent):**

- **27 Waterfalls of Damajagua** — *the region's signature excursion.* Hike upstream through a narrow river canyon and either climb or slide down 7, 12, or 27 limestone-pool waterfalls. Wet, physical, unforgettable. Ship excursions ~$130–180; independent operators ~$60–100. **Wear water shoes or sturdy trainers you don't mind getting soaked.**
- **Mount Isabel de Torres cable car** — to a 2,600-ft summit with a statue of Christ the Redeemer (smaller cousin of Rio's) and panoramic views. ~$30 independent / ~$70 ship-sold. **Verify operating status before booking** — the cable car has been undergoing a multi-year replacement and may be closed during your sailing; the ship's excursion desk will confirm.
- **Ocean World adventure park** — dolphin swims, shark encounter, sea lion show. ~10 min from port. Family-targeted; high cost.
- **Playa Dorada beach** — long white-sand beach with restaurants and bars. Public access; ~$30 round-trip taxi.
- **City tour of Puerto Plata** — Fort San Felipe, Amber Museum, Cigar Factory, Central Plaza. ~$40 independent.

**For first-time visitors to the Dominican Republic:** the 27 Waterfalls is the iconic excursion. If physical activity isn't right today (the climb requires fitness; the slides are not for the height-shy), the Mount Isabel cable car (if operating) + Playa Dorada combo is a strong half-day; if the cable car is closed, swap in Fort San Felipe and the Amber Museum.

**For each port day**, the [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html) tool builds an hour-by-hour timeline from your priorities — useful when juggling tender wave, group meet-ups, and ship-back time. You can also log your day to your [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) for future reference.

**Currency and tipping:** the Dominican peso is local currency; **USD is widely accepted at Amber Cove and major tourist spots**. Tip 10–15% on tour costs; $1–2 per taxi ride; $1 per drink at the bar.

**For accessibility:** Amber Cove terminal is fully accessible (paved, level paths). Most off-port excursions involve unpaved trails or non-accessible vehicles; confirm specific tour accessibility with the operator in advance.

**Back on board by 2:30 PM** for a 3:00 PM all-aboard. Don't cut this close — Caribbean roads and tour vans don't always keep schedule.

**5:30 PM: Dinner at Hudson's.** Even on port days. The whole point of the standing time is that it's standing.

### Day 4 — Tuesday, May 19 — St. Thomas, US Virgin Islands {#day-st-thomas}

![Charlotte Amalie harbor seen from above — the cruise ships, the colonial roofs, the green hills behind](/ports/img/st-thomas/city-view.webp)

**Arrival:** 10:00 AM. Depart 6:00 PM. NCL Luna typically docks at the **WICO (West Indian Company) Dock** or **Crown Bay**, both within taxi distance of **Charlotte Amalie** (the capital and main town).

**What St. Thomas is.** A US territory since 1917; population ~50,000; densely cruise-touristed (St. Thomas is one of the Caribbean's busiest cruise ports, often with 4–6 ships in port simultaneously). The downtown of **Charlotte Amalie** is a working town with a strong Danish-colonial layer (the Danes ruled until 1917 — the architectural fingerprint is everywhere). The duty-free shopping district along Main Street and around Drake's Passage is dense and aggressive. Beyond shopping, the island has good beaches, a real maritime history, and walking-distance fortifications.

**Free, walkable from the cruise terminal (or short taxi):**

- **Fort Christian** — 1672 Danish colonial fort, the oldest standing structure on St. Thomas. Small museum; ~10-minute walk from WICO.
- **Blackbeard's Castle** — historic complex up the hill with 99 Steps and Skytsborg Tower; pirate-era history. ~$15 entry.
- **Drake's Seat overlook** — panoramic view across St. John and the BVI. Reachable by safari taxi (~$15 round-trip per person).
- **99 Steps** — the iconic Charlotte Amalie staircase; built from Danish ballast bricks. Free, photogenic, brief uphill.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Magens Bay** — repeatedly listed among the world's most beautiful beaches; calm water, palm trees, the postcard image. $5 entry + ~$10 taxi each way. **For most cruisers visiting St. Thomas, Magens Bay is the right choice.**
- **Coki Beach + Coral World** — beach + small marine park; good for snorkeling beginners. Coki Beach is free; Coral World ~$30.
- **St. John day trip** — ferry from Red Hook to St. John (the quieter, mostly-national-park sister island), then a taxi to **Trunk Bay** or **Cinnamon Bay**. ~$80–120 round trip including ferry and taxis. **Worth the effort if you want the National Park experience.**
- **Sailing day trip** (catamaran + snorkel) — $130–180/person.

**For first-time St. Thomas:** Magens Bay is the default-good choice for ~half a day. If you have the energy for the ferry-and-taxi work, St. John is the deeper Virgin Islands experience.

**Currency:** US Dollar (it's a US territory). Standard US tipping practices apply.

**For accessibility:** WICO and Crown Bay terminals are accessible. Charlotte Amalie sidewalks are mixed; the 99 Steps is stairs (not accessible by definition). Magens Bay has accessible parking, restrooms, and beach access.

**Back on board by 5:30 PM** for 6:00 PM all-aboard. The 5:30 dinner at Hudson's still applies — you'll roll off the gangway, change quickly, and meet the group.

### Day 5 — Wednesday, May 20 — Tortola, British Virgin Islands {#day-tortola}

**Arrival:** **6:45 AM.** Depart **2:00 PM.** *This is the trip's earliest dock and earliest departure.* If you want any of the day, set an alarm.

NCL Luna docks at the **Tortola Pier Park** in Road Town, the BVI capital — directly adjacent to the cruise terminal complex, no tender required.

**What Tortola is.** The largest of the British Virgin Islands; population ~24,000; a British Overseas Territory still using the US Dollar despite the British flag. The BVI is *quieter* than St. Thomas — fewer ships, fewer aggressive vendors, a more deliberate pace. The island is mountainous; the iconic destinations are reached by safari taxi over winding roads, not on foot.

**Free, walkable from the pier:**

- **Road Town** — Main Street walkable; the J.R. O'Neal Botanic Gardens (~10-min walk, ~$5 entry — quietly excellent); the old courthouse and government complex.
- **Pier Park** — a planned cruise-port shopping/dining complex; restaurants and shops at the gangway.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Cane Garden Bay** — one of the BVI's signature beaches; a curve of white sand backed by green hills. ~$20–30 taxi each way. Calm water, restaurants, bars. **For most cruisers in Tortola, this is the day.**
- **The Baths on Virgin Gorda** — *the iconic BVI experience.* Massive granite boulders forming sea caves and natural pools you swim through. Requires a ferry from Tortola to Virgin Gorda + taxi. A full half-day; with the 2 PM all-aboard, this requires precise timing and is usually only practical via a ship-sold or pre-booked package. ~$150–200/person all in.
- **Sage Mountain National Park** — Tortola's high point and rainforest. Hiking; cool temperatures; less-crowded. Half-day excursion.
- **Sailing or snorkeling day trip** — catamarans to Norman Island or the BVI's small reef islands. $130–180/person; standout for water-lovers.

**For first-time Tortola:** Cane Garden Bay is the right call if it's a beach day. If you're already a Caribbean-beach veteran and want something different, The Baths on Virgin Gorda is the postcard — but only via a tightly-scheduled ship excursion given the 2:00 PM all-aboard.

**Weather on May 20.** Tortola in May runs 78–88°F daytime, moderate humidity, steady trade winds; brief afternoon showers are possible but the wet stretch holds off until August. The 6:45 AM dock is dawn-cool at ~73°F — a light layer helps until the sun climbs. **The Baths and Sage Mountain trails are where weather actually bites:** wet granite is treacherously slick, and the rainforest can be ten degrees cooler than Road Town. Shoes with tread matter more than fashion. The seasonal panel and live forecast at the [Tortola port page](/ports/tortola.html) are the right read 24 hours before docking.

**Currency:** US Dollar (despite the British flag).

**For accessibility:** Pier Park is accessible. Most off-port excursions involve open-air safari taxis and unpaved beach approaches; verify in advance.

**Back on board by 1:30 PM** for 2:00 PM all-aboard. *Tortola is the day with the least margin.*

**5:30 PM: Dinner at Hudson's.** You'll be on board well before; the early departure means an easy afternoon on the ship.

### Day 6 — Thursday, May 21 — Cruising at sea

The week's second sea day. By now the group has formed; the rhythm of the ship is familiar; the cabin feels lived-in.

**Morning ideas:**
- **Spa** — book treatments on sea days; the spa is at its busiest mid-morning.
- **Aqua Slidecoaster** — the second sea day is when most guests have done it once and lines settle.
- **Trivia / lectures / activities** in the day's program.
- **Ocean Boulevard** with a coffee and a book.

### Day 7 — Friday, May 22 — Great Stirrup Cay, Bahamas {#day-great-stirrup-cay}

![Great Stirrup Cay, Bahamas — NCL's private island in the Berry Islands seen from an approaching cruise ship, the blue-and-white tower visible at center. Photo by Larry D. Moore via Wikimedia Commons, CC BY 4.0](/ports/img/great-stirrup-cay/great-stirrup-cay-hero.webp)

**Arrival:** 10:00 AM. Depart 6:00 PM. Great Stirrup Cay is **NCL's private island** in the Berry Islands chain of the Bahamas, ~120 nautical miles from Miami. *Tender required for this sailing* — the new $150M pier is under construction with a closure window April 1 through summer 2026, so Luna anchors offshore and tenders ferry guests in during your visit. Once fully operational the new pier will accommodate two cruise ships at once and eliminate routine tendering.

**What Great Stirrup Cay actually is in May 2026.** NCL has had this island since 1977 — it was the first cruise-line private island in the Caribbean. As of 2026, NCL is mid-redevelopment with a **$150M investment**. By your May 22 visit, the existing experiences are in service (Vibe Shore Club, Flighthouse Zipline, cabanas, beach BBQ, Silver Cove for the premium-paying crowd) but the **headline new attraction — Great Tides Waterpark — is NOT yet open. NCL has now set its grand opening for September 4, 2026**, three-and-a-half months after your sailing. You'll see construction infrastructure in the future-waterpark zone and around the new **Welcome Plaza** (the upcoming tender-pier arrival area) and the **Great Life Lagoon** (a 1.4-acre pool area with swim-up bars). The island is in transition — prettier than it was, but not yet finished. If the waterpark is the attraction that would matter to you, **the May 16 sailing isn't the one for it** — September 2026 onward catches the opening.

**What "Great Tides Waterpark" will be** (per NCL's official September 2026 grand-opening press release, for context): a nearly six-acre, 19-slide complex anchored by the **170-foot Tidal Tower** featuring eight slides (including the **Breakwater Blasters** water coasters with inner-tube downhill drops and uphill blasts). Other named features per NCL's release: **Whitewater Dash** body slides, **Rapid Mat Racers** mat slides, **Tropic Storm / Tropic Spiral** tube slides, **Cliffside Cove** (which NCL bills as "the industry's first and highest cliff jumps"), **The Great Slide** (NCL-billed as "the Caribbean's only four-person body slide"), **Fearless Falls** racing slides, the **Wandering River** (over 800 feet, NCL describes as "a high-energy take on the classic lazy river"), and **Splash Cay** — a 9,000-square-foot kids' zone. The **Grotto Bar** is a hidden cocktail bar inside the waterpark. Pricing reservation-based via the NCL for Me app. **None of this is available on your May sailing** — but the *Freestyle Daily* may include construction walk-throughs or preview tours as the opening approaches.

**Three main beaches:**

- **Infinity Beach** — the central, busiest beach; convenient to bars, restaurants, the lighthouse.
- **Lighthouse Beach** — the south side, near the historic lighthouse and the new Lighthouse Zipline.
- **Cabana Beach** — for guests who pre-booked a cabana; quieter, premium.

**What's included (free):**

- Beach access at all open beach zones
- Loungers (first-come, first-served) and beach umbrellas
- Tender transport ship-to-island
- **BBQ buffet lunch** (typical Bahamas-American: jerk chicken, ribs, salads, hot dogs, burgers)
- Snorkel gear from the watersports hut (deposit required)

**Paid options:**

## Section 7 — Seasickness on a Caribbean Cruise {#section-7}

The Caribbean in May is generally calm. Norwegian Luna is large (156,300 GT) and her stabilizers handle most swell well. The transit between Miami and the Dominican Republic crosses about 800 nm of open water — that's the most you'll feel motion this week, and it's usually minimal.

**The baseline:** if you've never been seasick on land or in a boat, you almost certainly won't be on this itinerary. For most cruisers a 7-night Caribbean is the smoothest cruise itinerary they'll ever take.

### What to bring

- **Bonine** (meclizine) or **Dramamine Less Drowsy** — over-the-counter, take 1 hour before expected motion.
- **Sea-Bands** — pressure-point wristbands. Mixed evidence but harmless.
- **Ginger candies** — backup for mild queasiness.

### What helps once you feel it

- Get on deck — Ocean Boulevard is the best place on Luna for this.
- Eat something dry: crackers, dry toast, pretzels.
- Avoid alcohol.
- Take meclizine at the first hint, not after it's full-on.

If you booked a Studio cabin (interior, no window), you don't have horizon access from your cabin. Cure: spend daylight hours on Ocean Boulevard. Most motion-sensitive Studio cruisers are fine.

## Section 9 — The Group Cruise Rhythm {#section-9}

[![Tina Maulsby — your group host on this sailing](/authors/tina2.webp)](/authors/tina-maulsby.html)

This is the section to read first if you're already on the ship.

**[Tina](/authors/tina-maulsby.html)'s set one fixed point this week: dinner together at Hudson's, Deck 8, every evening at 5:30 PM.** Everything else — the morning, the port-day, the spa, the show, the late evening — you and the group will work out as you go, hour by hour and day by day. That's by design.

A hosted solo-traveler group works best when there's enough scaffolding to find each other at predictable times, and enough open water to let each traveler shape the rest of the week to their own pace. The 5:30 dinner at Hudson's is your standing point of reunion. Show up if you can. If a port day runs long, or you want a quiet night in your cabin, or a friendship from yesterday is calling you to a different table — you haven't missed the group. You'll see them tomorrow at 5:30.

That's the shape of solo group cruising: company without obligation, solitude without isolation.

### What to expect of the standing dinner

- **5:30 PM, Hudson's, Deck 8** — NCL's main dining room with 270-degree floor-to-ceiling windows. Sunset on most evenings is between 7:45 and 8:00 PM in May Caribbean latitudes; by the time you finish dinner the light over the water is at its best.
- The group is likely seated at a large communal table or several adjacent four-tops. Arrive a few minutes early; introduce yourself to anyone you haven't met yet.
- Order anything. There's no "group menu." Hudson's serves the full Freestyle Dining menu.
- Conversation tends to lean to the day — what each person did in port, what they're planning for tomorrow, the small surprises of the ship. Don't worry about being "interesting"; the room handles the interesting.

### What to expect of the rest of the day

Tina runs hosted activities that surface as they make sense — typically a **welcome meet-and-greet on Day 1 or Day 2**, periodic **gatherings in the [Penrose Atrium](/restaurants/ncl/penrose-bar.html) or a quiet lounge** like the [Studio Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html) or [Observation Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/observation-lounge.html), and a **farewell evening** late in the week. Watch the group communication channel (Tina sends app messages or texts) for the specifics. The rhythm is "show up when it fits; opt out when it doesn't."

### If you want company beyond the group

- **The [Studio Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html)** (if you're in a Studio cabin) — built-in solo community, hosted hours.
- **Pool deck mid-afternoon** — the social conversational time.
- **Trivia and games** — published in the daily program; teams form on the fly.
- **The bars before dinner** — Penrose Atrium bars and Ocean Boulevard bars host pre-dinner gatherings.

### If you want quiet

- **Your cabin balcony** (if you booked one) — entirely yours.
- **Ocean Boulevard** at sunrise — almost no one is up.
- **The Mandara Spa thermal area** — heated stone loungers, sauna, steam; quiet by design.
- **Hudson's at lunch** — much quieter than dinner; you can read while you eat.

The group is the floor, not the ceiling. Build the cruise you want on top of it.

## Section 11 — First Cruise Notes {#section-11}

If this is your first cruise of any kind (not just first NCL), a few things that won't be obvious:

**The ship is a small city.** ~3,500 passengers, ~1,400 crew, 20 decks. You won't see all of it. Make peace with this.

**Embarkation is logistical, not magical.** Day 1 is paperwork, lines, and finding things. The good part starts on Day 2.

**The buffet (Surfside Café) is not the best food on the ship.** It's fine. **Hudson's** is better — and you're eating there at 5:30 every night anyway. **Indulge Food Hall** is the casual standout: multi-vendor counters, small plates, well-paced.

**You're going to forget your keycard the first day.** Everyone does. Get a lanyard.

**The first sea day, you'll feel disoriented.** That's normal. Pick 2 things from the daily program, do them, sit down, stare at water. You'll find the rhythm.

**The crew remembers you.** Your stateroom steward, the Hudson's waiter you'll see every night — they learn your name, your drink preferences, your allergies. The relationship is real. It's worth being kind, and it's worth tipping in cash above the service charge if service warrants it.

**NCL is more casual than the other major US lines.** No formal nights. The pool deck is louder than Princess or Holland America. The vibe is "freestyle" — that's a brand word but it's also the truth of the experience.

**Luna is brand-new.** You're on a ship that's been in service for eight weeks. A few systems may have first-quarter rough edges. Most things will be smoother than on a 15-year-old ship.

**You'll spend more on excursions than you think.** Build a "shore-day budget" before sailing. The [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html) helps prevent the surprise.

**You'll come home wanting to go back.** Most first-time solo cruisers do another within 18 months. Plan accordingly — don't book the next cruise during the first 48 hours back, but know it's coming.

For more first-cruise context across cruise lines, see the broader [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html).
