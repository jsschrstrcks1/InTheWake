Cruise Tipping for 2026: What You'll Actually Owe, by Line

The daily charge is the part you've heard about. The rest of the math is what catches first-time cruisers off-guard.

by Ken Baker | May 8, 2026

Answer first: In 2026, ten major lines charge $17 to $25 per guest per day in daily gratuities. Five — Explora, Oceania, Regent, Seabourn, Silversea — bundle the tip into the fare. On top of the daily charge, expect 18% or 20% auto-grats on bar, spa, specialty dining, and room service. Cash for porters, butlers, and kids' club is optional and outside the daily charge.

Most first-time cruisers find out about gratuities the same way: a line on the booking page, vague enough to skim past. A friend mentions envelopes on the last night. The porter at the pier holds out a hand. The spa receipt comes back with a 20% line item already added. The "auto-charge" framing on the booking page makes it sound like one decision the cruise line makes for you. Most of the time it isn't.

What follows is what's actually charged in 2026, and where the gaps are. Numbers trace to each line's official policy page, verified within the last 60 days. Different lines call it different things — Carnival's "gratuity," Holland America's "Crew Appreciation," Cunard's "Hotel and Dining Service Charge" — but it's all one charge.

How the Daily Charge Works

The daily charge is a pooled tip. The stateroom attendant gets a piece, the dining team — head waiter, assistant waiter, busser — gets a piece, and so do the laundry crew, the buffet runners, and a long list of people you'll never see. Cruise Critic notes that for housekeeping and F&B crew, tips can represent "as much as 95 percent" of take-home pay, because base wages are sometimes only a few dollars a day. Cruise Critic and Cruise Hive list cabin stewards at $650–$1,150 a month gratuity-inclusive — wide range, role-by-role variance.

Children are treated differently by every line. Carnival exempts under 2. Norwegian exempts under 3. Costa exempts under 4 and charges half on ages 4 to 14 in EUR/South America regions. Cunard, Holland America, Royal Caribbean, and Celebrity publish no cutoff — every guest gets billed. Virgin Voyages is adults-only.

Prepaying locks the rate. Book Carnival before April 2, 2026 and you keep the prior $16/$18; after, $17/$19. Same logic on every line that raised rates this year. Refund policy is line-specific — most lines refund up to the cancellation deadline and none after sailing.

You can adjust the charge at Guest Services. Most ships reduce or remove it on request without much pushback. The path is real, and so is the consequence: the money comes out of the pool, and the pool is how the steward who turned your bed down twice a day gets paid. Adjust if service was genuinely poor. Don't adjust to save $112 on a 7-night because the buffet felt crowded.

How to Do This on Paper

Step one: pull three numbers from your booking. Line, cabin category (the line's exact tier name), nights. Embarkation day is night one.

Step two: multiply. Daily rate × nights × charged guests. A 7-night Carnival balcony with two adults and one toddler under 2 is $17 × 7 × 2 = $238. The toddler is exempt under Carnival. On Norwegian with the same party, he's still exempt (under 3); on Royal Caribbean or Celebrity, he isn't.

Step three: estimate the auto-grats. These attach to optional purchases, not the daily charge. Bar tabs run 18% or 20%. Spa runs 15% to 20%. Specialty dining mostly runs 20% in 2026. Room service is 18% on most lines, free on a few. Two specialty dinners at $80 each is $160; the 20% surcharge on Carnival, Celebrity, Norwegian, or Princess is $32. On Royal Caribbean at 18% it's $28.80.

Step four: plan the cash. Porters at embarkation expect about $2 per bag — they're port-authority employees at most US homeports, not cruise-line crew. Add a last-night cash thank-you for anyone who went past the job: the steward who hid extra towels in the closet, the head waiter who remembered your kid's allergy. $5 to $10 a day where lines publish a number, and most don't. Sum it conservatively. That's your number.

Extras the Daily Charge Does Not Cover

Bar. Carnival raised its bar service charge from 18% to 20% in December 2025. Celebrity, Norwegian, and Princess (after March 8, 2026) sit at 20%. Royal Caribbean and MSC are at 18%; Holland America moves to 20% on June 1. Costa and Cunard publish 15%. Drink-package buyers pay this once at purchase.

Spa and salon. 18% to 20%, applied on the receipt. Royal Caribbean prices spa at 20% even though its bar is 18%. Regent's Serene, Seabourn's spa, and Silversea's spa are explicitly excluded from the bundled fare.

Specialty dining. 18% on Royal Caribbean and MSC. 20% on Carnival, Celebrity, Norwegian, Princess, and Holland America (from June 1). Bundled lines roll this in.

Room service. Free on Royal Caribbean for most items; 18% on the rest. Most other lines apply the bar percentage. A small cash tip for the runner is conventional but not auto-charged.

Excursions. Ship-sponsored shore excursions have the daily charge baked in for the operating crew but not for the local guide and driver. $5 to $10 per person for a guide; $2 to $5 for a driver. Independent tours run closer to $10 to $20 per person.

Porters. $2 per bag at embarkation, cash, before they roll your luggage onto the conveyor. Port-authority employees on most US homeports, not cruise-line.

Butlers. Norwegian's Haven, Celebrity's Retreat, MSC's Yacht Club, Silversea (every suite), Regent (Penthouse and above), and Oceania (bundled) all run butlers. $5 to $10 per day in cash on the last morning is the convention.

Cash Thank-Yous, Role by Role

Stateroom attendant

Paid out of the daily charge. The last-night cash is the extra — for the towel animals, the chocolate on the pillow, the times he saw your "do not disturb" tag and came back later. $5 to $10 per night where lines publish a number; most don't.

Head waiter and assistant waiter

Fixed-seating diners use envelopes on the last night, split between the two. Same range as the steward. Anytime-dining diners who never see the same waiter twice are covered by the pool.

Butler

Suite-only on most lines. Cash on the last morning, handed direct. Bundled-fare lines (Oceania, Silversea, Regent) make it optional and many guests skip it. Norwegian Haven, Celebrity Retreat, and MSC Yacht Club expect it.

Concierge

Suite-class only on most lines. If yours actually booked dinners and shows, $5 to $10 per night is appropriate. Plenty of suite-class cruisers never use the concierge and don't tip; that's also fine.

Kids' club counselors

Not covered by the daily charge. $5 to $10 per child per day in cash on the last day, if your kids spent real hours at Camp Carnival or Splash Academy. Pinned to the lanyard works.

Casino dealers

Land-casino logic: fold a chip in if you win a hand or leave the table. No daily-charge contribution.

Excursion guide and driver

Outside the daily charge. $5 to $10 per person for the guide, $2 to $5 for the driver. Independent operators — the Cozumel snorkel boat you booked direct — expect more.

Three Reads

Family with toddlers

The line you book matters. Carnival exempts under 2; Norwegian exempts under 3. Celebrity, Royal Caribbean, Cunard, and Holland America bill the toddler at the full adult rate. A 7-night Royal Caribbean sailing for two adults and one 18-month-old is $388.50 in the daily charge ($18.50 × 7 × 3); the same family on Carnival is $238 ($17 × 7 × 2). That's a $150 swing on tips alone, before extras.

Solo traveler

The daily charge is per person, so a solo cruiser pays the full rate against a cabin she's already paying double for. There's no per-person discount on tips the way there sometimes is on the fare. A 7-night Norwegian club balcony adds $140 in daily gratuities for one person — meaningful against a fare that already absorbed the single supplement. Build the tip line into the cabin budget at booking, not at the end.

Traveler in grief

The crew are humans on long contracts away from their own families. Most are good at reading a quiet table and leaving it quiet. If you're sailing through a fresh loss, the daily charge already covers what you owe — you don't owe extra performance on top of that. A handwritten note with the cash on the last night, if you're up to writing one, lands harder than the dollar amount. If you're not up to writing one, the steward who saw you go to the same window on Deck 5 every morning already knows.
