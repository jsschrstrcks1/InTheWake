Martinique

Martinique: My French Caribbean Paradise

Sailing into Fort-de-France with Le Carbet mountains behind feels like France dropped a city in the Caribbean. This is a French overseas territory – you're truly in France here, using euros and speaking French (or Antillean Creole). My perfect day: off the ship and straight to Les Salines beach (40-min taxi south) – the most beautiful beach I've ever seen with coconut palms and calm water.

Lunch at Chez Carole – lobster grillé and accras (worth every euro). Afternoon wandering Saint-Pierre (the "Pompeii of the Caribbean") – once called the "Paris of the Caribbean," it was the island's glittering cultural and commercial capital until Mount Pelée erupted on May 8, 1902. In less than two minutes, the volcano destroyed the entire city, claiming 28,000-30,000 lives in what became the deadliest volcanic disaster of the 20th century. Walking among the theater ruins and old prison walls, I felt the weight of that history – yet also the resilience. Today Saint-Pierre is France's 101st "City of Art and History," quietly beautiful on the coast. Back in town for rhum agricole tasting at Depaz and pain au chocolat that rivals Paris.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Eating fresh coconut on Les Salines while a French family played pétanque beside me – perfect cultural mash-up.

A Capital Born from Tragedy

Fort-de-France wasn't always the star of Martinique. When the French settled in the 1600s, the city was called Fort-Royal, and for centuries it lived in the shadow of Saint-Pierre – the island's sparkling jewel. Saint-Pierre had the theaters, the culture, the commerce. Fort-Royal (renamed Fort-de-France in 1807) was the administrative backup.

Everything changed on that May morning in 1902. When Mount Pelée obliterated Saint-Pierre, Fort-de-France became the capital by default. What began as tragedy shaped the island's destiny. Today Fort-de-France is a vibrant blend of French elegance and Caribbean soul, while Saint-Pierre stands as a poignant memorial and a testament to nature's power.

Getting Around Martinique

Pointe Simon pier – 5-min walk to town. Taxis for beach day are shareable with others (great way to split costs).

  • Les Salines Beach: 40-min taxi south (share with others for great value)
  • Saint-Pierre Ruins: 30-min north – explore the theater ruins, old prison, and volcano museum. Now France's 101st "City of Art and History." Combine with Depaz distillery tour in the shadow of Mount Pelée
  • Anse Dufour: Great snorkeling alternative closer to port

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Best beach?
A: Les Salines or Anse Dufour for snorkeling.

Q: Currency?
A: Euro – this is a French overseas territory, so you're literally in France.

Q: Language?
A: French and Antillean Creole, though most tourism workers speak English.

Q: Is Saint-Pierre safe to visit?
A: Absolutely. Mount Pelée is monitored constantly, and Saint-Pierre is a beautiful, reflective place to explore history.
