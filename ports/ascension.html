Ascension Island

Photo: In the Wake

Ascension Island: Darwin's Volcanic Laboratory in the Middle of Nowhere

There are remote ports, and then there's Ascension Island. One thousand miles from the coast of Africa. Fourteen hundred miles from South America. Seven square miles of volcanic rock rising from the mid-Atlantic depths, surrounded by nothing but ocean in every direction. No commercial flights. No cruise terminal. No hotels, no restaurants, barely two hundred residents — most of them military contractors or communications staff. When Charles Darwin stepped ashore from HMS Beagle in July 1836, he called it "a cinder floating in the ocean." That description still fits. And yet, tendering ashore onto this improbable island, standing on black lava fields under the shadow of Green Mountain's impossible cloud forest, I felt something I rarely experience anymore: genuine awe at how strange and wondrous Earth can be.

Ascension shouldn't exist as a green island. Volcanically young — just a million years old — it erupted from the seafloor as barren rock. Then, in the 1840s, botanist Joseph Hooker and Royal Navy officers (inspired by Darwin's observations) decided to conduct an experiment: could a desert island be terraformed into paradise? They imported plants from Kew Gardens — Norfolk pines, eucalyptus, bamboo, ginger, guava — and planted them on Green Mountain's summit where clouds condense. Against all odds, it worked. Today, Green Mountain hosts a self-sustaining cloud forest ecosystem that didn't exist two centuries ago. It's a living monument to Victorian ambition and ecological hubris, beautiful and unsettling in equal measure. Nowhere else on Earth will you find a manmade rainforest growing on an active volcanic island in the middle of the Atlantic.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing at Green Mountain's summit, 2,817 feet above the Atlantic, surrounded by bamboo groves and tree ferns that have no business growing here. Below, volcanic cinder cones stretched across the island in shades of rust, black, and ochre — utterly barren, utterly alien. Beyond that, the South Atlantic in every direction, unbroken blue to the horizon. A frigatebird rode thermals overhead. Clouds rolled through the forest, condensing on leaves and dripping to the ground — the island creating its own rain. The ranger explained how this ecosystem exists solely because humans created it, how native species cling to coastal cliffs while the mountaintop belongs to botanical immigrants. "Darwin would have found this fascinating," he said. I thought: Darwin would have been horrified. Or delighted. Probably both.

Georgetown, Ascension's only settlement, clusters near the shore beneath volcanic peaks. The town exists to support the military and communications installations — RAF base, US Space Force tracking station, BBC relay station — that give this isolated speck of rock strategic importance. For cruise visitors, Georgetown offers little beyond a post office (your postcard from Ascension will impress geography enthusiasts), a small museum, and the surreal experience of walking streets where everyone knows each other because the entire population fits in a large theater. But Georgetown isn't why expedition cruises make the crossing. We come for the landscapes — volcanic craters that look like Mars, lava tubes you can walk through, nesting seabirds by the thousands, and beaches where green sea turtles haul themselves ashore to lay eggs, completing a 1,400-mile migration from Brazil that science still struggles to explain. Ascension is geology and biology laid bare, unpolished, utterly authentic. It's not for everyone. But if you're reading this, it might be exactly for you.

Port Essentials

What you need to know before you tender ashore.

  • Terminal: No pier — ships anchor offshore and tender passengers to Georgetown; weather-dependent landing
  • Distance to Settlement: Georgetown is the only settlement; beaches and Green Mountain require 4x4 transport (arranged through ship)
  • Tender: Yes — all passengers tender ashore; Atlantic conditions can prevent landing
  • Currency: British Pound Sterling (GBP); Saint Helena Pound also accepted; credit cards rarely used (cash preferred); no ATMs
  • Language: English
  • Driving: Left side (British); roads paved in Georgetown but rough 4x4 tracks elsewhere; visitors unlikely to rent vehicles (access restricted)
  • Best Season: December-May (calmer seas, sea turtle nesting season); June-November can have rougher Atlantic conditions making tender operations difficult

Top Experiences

How I'd spend my time on Ascension Island.

Green Mountain National Park — Cloud Forest Summit

2,817-foot volcanic peak hosting a unique Victorian-era artificial cloud forest. Bamboo, tree ferns, Norfolk pines, eucalyptus, and banana plants create a self-sustaining rainforest ecosystem. 360-degree panoramic views across volcanic landscapes and the Atlantic. Endemic plants survive at lower elevations. 4x4 access via winding mountain road. Half to full-day excursion with ranger-led interpretation. This is Ascension's signature experience — a living ecological experiment unlike anywhere on Earth. Bring layers (summit 10-15°F cooler than coast).

Long Beach — Green Sea Turtle Nesting

Ascension hosts one of the Atlantic's largest green sea turtle nesting populations. Thousands of turtles migrate 1,400 miles from Brazil to nest on these beaches (November-May peak season). Female turtles return to their exact birth beach — a navigational mystery. Guided beach walks at dawn or dusk during nesting season. Strict conservation protocols (respect nesting sites, no flash photography, maintain distance). Full-day experience when combined with other island sites. Witnessing a 300-pound turtle dig a nest and lay eggs is unforgettable.

Letterbox Peninsula — Volcanic Geology Tour

Raw volcanic landscapes showcasing cinder cones, lava fields, and recent eruptions (geological timescale). Walk through lava tubes and collapsed calderas. Colored mineral deposits create rust, yellow, and black striations. Minimal vegetation reveals geological structures clearly. Darwin studied these formations in 1836. 4x4 access required. Half-day excursion. Perfect for geology enthusiasts and photographers seeking alien landscapes. Wear sturdy boots — lava rock is sharp.

Boatswain Bird Island — Seabird Sanctuary

Small offshore islet hosting Ascension frigatebirds (endemic species with global population under 15,000 birds). Boatswain birds (red-billed tropicbirds), brown boobies, and sooty terns nest on cliffs. Viewed from shore or small boat (weather permitting). Critical breeding habitat for endangered species. Accessible as part of coastal tour. Binoculars essential. The frigatebird's iridescent green plumage and seven-foot wingspan are spectacular.

Sisters' Peak & Volcanic Crater Hikes

Challenging hikes to volcanic summits offering panoramic island views. Sisters' Peak (2,460 feet) and other cinder cones accessible via rough trails. Barren landscapes, loose volcanic scoria, and 360-degree Atlantic vistas. Requires good fitness and sturdy hiking boots. Half-day guided hikes. The sense of isolation at the summit — surrounded by ocean in every direction — is profound. Bring sun protection (no shade) and plenty of water.

Georgetown Walking Tour — History & Infrastructure

Ascension Island Heritage Society Museum (small but fascinating collection covering military history, volcanic geology, Darwin's visit, and island culture). Post office (send postcards with rare Ascension stamps). Exiles Club bar (social hub for island residents). Anglican church. Military communications installations visible from town (not open to visitors). 2-3 hours. Free or minimal fees. Good weather backup if beach or mountain trips are canceled. The museum's collection on the island's role in WWII and the space race is surprisingly comprehensive.

Getting Around

  • Walking: Georgetown is small and walkable (under 1 square mile). All other sites require vehicle transport.
  • Organized Tours: Essential — expedition cruise operators arrange all island tours with local guides and 4x4 transport. Independent exploration not practical due to access restrictions and limited infrastructure.
  • Vehicle Rental: Virtually unavailable to cruise visitors. Island has restricted access (military installations, conservation areas). Tours include necessary permits and transportation.
  • Tender Boats: Ship-organized tenders shuttle passengers between anchored ship and Georgetown landing. Tender operations weather-dependent (Atlantic swells can prevent landing).
  • Hiking: Some coastal and volcanic sites accessible on foot with guide. Trails unmarked and challenging (loose volcanic rock, no shade, remote locations). Always hike with guide and carry adequate water.

Local Food & Drink

  • Limited Dining: Ascension has no restaurants for cruise visitors. Georgetown has small shops (limited supplies) and Exiles Club bar (island residents' social club). Most expedition cruises provide packed lunches for shore excursions.
  • Fresh Fish: If invited to local gatherings (rare), you might encounter yellowfin tuna, wahoo, and barracuda caught in surrounding Atlantic waters. The waters off Ascension are exceptionally rich fishing grounds.
  • Imported Goods: Nearly all food shipped from UK or St Helena. Limited fresh produce. Island's isolation means grocery selection resembles a small British village shop circa 1985.
  • Water: Bring bottled water on shore excursions. Island has limited freshwater (desalination and Green Mountain springs). Tap water safe but may taste of minerals.
  • Exiles Club: Island's main social venue serves British pub staples (when open). May be accessible to cruise visitors if prearranged. Not guaranteed — this is a working community, not a tourist destination.
  • Self-Catering: If staying overnight (extremely rare for cruise visitors), Two Boats Village shop stocks basics. Expect UK brands at inflated prices reflecting shipping costs.

Pro Tips

  • Tender operations are WEATHER-DEPENDENT — Atlantic swells can prevent landing even when skies are clear. Be mentally prepared for a possible "cruise-by" without going ashore.
  • Bring British pounds cash — ATMs don't exist, credit cards rarely accepted. Small denominations useful for stamps or museum donations.
  • Sun protection essential — volcanic landscapes offer zero shade. High-SPF sunscreen, hat, sunglasses, and lightweight long sleeves recommended.
  • Sturdy hiking boots or trail shoes necessary for volcanic terrain. Lava rock is sharp, loose, and unforgiving. Tennis shoes inadequate.
  • Layers for Green Mountain — summit can be 10-15°F cooler than coast and often cloudy/misty. Bring light rain jacket.
  • Turtle nesting season (November-May) offers best wildlife encounters but visits governed by strict conservation rules. Follow guide instructions precisely — disturbing nesting turtles is prohibited.
  • Photography gear: Wide-angle for landscapes, telephoto for seabirds. Volcanic dust and sea spray hazardous to cameras — bring protective bags.
  • Ascension is a working military/communications base — photography restrictions apply near installations. Guides will indicate prohibited areas.
  • No medical facilities beyond basic clinic — bring necessary medications and first aid supplies. Ship's medical services your only advanced care option.
  • This is expedition cruising at its most authentic — embrace uncertainty, adaptability, and the privilege of visiting one of Earth's most isolated inhabited places.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where do cruise ships dock?
A: Ships anchor offshore at Georgetown and tender passengers ashore. There is no cruise pier. Landing depends on Atlantic weather conditions and may be canceled if seas are rough.

Q: Why is Ascension Island so remote and rarely visited?
A: Ascension sits isolated in the mid-Atlantic, 1,000 miles from Africa and 1,400 miles from South America. It serves as a British and US military communications base with restricted access. Only specialized expedition cruises and military/contractor flights reach the island.

Q: Can I visit Green Mountain?
A: Yes, if weather permits landing. Green Mountain National Park hosts a unique Victorian-era artificial cloud forest ecosystem. Tours require 4x4 transport and permission from island authorities, arranged through expedition cruise operators. The summit offers 360-degree views across volcanic landscapes.

Q: What makes Ascension important for sea turtles?
A: Ascension hosts one of the Atlantic's largest green sea turtle nesting populations. Thousands of turtles migrate 1,400 miles from Brazil to nest on Ascension's beaches (primarily Long Beach, November-May peak season). Females return to their exact birth beach — a navigational feat still studied by marine biologists.

Q: What did Charles Darwin discover here?
A: Darwin visited Ascension aboard HMS Beagle in July 1836, shortly after his transformative Galapagos voyage. He studied volcanic geology, endemic species, and barren landscapes. His observations contributed to theories on island biogeography and species adaptation. Darwin described Ascension as "a cinder floating in the ocean" — an apt description even today.

This guide draws from published accounts, fellow cruisers, and careful research — but it does not yet carry the weight of my own anchor.
