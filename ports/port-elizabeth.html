Port Elizabeth: Where Elephants Meet the Indian Ocean

My Visit to Port Elizabeth

I stepped off the ship into Port Elizabeth on a warm December morning and felt the Indian Ocean breeze press against my face like a greeting from an old friend. The harbour was quiet, almost modest compared to the vast commercial ports I had visited elsewhere on this voyage around the southern tip of Africa. A few longshoremen waved from the quayside. The sky was enormous, pale blue and cloudless, and the air carried a scent of salt and dried grass and eucalyptus that I would come to associate with this stretch of the Eastern Cape for the rest of my life. My wife stood beside me on the gangway, shielding her eyes from the sun, and I heard her whisper, "It is so open." She was right. After the close harbours of the Mediterranean, the vastness of Algoa Bay felt like the world had suddenly expanded.

We had booked an independent safari tour to Addo Elephant National Park, and our guide, a softly spoken man named Thabo, met us at the pier with a dusty Land Cruiser and a cooler of bottled water. The drive took about an hour through dry bushveld and small farming communities, and Thabo filled the silence with stories about the park's history. In 1931, only eleven elephants remained in the Addo area, hunted to the edge of oblivion. Now there are more than six hundred. I found myself gripping the armrest as we passed through the park gates, suddenly nervous that we might not see them, that the promise of guaranteed elephants was just tourist marketing. However, within twenty minutes of entering the reserve, Thabo slowed the vehicle and pointed through the windscreen. There, beside a muddy waterhole no more than thirty meters away, stood a family of nine elephants. The matriarch was enormous, her ears flapping slowly in the heat, her skin caked in red-brown mud. Two calves played at her feet, bumping into each other with the clumsy joy of creatures who did not yet know their own strength.

We watched them for nearly forty minutes. Thabo switched off the engine, and the quiet was extraordinary. I could hear the elephants breathing, a deep, slow rhythm like the earth itself was exhaling. I could hear the wet sound of trunks sucking water and the soft rumble of communication between the matriarch and her daughters, a sound felt as much as heard, vibrating somewhere deep in my chest. A dung beetle rolled its cargo past our front tyre. A lilac-breasted roller sat on a dead branch and watched us watching the elephants with an expression of magnificent indifference. The air smelled of warm dust and animal musk and the faint sweetness of wild sage.

Then something happened that I will carry with me for the rest of my days. The youngest calf, perhaps eighteen months old, detached itself from the group and walked directly toward our vehicle. Thabo held up a hand, a quiet instruction not to move. The calf stopped two meters from my open window and reached its trunk toward me. I could see the two delicate fingers at the tip of that trunk, quivering, tasting the air around me. I could smell its breath, earthy and green, like freshly turned soil after rain. My heart swelled with something I cannot name, a mixture of wonder and tenderness and a strange sorrow that we have made a world where these creatures need fences and rangers to survive. The calf held my gaze for what felt like minutes but was probably fifteen seconds, then turned and ambled back to its mother with the unhurried dignity that only elephants possess. My wife reached over and squeezed my hand. Neither of us could speak.

That afternoon, back in the city, we explored Route 67, the public art trail that climbs from the harbour through the old town to the Donkin Reserve. There are sixty-seven artworks along the route, one for each year of Nelson Mandela's public service, and they range from enormous mosaic murals to small bronze sculptures hidden in doorways. I paused at the Voting Line, a row of life-sized metal figures ascending the hill, each one representing a South African standing in line to vote for the first time in 1994. The figures were faceless but their postures spoke volumes: hope, exhaustion, patience, dignity. I thought about what it means to wait decades for the right to be counted, and I felt ashamed of every time I have taken my own freedoms for granted.

We walked to the Boardwalk area for a late lunch, and I ordered a boerewors roll from a street vendor near the beach. The spiced sausage was still sizzling, the bread soft and warm in my hands, and the taste was magnificent, smoky and peppery with a depth of flavour that no ordinary sausage could match. We sat on the seawall eating and watching surfers carve through the waves at a break just south of the pier. The Indian Ocean here is warmer than I expected, nothing like the frigid Atlantic water of Cape Town, and the light had a golden quality that softened everything it touched. Though the Boardwalk itself is a modern development with shops and restaurants, it sits against a natural backdrop so beautiful that even the commercial elements cannot diminish it. The beach stretched away in both directions, wide and golden, and the sand was warm under our bare feet when we took off our shoes and walked along the waterline.

On our second morning, we hired a taxi to Sardinia Bay, about twenty minutes southwest of the port. If Kings Beach near the Boardwalk is the city's public face, Sardinia Bay is its secret heart. The beach was nearly deserted when we arrived: white sand dunes rolling down to water so clear I could see the rocky bottom ten meters out. Although the wind picked up in the afternoon and drove sand against our legs, the morning was still and warm and perfect. I swam out past the small breakers and floated on my back, staring up at that immense African sky, listening to nothing but the gentle slap of water against my ears and the distant cry of a tern. For a moment I felt held by something larger than myself, suspended between ocean and sky, grateful beyond words for this improbable life.

Looking back, I realize what Port Elizabeth taught me. It is a city that does not try to impress you. It has none of Cape Town's dramatic swagger, none of Durban's tropical energy. Yet its gifts are profound and quiet: elephants that walk up to your window with the curiosity of old souls, art that tells the story of a nation's struggle for dignity, beaches where the sand is warm and the water is kind and the sky goes on forever. I learned that the places which change us most are not always the ones that shout the loudest. Sometimes they are the ones that simply open their hands and let you see what they hold. We sailed from Gqeberha at sunset, and as the harbour light grew small behind us and the stars began to appear above the Indian Ocean, I whispered a prayer of thanks for the elephants, for Thabo, for the taste of boerewors in the sunshine, for my wife's hand in mine, and for the grace of being alive in a world that still holds such quiet wonder.

The Cruise Port

What you need to know before you dock.

  • Terminal: Port Elizabeth Harbour cruise terminal — basic facilities with taxi stand at the pier. The terminal building has restrooms and a small tourist information desk. Wheelchair accessible ramps are available from the quayside to the terminal exit.
  • Distance to City Center: 3 km to downtown; beaches and Boardwalk 2 km (10 min by taxi). Walking is possible but exposed to sun along the harbour road.
  • Tender: No — ships dock directly at the commercial harbour pier
  • Currency: South African Rand (ZAR); credit cards widely accepted; ATMs available in the city centre
  • Language: English, Afrikaans, Xhosa (English universally spoken in tourist areas)
  • Driving: Left side (British style); organized tours recommended for Addo Elephant Park and Garden Route excursions
  • Best Season: Year-round destination; October to April warmest for beaches; whale season June to November in Algoa Bay
  • Time Zone: South Africa Standard Time (SAST), UTC+2

Getting Around

Transportation tips for cruise visitors.

  • Taxis & Ride-Hailing: Metered taxis are available at the cruise terminal, and Uber operates reliably throughout Port Elizabeth. This is the most practical way to get around as a cruise visitor. A taxi from the port to the Boardwalk or city centre costs approximately $5-10 (R80-150). For Addo Elephant Park, expect to pay $60-80 (R1000-1400) round-trip by taxi, though organized tours are generally a better value. Always confirm the fare before departing.
  • Walking: The city centre and beachfront are relatively flat, though the 3 km distance from the cruise terminal to the Boardwalk is exposed to sun with limited shade. The beachfront promenade from Kings Beach to Hobie Beach is a pleasant, paved walk of about 2 km with ocean views the entire way. The Donkin Heritage Trail is a 5 km self-guided walking route through the historic district, accessible for visitors with moderate mobility. Some of the Route 67 trail involves steep steps.
  • Organized Tours: The recommended option for Addo Elephant Park (70 km, 1 hour each way) and Garden Route excursions (Tsitsikamma is 2 hours west). Both ship excursions and independent local operators are available at the terminal. Independent tours typically cost $80-120 for Addo half-day and $100-140 for a Garden Route full-day, offering smaller groups and more flexibility than ship excursions. Book ahead during peak season to secure your spot.
  • Car Hire: Available at the airport and city centre but requires confidence driving on the left side. South African roads are generally well-maintained on main routes. Useful if you want to explore Sardinia Bay, the coastal villages, or drive the Garden Route at your own pace. Not recommended for first-time visitors to South Africa.
  • Public Transport: Algoa Bus Company operates local routes, but frequency and reliability may not suit time-limited cruise visitors. The Nelson Mandela Bay municipality has improved bus services in recent years, though taxi or ride-hailing remains more practical for port day schedules.

Excursions & Activities

How to spend your time ashore. For popular excursions like Addo Elephant Park, book ahead during peak season to secure your spot. Many visitors choose independent tours for flexibility and cost savings, though a ship excursion offers guaranteed return to the vessel before departure.

Addo Elephant National Park

Over 600 wild elephants in a malaria-free Big Five reserve, only 70 km from the cruise terminal (approximately one hour by road). Elephant sightings are virtually guaranteed, along with buffalo, warthog, kudu, and numerous bird species. Lion, rhino, and leopard are also present but sightings depend on luck and patience. Half-day tours run 4-5 hours and provide an excellent wildlife experience. Full-day tours allow deeper exploration of the park's varied ecosystems. Independent tours with local guides cost approximately $80-120 per person including transport. Book ahead for peak season departures. This is the best wildlife excursion accessible from any South African cruise port.

Beachfront & Boardwalk

Five kilometres of pristine golden beaches along Algoa Bay. Kings Beach and Hobie Beach are closest to the port, offering warm Indian Ocean swimming that is safe, clean, and patrolled by lifeguards. The Boardwalk entertainment complex has restaurants, shops, and ocean-view dining. Low-walking option ideal for visitors with limited mobility who prefer a relaxed day. Taxi or Uber from the terminal costs approximately $5-10. Allow 2-4 hours for a leisurely visit.

Route 67 & Donkin Heritage Trail

Self-guided 5 km walking route through Port Elizabeth's historic city centre, featuring 67 public artworks representing each year of Nelson Mandela's service to the nation. Landmarks include the Donkin Reserve pyramid and lighthouse, Victorian architecture, and the powerful Voting Line sculpture. Free to explore; allow 2-3 hours. Moderate walking with some steep sections. Pick up a trail map at the tourism office or download the free guide online.

Tsitsikamma National Park & Garden Route

Full-day excursion west along the spectacular Garden Route to Tsitsikamma, about 2 hours from Port Elizabeth. Ancient indigenous forests, dramatic coastline, suspension bridges over Storms River Mouth, and hiking trails through yellowwood groves. Many tours include a stop at Bloukrans Bridge, the world's highest commercial bungee jump at 216 metres (viewing only unless you are feeling adventurous). Long day but unforgettable scenery. Organized tours cost approximately $100-140 per person. A ship excursion is available and offers guaranteed return.

Sardinia Bay

One of the most beautiful unspoiled beaches in the Eastern Cape, about 20 minutes southwest of the port. White sand dunes, crystal-clear water, and relative solitude compared to the city beaches. Excellent for swimming, walking, and photography. No facilities on the beach itself, so bring water and sun protection. Taxi or Uber from port costs approximately $15-20 each way. Allow half a day.

Township & Cultural Tours

Respectful, guide-led tours visiting Port Elizabeth's historically Black townships. Meet local families, visit community projects, and learn about the apartheid era and the ongoing journey of reconciliation. These tours support local economic development and offer an authentic, eye-opening perspective on South African life. Half-day tours cost approximately $40-60 per person. Book ahead through a reputable local operator for the most meaningful experience.

Local Food & Drink
  • Boerewors Roll: Spiced South African sausage in a soft roll, the local hot dog elevated to an art form. Smoky, peppery, and satisfying. Available from street vendors and restaurants throughout the city. Cost about $3-5.
  • Peri-Peri Chicken: Portuguese-Mozambican influence runs deep in the Eastern Cape. Flame-grilled chicken with chili sauce is everywhere and ranges from mild to eye-watering.
  • Bunny Chow: A hollowed-out bread loaf filled with fragrant curry, originally from Durban but popular throughout the Eastern Cape. Hearty, messy, and delicious.
  • Koeksisters: Twisted, syrup-soaked doughnut pastry. Sweet, sticky, and addictive with afternoon tea.
  • Snoek: Local fish traditionally smoked and served with apricot jam. An acquired taste but authentically Cape.
  • Eastern Cape Wine: The region produces excellent Shiraz and Sauvignon Blanc. Less famous than the Western Cape but quality is improving rapidly.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Lessons learned the hard way.

  • Addo Timing: Start early. The one-hour drive each way means a half-day tour needs 4-5 hours minimum. Animals are most active in the cool morning hours, so an early departure is worth the effort. Afternoon heat drives elephants into shade and reduces sightings.
  • Wind Factor: Port Elizabeth is one of the windiest cities in South Africa. The southeast wind can blow hard in summer, driving sand along the beaches and making waterfront dining unpleasant. Ask locals about conditions before choosing an outdoor restaurant. Despite the wind, the warmth of the Indian Ocean makes swimming comfortable year-round.
  • Sun Protection: The South African sun is deceptively fierce, even on overcast days. High UV index means sunburn happens fast. Bring SPF 50+, a hat, and plenty of water, especially for safari and beach days.
  • Rand Value: The South African Rand offers excellent value for international visitors. Meals, taxis, and tours cost a fraction of European equivalents. Budget planning becomes easy when a full restaurant meal costs $10-15 per person.
  • Safety: Stick to tourist areas and organized tours. The Boardwalk, beachfront, and Donkin Heritage Trail are safe during daylight hours. Avoid walking alone in unfamiliar areas after dark. Use Uber or metered taxis for evening transport.
  • Gqeberha Pronunciation: The city was officially renamed Gqeberha in 2021. Locals pronounce it roughly as "kWEBB-erra." Both names are understood and accepted, so do not worry about getting it wrong.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where do cruise ships dock in Port Elizabeth?
A: Ships dock at the Port Elizabeth Harbour cruise terminal, about 3 km from the city centre. Taxis and Uber are available at the pier for transport to the beachfront, city attractions, or Addo Elephant Park tours.

Q: Is Addo Elephant National Park worth visiting on a cruise day?
A: Absolutely. Addo is only 70 km away (one hour drive) with virtually guaranteed elephant sightings. The park is completely malaria-free, which is rare for African safari destinations. Both half-day and full-day tours work well from the cruise terminal, costing approximately $80-120 per person with independent operators.

Q: What does Gqeberha mean and should I use that name?
A: In 2021, the city was officially renamed Gqeberha (pronounced roughly as "kWEBB-erra"), honouring the Xhosa name for the Baakens River. Both names are understood and accepted, though locals increasingly use Gqeberha in formal contexts.

Q: Are the beaches safe for swimming?
A: Yes. Kings Beach and Hobie Beach are clean, safe, and patrolled by lifeguards. The Indian Ocean water here is notably warmer than Cape Town's Atlantic beaches, making comfortable swimming possible year-round.

Q: Can I do a Garden Route excursion in one day?
A: A full Garden Route tour is ambitious but possible with organized excursions. Most visit Tsitsikamma National Park (2 hours west) with coastal stops and the Bloukrans Bridge viewpoint. Expect a long but spectacular day. Ship excursions and independent tours are both available.

Q: Is Port Elizabeth accessible for visitors with mobility needs?
A: The cruise terminal has wheelchair accessible ramps. The beachfront promenade is paved and flat. The Boardwalk complex is fully accessible. Addo Elephant Park has accessible viewing hides and vehicle-based safari is suitable for visitors who cannot walk long distances. Route 67 involves steep sections that may be challenging for wheelchair users.
