Antigua: Where Naval History Meets 365 Beaches

They say Antigua has a beach for every day of the year. Standing at the ramparts of Shirley Heights, looking down at English Harbour where Georgian naval vessels once sheltered from hurricanes, I could believe it. The coastline below was an impossible jigsaw of coves, bays, and crescents of white sand, each catching the Caribbean light differently.

What sets Antigua apart from other Caribbean ports is the sheer weight of maritime history here. In 1723, a hurricane swept thirty-five ships ashore across the Leeward Islands — but two Royal Navy vessels moored in English Harbour survived without a scratch. That storm sealed the harbour's reputation as the safest natural anchorage in the region, and the British spent the next century building what would become their most important naval base in the Western Hemisphere.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Walking through Nelson's Dockyard, the world's only continuously working Georgian-era dockyard, and realizing that the same capstans used to careen Admiral Nelson's ships in the 1780s still turn today. Not a recreation. Not a museum piece. The actual equipment, still in use after nearly 250 years. History doesn't get more tangible than this.

Nelson himself spent three years here as commander of HMS Boreas in the 1780s, enforcing trade laws and apparently hating every minute of it — he famously called English Harbour "an infernal hellhole." Yet his name now graces the most beautifully preserved naval yard in the Caribbean, restored beginning in 1951 after decades of decay following the Royal Navy's departure in 1889.

The local bus from St. John's to English Harbour costs about $1.50 and takes 35-45 minutes on a winding road through the island's interior. It's the most popular excursion for cruise passengers, and deservedly so. Pack your patience for the journey, but the destination rewards it tenfold.

Port Essentials

What you need to know before you dock.

  • Terminal: Heritage Quay & Redcliffe Quay in St. John's — duty-free shopping right at the pier
  • Distance to Nelson's Dockyard: 40 min by taxi or #17 bus
  • Tender: No — ships dock directly at the pier (some anchor and tender during peak season)
  • Currency: Eastern Caribbean Dollar (XCD); US dollars widely accepted
  • Language: English (local Antiguan Creole)
  • Driving: Left side (British style)
  • Best Season: December–April (dry); hurricane season June–November

Top Experiences

How I'd spend my time.

Nelson's Dockyard National Park

UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2016. The restored Georgian buildings house restaurants, boutiques, and maritime exhibits. The only working dockyard of its era anywhere in the world. Entrance fee around $8. The most historically significant site on the island.

Shirley Heights Lookout

Panoramic views over English Harbour from 18th-century military fortifications. Sunday afternoon barbecue and steel band party is legendary — if your ship timing allows. Even on quiet days, the vista alone justifies the climb.

Dickenson Bay

The island's most popular beach — calm water, white sand, beach bars, water sports. About 10 min from the cruise port. Chair and umbrella rentals available. The classic Caribbean beach day.

Fort Berkeley & Fort George

The oldest fortification in English Harbour (1704). Walk the ramparts, explore tunnels, and imagine guarding the entrance to Britain's most strategic Caribbean harbour. Free to explore.

St. John's Walking Tour

The capital offers pastel-painted colonial architecture, St. John's Cathedral (originally built 1681), and the Public Market. Walkable from the cruise pier in 15 minutes.

Half Moon Bay

A more secluded beach on the Atlantic side — stronger waves, fewer crowds, pristine natural setting. About 45 min from port. Bring your own supplies; facilities are minimal.

Local Food & Drink

  • Fungee & Pepperpot: The national dish — cornmeal dumplings with a hearty stew of meat and vegetables
  • Saltfish: Dried salted cod, often served for breakfast with vegetables
  • Ducana: Sweet potato dumplings wrapped in banana leaves
  • Wadadli Beer: Local lager, named for the island's indigenous name
  • English Harbour Rum: Premium aged rum distilled on the island since 1933
  • Sea Moss Drink: Creamy, seaweed-based beverage — an acquired taste but nutritious

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Where do cruise ships dock?
A: Heritage Quay and Redcliffe Quay in St. John's, with duty-free shopping at the pier.

Q: How do I get to Nelson's Dockyard?
A: Take the #17 bus (~$1.50, 40 min) or taxi (~$25-30 one way). Most popular day trip.

Q: Which beach is best near the port?
A: Dickenson Bay is closest and most developed. Half Moon Bay is worth the drive for seclusion.

Q: Is English Harbour worth the drive?
A: Absolutely. The UNESCO site, restored dockyard, and Shirley Heights views make it the island's premier attraction.

Q: What's the Shirley Heights Sunday party?
A: Weekly afternoon barbecue with steel band, rum punch, and panoramic views. If your ship timing allows, don't miss it.
