Charlottetown: My Anne of Green Gables Adventure

Walking into Charlottetown feels like stepping into the pages of history itself. This little capital — surveyed in 1764 by Captain Samuel Holland and named for Queen Charlotte, wife of George III — carries the gentle dignity of its origins. When the street grid was laid out between 1768 and 1771, few could have imagined what would unfold here a century later.

We wandered Victoria Row with its buskers and outdoor cafés, then stepped into Province House, and I felt the weight of the moment. Here, in these chambers, from September 1 to 9, 1864, delegates gathered for what was meant to be a Maritime Union conference. Instead, they sparked something far greater — the Charlottetown Conference that became the birthplace of Canadian Confederation. Standing in those rooms, I thought about how conversations change the course of nations. The dream born here would take shape through subsequent meetings in Halifax and Quebec City, culminating in Canada's founding on July 1, 1867. Ironically, Prince Edward Island itself didn't join Confederation until 1873 — the island that gave Canada its beginning took six more years to come home.

The city's motto, Cunabula Foederis — "Birthplace of Confederation" — isn't mere civic pride. It's historical fact, commemorated beautifully in the Confederation Centre of the Arts, opened in 1964 for the centennial. We explored its galleries before surrendering to the island's other great temptation: Cows Creamery. The factory tour was delightfully cheesy (pun intended), but the ice cream was worth every calorie and then some.

In the afternoon we drove north to Cavendish to visit Green Gables, where Lucy Maud Montgomery's imagination gave us Anne Shirley and her kindred spirits. Walking the red sand beaches afterward, those dramatic rust-colored cliffs glowing in the late sun, I understood why this island inspires such devotion. Dinner was a perfect lobster roll at Point Prim Chowder House, lighthouse keeper's view included, watching the maritime light fade over waters that have carried countless stories to these shores.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Walking the red sand at Cavendish at sunset, red cliffs glowing, feeling like I stepped into the book.
