Ten Days on Quantum of the Seas

Ten Days on Quantum of the Seas: A Pacific Coastal Logbook

A back-to-back from Los Angeles. Two days at Cabo San Lucas, one at Ensenada, the turnaround, and a second pass that ended with a male lion watching me through a diamond grate at a sanctuary outside Ensenada. Day-by-day notes, written honestly.

by Ken Baker | May 18, 2026

The trip in one paragraph: Ten days aboard Quantum of the Seas from Los Angeles, back-to-back. Same ship, same crew, two consecutive sailings: LA → Cabo San Lucas (two days, the second a whale-watching tour) → Ensenada → LA → sea day → Ensenada → LA. Quantum is a 168,666-gross-ton Quantum-class ship, the original of the series, holding around 4,180 guests at double occupancy (closer to 4,900 at full occupancy). The trip was a logbook from the start.

Who This Is For

Anyone considering a Pacific Coastal sailing out of LA — first-time cruisers, returning ones, people picking ports the brochure doesn't push as hard as the Caribbean. The trip covers ports that get less written about than they should: Cabo's a working town as much as a vendor strip, Ensenada has an animal sanctuary you can reach in a port-day window, and the back-to-back format gives you ten days on a single ship for less money than two separate cruises would cost.

Day 1 — Los Angeles, Embarkation in the Dark

Embarkation began unexpectedly — in the dark. A power outage swept through the terminal, halting the lines and suspending the forward motion of boarding day. What could have quickly become frustration instead became fellowship.

In that line I met Pablo and his brother, C. C has Down syndrome and radiates joy in a way that disarms you immediately. He studied my beard, grinned wide, and declared me "Shaggy." He appointed himself Fred. Pablo, naturally, became Scooby — because Scooby is his favorite, and "dogs are better than people." I could not argue with the logic.

What struck me most was not the cleverness of the comparison, but the love. They shared that cruising gives C freedom — space to move, to explore, to leave an activity midway without pressure. Structure can sometimes feel confining; a ship, strangely enough, offers elasticity. If something feels overwhelming, you simply step away. Try something else. Circle back later.

What I witnessed in that hour was a family marked by affection, patience, humor, and unembarrassed delight in one another. C would have gladly absorbed every hug available, and they gave them freely. Love expressed without hesitation. Support without embarrassment. Positivity without performance. They were a blessing to meet, and I hoped to run into them again around the ship.

The outage lasted roughly an hour. While the terminal systems were down and boarding paused, the only food available was from a truck positioned outside the terminal. With no other options moving, it became the de facto galley for a captive crowd.

I ordered a carne asada burrito and a Coke Zero for $15. The beef was properly cooked and well seasoned, with ample cilantro and balanced flavor throughout. Substantial without being sloppy — no small victory when eaten standing in a stalled embarkation line. Considering it was the only option available during the outage, the price felt less like a surcharge and more like supply and demand at work. In context, it was worth it. I would order it again without hesitation.

Once power stabilized and we were onboard around 1:00 p.m., I made my way to the Windjammer's aft outdoor deck — my customary embarkation perch. It is my favorite place on the ship to sit with breakfast when sailing, especially with open water behind me. I journaled, photographed the port, and eventually surrendered to the chill in the air and found somewhere warmer inside.

I took photos of various venues — the hot dog stand and its menu, the Main Dining Room menu, and other small details that help complete the record later. When staterooms opened, I was more than ready to shed my carry-on. I retreated to my balcony and took a restorative nap.

Sailaway never disappoints. I photographed the lines released, the widening distance from dock to hull, and the first stretch of white wake unfolding behind us — that signature brushstroke of departure that gives this project its name. The sun finished its day with the Pacific.

The afternoon also included the magnet exchange. I walked eleven decks dropping off branded duck magnets and preparing additional ducks for distribution — twenty larger ducks and about ten smaller glow-in-the-dark ones, with branding rubber-banded on. Note to self: acquire smaller rubber bands. Even the "small" ones were comically oversized for these tiny birds.

That evening I attended the Welcome Aboard show. The orchestra was fantastic — tight, energetic, polished. The comedian was enjoyable and confident. The cruise director's material felt overly familiar, and the timing lacked sharpness. I returned for the later set by the comedian, only to discover it was word-for-word identical, so I slipped out quietly.

I ended the evening back on my balcony, taking night photos of the dark Pacific and the quiet trail of wake stretching behind us. Day one closed not with spectacle, but with gratitude. Even in a blackout, there was light.

Day 2 — Sea Day, the Solarium Tip

Sea days have a different cadence. There is no rush toward gangways or shore excursions — only the steady rhythm of open water and the quiet understanding that the ship herself is the destination.

I was headed toward the Windjammer for breakfast when a man I met in the elevator offered a better idea. "Try the Solarium," he said. "Bagel and lox. Bacon. Fresh fruit. No line. And it's warm." The kind of insider tip you hope for early in a sailing.

He was right. The Solarium was calm, bright, and comfortably warm — a contrast to the breeze outside. I built a plate of smoked salmon over a toasted bagel, added crisp bacon and fresh fruit, and sat facing the ocean through the sweeping glass. No crowds. No scrambling for tables. Just low conversation and the endless Pacific sliding past. On Quantum, the Solarium is an adults-only retreat — that's why the tip worked: families default to the Windjammer in the mornings, and the Solarium stays half-empty until later.

After breakfast, I returned to my stateroom and stepped onto the balcony with the camera. Whale season had been active along this stretch of coast, and I scanned the horizon patiently. No confirmed sightings, but the act of watching felt worthwhile — attentive, expectant, quiet.

The afternoon was practical. I stood in line for roughly fifteen minutes to secure a tender ticket for tomorrow's port. I was assigned Group 21 — an early grouping. If operations begin on time, I should be stepping into a tender roughly ninety minutes after disembarkation opens. A favorable window that avoids the heaviest congestion.

The rest of the day was restorative. I caught up on rest and moved deliberately through the ship photographing venues — signage, menus, layout details — building the archive while most guests were enjoying the amenities. Sea days offer that margin. Two70 was empty in the mid-afternoon; SeaPlex was set up for bumper cars. The Music Hall was prepping for the evening's set. The ship felt less like a venue and more like a backstage tour you were quietly invited on.

I ate dinner in the Main Dining Room — my Time Dining reservation. The service was attentive without being intrusive, the food honest if unspectacular. The kind of dinner you remember not for the food but for the rhythm of being served by someone who has done it ten thousand times and still cares about doing it well.

The day was not about spectacle. It was about stillness, preparation, and perspective — the kind of day that steadies you for what comes next.

Day 3 — Cabo San Lucas, First Day

Two big ships in port. Chaotic and packed. Everywhere I went, people were "señor" and "amigo," trying to sell me something. If someone is prone to overstimulation, today would have done it.

On the plus side, the sidewalks are ample and unobstructed, with adequate cut-outs for those in wheelchairs. I feel like this was once a striking port that's been allowed to age without enough maintenance in places — it felt worn. But the thing I won't forget is standing on the dock being serenaded by sea lions. Arp arp arp. Pelicans everywhere, and more luxury yachts than I've seen in any other single port.

People have been asking where all the ships that used to be in the Bahamas have gone, and it turns out, a lot of them are probably in Cabo San Lucas. It's a bright and bustling port. Lots of vendors selling the same ten or twelve things — hats, shirts, magnets, blankets, and so on. The smell of seafood and freshly cooked Mexican food drifted through the whole facility. The warmth of the sun and the coolness of the breeze paired well, reminding me of the best parts of spring and fall in Florida.

There was an unsavory element. Everywhere I went both days, someone was trying to sell me illegal drugs. Every third store is a pharmacy selling muscle relaxers and similar. People haggle with you in USD and then bill you in MXN. I bought a hat, haggled the guy down to $14 from $20, and he billed me $16 after currency conversion. Know what your currency is worth and keep that math in the back of your mind while negotiating.

Cash is king. Often you can save another few bucks by paying in cash, in pesos or USD. The vendors who accept card add the processing fee back through the conversion rate; the vendors who take cash do not.

I walked over to Médano Beach, the long sandy beach on the bay side that's the main public beach in Cabo. Resort buildings line the back of it; the water is calm and warm; the rocks at the eastern end give the shot below its frame.

I enjoyed the warmth of the sand and the coolness of the ocean for a stretch, then booked a whale-watching excursion for the next day and headed back to the ship. Seven miles round trip on foot. The cruise app shows the steps; the soles of the feet keep the more honest record.

Day 4 — Cabo, Whales

I left the ship early — around 7:30 a.m. — for my 10:30 whale-watching excursion, determined to find a good breakfast first. By the time I was ashore around 8 a.m., things were much less crowded. Only one ship in port, and most of those passengers were still asleep.

I followed my nose and ended up at a place called Solomon's, across from Galati Yacht Sales. I ordered the Baja Omelette with Mexican sausage, cheese, avocado, and bacon. Flavorful, properly cooked, $40 with the tip. Not cheap. Worth it.

After breakfast I found the meeting place for the eco-cat — the large catamaran the whale-watching tour runs on — and got checked in. The crew went out of their way to make us comfortable. The cat was huge, and so quite stable. The captain handled little boats that weren't following maritime law without being flustered or losing his sense of cheer.

Our first stop was Lover's Beach, the small beach that sits between the Pacific and the Sea of Cortez at the southern tip of the Baja Peninsula. On the Cortez side, the cliff faces are dramatic and the water clear and bright. On the Pacific side — locally known as Divorce Beach — it is wise to heed the captain's warnings against swimming. The riptide is something else.

From there we pointed at Land's End — the impressive rock formations that made the huge catamaran feel small — and El Arco, the natural rock arch that gives Cabo its postcard image.

A pelican on a rock near the base — the way wildlife shows up here without prompting.

Then we pointed to the left and headed toward the cluster of boats already watching the whales. The bulk of our two hours was spent watching mothers and their calves surface, blow water out, slap their tails and fins on the surface — and at one point a breach. The cool thing about Cabo whales is that the humpbacks here are Alaskan whales that migrate roughly 10,000 miles to have their babies, and then migrate back. The local joke is that if a calf was born in Mexico, it's a Mexican whale.

We saw a grey whale slapping her fins on the surface and maybe two dozen humpbacks. The pectoral fin shot below is the one that stuck — barnacled, raised vertical out of the water, the resort coast of Cabo behind.

And the fluke as it dove — the photograph everyone wants from one of these trips and very few actually get.

At some point the crew brought around homemade chips and salsa, which was delightful. The right amount of whale watching. The captain brought us back to port without rushing it.

Have I mentioned the sea lions barking, and the sheer number of boats in port? Luxury yachts, catamarans, trimarans, motor boats, sail boats, water taxis, and so on. There are a significant number of boats here all the time. The replacement value would be measured in the tens of billions.

Late that afternoon, as we sailed away from Cabo, the last whale of the day surfaced one more time. A fluke against a pink-violet sky and a couple of small boats in the distance — the kind of moment you don't try to compose, you just press the shutter.

Cabo San Lucas is a port I'll return to. Not for the chaos. Not for the wealth on the water. For the wildlife. The whales and the sea lions alone make it a port I won't miss.

Day 5 — Sea Day, the Ship as Destination

A restful day spent in the hot tub and eating well. Sea days do not require justification. They are the rhythm the rest of the trip is paced against.

Morning began quietly. Coffee in hand. The salt air across the face. The ocean stretching in every direction. The wake fanning out behind the stern — visible testimony that the ship was moving even when I was doing nothing at all.

I went up in the North Star later — that glass capsule that lifts you above the sea — for a short ride at midday. From there the ship seems small against the open water, and at the same time entirely sufficient. There is nothing else in any direction; what is here is what you have. It is enough.

The Solarium hummed with unhurried rest. The pool deck carried laughter. Trivia gathered strangers into teams in the Schooner Bar. Elevators glided; music drifted from Boleros; plates were carried; conversations deepened. Time loosened its grip.

One small detail caught my eye — a life ring lashed to the railing, the ship's name in block lettering, the port of registry below. Nassau. Quantum of the Seas sails the Pacific Coast under a Bahamian flag, like most of the major cruise fleet. The kind of administrative trivia you only notice on a slow afternoon at sea.

I ate at Coastal Kitchen that evening — quieter than the Main Dining Room, with a Mediterranean lean. The kind of meal that doesn't try to wow you and benefits from it.

A sea day teaches something quiet: you do not always need a destination to justify the journey. Sometimes the ship is the destination, and the next port is simply where the ship happens to stop.

Day 6 — Ensenada, the North Star at Port

We saw dolphins on the way in. A small pod off the starboard side as we made the harbor approach, perhaps a dozen of them in the bow wave of a smaller boat ahead. The kind of welcome a port does not advertise but a passenger remembers.

There is something almost ceremonial about stepping into the North Star while docked in Ensenada. The capsule closes with a soft hush and then, slowly, you rise. The ship falls away beneath you deck by deck. The pool becomes geometry. The promenade shrinks to a ribbon of color. The arm extends outward, gently lifting you beyond the edge of the hull until you are suspended over open air, with the Pacific quiet below.

Ensenada unfolds in every direction. To one side, the harbor curves inward like a hand. Fishing boats drift in patient clusters. The waterfront hums with small movement — taxis, vendors, passengers filtering down the pier. Beyond it all, the hills of Baja California rise dry and gold-brown, their contours sharp against the sky.

From this height the town feels both intimate and ordered. You see the grid of streets, the breakwater holding back the swell, the faint line where sea meets horizon. What feels busy at ground level becomes legible from above. The wind replaces the noise. The air is cooler. Conversation softens. Even the ocean seems calmer from up there.

The whole North Star ride is about ten minutes. You descend reluctantly, carrying the widened view back to the deck below. Then you go find a taco.

The rest of the day was simple. Lunch in town — fish tacos at a place a passenger ahead of me in line recommended — and back to the ship before the sailaway window. I had two more port days ahead with this one ship; no need to push the first.

Day 7 — Turnaround at LA, In-Transit

Turnaround days have a shape of their own that doesn't get written about because nothing dramatic happens. On a back-to-back, you stay on the ship. Departing guests leave; new guests board; the crew somehow turns the entire ship over in the same window where most people unpack a carry-on.

The in-transit process at Los Angeles is straightforward. Guest Services issues you a new SeaPass card the night before. You meet in a designated lounge in the morning — for Quantum, the Music Hall — and walk off as a small group for the immigration formality. You are back onboard inside thirty minutes. Customs and Border Protection set up a temporary station for in-transit guests; the line is short because there are not many of you.

The ship feels different around the changeover. The Windjammer thins out at lunch and refills by dinner. The cabin steward checks in — sometimes a new one, sometimes the same. The crew, somehow, looks neither tired nor different. They have done this hundreds of times. They will do it hundreds more.

I took the afternoon to do nothing of consequence. The pool was empty for the first time all trip — every embarking guest was still in the Windjammer or in the line at Guest Services. I had a Sorrento's slice and a Café Promenade coffee and read on the balcony with the ship docked.

The new sailaway felt familiar in a way the first one had not. Same horn, same tug, same widening strip of water between the hull and the pier — but I knew the muster drill was a video on the app this time, knew which elevator bank was fastest, knew that the Solarium would be quiet at breakfast tomorrow. The second leg of a back-to-back starts with momentum the first doesn't get.

Dinner was Chops Grille. A real steak, plated cleanly. A New York strip cooked medium-rare. A glass of malbec. The kind of meal that justifies the upcharge if you book one specialty dinner per week and pick the one most likely to deliver.

Day 8 — Sea Day, Second Pass

The second sea day of a back-to-back is different from the first. You know where the coffee is best. You know which deck has the quiet outdoor seating that nobody finds in the first three days. You know not to bother with the noon trivia in the Schooner Bar because the same answers will be back in the rotation tomorrow.

I spent the morning at the Solarium again, because the man in the elevator was right. The breakfast was the same. The view was the same. The chair I sat in was the same. The repetition was the point.

Mid-morning I tried RipCord by iFly for the first time — Quantum's indoor skydiving simulator. Sixty seconds in the wind tunnel; the instructor took me up about three feet on my second turn; I came out smiling like an idiot. Worth doing once. Worth saving the ten-dollar photo package by just buying the experience without it.

The afternoon was North Star again — a slightly different view this far from any port, the ship turning on the open ocean — and then a long stretch on the balcony reading. The kind of stretch where the book reads slower than the wake outside.

Dinner was Jamie's Italian — pasta, a glass of house red, the kind of place where you order the simplest thing on the menu and it's the best thing on the menu. The Coastal Kitchen the night before had been the better meal, but Jamie's is the better atmosphere. Different jobs, both done well.

The Two70 show that night used the projection wall and the robotic screens together — the kind of set piece you don't see on smaller ships. Worth catching at least once on a Quantum-class sailing.

Day 9 — Ensenada, the Sanctuary

The second Ensenada day. I had done the city on the first pass, so for this one I booked a sanctuary excursion that takes a small bus inland from the port to Paipai Zoo Safari Park — a private sanctuary about half an hour outside town.

The sanctuary is what it sounds like — a private operation with enclosures for big cats, kangaroos, and a number of cubs being raised by hand. The first encounter set the tone.

The male lion watched me through the grate for a long stretch before deciding I was not interesting enough to keep watching. Then he turned, walked five steps to a flat rock in the sun, and lay down with his back to me. The dismissal was complete. There is something humbling about being categorized that fast by an animal that could end you in a second if anything between you broke.

The lioness was in a separate enclosure, sunning herself on a wooden platform with the kind of stretched-out indifference cats reserve for their best moments.

Then the cubs. Two lion cubs of slightly different ages — one tawny, one darker — wrestling and resting in the same enclosure. The wrestling was real but slow, the way young cats do everything; the resting was complete.

A jaguar cub came over to inspect me with the patient suspicion only a jaguar cub can manage — bigger eyes than the lion cubs, the rosettes already clear on the coat, and the unmistakable shorter snout of a jaguar instead of a leopard.

The kangaroos were on the way back. Red kangaroos in a low-walled enclosure, comfortable enough with visitors to come right up. The handler gave me a small bowl of greens to share. They eat without much ceremony — the same calm with which they do everything else.

And then the moment I will remember from this trip. A white tiger cub — maybe six weeks old, maybe a little older, blue eyes still adjusting — climbed up against my leg in the straw and went to sleep, paw curled across the fabric of my jacket. The handler did not rush me. The cub did not flinch. It just slept.

You realize, sitting next to a sleeping tiger cub, that what you are looking at is the same animal as the male lion in the first enclosure — same family, same lineage of teeth and muscle and indifference — just a few months earlier in the curve. And that the cub will never have to choose to trust a human again. Whatever was wired in those first weeks is wired now. The handlers' patience was the actual gift; the cub's sleep was the receipt.

I walked out smelling like straw and a cat that had decided I was acceptable. The bus ride back to the ship was forty-five minutes of looking at nothing in particular through the window with my hand still on the camera bag.

The lions and tigers alone make Paipai Zoo Safari Park worth a return to Ensenada. I would do this excursion again on the same itinerary, just to see if the white tiger cub remembers me. He won't, of course. That isn't how it works. But the photograph remembers, and so do I.

Day 10 — Disembarkation

Smooth. In the way the cruise line works hard to make smooth.

Bag tags out the night before, in the color group that matched my flight time. Breakfast at the Solarium one more time — the man in the elevator had been right about that for ten days running. A quiet walk through Quantum's promenade as the ship emptied around me. A short wait in the lounge. Off the ship by 9 a.m., through customs by 9:20, in the rideshare line by 9:45. The Vincent Thomas Bridge was at my back as we pulled away from the terminal.

Ten days, two ports each twice, sea days at the right intervals, and the white tiger cub asleep against my jacket. The ship pulled away from her berth that evening to pick up another set of guests and do it all again. I was already home.

What I'd Tell Someone Booking the Same Trip

Book a back-to-back if your schedule allows it. Ten days on a single ship costs noticeably less than two consecutive cruises, the crew gets to know you by name, and the second pass through each port is calmer because you already know the lay of the land.

The Solarium for breakfast. Quantum's Solarium is adults-only and quieter at breakfast than the Windjammer by a wide margin. Bagel and lox, bacon, fresh fruit, no line.

Go to Cabo for the wildlife, not the shopping. The vendor strip is wearying. The whales, the sea lions, and the eco-cat tour out to Lover's Beach and El Arco are the reason to be there. Book the whale-watching excursion on your second port day, not the first, when the morning is less crowded.

Cabo is a cash-and-haggle port. Know what your dollars are worth in pesos before you negotiate. Cash saves you about 10-15% over card. Haggle in the currency you intend to pay in.

Ensenada has the North Star. Quantum's North Star ride is one of the best on the ship and runs cheaper at port than at sea. The view down the length of the ship with the town climbing into the hills behind is the photograph everyone takes.

The Paipai Zoo Safari Park excursion is the Ensenada day. It's about half an hour each way from the port. Big cats, kangaroos, hand-raised cubs. Plan a long port day if you want to do it without rushing the bus back.

Pre-cruise hotel near LAX or San Pedro. The terminal is a known-good operation but the power-outage morning is a reminder that anything can happen. A hotel within driving distance of the terminal absorbs nearly every embarkation-day failure. Worth the $150.
