Hola! Tacos & Cantina

MSC Cruises — Specialty Dining

Quick Answer: MSC’s Mexican street-food specialty venue. Tacos, burritos, enchiladas, quesadillas, tamales, tableside guacamole, plus a tequila-and-margarita program with Patrón, Casamigos, and Fortaleza flights. Two pricing models on one menu: all-you-can-eat at roughly $24.99 per person, or à la carte where street tacos start around $2.39. On Seaside EVO, Meraviglia-class, and the newest World class ships.

Best For: Hungry parties who can eat four-plus tacos (take the AYCE), couples and small groups who want one or two specific items at lower cost (order à la carte), and anyone whose evening is built around margaritas. Skip it if you’re sceptical of cruise-ship Mexican food — the guacamole and the tacos are honest, but the room is built for the bar.

Key Facts

  • Ships: MSC Seashore, MSC Seascape, MSC Bellissima, MSC Meraviglia, MSC Virtuosa, MSC World Europa, MSC World America. Reported expansion to MSC Seaside on later sailings.
  • Deck (varies by ship): Seashore Deck 8 (Atrium); World America Deck 7 (Terraces District over the Galleria)
  • Price: AYCE ~$24.99 per person, or à la carte (street tacos ~$2.39, burritos ~$10.79, enchiladas ~$11.99)
  • Hours: Dinner daily (typical 17:30–22:00); select lunch days 12:00–14:00
  • Dress Code: Casual / smart casual
  • Reservations: Recommended — MSC for Me app or Guest Services

Menu & Prices

Price: Choose one model per visit. All-you-can-eat (AYCE) roughly $24.99 per person in 2026 (up from $20 earlier in 2025). À la carte: street tacos ~$2.39 each, burritos ~$10.79, enchiladas ~$11.99, sides and quesadillas $2.99–$5.99. Drinks are billed separately on either model: bottled beer ~$9; Chelada and Michelada $9.99–$15.99; margarita variations and tequila / mezcal flights priced individually. Reviewers consistently recommend using MSC dining-package credits at Butcher’s Cut or Kaito Teppanyaki rather than here — the per-meal value of the package goes further at the higher-priced venues.

Special Accommodations

Allergen & Dietary Notes: Flag allergies at check-in — the kitchen routes around gluten (corn tortillas are the default; flour tortillas on request, so cross-contact is the bigger risk than ingredient swap), dairy (request no cheese / no crema), and shellfish (shrimp tacos and some salsas). Vegetarian options are real: bean-and-cheese tacos, veggie burritos, the elote and refried beans hold up as a meal. Vegan requires advance heads-up — some sauces use dairy. Speak with the maitre d’ or your server for assistance.

Where You’ll Find It

Hola! Tacos & Cantina is on a growing portion of the MSC fleet but not yet ship-wide. Confirmed locations as of mid-2026:

  • MSC Seashore — Deck 8, opening onto the Atrium
  • MSC Seascape — Seaside EVO layout, location varies
  • MSC Bellissima, MSC Meraviglia, MSC Virtuosa — Meraviglia-class deployments
  • MSC World Europa and MSC World America (Deck 7, Terraces District over the Galleria)

Cruisers have also reported Hola Tacos added to MSC Seaside on later sailings; confirm in the MSC for Me app for your specific sailing. Hours and location can shift by deployment.

3.7 ★ out of 5 MSC Fleet • 2023–2025

Hola! Tacos: When to Eat It, When to Drink Through It

Hola! Tacos sits in the awkward middle of the MSC specialty-dining lineup. It’s not the cheapest casual spot — the buffet is free — and it’s not the splurge venue that earns a multi-meal package. It’s a focused taqueria with a serious bar attached, and the pricing model (AYCE or à la carte) lets you treat it as either. The trick is knowing which version of the venue you’re booking.

Food

The tableside guacamole is the dish nearly every recent review names first. Avocado, lime, cilantro, chili, salt, mashed in front of you in a stone molcajete. It’s good. The carne asada and al pastor street tacos are the strongest protein picks — properly seasoned, char on the meat, salsa that doesn’t taste from a jar. The carnitas tacos are competent. Fish tacos vary by sailing: when the kitchen is hitting them they’re crisp battered white fish with slaw and chipotle crema, when they’re missing they’re soft and underseasoned. The queso dip is the casual standout, and the daily salsas are made in-house and are worth ordering even if you’ve just had chips. Tamales are a recent menu addition and the house masa is good. Where the menu thins: the burritos arrive overstuffed and underseasoned, the nachos lose their crisp under the toppings, and any item that involves layered ingredients sitting for more than a few minutes is the wrong order. The dessert program is modest — churros with chocolate hold up, the other sweets feel afterthought.

Service and the bar

Service is paced for the bar. Drinks arrive fast, which matters because the margarita program is the room’s most-loved feature. The Hola Classic Margarita is well-balanced; the Mangorita is the sleeper hit; the spicy variants land for those who want them. The tequila and mezcal flights are a genuine education — Patrón, Casamigos, and Fortaleza across Blanco, Reposado, and Añejo, with the server walking through the differences if you ask. Food service is slower than drinks during dinner peaks on sea days. Tableside guacamole is interactive in the right way — the server takes their time, the table watches, the bowl gets to you fast.

Atmosphere

The room reads more bar than dining room. Music is loud (Latin and pop mixed); the lighting is warm and uneven (table-by-table, not the dimmed-restaurant blanket). On Seashore the Deck 8 Atrium location means foot traffic past the door and a sightline into the venue from the corridor. On World America the Deck 7 Terraces position over the Galleria runs even louder — the cantina becomes part of the open atrium soundscape. Murals, papel-picado-style decoration, hanging lights over the bar. It’s a venue built for groups; for a quiet two-top dinner the room fights you.

The honest tradeoff

Two real complaints turn up across reviews. First, the menu is a European kitchen’s interpretation of Mexican street food rather than the food a taqueria in Mexico City or Los Angeles would plate. Travelers who know good Mexican often find the seasoning level dialled down for a broader audience. Second, the à la carte pricing is steep for tacos — $2.39 sounds reasonable until you remember the cruise fare paid for the meal at the MDR. The AYCE makes sense if you want to graze through the menu and try the tamales, the queso, the guacamole, three or four tacos; it stops making sense if you wanted to stop in for a single $5 lunch.

Pro Tips

  • Book the dinner slot if you want the room; book lunch (where the ship offers it) if you want the food without the noise.
  • Order à la carte unless you’re committed to grazing. The AYCE math works at four-plus tacos plus appetizers; below that, you’re paying for food you won’t eat.
  • Tableside guacamole and the carne asada / al pastor tacos are the “don’t skip” orders. The burritos and nachos are the “maybe skip.”
  • Save your MSC dining-package credits for Butcher’s Cut and Kaito Teppanyaki, not for here. The per-meal value of the package is stronger at the more expensive venues.
  • The bar program rewards curiosity — ask the server about the tequila tier you’ve never tried. The Reposado-Patrón comparison alone is worth a flight.

Conclusion

Rating: 3.7/5. Hola! Tacos is a fun specialty venue that does the guacamole, two of the tacos, and the bar program well, and stretches when it tries to be a full Mexican restaurant. It earns its score on the things it does honestly (the tableside, the carne asada, the margarita program); it loses points on the burritos, the nachos under load, and the slightly cruise-line-tuned seasoning across the board. Tip: walk in with a bar plan and a taco short list. Walk out before the room gets late-loud.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Hola! Tacos & Cantina on MSC Cruises?

Hola! Tacos & Cantina is MSC’s Mexican street-food specialty venue — tacos, burritos, enchiladas, quesadillas, tamales, tableside guacamole, and a tequila and margarita program. It runs two pricing models on the same menu: all-you-can-eat at roughly $24.99 per person (was $20 earlier in 2025), or à la carte where street tacos start at roughly $2.39, burritos around $10.79, and enchiladas around $11.99. The cantina sits alongside an adjacent bar serving margarita variations (Classic, Mango, Passion Fruit, Strawberry, Pineapple) and tequila and mezcal flights with Patrón, Casamigos, and Fortaleza.

Which MSC ships have Hola! Tacos & Cantina?

On the Seaside EVO sisters MSC Seashore (Deck 8, opening onto the Atrium) and MSC Seascape; on the Meraviglia-class ships MSC Bellissima, MSC Meraviglia, and MSC Virtuosa; and on the newest World class flagships MSC World Europa and MSC World America (Deck 7, Terraces District, overlooking the Galleria). Cruisers have also reported Hola Tacos added to MSC Seaside on later sailings — verify in the MSC for Me app for your specific ship and date.

How much does Hola! Tacos & Cantina cost?

Two pricing models, same menu. All-you-can-eat (AYCE): roughly $24.99 per person in 2026 (was $20 earlier in 2025). À la carte: street tacos from ~$2.39 each, burritos around $10.79, enchiladas around $11.99, sides and quesadillas $2.99–$5.99. Drinks are separate: bottled beers around $9; Chelada and Michelada $9.99–$15.99; margarita variations and tequila flights priced individually. Most experienced reviewers recommend ordering à la carte unless you can put away four-plus tacos, and recommend skipping the multi-meal MSC dining package here in favour of using those credits at Butcher’s Cut or Kaito Teppanyaki where the per-meal value is stronger.

Do I need a reservation for Hola! Tacos & Cantina?

Yes — book through the MSC for Me app or at Guest Services on Day 1. Hola Tacos consistently fills prime dinner slots on sea days. Lunch service (where the ship offers it) is less crowded; walk-up sometimes works at lunch, rarely at dinner.

What are the standout dishes at Hola! Tacos & Cantina?

Recurring picks across recent reviews: the tableside guacamole made in front of you with avocado, lime, cilantro, and chili; the Carne Asada and Al Pastor street tacos; the queso dip; the homemade daily salsas; and the margarita variations — the Mangorita and the spicy variants are the most-named in reviews. Tamales are a more recent menu addition worth ordering for the kitchen’s house-made masa. The Hola Classic Margarita and the tequila and mezcal flights (Blanco, Reposado, Añejo across Patrón, Casamigos, and Fortaleza) anchor the bar program. Weaker performers: the burritos can arrive overstuffed and under-seasoned, and the nachos lose their crisp under the toppings if they sit.
