Busan

South Korea's Coastal Gem

Last reviewed: December 2025

Captain's Logbook

Busan announced itself before I even stepped off the ship. South Korea's second-largest city sprawls across hills that tumble dramatically into the East Sea, a landscape of mountains and monasteries, skyscrapers and seafood hubs. The port itself is massive – one of the world's busiest container terminals – but somehow the city retains an intimacy that Seoul's vastness can't match. Three and a half million people live here, yet Busan feels manageable, navigable, distinctly coastal in a way that sets it apart from other Asian megacities. I had expected efficiency and modernity; I hadn't expected beauty. But beauty is what Busan delivers – in ancient temples perched impossibly on sea cliffs, in villages painted every color of the rainbow, in the way morning light catches fishing boats returning to harbor.

The city's origins stretch back millennia, though Busan rose to prominence as a fishing village before becoming Korea's gateway to Japan and the wider world. During the Korean War, this was the last stronghold – the "Busan Perimeter" where UN forces made their stand in 1950, holding on until MacArthur's Incheon landing turned the tide. That wartime refugee history lingers in unexpected places, particularly Gamcheon Culture Village, where displaced families built improvised homes on steep hillsides during the conflict. What was once a shantytown is now one of Korea's most photographed destinations – pastel houses cascading down cliffs like a Mediterranean fantasy transposed to East Asia. I walked those narrow alleys, discovering art installations around every corner, thinking about how hardship can transform into beauty given enough time and creative spirit.

However modern Busan appears – and it is strikingly modern, with subway lines, high-speed rail, and 5G connectivity everywhere – the old Korea persists. At Haedong Yonggungsa Temple, perched on rocky cliffs where crashing waves provide constant prayer accompaniment, I watched elderly women in traditional hanbok making offerings to Buddha while teenage couples took selfies. Both activities felt equally natural, equally Korean. The temple dates to 1376, though it's been rebuilt many times since. The setting is so dramatic it seems designed for Instagram, yet the devotion is genuine. Incense smoke drifted across stone courtyards as I descended hundreds of steps toward the sea, passing golden Buddha statues and granite guardians, feeling centuries collapse into the present moment.

The moment that crystalized Busan for me came at Jagalchi Seafood Bazaar, the largest seafood bazaar in all of Korea. I wandered through aisles of tanks holding creatures I couldn't identify – sea squirts and geoducks, octopi and king crabs, fish of every conceivable size and color. Vendors called out prices; buyers haggled with theatrical intensity. The smell was overwhelming – brine and fish and the particular sweetness of fresh catch. An elderly woman beckoned me to her stall, gestured at a tank of live prawns, mimed cooking. I nodded without understanding; she laughed, scooped up a dozen prawns, and disappeared into a back kitchen. Five minutes later I was eating the freshest seafood of my life, prawns so sweet they needed nothing but a squeeze of lemon. The cost? About $15 USD. The grandmother watched me eat with obvious satisfaction, then pressed a piece of dried squid into my hand as I left – a gift, she indicated, for being a good customer.

Busan rewards exploration. Haeundae Beach, Korea's most famous stretch of sand, offers sophisticated cafés and nightlife against a backdrop of luxury hotels. The Gwangalli Beach area frames views of the illuminated Gwangan Bridge, particularly stunning after dark. Street food vendors sell tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes) and hotteok (sweet pancakes filled with cinnamon and sugar) for a few dollars. Korean BBQ restaurants – smoky, convivial, endlessly delicious – cluster in every neighborhood, charging perhaps $20-30 per person for all-you-can-eat grilled meats. Budget $50-80 USD for a full day including transportation, street snacks, and a proper Korean BBQ dinner. The subway costs about $1.50 per ride and covers most attractions. Taxis are affordable and honest; drivers use meters without exception. Although English isn't widely spoken outside tourist areas, Koreans are extraordinarily helpful – I was guided to my destination by strangers more times than I could count, their kindness transcending language barriers.

What surprised me most about Busan was its humility. This is a major international city, yet it doesn't strut. The mountains press close; the sea dominates the horizon; ancient temples remind you that human achievement is temporary. Seoulites sometimes dismiss Busan as provincial, but that provincial quality is precisely its charm. People here seem less hurried, more rooted, more connected to the natural world that surrounds them. By the time my ship departed, I understood why Koreans vacation here – not for the beaches alone, but for the particular enduring peace that comes from a city that truly knows where it belongs in the world.

Cruise Port

Cruise ships dock at the Busan Port International Passenger Terminal in the Jungang-dong area, centrally located and well-connected to the city. The modern terminal offers tourist information, currency exchange, and a taxi stand directly outside. From the terminal, downtown Busan attractions are easily accessible. Jagalchi Seafood Bazaar lies within walking distance (about 15 minutes). The subway (Line 1, Jungang Station) is nearby, connecting to all major sights. The port area itself, while industrial in places, opens onto a waterfront promenade with views of mountains rising behind the city. Immigration is typically efficient; bring your passport and any required visas. Ships dock directly – no tender required. Facilities meet international standards with restrooms, WiFi, and luggage services available. The terminal's central location makes Busan one of Korea's most convenient cruise ports – you can start exploring immediately upon disembarkation.

Getting Around

Busan's transportation system is excellent. The subway runs four lines covering most tourist areas; single rides cost about 1,400 won ($1 USD). Buy a T-money card at any convenience store for discounted fares and easier transfers. Taxis are metered, honest, and affordable – expect about 5,000-15,000 won ($4-12 USD) for most trips within the city. Uber operates here but regular taxis are equally reliable. The KTX high-speed train connects Busan to Seoul (2.5 hours) for day-trippers extending their visit.

From the cruise port, Jagalchi is walkable. Gamcheon Culture Village requires a taxi or bus (about $8-10 by taxi). Haedong Yonggungsa Temple is about 40 minutes by taxi ($20-25) or accessible via bus routes. Haeundae Beach takes about 25 minutes by subway (Line 2).

Accessibility note: Busan's subway system has elevators at most major stations, though older stations may have limited accessibility. Newer attractions like Haeundae Beach have accessible facilities. Older neighborhoods like Gamcheon Culture Village involve significant stair climbing and steep narrow streets – difficult for wheelchair users or those with mobility limitations. Traditional temples have extensive stairs with limited alternatives. Ship excursion desks can arrange accessible transportation for those with specific needs. Major commercial areas and new developments generally offer good wheelchair access.

Beach Guide

Haeundae Beach: Korea's most famous beach – a 1.5 km crescent of white sand backed by luxury hotels, seafood restaurants, and nightlife. Clean facilities, lifeguards in season (July-August), and easy subway access (Haeundae Station, Line 2). Very crowded during Korean summer holidays. Water temperature comfortable June-September. The surrounding neighborhood offers boutiques, cafés, and the Busan Aquarium.

Gwangalli Beach: Known for spectacular nighttime views of the illuminated Gwangan Bridge. More relaxed atmosphere than Haeundae, with bars and restaurants lining the waterfront. Popular for evening strolls. Accessible via Gwangan Station (Line 2).

Songjeong Beach: Quieter, more local feel – popular with surfers and those seeking escape from Haeundae crowds. Accessible via Songjeong Station. Good for swimming with gentler waves.

Shore Excursions

Haedong Yonggungsa Temple: Busan's most spectacular sight – a Buddhist temple built in 1376, perched dramatically on rocky cliffs overlooking the East Sea. Unlike most Korean temples hidden in mountains, this one embraces the ocean. Hundreds of stairs descend through stone guardians and golden Buddha statues to the main hall where waves crash below. Ship excursions ($60-80 for half-day) provide transportation and guided context. Independent visitors can reach it by taxi ($20-25 each way, about 40 minutes from port) or bus 181 from Haeundae. Book ahead during cherry blossom season (April) when crowds intensify. Free admission. Allow 2-3 hours including travel.

Gamcheon Culture Village: The "Machu Picchu of Busan" – pastel-colored houses cascading down steep hillsides, transformed from wartime refugee settlement into open-air art gallery. Murals, sculptures, and quirky installations reward wandering. Purchase the village map ($2) with stamps to collect at various locations. Ship excursions combine this with other sights. Independent visitors take taxi ($8-10 from port) or buses from Toseong-dong Station. Significant stair climbing required – not suitable for mobility limitations. Allow 2-3 hours. Small admission fee ($1-2) for some installations.

Jagalchi Seafood Bazaar: Korea's largest seafood bazaar – a sensory overload of live tanks, vendor calls, and incomparable freshness. Walk through aisles of creatures you've never seen, then have your selection cooked on-site or at upstairs restaurants. Walkable from cruise port (15 minutes). Ship excursions often include this in city tours. Independent exploration works perfectly – vendors are accustomed to tourists and will help navigate. Budget $15-30 for a seafood meal. Morning visits offer peak activity. Allow 1-2 hours.

Beomeosa Temple: One of Korea's five great temples, founded in 678 AD on the slopes of Mt. Geumjeongsan. More traditional than Haedong Yonggungsa – forest setting, ancient architecture, monks in residence. Peaceful atmosphere; fewer tourists. Accessible via subway (Beomeosa Station, Line 1) then taxi or walk uphill. Allow 2-3 hours including travel. Free admission to grounds.

Korean BBQ Experience: No visit to Korea is complete without Korean BBQ – grilled meats at your table over charcoal, accompanied by banchan (side dishes), rice, and soju. Restaurants cluster throughout the city. Ship excursions may include cooking classes or guided food tours. Independent visitors can simply walk into any BBQ restaurant – choose places packed with locals. Expect $20-30 per person for all-you-can-eat. Book ahead for popular spots during peak dinner hours.

Independent vs. Ship Excursions: Busan's excellent public transportation makes independent exploration highly feasible. The subway is cheap, clean, and covers major attractions. However, ship excursions offer value for distant temples (transportation and interpretation), food tours (local knowledge and access), and comprehensive city tours covering multiple sites efficiently. Book ahead for popular temple tours during peak season. Independent travelers should download a translation app – English signage exists but isn't universal. Allow extra time returning to ship – Busan traffic can be heavy during rush hours.

Local Cuisine

Korean BBQ: Grilled meats at your table – beef (galbi), pork belly (samgyeopsal), or chicken. Wrapped in lettuce with garlic, peppers, and ssamjang sauce. All-you-can-eat restaurants typically $20-30 per person. A quintessential Korean experience.

Fresh Seafood: Jagalchi Bazaar offers the freshest possible seafood – choose from tanks and have it prepared on-site. Raw fish (hoe), grilled shellfish, spicy fish stew (maeuntang). Budget $15-30 for generous portions.

Dwaeji Gukbap: Busan's signature dish – pork and rice soup, rich and warming. Local specialty found throughout the city. About $8-10 per bowl.

Street Food: Tteokbokki (spicy rice cakes), hotteok (sweet pancakes), odeng (fish cake skewers), mandu (dumplings). Found at food stalls and vendors. Most items $2-5.

Soju: Korea's national spirit – clear, slightly sweet, traditionally consumed with meals. About $3-5 per bottle. Drink responsibly; it's stronger than it tastes.

Travel Notices

Language: Korean is the primary language. English is limited outside major tourist areas but improving. Download a translation app. Koreans are incredibly helpful – gestures and patience work wonders.

Temple Etiquette: Dress modestly at temples (cover shoulders and knees). Remove shoes when entering buildings. Speak quietly. Photography often allowed but ask first. Small donations are appreciated but not required.

Tipping: Not customary in Korea. Service is included. Tipping can even cause confusion. Simply pay the bill and say thank you (gamsahamnida).

Currency: Korean Won (KRW). About 1,300 won = $1 USD. ATMs widely available; credit cards accepted almost everywhere. Some traditional vendors prefer cash.

Depth Soundings

The Temple on the Sea: Haedong Yonggungsa Temple represents something profound about Korean spirituality – the belief that sacred spaces should engage with natural beauty rather than retreat from it. Most Buddhist temples hide in mountain valleys; this one embraces crashing waves. As I descended those hundreds of stairs toward the sea, incense smoke mixing with salt spray, I understood why Koreans consider this place special. The temple was rebuilt after Japanese colonial destruction, making it both ancient and modern – a survival story carved in stone and devotion.

Gamcheon's Transformation: The pastel village perched on those impossibly steep hillsides began as desperation – refugees fleeing the Korean War, building shelters from whatever materials they could find. Decades later, artists and urban planners transformed poverty into poetry. But the original residents still live here, their homes now surrounded by tourists and murals. This tension – between authentic community and curated experience – defines many of Busan's attractions. Visit thoughtfully. The beauty you photograph was born from hardship.

The Korean Welcome: Koreans possess a hospitality that transcends language barriers. The grandmother at Jagalchi who pressed dried squid into my hands, the subway passenger who walked me to my connection, the temple volunteer who patiently explained proper offerings – these encounters revealed something about Korean culture that guidebooks miss. Hierarchy matters here; respect for elders shapes daily life. But within that structure exists genuine warmth toward strangers, particularly those who show interest in Korean traditions. Come with curiosity and you'll find doors opening everywhere.

Practical Information

Currency: Korean Won (KRW). Approximately 1,300 won = $1 USD. ATMs everywhere; credit cards widely accepted.

Language: Korean. English limited but improving in tourist areas. Download translation apps.

Time Zone: Korea Standard Time (KST), UTC+9. Same as Japan, no daylight saving.

Climate: Four distinct seasons. Summer (June-August) hot and humid with monsoon rains. Fall (September-November) ideal – mild temperatures, autumn colors. Winter (December-February) cold. Spring (March-May) pleasant with cherry blossoms in April.

WiFi: Excellent public WiFi throughout Korea. Available in subway stations, cafés, and most public spaces.

Safety: Korea is extremely safe. Low crime rates; solo travelers report feeling secure at all hours.

Frequently Asked Questions
Where do cruise ships dock in Busan?

Ships dock at Busan Port International Passenger Terminal in the Jungang-dong area, centrally located with easy access to the subway and Jagalchi Seafood Bazaar within walking distance.

What is Haedong Yonggungsa Temple?

A spectacular Buddhist temple built in 1376 on sea cliffs overlooking the East Sea. Unlike mountain temples, this one embraces the ocean with dramatic views and crashing waves below. Hundreds of stairs lead down through golden Buddha statues to the main hall.

Is English widely spoken in Busan?

Limited but improving. Major tourist sites have English signage. Download a translation app and be prepared to use gestures. Koreans are exceptionally helpful and will go out of their way to assist confused tourists.

What is Gamcheon Culture Village?

A hillside village of pastel-colored houses that began as a Korean War refugee settlement and transformed into an open-air art gallery. Murals, sculptures, and quirky installations make it Instagram-famous. Significant stair climbing required.

Should I try Korean BBQ?

Absolutely – it's a quintessential Korean experience. Grilled meats at your table, wrapped in lettuce with various condiments. All-you-can-eat restaurants cost $20-30 per person. No reservation needed; just walk in.

Is Busan safe for tourists?

Extremely safe. Korea has very low crime rates. Solo travelers, including women, consistently report feeling secure throughout the country.

Quick Answer

Busan is South Korea's second-largest city and busiest port. Visit Haedong Yonggungsa Temple perched on sea cliffs, explore colorful Gamcheon Culture Village, devour seafood at Jagalchi, and relax at Haeundae Beach. Excellent subway system, affordable taxis, Korean BBQ everywhere. Very safe; limited English but incredibly helpful locals.
