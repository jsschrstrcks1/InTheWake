The Cruise Duck Tradition: Spreading Joy One Duck at a Time

How rubber ducks became the unofficial mascot of the cruising community

by Ken Baker | January 18, 2026

Disclosure: This page contains affiliate links. If you purchase through these links, we may earn a small commission at no extra cost to you. Learn more.

If you've cruised recently, you may have discovered a small rubber duck perched on a railing, tucked behind a plant, or smiling at you from a pool chair. Congratulations — you've been ducked.

The cruise duck tradition has taken the cruising world by storm, turning ordinary voyages into treasure hunts and strangers into a community. It's simple, it's silly, and it brings genuine joy to thousands of passengers on every sailing.

What Is Cruise Ducking?

Cruise ducking is exactly what it sounds like: bringing small rubber ducks aboard your cruise ship and hiding them in public areas for fellow passengers to discover. When someone finds a duck, they typically:

  • Keep it as a souvenir
  • Re-hide it for someone else to find
  • Post about it on social media with hashtags like #CruiseDucks or #YouveBeenDucked
  • Join Facebook groups to share their finds

Most ducks come with small tags attached explaining the tradition and inviting finders to participate in the community.

Where Did Cruise Ducks Come From?

The tradition gained momentum around 2020-2021, during the cruise industry's COVID pause and return. As ships began sailing again, cruisers wanted to create moments of connection and joy — something tangible to share with fellow passengers they might never meet face-to-face.

Facebook groups like "Cruising Ducks" grew to hundreds of thousands of members, with cruisers sharing hiding spots, duck finds, and creative duck designs. The tradition spread organically, ship to ship, until it became an expected part of the cruising experience.

Some trace the broader "ducking" phenomenon to Jeep owners who hide ducks on other Jeeps, but the cruise community made it their own — adapting it to the unique environment of a floating city where thousands of strangers share space for days at a time.

How to Participate: A Beginner's Guide

Step 1: Get Your Ducks

You'll need rubber ducks — and probably more than you think. Many cruisers bring 20-50 ducks per voyage, hiding several each day. Here are some popular options:

Recommended Duck Options

Classic Rubber Ducks (Bulk Packs)

Standard yellow ducks are the most recognized. Bulk packs are economical for serious duckers.

  • Assorted rubber ducks (no tags) — Great for customizing with your own tags
  • Mini rubber ducks — Tiny ducks perfect for creative hiding spots
Ducks with Tags (Ready to Hide)

Pre-tagged ducks save time and come ready for hiding.

  • Ducks with "You've Been Ducked" tags — Ready to go, no assembly needed
Magnetic Ducks (Cabin Mounting)

Cruise ship walls are steel — magnetic ducks stick anywhere!

  • Duck magnets — Perfect for hiding on metal surfaces throughout the ship
Themed Ducks

Special occasion or themed ducks add personality to your hiding.

  • Holiday-themed ducks (Christmas, Halloween, etc.)
  • Profession ducks (nurse, firefighter, teacher)
  • Character ducks (pirates, superheroes)

Step 2: Prepare Your Tags

Tags transform a random rubber duck into a community experience. Your tag should include:

  • "You've been ducked!" or similar greeting
  • Brief explanation of the tradition
  • What to do (keep it, re-hide it, share on social media)
  • Optional: Your social media handle or Facebook group name
  • Optional: Ship name and sailing date

Tip: Use waterproof paper or laminate your tags. Ducks near pools get wet!

Step 3: Hide Thoughtfully

Good hiding spots are discoverable but not obvious. Think about the joy of finding, not the frustration of searching. Great spots include:

  • Pool deck railings and planters
  • Elevator lobbies (above buttons, on ledges)
  • Behind napkin dispensers in casual dining
  • Library shelves (peeking out from books)
  • Promenade deck seating areas
  • Near photo galleries
  • At the base of staircase railings

Where NOT to Hide Ducks

  • Food service areas — Health code concerns
  • Restricted/crew areas — You shouldn't be there anyway
  • Safety equipment — Never near life jackets, lifeboats, or emergency gear
  • Other passengers' private spaces — Balconies, cabin doors without permission
  • Children's areas — Unless it's clearly for kids, avoid Adventure Ocean, etc.

A Reflection: Small Acts of Joy

There's something beautiful about the cruise duck tradition that goes beyond silliness. In a world that can feel disconnected and cynical, someone takes time to bring ducks on vacation, attach little notes of encouragement, and hide them purely for the joy of strangers.

No one gets recognition. No one gets paid. It's an anonymous gift — a small act of love that asks nothing in return.

For those who share our faith perspective, this echoes something deeper: "Let your light shine before others, that they may see your good deeds and glorify your Father in heaven" (Matthew 5:16). Not every light-shining moment is profound. Sometimes it's a rubber duck on a pool chair.

Some cruisers have even started hiding small faith-themed items alongside or instead of ducks — tiny figurines with encouraging Scripture references attached. It's a gentle way to share hope without being intrusive.

Join the Community

The cruise duck community thrives on Facebook, where groups with hundreds of thousands of members share finds, hiding spots, and creative duck ideas. Popular groups include:

  • Cruising Ducks — The largest and most active group
  • Cruise Ship Ducks — Another major community
  • Line-specific groups (Carnival Ducks, Royal Caribbean Ducks, etc.)

Posting your finds helps the original duckers know their ducks brought joy — which is often the best part of the whole tradition.

Bonus: Cabin Organization for Duck Hiders

If you're bringing 20-50 ducks, you'll need some cabin organization help. The same magnetic properties that make cruise ship walls perfect for hiding magnetic ducks also make them perfect for organizing your cabin:

  • Magnetic hooks — Hang bags of ducks, lanyards, hats, and more on any wall
  • Over-the-door organizer — Perfect for toiletries, accessories, and yes, ducks
  • Packing cubes — Keep ducks organized in your luggage

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the cruise duck tradition?

The cruise duck tradition involves bringing small rubber ducks on your cruise and hiding them around the ship for fellow passengers to find. Finders can keep the duck, re-hide it, or post about it on social media. It's a fun way to spread joy and connect with other cruisers.

Where did cruise ducks start?

The tradition gained popularity during the COVID pandemic around 2020-2021 when cruisers returning to ships wanted to create moments of joy and connection. It spread through Facebook groups and social media, becoming a beloved cruise community tradition.

What should I attach to my cruise ducks?

Most cruisers attach small tags explaining the tradition, with messages like "You've been ducked!" and optional social media hashtags or Facebook group names. Some include their cruise ship name and date. Tags can be handwritten or printed, and should be waterproof if possible.

Are cruise ducks allowed on all cruise lines?

Most major cruise lines allow and even embrace the duck tradition. Carnival, Royal Caribbean, Norwegian, and others have not banned ducks. However, be mindful not to hide ducks in restricted areas, food service areas, or anywhere that could create safety concerns.

How many ducks should I bring?

Many active duckers bring 20-50 ducks per cruise, hiding several each day. For a 7-day cruise, 25-35 ducks is a good starting point. Remember they're small and light, so they don't take much luggage space — especially mini ducks.

Start Your Ducking Journey

The cruise duck tradition is one of those beautiful, unexpected things that makes cruising special. It costs almost nothing, requires no talent, and brings genuine smiles to complete strangers.

Whether you're a first-time cruiser or a seasoned sailor, consider packing some ducks on your next voyage. You might just make someone's vacation a little brighter — and isn't that what travel is all about?

Happy ducking!
