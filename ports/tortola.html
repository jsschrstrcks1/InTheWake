Tortola: My British Virgin Islands Gateway

Sailing into Road Town harbor feels like entering the "Sailing Capital of the Caribbean" – and that's exactly what it is. Tortola means "Land of the Turtle Dove" (Columbus named it in 1493), and everywhere I look there are more yachts than I can count. This isn't just a cruise port – it's where sailors from around the world come to charter boats and explore the BVI archipelago.

The island has pirate blood in its veins. Blackbeard himself terrorized these waters in the 1700s when Tortola was a haven for buccaneers. Now it welcomes over 720,000 visitors a year as the busiest cruise port in the British Virgin Islands, thanks to that $82 million port renovation in 2015 with its impressive 1,039-foot pier.

My perfect day: off the ship and straight to The Baths on Virgin Gorda by ferry plus open safari taxi combo (entrance included) – swimming through boulder caves and grottoes is pure Indiana Jones.

Lunch at Top of the Baths – best conch fritters and painkillers. Afternoon at Smuggler's Cove – the most beautiful secluded beach with Nigel's Boom Boom Beach Bar (cash only, best rum punch ever). Sunset sail back watching the islands glow.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Emerging from the cave at The Baths into Devil's Bay – the most perfect beach I've ever seen.

A Walk Through History

Road Town has layers of history you can still touch. I love wandering Main Street to see St. George's Anglican Church (the craftsmanship is beautiful) and the Virgin Islands Folk Museum – small but full of stories about life here before and after slavery was abolished in 1834. The old sugar and tobacco plantations are gone now, replaced by yacht marinas and tourism.

Fort Burt sits on the hill overlooking the harbor – it's been watching over this anchorage since the 1700s when the Dutch and British fought over these islands. The views alone are worth the climb, and standing up there I can imagine what the lookouts saw when they spotted pirate sails on the horizon.

The Dutch first settled here in 1648, then the English came and stayed for 300 years. That British influence is everywhere – driving on the left, afternoon tea culture, and the proper cricket pitch in town.

Getting Around Tortola

Tortola Pier Park – walk off into town. Ferries to Virgin Gorda from Road Town dock.

  • The Baths (Virgin Gorda): Ferry plus taxi combo (totally worth it)
  • Smuggler's Cove: West end of Tortola, taxi or rental
  • Cane Garden Bay: Popular beach, 20-min taxi

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

The open-air safari taxis and bumpy roads are Tortola's way of giving you a free island roller-coaster with ocean views! Road Town can feel busy during cruise days – those 720,000+ annual visitors have to go somewhere – but the yacht charter scene means you'll also spot serious sailors provisioning their boats and talking wind speeds and anchorages.

The harbor is always full of beautiful sailing yachts. Don't be surprised if you see more sailboats than powerboats – this really is the yacht chartering capital of the Caribbean, and people fly in from all over the world just to pick up their bareboat charters here.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: The Baths worth the trip?
A: #1 thing to do in the entire BVI.

Q: Best painkiller?
A: Soggy Dollar Bar on Jost if you have time, otherwise Pusser's in Road Town.

Q: Is the pirate history real?
A: Absolutely. Blackbeard and other pirates used the BVI as a base in the 1700s. The narrow channels and hidden coves made it perfect for ambushing merchant ships.

Q: Why so many sailboats?
A: Tortola is the yacht charter capital of the Caribbean. Perfect winds, protected anchorages, and island-hopping sailing make this a sailor's paradise.

Q: Hurricane damage?
A: Almost everything rebuilt and beautiful.
