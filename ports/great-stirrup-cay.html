Norwegian Cruise Line's Private Island in the Bahamas

Great Stirrup Cay is Norwegian Cruise Line's private island — a 270-acre cay in the Berry Islands of the Bahamas that NCL has owned and operated since 1977, which makes it the oldest cruise-line private destination in the Caribbean. For most of its life it was a straightforward beach-and-buffet day. It is not that anymore. The island is finishing the biggest transformation in its history under a roughly $150 million plan, and the headline addition — the Great Tides Waterpark — opened in September 2026. What has not changed is the shape of a good day here: warm shallow water, an open-air lunch, a lounger under an umbrella, and the freedom to do very little.

This guide covers what your fare already buys you, what the paid add-ons cost, how you actually get ashore now that there is a pier, and the honest caveats — chief among them that Great Stirrup Cay sits in the Atlantic hurricane belt and is the call NCL most often reshuffles when a storm is in the Bahamas.

Last reviewed: July 2026

Price & Policy Notice

Prices, hours, venue names, and policies on this page reflect Norwegian Cruise Line's published materials and are subject to change without notice — the island was under active construction through 2026. Always verify current details in the NCL for Me app before you sail.

From the Pier

  • Welcome Plaza to the main beach: a few minutes on the complimentary tram, or a short flat walk.
  • Welcome Plaza to Great Tides Waterpark: tram service runs the spine of the developed area; reserve waterpark entry before you sail.
  • Welcome Plaza to the historic lighthouse (south end): the far end of the cay — a walk beyond the developed zone, not tram-served.

There are no cars, taxis, or roads to a town here — this is a private island, and the tram plus your own two feet are the whole transport system.

Captain's Logbook

An honest note before I begin: I have not stood on Great Stirrup Cay yet. What follows is a reading of the place from the published record and from the accounts of people I trust — soundings taken in another's wake, not my own footprints in that sand. I would rather tell you that plainly than pretend to a day I have not had. When I do sail here, I will come back and log what I actually found.

So let me tell you what I see when I look at this island honestly. Great Stirrup Cay is a manufactured thing, and I mean that without a sneer. Norwegian has owned it since 1977, longer than most of the people reading this have been cruising, and for decades it was the humblest kind of stop: you tendered ashore, you found a chair, you ate a burger off a buffet line, and you swam until it was time to go. There is something clarifying about that. A private island is not a place you visit so much as a day you are given — a curated, weather-permitting, all-arranged pause with the ship still visible offshore, waiting to take you home.

What strikes me now is how hard NCL is working to make the pause bigger. The roughly $150 million they have poured in — the new multi-ship pier so you can walk ashore instead of waiting on a tender, the Welcome Plaza, the Great Life Lagoon, and the Great Tides Waterpark with its nineteen or so slides — is a bet that a beach and a buffet are no longer enough. Maybe they are right. I have watched enough cruisers come back from a private-island day to know that the waterpark will thrill the families and that the lines will be real. But I would gently say this to a first-timer: the most valuable thing on this island is still free, and it is the thing the brochures spend the least ink on. It is the chair, the umbrella, the shallow warm water, and a few unhurried hours with nothing you have to accomplish.

I keep coming back to the lighthouse. There has been a light on the south end of this cay since long before Norwegian's helicopters and swim-up bars — a plain white tower that once did the only job that mattered out here, which was to keep ships off the rocks in the dark. Everything else on the island has been built, rebuilt, and rebranded around the cruise day. The lighthouse just stands there, doing nothing for the itinerary, and I find that I trust it more than anything with a reservation code. If I ever do walk this island, that is where I want to end up — at the old true thing on the far end, away from the slides, looking back at the crowd I came in with.

There is a tension here worth naming, because the site I write for is honest about that kind of thing. A private island is a beautiful convenience and also a small fiction. The Berry Islands are a real Bahamian place with real people, and Great Stirrup Cay is the one corner of them rented out, groomed, and gated for a day of American leisure. I do not think that is a sin — rest is good, and a family that saved all year for this deserves their slides and their shallow lagoon. But I would want a thoughtful traveler to hold both things at once: the genuine gift of a warm, easy day, and the awareness that the ease was manufactured for you, and that the real Bahamas is the water on the far side of the buoys.

So here is what I would tell you, from the record, until I can tell you from experience. Come ashore early if you want a shaded lounger, because the shade is the scarce resource and the crowd knows it. Decide before you sail whether the waterpark is worth it to you, and if it is, reserve it in the app rather than chancing a walk-up. Bring water shoes, because parts of this beach are made of broken coral and your bare feet will not forgive you. Do not expect your phone to work. And leave yourself at least one hour with no plan at all — no slide, no zipline, no reservation — because that hour is the thing you actually came for, and it is the one part of the day nobody can upsell you.

When I finally log my own steps ashore, I will tell you whether the lighthouse looked the way I imagined, and whether the free chair really was the best thing on the island. Until then, take these as careful soundings, and go make your own.

Cruise Port Guide

For most of its history you reached Great Stirrup Cay by tender — the ship anchored offshore and small boats ferried you in. That changed with the roughly $150 million expansion: NCL built a new multi-ship pier, completed in late 2025, so larger ships now come alongside and you simply walk ashore. Your arrival point is the Welcome Plaza, the new landing area built alongside the waterpark expansion, where the tram service begins.

A few honest caveats on getting ashore. The pier was closed for further construction from spring into the summer of 2026, and NCL ran tenders during that window; by later in 2026 it had reopened. Even with the pier in service, tendering can return as a storm-day contingency — if weather is marginal, plans can revert. And because this is a private island, there is no independent port town, no taxi rank, and no customs line: you step off the ship onto Norwegian's own ground, and everything ashore runs on your cruise keycard. Always confirm your specific arrival method and times in the NCL for Me app, which is also where you reserve the paid attractions before you sail.

Getting Around Great Stirrup Cay

Getting around Great Stirrup Cay is refreshingly simple, because there is not much of it to get around and no vehicles for hire. From the Welcome Plaza, a complimentary tram runs the developed spine of the cay — from the arrival area out to the beach zones and the Great Tides Waterpark. If you would rather walk, the developed area is flat and the distances are short; the main beach is only a few minutes on foot.

On accessibility: the new pier is a real improvement for anyone with limited mobility, because for most sailings it removes the tender step entirely — you walk down a gangway rather than stepping into a rocking boat. The tram provides step-free transport along the main route, and beach wheelchairs are available on request — ask at the shore-excursion desk on the ship, or at the Welcome Plaza, before you head out. What no cruise line can fix is the sand itself: soft beach is hard going for wheels and canes alike, so if walking is difficult, ask which beach zone has the firmest access and the closest tram stop. On days when the ship tenders instead of docking, the tender process is less accessible; if that matters to you, confirm the arrival method for your sailing in advance.

One thing worth planning around: there is little natural shade on the newer parts of the island, and the shaded loungers, cabanas, and umbrellas go quickly. Coming ashore in one of the earlier waves is the single best move for claiming a shaded spot near the water.

Excursions & Experiences

Great Stirrup Cay works differently from a normal port. There are no independent operators here and no local vendors to bargain with — because it is a private island, every paid experience is essentially a ship excursion arranged through Norwegian, and the smart move is to book ahead in the NCL for Me app before you sail rather than hoping for walk-up space. Here is the honest split between what is included and what will show up on your onboard account.

Free with your cruise fare

  • Beach access across multiple zones, with the main beach the most developed.
  • Buffet lunch, typically served 11:30 AM–2:30 PM at the open-air pavilion.
  • Lounge chairs and umbrellas — first-come, first-served; the shaded ones go early.
  • Great Life Lagoon, the new central pool with beach-style entry and swim-up bars.
  • Tram service from the Welcome Plaza, plus restrooms and shower facilities.

Paid add-ons (reserve in the app)

  • Great Tides Waterpark — the headline attraction, opened September 2026, with roughly nineteen slides across features like the Tidal Tower racing slides, the Wandering River, Cliffside Cove platform-jumping, and the Splash Cay kids' pad. Reservation-based; price varies by date.
  • Flighthouse Zipline — the multi-line zipline complex anchored by the Flighthouse take-off tower; around $20 for the multi-line option.
  • Underwater Snorkel Garden — a guided reef-snorkel at a curated underwater garden offshore.
  • Kayak tours and waverunners — small-group, roughly $50–80 per person; jet-ski rentals and other watersports are also sold at the island's watersports hut, along with snorkel-gear rental.
  • Beachfront cabana and villa rentals — premium shaded beach spaces; a cabana runs from a few hundred dollars up depending on the sailing.
  • Silver Cove — a separate ultra-premium, adults-only area with a private cove, lagoon villas, spa, and bar; day passes are available and full villas are the splurge option.

If the budget is tight, the honest truth is that the free beach-and-buffet day is a complete day on its own. If you have one splurge in you and the waterpark is open on your sailing, that is the reason this call is more interesting in 2026 than it has been in decades.

One planning note that applies to almost everything here: because the paid attractions are reservation-based and can sell out on busy sailings, the travelers who are happiest ashore tend to be the ones who decided what they wanted weeks earlier and booked it in the app, rather than arriving with a full wallet and an empty plan. If you are torn between two paid options, pick the one you cannot do at home — a waterpark and a zipline follow you to almost any beach; a curated reef-snorkel garden in clear Bahamian water does not.

Food & Drink Ashore

Lunch on Great Stirrup Cay is included and uncomplicated: a buffet at the open-air pavilion, typically open from around 11:30 AM to 2:30 PM, with the familiar beach-day spread of burgers, barbecue, salads, and fruit. It is not a culinary destination and does not pretend to be — it is a solid, free meal a few steps from your chair, which on a beach day is exactly the point.

For drinks, the island has several bars, including swim-up bars at the new Great Life Lagoon and the Grotto Bar tucked into the family pool zone so parents can order while the kids play. Soft drinks and water tie back to your ship's beverage arrangement; specialty cocktails are charged to your keycard unless your fare includes a drink package. If you are weighing an NCL beverage package for the whole cruise, our drink calculator lets you run your real consumption pattern before you commit. Bring a refillable water bottle regardless — hydration matters more than usual under this much direct sun.

Know Before You Go
  • Hurricane season is real here. Great Stirrup Cay is the Bahamas call NCL most often skips or reschedules when a storm threatens, especially in September and October. Build a "sea day instead" into your mental plan and consider travel insurance for autumn sailings.
  • No cell service. There are no terrestrial carriers on the cay — download entertainment, boarding info, and your reservations before you go ashore.
  • Reef-safe sunscreen is the responsible choice in these waters, and there is little natural shade on the newer areas.
  • It all runs on your keycard. There are no ATMs and no cash is needed for island purchases; a few small bills for tips are the only cash worth carrying.
  • Reserve the paid attractions early in the NCL for Me app — walk-up space for the waterpark and premium areas can be limited on busy sailings.

Depth Soundings Ashore

The small, specific things that make a Great Stirrup Cay day go smoothly — the details that are easy to miss until you are standing on the sand wishing someone had told you:

  • Shade is the scarce resource. The island has little natural tree cover on its newer areas, so the umbrellas, cabanas, and shaded loungers are the whole shade supply — and they are claimed early. Coming ashore in an earlier wave is the single best move for a shaded spot near the water.
  • Water shoes, not bare feet. Some beach zones are made of broken coral fragments rather than soft sand. They are hard on bare soles and worth protecting against.
  • Plan for no signal. With no carriers on the cay, your phone is a camera and not much else. Save anything you want offline first, and agree with your group on a meeting point and time rather than relying on texts.
  • Pack the day like a beach day, not a port day. Swimsuit worn ashore or in a beach bag, reef-safe sunscreen, a sun hat, a refillable water bottle, and a waterproof phone pouch cover almost everything you will want.
  • Decide the waterpark question before you board. Great Tides is reservation-based; on opening-window sailings the walk-up windows are unpredictable, so reserve in the app if it matters to you.
  • Tram or feet, not wheels-for-hire. There are no taxis or rental vehicles; the free tram plus short flat walks are the whole transport system.

Practical Information
  • Country: The Bahamas (Berry Islands). English is the language.
  • Currency: None needed ashore — every island purchase runs on your ship keycard. There are no ATMs. A few small US bills are handy only for tipping.
  • Tipping: Gratuities are generally bundled into your fare; a dollar or two to a bartender who looks after you is a kindness, not an obligation.
  • Connectivity: No terrestrial cell carriers reach the cay. Your ship's Wi-Fi and cellular-at-sea plans work while the ship is in range; download anything you need before you go ashore. See our internet at sea guide.
  • Time zone: Eastern (same as Florida departure ports); the island runs on ship time.
  • Safety: This is a controlled private island with lifeguarded swim areas — the main cautions are sun exposure, dehydration, and coral-fragment sand underfoot rather than anything you would worry about in a town.

Frequently Asked Questions
What is the best time of year to visit Great Stirrup Cay?

December through April is the sweet spot: warm, drier, and outside hurricane season. It is also the busiest stretch on the cay. If you want thinner crowds and can accept a small weather risk, late spring works well too.

When is hurricane season at Great Stirrup Cay?

Atlantic hurricane season runs June 1 to November 30, with the Bahamas most active in September and October. Great Stirrup Cay is the call NCL most often skips or reschedules when a storm threatens the Bahamas chain, so travel insurance is worth it in that window.

What should I pack for Great Stirrup Cay's weather?

Reef-safe sunscreen, a sun hat, water shoes for the coral-fragment beach areas, a refillable water bottle, and a waterproof phone pouch. There is little natural shade on the newer areas, so plan for full sun.

Will rain ruin my Great Stirrup Cay port day?

Bahamian showers are usually brief and pass quickly, so a short rain rarely ruins a beach day. A full washout is uncommon in the dry season. The bigger weather concern is a tropical storm that reroutes the ship, not an afternoon shower.

Is Great Stirrup Cay a tender port or does the ship dock?

As of the 2025 pier project, larger NCL ships dock at the new multi-ship pier and you walk ashore at the Welcome Plaza. Tendering can return as a storm-day contingency, so check the NCL app for your sailing.

What is free at Great Stirrup Cay and what costs extra?

Free: beach access, the buffet lunch, lounge chairs and umbrellas, the Great Life Lagoon pool, tram service, and restrooms. Extra: the Great Tides Waterpark, the Flighthouse Zipline, snorkel and watersports rentals, cabana or villa rentals, and the Silver Cove adults-only area.

Do I need cash on Great Stirrup Cay?

Not really. Purchases run on your cruise keycard, and there are no ATMs on the island. Some travelers bring a few small US bills to tip a bartender who takes good care of them, though gratuities are generally bundled.

Is Great Stirrup Cay accessible for limited mobility?

The new pier removes the tender step for most sailings, tram service runs from the Welcome Plaza, and beach wheelchairs are available on request. Soft sand is still soft sand, so ask your ship's shore-excursion desk about the best beach zone for your needs.
