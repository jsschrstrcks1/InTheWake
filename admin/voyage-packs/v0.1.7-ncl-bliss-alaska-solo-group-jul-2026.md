## Welcome

> Made for the solo cruiser who has already booked this specific hosted sailing and wants one trusted read on the week — the ship, the five ports, and the group's rhythm, in one place.

This pack exists to make one specific cruise easier: a 7-night Alaska round-trip from Seattle on **Norwegian Bliss**, July 11 – 18, 2026, calling at **Sitka, Juneau, Icy Strait Point, Ketchikan, and Victoria BC**. It's a **hosted solo cruisers group cruise** organized by **Kristie McClung Escoe**.

It pulls together the ship-specific tips, port-specific advice, packing notes calibrated for an Alaska summer, the group's pre-cruise and onboard rhythm Kristie has set, and the budget math you'd otherwise assemble from a dozen browser tabs.

It is calm by design. There is no urgency, no upsell, no countdown timer. The site that produced this pack is independent — no cruise-line affiliations, no shore-excursion commissions, no display ads.

### A note on what's first-hand here and what isn't

I haven't sailed this itinerary on this ship. Two kinds of experience stand behind the pack anyway: **Kristie has been to Alaska three times** — her host notes and the schedule she's set are hers, and they're quoted as hers. Everything else — the docking arrangements, shuttle systems, excursion operators and their prices, port layouts, transit timetables — comes from primary-source research: the port terminals' own websites, the operators' own price pages, the National Park Service, NCL's published materials, and the firsthand traveler entries in this site's port logbooks. Where a number couldn't be confirmed against a source like that, it isn't asserted here.

**Includes an offline-capable Emergency Contacts section** — a fillable family handoff card, cruise-line and embassy numbers tailored to this itinerary, available offline after first load via [In the Wake](/index.html)'s PWA. Designed to share with one trusted person ashore before sailing.

**This pack is written for first-time Alaska cruisers and solo cruisers** in Kristie's group. NCL Bliss is one of the strongest solo-cabin ships afloat (no single supplement on Studios, dedicated Studio Lounge, built-in solo cohort), and Alaska in July is the Inside Passage at its best — long daylight, green mountains, glaciers within reach, the small towns of southeast Alaska in their summer high season.

If this is also your very first cruise of any kind, the [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html) is a useful read alongside this pack. If you've sailed before and are wondering what's different about NCL specifically (Freestyle Dining, Studio Lounge culture, no formal nights), Section 1 below covers it.

## Section 1 — Your Ship: Norwegian Bliss {#section-1}

![Norwegian Bliss anchored off Portland, Dorset — the Robert Wyland humpback whale mural visible on the bow, the Aqua Park waterslides on the upper deck. Photo by Andrew Bone via Flickr, CC BY 2.0](/assets/ships/Norwegian_Bliss_Portland_Port_2020.webp)

### A purpose-built Alaska ship

[Norwegian Bliss](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-bliss.html) was delivered on April 21, 2018 by Meyer Werft at Papenburg, Germany — the third ship of NCL's **Breakaway Plus class** (after Norwegian Escape 2015 and Norwegian Joy 2017). She was **purpose-designed for Alaska**: NCL built her with wraparound observation decks and what the line billed at her debut as the largest observation lounge in its fleet (Deck 15 forward), and gave her hull to marine-life artist **Wyland** — the muralist behind the "Whaling Walls" — for a humpback-whale mural that wraps the bow. NCL has carried hull art since 2002 and named-artist murals since Peter Max painted Norwegian Breakaway in 2013; the Wyland whale is Bliss's own. By your July 2026 sailing she's eight years into service — well past any new-ship issues, her crew rhythms long settled.

Her direct sisters are Norwegian Joy (2017) and Norwegian Encore (2019). Norwegian Escape (2015) is the same hull but technically the lead ship of the original Breakaway Plus class, sometimes counted separately. If you've sailed Joy or Encore, Bliss feels familiar; if you've sailed older Breakaway-class ships (Breakaway, Getaway), Bliss is the bigger, more refined version.

Worth opening on your phone before you sail: [video walkthroughs](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-bliss.html#video-highlights) of her public spaces, [logbook entries](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-bliss.html#logbook) from travelers who've sailed her, and the [deck plans](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-bliss.html#deck-plans) so the first day's wayfinding is less guesswork.

### What's distinctive about Bliss

She's a ship designed around **outdoor observation** — which matches Alaska perfectly. The signature features:

- **Observation Lounge** (Deck 15 forward) — NCL's largest. Floor-to-ceiling windows wrapping the bow, indoor seating, a coffee bar, books, board games. Open all day; the quietest place on the ship in the early morning. This is where most Alaska cruisers spend their best hours.
- **Sea View Bar** (Deck 17) — open-air bar with panoramic views; less crowded than the main pool deck. *(This is where Kristie is holding the group's sailaway — see [Section 9](#section-9).)*
- **Aqua Park** with the **Ocean Loops** double-loop trap-door waterslide (159 ft above the sea, the loops extend 11 ft beyond the hull), plus the **Aqua Racer** mat slide and the **Free Fall** drop slides. Free; lines build by mid-morning on sea days.
- **Galaxy Pavilion** — interactive arcade and VR experiences (paid extra).
- **The Cavern Club** — Beatles tribute venue; full original-set songs by a resident band.
- **Headliners Comedy Club** — late-night comedy in a small-room setting.
- **The Haven** — NCL's ship-within-a-ship luxury enclave with private restaurant, courtyard, pool, and lounge.

What's NOT on Bliss (because she's Breakaway Plus, not Prima Plus): no Ocean Boulevard 360 wraparound (that's a Prima-class feature), no infinity pools. The closest visual equivalent is the Waterfront on Deck 8 — a partial outdoor promenade with bars and seating.

### Cabin categories — the solo calculus

**Before you commit, run the [stateroom sanity check](/articles/stateroom-sanity-check.html)** — a four-question filter that catches the bookings most likely to leave a cruiser unhappy. For Alaska specifically, the balcony-vs-interior question hits hardest because the trip is partly scenic-cruising; if you'd regret not having a balcony for Dawes Glacier, the filter tells you.

NCL's signature solo offering is at its strongest on Bliss: dedicated **Studio cabins** with **no single supplement**, [**Studio Lounge**](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html) **access** (free coffee, social space, scheduled hosted hours for solo cruisers), and a built-in cohort of fellow solos. Studio cabins are roughly 100 sq ft and windowless — compact and efficient.

**Decision rule for the July 11 sailing (group rates):**

- **Solo Studio Cabin ($1,269)** — the flyer-advertised rate. Best value for solo cruisers who'll spend daylight hours off the ship or in public spaces. Studio Lounge is your social anchor.
- **Oceanview / Balcony / Mini-Suite / Suite** — verify category and pricing with Kristie. Most other categories don't waive the single supplement, so the solo-traveler value drops sharply above Studio.
- **The Haven** — NCL's premium enclave with private restaurant, lounge, and pool. Real if budget allows, but a different experience; the Haven cabins are unconnected to the Studio Lounge culture, so you'd be opting out of the built-in solo community.

For Alaska specifically, the **balcony argument** is real: the ship spends hours threading the Inside Passage; a private outdoor space earns its keep. But the Studio cabin + Observation Lounge combo covers the same need with less spend.

For ship-size context, the [Ship Size Atlas](/tools/ship-size-atlas.html) lets you compare Bliss to other ships. Follow her live position throughout your week via the [Ship Logbook](/tools/ship-tracker.html) — a small comfort if family ashore wants to know where you are.

### Day 1 — Saturday, July 11 — Seattle (embarkation)

![Seattle skyline at twilight from Kerry Park](/ports/img/seattle/seattle-hero.webp)

**Terminal:** **Bell Street Cruise Terminal at Pier 66** — 2225 Alaskan Way, Seattle. Different terminal from the Pier 91 used by RCL/Princess/HAL; Pier 66 is the working downtown terminal, an easy walk from Pike Place Market, the Seattle Aquarium, and the Great Wheel. About 30–40 minutes from Sea-Tac (SEA) by Uber/Lyft.

**If you're driving to Pier 66.** The **Bell Street Pier garage** directly across from the terminal has 1,700 covered, secure spaces at **~$33/day** (Republic Parking; max vehicle height 6'6"; 25 handicap stalls; reservable in advance). Most Bliss cruisers fly into Sea-Tac, but if you're driving from Seattle, Tacoma, or the Pacific Northwest, Bell Street Pier is the convenient choice. Offsite long-term lots near Sea-Tac with shuttle service can run cheaper but add ~45 min each way to your embarkation morning — Pier 66's on-terminal garage is usually the right call for a week-long stay.

**Embark window:** 11:30 AM – 3:00 PM. Ship departs ~4:00 PM. **You must be back at the terminal by 2:00 PM** to complete embarkation processing.

**The Saturday morning routine Kristie laid out:**

NCL allows **early luggage drop-off from 8:00 AM** at the terminal — so you can drop bags, then enjoy a few hours of Seattle before boarding.

- **Option 1 (longer Pike Place):** Drop luggage 8–9 AM → walk ~12 minutes to **Biscuit Bitch Belltown** (2303 3rd Ave, open Sat 8 AM – 3 PM) for breakfast (cult-favorite breakfast biscuits) → walk to **Pike Place Market** to explore → back to the terminal by 2 PM. *Note: Biscuit Bitch has a downtown sister location (621 3rd Ave) that's closed; the Belltown shop is the active one.*
- **Option 2 (Pike Place first):** Drop luggage 8–9 AM → walk straight to **Pike Place Market** — breakfast vendors and fresh produce/seafood stalls open from **7 AM**; farm tables 9 AM – 4 PM; craft market 11 AM – 4 PM; most of the market is active 10 AM – 5 PM → continue exploring waterfront, the Seattle Aquarium, or the museums district → back by 2 PM.

Either option works. Pike Place is at its quietest before 9 AM (good if you want to walk through without crowds); the flying-fish performance gets going around 11.

For deeper Seattle pre-cruise reading: the [Seattle port page](/ports/seattle.html) covers the broader picture, and the [Seattle logbook](/ports/seattle.html#logbook) collects what other cruisers thought of their embarkation-day morning.

**The realistic Day 1 timeline:**

- **8:00 AM – 9:00 AM:** Early luggage drop-off if you're using it.
- **9:00 AM – 1:00 PM:** Seattle morning (Option 1 or 2 above).
- **1:30 – 2:00 PM:** Return to Pier 66 with margin.
- **2:00 PM:** All-aboard begins for late-embarkation guests; lines at security may build.
- **2:30 PM:** Find your cabin (cabins typically open by 1 PM; if you boarded earlier, they're ready now). Meet your stateroom steward.
- **3:30 PM:** Mandatory muster drill — NCL's drill is partly app-based (watch the safety video in the NCL app) plus an in-person check-in at your assigned station.
- **4:00 PM:** **Sailaway at the Sea View Bar, Deck 17.** *Kristie is here.* Less-crowded than the main pool deck; panoramic views; she'll be passing out the week's printed info. If you want sailaway outside first, come find her at Sea View afterward.
- **5:00 PM:** **Group dinner reservation** — check with Kristie for the venue. Open to anyone who wants to join.
- **Later evening:** NCL hosts a **solo-group meet-and-greet** sometime on Day 1 — Kristie will share the time once NCL confirms it.

**What to pack in your carry-on for Day 1:** swimsuit (pools open before checked bags arrive), light jacket (Seattle evenings can be 60°F even in July), all medications, change of clothes, passport (preferred — Victoria BC on Day 7 is a foreign port), phone charger, refillable water bottle, your printed group itinerary if Kristie sent one. Your checked bags may not arrive until 7–9 PM.

**Money/card:** Your NCL keycard is your ID, room key, and onboard charge. You'll receive it at check-in. Currency aboard: US Dollar.

### Day 2 — Sunday, July 12 — At sea, the Pacific run toward Sitka

A sea day. The settling-in day; the group's rhythm starts taking shape.

**A route note worth knowing:** Sitka faces the open ocean — it sits on the outer coast of Baranof Island, not inside the protected channels. To reach it from Seattle, the ship runs up the Pacific side of Vancouver Island and across open water. Today is the most ocean-exposed day of the week (see [Section 7](#section-7) if motion is a concern), and the scenery is open horizon rather than close-in fjord walls. The threading-the-channels Inside Passage scenery arrives mid-week, between the Alaska ports.

**Morning:**
- Coffee in the [**Observation Lounge**](/restaurants/ncl/observation-lounge.html) (Deck 15 forward) — the day's best move. Quiet, panoramic, the first hour after sunrise has the best light.
- Breakfast at the **Garden Café** (buffet, busier) or **Taste/Savor** (sit-down, calmer).

**Mid-morning to lunch — pick ONE:**

- **The Studio Lounge** if you're in a Studio cabin — Day-2 morning is often when the hosted Studio coffee hour happens; check the daily program.
- **The Mandara Spa thermal-suite day pass** — sea-day mornings are when introductory rates appear.
- **Ocean Loops** waterslide (and the Aqua Park siblings) — first thing has the shortest lines.
- **A bridge tour or behind-the-scenes ship tour** if the Cruise Planner offers it.

**Afternoon:**
- The open-ocean run is its own show — horizon, seabirds, and a real chance of whale spouts off the west coast of Vancouver Island. The Observation Lounge or your balcony.
- Trivia, lectures, activities on the daily program.

**Evening — group specialty dining:**

- **5:00 PM: [Teppanyaki](/restaurants/ncl/teppanyaki.html).** Kristie's group has a reservation for whoever's joining. Teppanyaki is the showy hibachi-style room; arrive 5–10 minutes early to be seated together.

### Day 3 — Monday, July 13 — Sitka, Alaska {#day-sitka}

![Sitka harbor in a Library of Congress archive photograph — the town's Russian-Tlingit heritage shows in any era](/ports/img/sitka/sitka-hero.webp)

**Arrival:** 10:00 AM. **Depart:** 6:00 PM. Bliss docks at the **Sitka Sound Cruise Terminal** at Halibut Point, about 5 miles north of downtown — no tendering. A **free shuttle** runs continuously between the terminal and downtown (330 Harbor Drive) during port hours; the ride is 10–15 minutes. The terminal manages boarding with a reservation system on busy days (reserve at reservation.sitkadock.com, or use the standby line), and **the last shuttle leaves downtown 30 minutes before all-aboard** — build that into your afternoon.

**Sitka — the most historically dense port of the week.** Capital of Russian America from 1808 until the 1867 sale of Alaska to the United States; the only Russian Orthodox cathedral in North America (St. Michael's, rebuilt after a 1966 fire); deep Tlingit history that shaped its first centuries before Russian contact. Most Alaska itineraries skip Sitka in favor of Ketchikan; **this itinerary covers both** — take advantage. The walkable downtown is small (under a mile end-to-end) and the [Sitka National Historical Park](https://www.nps.gov/sitk/index.htm) totem trail is among the most affecting easy walks in southeast Alaska.

**Free, walkable once the shuttle drops you downtown:**

- **St. Michael's Cathedral** — the rebuilt Russian Orthodox cathedral (small donation suggested for entry).
- **Sitka National Historical Park** — totem-pole trail through old-growth forest, 30-minute walk from downtown. Free entry. The Tlingit and Russian-era exhibits at the visitor center are quietly excellent.
- **Sheldon Jackson Museum** — Alaska's oldest museum; one of the best ethnographic collections of Alaska Native material culture in the state.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Sea otter and wildlife boat tour** (~$210–225/person) — Sitka has the densest sea-otter population in southeast Alaska. The top-rated operator is **True Alaskan Tours' "Sitka Whale Watch and Sea Otter Quest"** (2-hour tour, Alaska Native-owned, ~$210).
- **Fortress of the Bear** sanctuary — rescued bears in semi-natural habitat. **$20 adult / $15 ship crew** self-guided admission; ethically run, not a roadside zoo. (Packaged shore-excursion tours bundling Fortress + Raptor Center + transport run $50–80.)
- **Russian Bishop's House tour** — NPS, **free admission** (inside the National Historical Park); ranger-led upper-floor tours also free, ticket at the front desk.
- **Independent transit:** **The RIDE Sitka** runs a $5 day pass / $2 one-way between downtown, the park, and the Industrial Park area. Note: the Blue Line (Sawmill Creek Rd) stops at Gary Paxton Industrial Park, a 5–10 min walk short of Fortress of the Bear — plan the walk. (The terminal-to-downtown leg is covered by the free terminal shuttle; The RIDE is for getting beyond downtown.)

**Pack for Sitka:** rain jacket (Sitka rains 220+ days per year — your day might be one), waterproof or treated walking shoes, fleece layer, $30–50 cash USD for transit and tips.

**Monday evening is open** — no group specialty dinner tonight. Solo cruisers tend to land at the [Studio Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html), [Sea View Bar](#) (Deck 17, where Kristie hosted sailaway), or the [Observation Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/observation-lounge.html) for pre- and post-dinner gatherings. Check the daily Freestyle Daily program for Monday-night theatre and live-music options.

**Worth a look on the way in:** the [Sitka port page](/ports/sitka.html), its [photo gallery](/ports/sitka.html#gallery), and the [Sitka logbook](/ports/sitka.html#logbook) — what other travelers noticed at the totem trail, around St. Michael's, on the transit-bus loop. Build your day in the [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html) before you go ashore; record what actually happened in the [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) on the way back.

### Day 4 — Tuesday, July 14 — Juneau in the morning, Dawes Glacier in the afternoon {#day-juneau}

![Juneau seen from Mount Roberts, with cruise ships docked along the waterfront](/ports/img/juneau/juneau-hero.webp)

**Two stops in one day. Plan accordingly.**

- **Juneau:** 6:30 AM arrival, **1:30 PM departure** — about 6.5 hours ashore. Early start; you'll want to be off the ship by 7:30 AM to make the most of it.
- **Endicott Arm / Dawes Glacier:** scenic cruising from ~3:00 PM into the evening — the ship turns into the fjord shortly after leaving Juneau and approaches the glacier face by late afternoon / early evening. No off-ship excursion; the day's second half is what you see from deck.

**Juneau — the only U.S. state capital you can't reach by road.** Reachable only by ship, plane, or float plane. Population ~32,000 year-round; ~17,000 daily cruise passengers in peak summer. Downtown is built into a near-vertical mountainside; the Mount Roberts Tramway is the up-the-mountain ride most cruisers take.

**Free, walkable from the cruise dock:**

- **Downtown Juneau** — South Franklin Street's bars, the Red Dog Saloon (touristy but historic), state-museum-quality Sealaska Heritage Institute (free; among the best Alaska Native cultural exhibits in the state), Alaska State Capitol (free 30-minute tours weekdays).
- **Last Chance Mining Museum** — short walk uphill, free, gold-mining history.

**Paid (decide ahead — the early all-aboard at 1:00 PM is tight; book a half-day, not a full day):**

- **Mendenhall Glacier visit** — the glacier is 13 miles from downtown. **M&M Tours' "Glacier Express"** ([mmtoursofjuneau.com](https://mmtoursofjuneau.com/glacier-express/)) is now a **$79 adult round-trip 3-hour tour** (transport + 2 hours at the visitor center, Nugget Falls walk, glacier viewpoints). NCL ship excursion ~$80–120. The old "Blue Bus" shuttle-only rate is no longer the product offering; this is a full tour.
- **Mount Roberts Tramway (Goldbelt Tram)** — 1,800-foot vertical tram up Mount Roberts; **$65 adult all-day pass** (Goldbelt no longer sells a round-trip-only ticket). Easy hiking trails at the top. The base station is right at the cruise dock, which makes it the easiest thing to combine with anything else on this short Juneau morning.
- **Whale watching** — humpback season peaks June–September. **Harv & Marv's** ([harvandmarvs.com](https://www.harvandmarvs.com/)) runs out of Auke Bay; among the highest-quality wildlife excursions on this itinerary. **$159–210/person**. *Tour is ~4 hours total (1 hr transfer + 3 hr on water). The earliest morning slot (typically 7:30 AM pickup) is the only one that fits the 1:30 PM all-aboard with margin — confirm the departure slot at booking.* **Dress for it like glacier viewing, not like a port walk** — a 28-foot boat at 20 knots in 55°F air feels like 35°F at the rail, and humpback breach-splash carries 20 feet. Full layers, waterproof shell, hat that won't blow off, hand warmers, non-slip shoes. Cold fumbles ruin breach photos.
- **Helicopter to Juneau Icefield + glacier landing** — **TEMSCO** ([temscoair.com](https://temscoair.com/)) is the local operator. Mendenhall guided glacier walk from ~$409; Pilot's Choice (2 glacier landings on the Juneau Icefield) from ~$579; dog-sledding combo from ~$699. *Verify the morning slot fits the 1:30 PM ship-back call.*

**For first-timers given the short Juneau day:** Mendenhall Glacier via the Glacier Express ($79, 3 hours door-to-door) is the highest-value half-day, and it leaves time for the Mount Roberts tram at the dock afterward. If you want to splurge on whale watching instead, take a confirmed early-morning trip; afternoon trips don't fit — and doing both the glacier and the whales doesn't fit either (7+ hours of touring against 6.5 ashore). Pick one.

**Then — Dawes Glacier in the afternoon and evening.**

The ship enters **Endicott Arm**, a 30-mile fjord south of Juneau, and approaches the face of **Dawes Glacier**. The Captain typically narrates entry to the fjord; naturalists may be on the PA system at the glacier face.

![Endicott Arm fjord with floating ice from the Dawes Glacier face](/ports/img/endicott-arm/endicott-arm-hero.webp)

**Approximate timing** (varies by sailing):
- ~3:00 PM: Ship enters Stephens Passage, turns into Endicott Arm.
- ~4:30–6:30 PM: Approach to Dawes Glacier face. Ship holds position 0.5–1.0 nautical miles off — the safety distance is set by ice-fall risk.
- ~7:00 PM: Reverse out of the fjord; cruising resumes overnight toward Icy Strait Point.

**Calving** (large pieces of ice breaking off and crashing into the sea) is unpredictable but common; the sound carries half a mile. Dress for cold — the fjord traps cold air, even in summer wind chills can drop into the 30s°F at the rail. The glacier face creates its own micro-climate: Endicott Arm typically runs 10–15°F colder than Juneau two hours earlier, which is why you'll feel underdressed at the rail even if you were comfortable downtown that morning.

**Best viewing locations on Bliss:**

- **Your own balcony** (warm coffee, your own pace) if you have one.
- **Observation Lounge** (Deck 15 forward) — heated, glass-roofed, the best of both worlds.
- **Sea View Bar** (Deck 17, where Kristie holds sailaway) — open-air, panoramic.
- **Deck 8 Waterfront** — open to the bow but cold and windy.

**Pack for the glacier afternoon:** thermal layer, fleece, waterproof shell, hat, gloves, scarf, **air-activated hand warmers** (1–2 pairs in your pockets — for static deck viewing they're the difference between two hours of comfort and two hours of suffering), hot coffee in a thermal mug, binoculars (rent on board if you don't have your own), camera or phone with battery topped up (cold drains them fast).

**Kristie's Cagney's Steakhouse 5:30 PM group dinner is RIGHT in the middle of the Dawes Glacier approach window.** Real choice tonight: hold the dinner reservation (Cagney's is a 90-minute commitment) and accept that you'll see less of the glacier face from the indoor dining room; OR step out of dinner mid-meal if the Captain announces calving activity; OR have a quick dinner elsewhere earlier and prioritize the glacier. Cagney's Deck 8 has SOME windows but isn't an Observation Lounge view. Discuss with Kristie if you want to suggest a later seating.

**Background reading for this two-stop day:** the [Juneau port page](/ports/juneau.html), its [logbook entries](/ports/juneau.html#logbook), and the [Endicott Arm scenic-cruising page](/ports/endicott-arm.html) — the [Endicott Arm gallery](/ports/endicott-arm.html#gallery) is the closest thing to a preview of what the Captain will narrate at the glacier face.

### Day 5 — Wednesday, July 15 — Icy Strait Point, Alaska {#day-icy-strait-point}

![Huna Tlingit cultural performance at Icy Strait Point — the contemporary expression of the village's pre-contact heritage](/ports/img/icy-strait-point/icy-strait-point-huna-totem.webp)

**Arrival:** 6:00 AM. **Depart:** 3:00 PM. Bliss docks — no tendering. Icy Strait Point has two berths: the original cannery-side **Adventure Landing** floating pier and **Wilderness Landing**, the deep-water dock the Huna Totem Corporation built in partnership with NCL specifically so mega-ships could come alongside. The free **Transporter Gondola** (a 4–5 minute enclosed ride, distinct from the paid SkyGlider) and walking paths connect Wilderness Landing to the cannery and beach areas.

**Icy Strait Point is a built-for-cruising port with a real village behind it.** A modest cruise destination on Chichagof Island, adjacent to the village of **Hoonah** (population ~750), the largest Tlingit settlement in Alaska. The cruise port was built around a restored 1912 salmon cannery, with the Huna Tlingit tribal corporation as the principal owner and operator. The **World's Largest ZipRider** (5,330 feet, ~1,300 ft vertical drop) is the signature Icy Strait Point excursion. Unlike Sitka or Juneau, this is a port purpose-built for cruise visits — no large downtown to wander.

**Free or low-cost, walkable:**

- **Cannery Museum** — restored 1912 cannery building (the big red Hoonah Packing Co. structure at the Cannery Dock); cultural and industrial history. Free.
- **Hoonah Village walk** — ~1.6-mile flat walk into the village along the waterfront road (~30 min); small museum, Russian Orthodox church, the Hoonah Indian Association heritage building.
- **Beach walks** — the gravel/cobble shoreline; quiet; eagle-watching common.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **World's Largest ZipRider** — $199 walk-up; the marketing name is "Largest" though it is also the longest single-drop zip. Three minutes of flight; once-in-a-lifetime. **Book via NCL pre-cruise or walk-up at the port** — this isn't a Viator product. Reserve early; the line is real on a Bliss-sized ship.
- **Whale watching** — Icy Strait is one of the best whale-watching waters in southeast Alaska; humpback feeding grounds. ~$150–180/person via large-operator Viator listings; small-boat 14-passenger options also exist and tend to be the stronger experience.
- **Chichagof Island Tour: Brown Bear Search** (~$163, ~3-hour van tour) — Chichagof has one of the highest brown-bear densities in the world. Tours go inland to bear-frequented salmon streams.
- **Tribal Dance & Cultural Legends** (~$25) at the Heritage Center Theater — Huna Tlingit performance. Sold direct at the port or via cruise line; not a Viator product.

**For first-timers in Icy Strait Point:** the ZipRider if you want the drop, or whale watching for the wildlife. The ship-built nature of the port means there's less "wander town" than at Sitka/Juneau — plan an excursion or settle in for a peaceful beach-and-cannery afternoon.

**Worth a look on the way in:** the [Icy Strait Point port page](/ports/icy-strait-point.html), its [Huna Tlingit gallery](/ports/icy-strait-point.html#gallery), and the [ISP logbook](/ports/icy-strait-point.html#logbook) — the heritage notes from other travelers give Hoonah's pre-contact history depth the cannery-museum brochure doesn't. Build your day in the [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html); log it in the [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) on the way back.

**Tonight is [Le Bistro](/restaurants/ncl/le-bistro.html) 5:45 PM** — the easiest of the three group specialty nights to make on time. ISP departs 3:00 PM; you have ~2:45 to shower, change, and walk to Deck 6. The pace after a brisk ISP day is a good fit for Le Bistro's slower French rhythm.

### Day 6 — Thursday, July 16 — Ketchikan / Ward Cove, Alaska {#day-ketchikan}

![Ketchikan waterfront in a Fisher Studio archive photograph — the town's salmon-canning heritage in sepia](/ports/img/ketchikan/ketchikan-1.webp)

**Arrival:** 6:00 AM (early). **Depart:** 1:15 PM (early). This is the shortest port day of the week — 7 hours dock-to-dock with the shuttle ride eating into both ends. Plan a single focused excursion, not two.

**Read this first about Ward Cove.** NCL holds a long-term preferential docking arrangement at **Ward Cove**, ~7 miles north of downtown Ketchikan. Norwegian Bliss berths there — **not** at the main downtown pier most cruise ships use. To reach downtown Creek Street, Saxman, or the Lumberjack Show, you'll take **NCL's complimentary shuttle**, which runs continuously every 15–20 minutes and drops at Berth 4 downtown (near Safeway, about a 15-minute walk to Creek Street). The one-way shuttle ride is 20–30 minutes; build margin for the all-aboard return.

**Ketchikan — the "salmon capital of the world" and the southernmost major town in Alaska.** Population ~8,200. The town clings to a narrow shelf between mountains and the Tongass Narrows. Wood-plank Creek Street (a stilted boardwalk over Ketchikan Creek) was the historical red-light district; today it's preserved as a shopping/walking area with a salmon-spawning creek running beneath. The world's largest collection of standing **totem poles** is split between Saxman Native Village (south of downtown) and Totem Bight State Historical Park (north — closer to Ward Cove).

**Plan on rain.** Ketchikan averages ~150 inches of rain a year — among the wettest cities in the United States, and three times what Juneau gets. On any given July afternoon, the boardwalks, dock approaches, and Creek Street planks are wet whether the sky is open or not. Waterproof shoes with grip matter more here than at any other port on the itinerary; the wet wood is treacherously slick. A hooded shell, not an umbrella — Ketchikan's funnel-wind off the Tongass Narrows turns umbrellas inside out fast.

**From Ward Cove (walkable or short-shuttle):**

- **Ward Cove venue** — by 2026, the **Great Alaskan Lumberjack Show** runs a Ward Cove venue in addition to its downtown location. If you'd rather skip the shuttle for the show, the Ward Cove venue is right there. $37/adult.

**Downtown (via NCL shuttle):**

- **Creek Street** — the iconic boardwalk; restaurants, shops, the historic Dolly's House Museum ($10, **cash only**). Salmon visible in the creek in season.
- **Totem Heritage Center** — short walk from the downtown drop-off; $7 entry (adult), $6 senior, free under 13. Nationally significant totem collection.
- **Lumberjack Show** ($37 adult / $18.50 child) — touristy and well-rated; right by the downtown dock if you want a downtown ticket.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Saxman Native Village** ($90–230 cruise-style packaged tours; standalone Cape Fox Dancers performance is cheaper if booked at the village directly) — Tlingit cultural village 2.5 miles south of downtown; totem park + carving studio + dance performance. The Viator product to look for is **"Saxman Native Village and Ketchikan Highlights"** (often bundled with the Lumberjack Show).
- **Totem Bight State Park** — totem park 10 miles north of downtown (closer to Ward Cove); less crowded than Saxman; quieter walking. **Park entry $5/person** May–Sep. Cab from downtown is **$60–75 one-way**; the Blue Line public bus runs Mon–Fri only for $1–2 if you want the budget alternative. From Ward Cove the cab is shorter but no specific fare is published.
- **Misty Fjords flightseeing** ($309–500) — small floatplane to the fjords inland. The Ketchikan splurge worth saving for. Strong direct operators: **Island Wings Air Service**, **Carlin Air**, **Misty Fjords Air**. Floatplane departures from downtown.

**For first-timers in Ketchikan (Ward Cove-docked):** allow ~45 min round-trip on the shuttle to reach downtown. A focused half-day is Creek Street + Totem Heritage Center + the Lumberjack Show. Misty Fjords flightseeing is the splurge if budget allows; plan tightly because the shuttle and the all-aboard call don't forgive late returns.

**Worth a look on the way in:** the [Ketchikan port page](/ports/ketchikan.html), its [photo gallery](/ports/ketchikan.html#gallery) (the sepia Fisher Studio panoramas are worth a slow scroll), and the [Ketchikan logbook](/ports/ketchikan.html#logbook) — other travelers' notes on Saxman vs. Totem Bight, Misty Fjords operators, and the Ward Cove shuttle reality. The [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html) earns its keep here given the tight Thu schedule; [log the day](/ports/ketchikan.html#logbook) when you're back on board.

**Thursday evening is open** — no group specialty dinner tonight. With the 1:15 PM ship-back call and a long afternoon at sea before Victoria, this is the week's most relaxed evening. The [Studio Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html), [Observation Lounge](/restaurants/ncl/observation-lounge.html), and [Sea View Bar](#) tend to fill with solo cruisers around sunset; the [Manhattan Room](/restaurants/ncl/the-manhattan-room.html) has live music most nights if you want a longer dinner on your own pace.

### Day 7 — Friday, July 17 — Victoria, British Columbia {#day-victoria}

![Victoria's Inner Harbour at night — Parliament Buildings illuminated](/ports/img/victoria-bc/victoria-bc-1.webp)

**Arrival:** 8:00 PM. **Depart:** 11:59 PM. **A short evening stop** — just under 4 hours dock-to-dock, of which 30 min each end is shuttle and security. Realistic shore time: 2.5 hours.

**Why Victoria is on every Seattle-Alaska itinerary.** The **Passenger Vessel Services Act of 1886** prohibits foreign-flag ships (Bliss is Bahamian-flagged, like most large cruise ships) from carrying passengers between two U.S. ports without calling at a foreign port in between. Seattle to Sitka, Juneau, Icy Strait Point, and Ketchikan is otherwise five U.S. ports in a row. Victoria is the nearest foreign port to Seattle, and the late-evening stop is the shortest, cheapest way for the line to satisfy the law on the way home. Every Seattle-Alaska sailing on every line makes this same call for this same reason — which is why the stop is short and why nobody's brochure explains it.

The ship docks at the **Ogden Point** breakwater, ~3 km from downtown. Free shuttle buses or a $10 round-trip cab/rideshare run between Ogden Point and the Inner Harbour.

**Victoria — the trip's late-night punctuation mark.** Provincial capital of British Columbia; a small city of Edwardian bones — Edwardian architecture, the Empress Hotel, the Royal BC Museum (closed by the time you arrive), the famous **Butchart Gardens** 30 minutes outside town. The 8 PM – midnight slot limits realistic options: this is a walk-the-Inner-Harbour-at-night stop, not a half-day excursion.

**Realistic 2.5-hour evening options:**

- **Inner Harbour walk** — the iconic Parliament Buildings are lit up at night and worth seeing; the Empress Hotel exterior; harbor walking path; totem poles at Thunderbird Park. Quietly the best use of a short Victoria stop.
- **Dinner at a downtown pub or bistro** — a real Canadian dinner ashore is a small treat to close the week.
- **Butchart Gardens with evening lighting** — gardens stay open until 10:00 PM Wed–Sun in summer (Jul 17 is a Friday, so they're open); lighting is theatrical. **CA$44.25 adult admission incl. tax** + transport ~$60 round-trip. Tight to fit in 2.5 hours including transit. Worth it only if you commit early and accept rushing.
- **Gray Line Evening CitySights (Hop on Hop off)** — **~$50 adult, 40-minute narrated evening loop** (not the 90-min daytime Hop-On version, which has a 5:30 PM last departure and won't run). Gray Line's evening summer 2026 schedule is being finalized — verify the specific July 17 departure at booking.

**The [Royal BC Museum](https://royalbcmuseum.bc.ca/) closes 5 PM daily** and is therefore not available on this 8 PM arrival. (Worth the trip on a different Victoria visit.)

**Currency:** Canadian dollars are local; USD widely accepted near the harbor; credit/debit cards universal — you don't need to exchange.

**For first-timers in Victoria:** this short stop is the trip's gentle decompression. A walk through Inner Harbour at night and a quiet dinner ashore covers it. Save Butchart Gardens for a longer Victoria-specific trip.

**Worth a look before you tender in:** the [Victoria BC port page](/ports/victoria-bc.html), its [evening-harbour gallery](/ports/victoria-bc.html#gallery), and the [Victoria logbook](/ports/victoria-bc.html#logbook) — what other Alaska-cruise travelers actually did with a short evening stop. If you've never used the [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) all week, this is the night to start — Victoria is the week's punctuation, worth saving a sentence about.

### Day 8 — Saturday, July 18 — Seattle (debarkation)

**The realistic timeline:**

- **6:00 – 8:00 AM:** Breakfast at Garden Café or Manhattan Room.
- **6:30 AM:** Bags must be in the hall the night before (NCL color-codes by tag).
- **7:00 – 9:30 AM:** Walk-off in waves by tag color. **Self-disembarkation** (carry all bags off; first off the ship around 7:00 AM) is the fastest if you have a flight to catch.
- **10:00 AM (latest):** All passengers off the ship.

**Tipping at the end of the cruise.** NCL's auto-gratuity is added daily ($20/day per person at most cabin categories; $25/day in Suites and Haven; verify). Additional cash tips for outstanding service: stateroom steward $30–50; dining-room waiter $20–40; group-dinner servers if a consistent person handled the three specialty nights.

**Port Valet — use it.** The Port of Seattle's free Port Valet service transfers your checked bag from the ship directly to your final-destination airline check-in. Walk off Bliss with only your carry-on; your bag meets you in baggage claim at home. Available for most major airlines on most departure days; sign-up sheet is distributed onboard mid-cruise (typically Day 4 or 5 — watch the *Freestyle Daily*). Free. The piece of Seattle post-cruise logistics that saves the most luggage friction.

**Killing time before an evening flight.** Pier 66 has a Crew Center bag-storage service: $6/bag/day, pickup deadline 3 PM. If you'd rather hand the bag off to your airline via Port Valet (above), do that — but Crew Center storage works if Port Valet didn't fit your routing.

Pier 66 is downtown — most of central Seattle is walkable from the terminal. With four to six hours free:

- **Pike Place Market** is a 10-minute walk from Pier 66 (uphill on the way back). Quietest before 9 AM, busy from 10. Coffee at the original Starbucks line is a tourist tax; the Crumpet Shop and Beecher's are better food.
- **Olympic Sculpture Park** is a 5-minute walk north from Pier 66 along the waterfront — free, 30-minute outdoor loop overlooking Elliott Bay.
- **Seattle Aquarium** is 5 minutes south along the waterfront ($40, ~90 min).
- **Seattle Art Museum** is 10 min on foot from Pier 66, downtown ($30, the Hammering Man sculpture is the landmark; the special exhibits are usually worth the ticket).
- **Space Needle / Chihuly Garden** is a 15-min walk or a 5-min Uber to Seattle Center ($35 each; a 90-min combined visit).
- **Pioneer Square** is a 15-min walk south — historic district, used-bookstores, the Underground Tour if a Bliss-adjacent group activity appeals.

**SEA without an Uber — Pier 66 is the pack where this actually works.** From Pier 66, walk 8 minutes to Westlake Station (or take the free downtown circulator) and catch the **Link light rail** directly to SEA: **$3.50, ~40 minutes, no transfer.** This is the cheapest, most reliable airport route from any Seattle cruise pier. Cost-vs-Uber math: Link saves $50–70. With bags, it's manageable on Link's roomy trains; the airport-direction trains run every 8 min. Alternative: Quick Shuttle / Capital Aeroporter shared shuttle from Pier 66, $20–30/person, slower than Link.

Door-to-door Uber/Lyft from Pier 66 to SEA: $40–60, 25–35 minutes — usable if you're traveling with more bags than Link comfortably handles or if your group splits the cost.

**Airport-arrival timing.** For a domestic flight after 5 PM, leave Pier 66 by 2 PM if on Link, by 2:30 PM if in an Uber. For an international flight after 7 PM, leave by 3 PM. SEA TSA lines run 30–60 min during Saturday afternoon cruise debark.

## Section 4 — Packing List (calibrated for Alaska, 7 nights, July) {#section-4}

This list is tuned for: **Alaska summer (July)** — generally 55–70°F daytime, 50–60°F evenings, frequent rain, occasional 75°F afternoons. **Five port days** mostly walking + outdoor activity. **One sea day with significant deck-observation time.** **NCL's smart-casual evening dress culture** (no formal nights; one optional "Norwegian's Night Out" is a slightly dressier evening but jacket not required).

If you remember nothing else: **the Alaska packing rule is layering.** A thermal, a fleece, a waterproof shell — you'll wear all three on the same morning some days.

### Documents and money

- Passport (preferred). Victoria BC on Day 7 is a foreign port; passport is the cleaner path. Birth certificate + state ID is technically accepted but a passport eliminates Day-1 check-in friction.
- NCL booking confirmation and check-in QR (printed and saved on phone).
- Kristie's group contact card / printed schedule (she's distributing at sailaway).
- Credit card you trust (notify the issuer of travel; international fees waived if available).
- ~$200 USD cash in $5/$10/$20 bills for tips, excursion guides, taxis, small purchases. **No CAD needed** for Victoria — USD is accepted.

### Carry-on (must arrive on the ship with you)

- All medications + 3-day buffer (NEVER in checked bags).
- Daily / scheduled / controlled-substance prescriptions in original labeled bottles.
- Swimsuit (the heated Aqua Park pools open before bags arrive).
- Phone, charger, backup battery.
- Reading material for the pre-cabin window.
- Small refillable water bottle.
- Travel documents.

### Outerwear (the layering system)

- **Base layer:** thermal long-sleeve top + thermal long underwear (for cold mornings on deck — synthetic or merino, not cotton).
- **Mid layer:** fleece pullover or down sweater. Worn most port days.
- **Outer shell:** waterproof and windproof rain jacket. Not a fashion jacket — a real shell. **The most important single item.**
- Hat (warm beanie, not just a baseball cap).
- Light gloves.
- Scarf or buff for cold mornings on Observation Lounge / Sea View Bar.

### For the port days

- **Sturdy walking shoes** — broken in. The Sitka totem trail, Saxman Native Village, Mount Roberts Tramway top trails all involve walking distance.
- **Daypack** (hiking-style, ~15–20L).
- **Binoculars** (8x42 or 10x42) for whale-watching and wildlife.
- **Sun hat / cap** (rare clear day at altitude burns fast).
- **Sunglasses** (polarized for water glare).
- **Sunscreen** (SPF 30+; reef-safe if going on whale-watching tours).

### For the ship — daytime

- 7 daily outfits — comfortable casual; jeans or cargo pants are fine.
- 5–6 t-shirts or long-sleeve casual tops.
- Lightweight long-sleeve shirts for sun protection.
- Comfortable walking shoes (in addition to your port-day pair).
- Sandals or flip-flops for the Aqua Park.

## Section 7 — Seasickness on an Alaska Cruise {#section-7}

Alaska is calmer than people fear — but this specific route has more open ocean than the phrase "Inside Passage" suggests, and it's worth knowing where. **Sitka faces the open Pacific**, so the Seattle-to-Sitka leg (Day 1 evening through Day 2 and overnight into Monday) runs up the ocean side of Vancouver Island — that's the most exposed stretch of the week. The middle of the itinerary (Sitka → Juneau → Endicott Arm → Icy Strait Point → Ketchikan) threads properly protected inside waters. Then the **Ketchikan-to-Victoria leg** (Day 6 afternoon through Day 7 evening) returns to the open Pacific before entering the **Strait of Juan de Fuca** for the Victoria approach.

The calibration: if you've been seasick on a Caribbean cruise, you'll most likely still be fine here — July Pacific swell off this coast is usually long-period and gentle, and the ship is large enough (168,028 GT) that moderate water is filtered substantially. If you've never been seasick anywhere, you almost certainly won't start on this itinerary. But the two ocean legs are real ocean; pack the meclizine rather than betting the week on "Inside Passage" branding.

### What to bring

- **Bonine** (meclizine) or **Dramamine Less Drowsy** — over-the-counter, take 1 hour before expected motion.
- **Sea-Bands** — pressure-point wristbands.
- **Ginger candies or chews** — backup for mild queasiness.

### What helps once you feel it

- **Get on deck.** The Observation Lounge has horizon access in any weather.
- **Eat something dry.** Crackers, dry toast, pretzels.
- **Avoid alcohol** (alcohol exacerbates motion sickness).
- **Take meclizine** at the first hint, not after it's full-on.

If you're motion-sensitive and booked a Studio cabin (interior, no window), spend daylight hours on the Observation Lounge or Sea View Bar — horizon access fixes the problem.

### Safety in the Alaska ports

Alaska's cruise-port towns are among the safest stops in any cruise itinerary. Crime rates are low; "tourism crime" is mostly opportunistic theft, easily avoided. The bigger risks are weather (slip-and-fall on wet boardwalks; getting cold and wet) and wildlife (don't approach bears; if a bear is on a Ketchikan trail, walk slowly the other way; never run). ### Solo safety — cabin and onboard

Solo cruising is overwhelmingly safe — cruise ships are among the most surveilled travel environments anywhere, with CCTV in common areas, 24-hour security, controlled deck access, and crew trained in emergency response. The Cruise Vessel Security and Safety Act (federal U.S. law since 2010) mandates specific protocols including camera coverage and reporting obligations. None of that makes risk zero, but the baseline is high. A few habits still make sense:

**Your cabin door.** The standard NCL cabin door has both a deadbolt and a privacy latch (a small swing-arm that keeps the door from opening even with a master key). Use both at night. Some solo cruisers add a portable door wedge — under $10, takes no space, and for travelers who sleep uneasily in unfamiliar rooms it's calming beyond any actual security gain.

**Share your itinerary before sailing.** Send the day-by-day to one trusted person ashore, with your cabin number once assigned and your travel insurance policy number. The family handoff card in [Section 6](#section-6) is built for exactly this — including the State Department's 24/7 line, which can locate the ship and reach NCL through official channels at no cost to your family.

**Friendliness is not obligation.** A good conversation at trivia is not an invitation to dinner; a shared dinner is not an invitation to a port day; a port day is not an invitation to anything else. You set the pace, and on a hosted solo cruise specifically, declining doesn't fracture anything — see the group-dynamics notes in [Section 9](#section-9).

**If something feels off,** Guest Services and the 24-hour security desk respond fast — for an intrusive fellow passenger, an overstepping crew member, anything. Most solo cruisers go a lifetime without needing this; the option exists if you do.

Solo cruisers often find this cheerfully ridiculous — which is the point. If you want to participate, see [the cruise duck tradition article](/articles/cruise-duck-tradition.html).

## Section 11 — First Cruise Notes {#section-11}

If this is your first cruise of any kind (not just first NCL or first Alaska), a few things that won't be obvious:

**The ship is a small city.** 4,000+ passengers, 1,700 crew, 20 decks. You won't see all of it. Make peace with this.

**Embarkation is logistical, not magical.** Day 1 is paperwork, lines, and finding things. The good part starts on Day 2.

**The buffet (Garden Café) is not the best food on the ship.** It's fine. The Manhattan Room dinner is the better meal. Eat there at least 2 of the 4 nights you're not doing group specialty dining.

**You're going to forget your keycard the first day.** Everyone does. Get a lanyard.

**The first sea day, you'll feel disoriented.** Normal. Pick 2 things from the daily program, do them, sit down, stare at water. You'll find the rhythm.

**The crew remembers you.** Your stateroom steward, the bartender at Sea View Bar — they learn your name, your drink preferences, your allergies. The relationship is real. It's worth being kind, and worth tipping in cash above the auto-gratuity if service warrants it.

**NCL is more casual than the other major US lines.** No formal nights. The Freestyle Dining model means you set your own schedule.

**The Observation Lounge is where you'll spend your best Alaska hours.** Make peace with this too. Coffee, a book, the Inside Passage moving past.

**You'll spend more on excursions than you think.** Build a shore-day budget before sailing. The [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html) helps prevent the surprise.

**You'll come home wanting to go back.** Most first-time Alaska cruisers do another within 2 years. Plan accordingly.

For more first-cruise context across cruise lines, see the broader [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html).

## Closing {#closing}

A hosted solo cruise on a Breakaway Plus ship in Alaska in July is a particular kind of week: long daylight, the Inside Passage drifting past your coffee, the company of fellow solos when you want it, and the quiet of your Studio cabin or the Observation Lounge when you don't. Kristie's six anchor moments give the week shape; everything else, you build.

A few things worth remembering as your sailing date approaches:

- **Show up at Sea View Bar at 4 PM Saturday for sailaway.** That's where the group becomes the group, and where Kristie hands you the week's printed info.
- **Three specialty dinners are pre-booked** — Sun Teppanyaki, Tue Cagney's, Wed Le Bistro. A Specialty Dining Package (3 meals) prepaid in the Cruise Planner covers them and costs less than paying individually.
- **The Studio Lounge** is your daily social anchor outside the group meetups. Hosted hours, free coffee, fellow solos.
- **The Observation Lounge** is where Alaska happens. Coffee at sunrise on a sea day is the trip's quietest joy.
- **Layers matter more than the right brand.** A $30 fleece, a $50 rain shell, a $20 thermal — you'll wear them all.
- **The crew makes the trip.** Tip in cash, in envelopes, at the end.

If you want a one-line preflight reminder: **the goal is calm.** Pack early. Sleep before you fly. Get to Pier 66 with margin Saturday morning, drop the bags, walk Pike Place, and come back for sailaway with Kristie at Sea View at 4.

Soli Deo Gloria.
