## Welcome

This pack exists to make one specific cruise easier to plan: a 4-night Bahamas round-trip from PortMiami on **MSC Seaside**, calling at **Nassau** and **Ocean Cay MSC Marine Reserve**. It pulls together the ship-specific tips, port-specific advice, packing notes calibrated for a short Bahamas hop, and budget math you'd otherwise have to assemble from a dozen browser tabs.

It is calm by design. There is no urgency, no upsell, no countdown. You can read it once and put it away, or you can use it as a working document between booking and embarkation.

The site that produced this pack is independent — no cruise-line affiliations, no shore-excursion commissions, no display ads. Your purchase is the funding model. We do this so you can plan a short Bahamas cruise from a source that has nothing to gain by talking you into a longer excursion or a more expensive cabin.

**Includes an offline-capable Emergency Contacts section** — a fillable family handoff card, cruise-line and consulate numbers tailored to this itinerary, available offline after first load via [In the Wake](/index.html)'s PWA. Designed to share with one trusted person ashore before sailing.

**This pack is written for first-time MSC cruisers and short-trip cruisers.** MSC Cruises is European-built and Italian-flavored, and the on-board rhythm differs from the major US lines (Royal Caribbean, Carnival, Norwegian) in small but real ways — the cabin "Experience" tiers, the multi-language announcements, the service-charge model, the food culture. If you've sailed a US line before and are wondering what's different, this pack flags it. If this is your very first cruise of any kind, the [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html) is the right read alongside this pack.

## Section 1 — Your Ship: MSC Seaside

### The lead Seaside-class ship, year-round Miami-based since 2017

[MSC Seaside](/ships/msc/msc-seaside.html) was delivered by **Fincantieri at Monfalcone, Italy** on 29 November 2017 and christened three weeks later at PortMiami Terminal B by **Sophia Loren** — MSC Cruises' first ship christening ever held in North America, with host Mario Lopez and live performances from Andrea Bocelli (with the Voices of Haiti children's choir) and Ricky Martin. She entered service on a 14-night inaugural cruise from Miami on 23 December 2017 and has been the principal MSC ship at PortMiami ever since, with a short Mediterranean season each spring before returning for the long Caribbean season. By your May 2026 sailing she's nearly nine years old — her crew rhythms long settled, her quirks well-known. For broader context on MSC's Caribbean positioning relative to Royal and the other lines, [Caribbean Cruise Trends 2026](/articles/caribbean-cruise-trends-2026.html) covers the deployment shifts.

She's the **lead ship of the original Seaside Class** — a two-ship pair (with MSC Seaview, 2018). The newer MSC Seashore (2021) and MSC Seascape (2022) belong to the "Seaside EVO" sub-class — bigger ships at 170,412 GT, similar aesthetic but not Seaside's direct sisters in the strict sense. If you've sailed Seaside before, Seaview is the closest match; the EVO pair are larger, busier ships.

Worth opening on your phone before you sail: [video walkthroughs](/ships/msc/msc-seaside.html#video-highlights) of her public spaces, [logbook entries](/ships/msc/msc-seaside.html#logbook) from travelers who've sailed her, and the [deck plans](/ships/msc/msc-seaside.html#deck-plans) so the first-day's wayfinding is less guesswork.

### What's distinctive about a Seaside-class ship

The design brief was **"Miami Beach condo at sea"** — and you can feel it. The signature features:

- **Waterfront Boardwalk** (Deck 8) — an outdoor wrap-around promenade that hugs the hull at sea level, with bars and quick-service restaurants opening onto it. Most ships put their promenade indoors or on a higher deck; Seaside's is right at the water and is the single most-photographed space on board.
- **Bridge of Sighs** (Deck 16) — a glass-floored walkway above the Waterfront Boardwalk. Not for the height-shy; the floor is fully transparent and you're looking straight down at the sea passing below.
- **Twin 120-metre ziplines** (Deck 19) — running the length of the upper deck, side by side, so two riders can race. Free; first-come, first-served; lines build by noon on sea days.
- **Forest Aquaventure Park** (Deck 18 aft) — a multi-slide waterpark, including the Slideboarding interactive race slide.
- **Jungle Pool Lounge** — a pool with a retractable glass-roof sky dome; the only weather-contingency pool on board, useful when an afternoon Bahamas squall passes through.
- **MSC Yacht Club** — a full **ship-within-a-ship** enclave (decks 16, 18, 19) with 86 suites, butler service, the two-story [Top Sail Lounge](/restaurants/msc/top-sail-lounge.html), the private [MSC Yacht Club Restaurant](/restaurants/msc/yacht-club-restaurant.html), and the 1,600 m² MSC Yacht Club Pool. If you booked a Yacht Club suite, your week is fundamentally different from the rest of the ship.

What's NOT on Seaside: she's not an Oasis-class scale ship. No Boardwalk-and-Central-Park split deck; no ice rink; no Broadway-licensed productions. The Metropolitan Theater (944 seats) runs MSC's signature variety-show production — closer to a Las Vegas revue than a Broadway musical. That's the right comparison to set expectations.

### MSC's "Experience" categories — read this before you sail

**Before you commit, run the [stateroom sanity check](/articles/stateroom-sanity-check.html)** — a four-question filter that catches the bookings most likely to leave a cruiser unhappy. MSC's Bella tier in particular ("cabin assigned at sailing") is where the filter earns its keep, since you can't pick a specific room and the cabin-lottery downside is real on a 4-night cruise where there's no time to absorb a bad assignment.

MSC bundles cabins into **Experience tiers** rather than just cabin types. Your booking class determines not only your room but also a package of inclusions — drinks, Wi-Fi, dining, and access to specific areas. The four standard tiers (plus Yacht Club at the top) are:

- **Bella** — entry-level. Cabin assigned at sailing (you don't pick a specific room), no perks bundled. Cheapest fare.
- **Fantastica** — mid-tier. You choose your specific cabin; includes 24-hour room service, priority embarkation, free children's package.
- **Aurea** — premium. Adds the included drinks package, MSC Aurea Spa thermal area access, premium dining time choice, and reserved sun-deck space.
- **Wellness** — niche tier focused on the spa; includes thermal area + treatments.
- **Yacht Club** — the suite enclave (separate experience; covered above).

**Decision rule:** for a 4-night MSC sailing, Fantastica is the practical sweet spot for most cruisers. Bella saves money but the cabin lottery is a real risk on a short trip (no time to absorb a bad assignment); Aurea is good value if you'd buy a drinks package anyway. Run your specific numbers through the [drink-calculator](/drink-calculator.html) before deciding — at MSC's 4-night drink-package pricing, the Aurea premium often pencils out only if both adults drink alcohol regularly.

The [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html) helps put real numbers on the comparison. For ship size and class context, the [Ship Size Atlas](/tools/ship-size-atlas.html) lets you compare Seaside to other ships you may have sailed. You can also follow her live position throughout your cruise via the [Ship Logbook](/tools/ship-tracker.html) — a small comfort if family ashore wants to know where you are.

### Dining

Seaside's dining structure follows MSC's standard pattern:

- **Two main dining rooms** — [**Ipanema Restaurant**](/restaurants/msc/ipanema-restaurant.html) (Deck 6, Copacabana area) and [**Seashore Restaurant**](/restaurants/msc/seashore-restaurant.html) (Deck 5, Cape Cod area). Both included; both serve the same menu. Your dining time and venue are assigned at booking but flexible at Guest Services. Seashore is also the breakfast and brunch MDR.
- **Two buffets** — [**Marketplace Buffet**](/restaurants/msc/marketplace-buffet.html) (Deck 8, Ocean Cay area) and [**Biscayne Bay Restaurant & Buffet**](/restaurants/msc/biscayne-bay-buffet.html) (Deck 16, Miami Beach area). Long hours; the upper-deck Biscayne Bay buffet has the better view and the best made-to-order pizza on the ship.
- **Specialty restaurants (paid extra):**
  - [**Asian Market Kitchen by Roy Yamaguchi**](/restaurants/msc/asian-market-kitchen.html) — a three-concept space: Pan-Asian, sushi, and teppanyaki under one chef. (This is the Seaside/Seaview-only Yamaguchi collaboration; newer MSC ships rebrand the equivalent venues as **Kaito**, MSC's in-house Asian dining program — different chef, different menu.)
  - [**Butcher's Cut**](/restaurants/msc/butchers-cut.html) — American steakhouse.
  - [**Ocean Cay**](/restaurants/msc/ocean-cay.html) — seafood restaurant (yes, named after the private island; not located on it).
- [**Venchi 1878**](/restaurants/msc/venchi-1878.html) — Italian chocolate and gelato. Two outlets: Chocolate Bar (Deck 6) and Gelato & Creperie (Deck 16). Cash bar; not included.
- **MSC Yacht Club guests:** the private [**Top Sail Lounge**](/restaurants/msc/top-sail-lounge.html) (Deck 16, two-story) and [**MSC Yacht Club Restaurant**](/restaurants/msc/yacht-club-restaurant.html) (Deck 18, balcony above the lounge) are open-seating, all included.

The included Main Dining Room food on MSC is real Italian — pasta courses, proper bread, competent service. For a 4-night cruise, most travelers can skip specialty dining entirely and not feel deprived. If you're a steak-and-bourbon traveler, **one** night at Butcher's Cut is a reasonable splurge; Asian Market Kitchen rewards travelers who want a longer evening's entertainment built around food.

### Drinks

MSC's drink-package math is more conservative than Royal's or Norwegian's. The Easy Plus Drinks Package (mid-tier alcohol included) commonly runs $50–60 per person per day. **At those numbers, the package pays off if you drink 4+ alcoholic drinks per day or actively use the included specialty coffees, mocktails, and bottled water.** For a 4-night cruise without a heavy drinker, pay-as-you-go is often cheaper. The [drink-calculator](/drink-calculator.html) lets you put in your real consumption pattern and see the breakeven. The companion article [Is the drink package worth it?](/articles/is-drink-package-worth-it.html) walks through the breakeven math with worked examples — short cruises like this 4-night give you very few days to recover the upfront cost.

If you booked the **Aurea Experience**, your drinks package is already included — don't double-buy.

### MSC Voyagers Club — status match before you sail

If you've sailed Royal Caribbean (Crown & Anchor), Norwegian (Latitudes), Princess (Captain's Circle), Carnival (VIFP), Holland America (Mariner), or several other lines, MSC's **Voyagers Club status match** can elevate your MSC tier before this sailing. Submit a screenshot of your existing line's status page to MSC's status-match form 1–2 weeks before sailing. It's free, takes ~48 hours to approve, and the resulting tier gets you on-board discounts, drink coupons, and (at higher tiers) free Wi-Fi minutes. Easy money for a first-time MSC cruiser with prior cruise history.

## Section 2 — Day by Day

A representative day-by-day rhythm. Verify your exact dates and port order in the MSC for Me app once your booking is final. **MSC's 4-night Bahamas itineraries swap Nassau and Ocean Cay order between sailings**; the pack covers both ports in their typical depth so you can match to your actual schedule.

### Day 1 — Monday — PortMiami (embarkation day)

![Miami harbor at sunset — the city sees more cruise embarkations than any other port in the world](/ports/img/miami/miami-harbor.webp)

**Arrival:** MSC Seaside sails from **PortMiami** on Dodge Island, ~10 minutes from downtown Miami and ~20 minutes from Miami International (MIA) by Uber/Lyft. The MSC terminal is typically **Terminal AA** or **Terminal F** depending on the season — verify your assignment from your booking documents 2–3 weeks before sailing. Embark window typically 11:30 AM – 3:30 PM; ship departs ~5:00 PM.

**Parking at PortMiami — read this before you drive.** The on-terminal garage at **Terminal AA runs $35/day** (firsthand May 2026 — verify at booking; ~$140 for a 4-night cruise) and is heavily congested during Monday and Friday turnaround windows; the queue for the entry kiosk routinely backs up onto the port access road. The cheaper and calmer move is to **park offsite and ride in**: lots near Miami International (Park 'N Fly, ParkSleepFly, AAA-affiliated lots, and several independent operators) offer pre-booked rates of $7–14/day with a shuttle to PortMiami, and an Uber or Lyft from any Miami hotel or offsite lot drops you at the terminal curb in 10 minutes. Reserve offsite parking in advance if you can — same-day rates run notably higher than the pre-book rate.

**Pre-cruise overnight strongly recommended.** Same-day flights to a 3:30 PM all-aboard call are risky — MIA delays are common on summer afternoons. A Sunday-night stay in Miami Beach, Brickell, or Downtown gives you margin and lets you use your Sunday afternoon at Wynwood (street art), South Beach (Art Deco walking tour), or Little Havana (Calle Ocho, Cuban coffee at Versailles, domino park).

**Pre-cruise reading:** the [Miami port page](/ports/miami.html) covers the broader picture; the [Miami logbook](/ports/miami.html#logbook) collects what other cruisers thought of their pre-cruise Sunday and embarkation morning.

**The realistic Day 1 timeline:**

- **11:30 AM – 1:00 PM:** First-wave embarkation at the terminal. Drop bags with porter at curb (tip $1–2/bag in cash). Proceed through security and MSC check-in. MSC's check-in is generally smooth in Miami; expect 30–45 minutes during the lunchtime rush.
- **1:00 – 2:00 PM:** Cabins typically NOT ready until 1 PM. Carry-on with you to the **Marketplace Buffet** (Deck 8) or the **Biscayne Bay Buffet** (Deck 16) for a low-key embarkation lunch.
- **1:00 PM:** Cabins open. Find your cabin. Introduce yourself to your stateroom steward — they remember names, and the relationship makes the week.
- **2:30 – 4:00 PM:** Light exploration. Don't try to see everything. Find your nearest elevator bank, your closest bar, and the **Waterfront Boardwalk** (deck 8 outdoor wrap-around) — you'll be back.
- **3:30 PM:** Mandatory muster drill — MSC uses an in-person muster check-in at your assigned station rather than a video-on-app approach. Don't skip; the captain won't sail until 100% completion.
- **5:00 PM:** Sailaway. Best viewing from the Waterfront Boardwalk on the side of the ship facing the channel, or from your balcony. The departure past Star Island, Fisher Island, and out into Government Cut is a Miami signature view — superyachts on one side, downtown towers on the other.
- **6:00 PM or 8:30 PM:** Dinner. Main dining room or your specialty reservation. Italian wine and an actual antipasti course are part of the MSC dining experience; arrive hungry.

**What to pack in your carry-on for Day 1:** swimsuit (the pools open before bags arrive), a light layer (Miami evenings can be 70°F even in May), all medications, change of clothes, passport (US citizens on this closed-loop Bahamas itinerary can sail on a birth certificate + state ID, but a passport is strongly preferred), phone charger, refillable water bottle, any embarkation paperwork. Your checked bags may not arrive until 7–9 PM.

**Money/card:** Your MSC cruise card is your ID, room key, and on-board charge. You'll receive it at check-in. **MSC's on-board currency is US Dollar** on Caribbean sailings out of Miami — no exchange rate surprises.

**The Day 1 mistake to avoid:** Don't sign up for the Drinks Package mid-sailaway unless you've already done the [drink-calculator](/drink-calculator.html) math. Decide before you board. Mid-sail upgrades are pricier per day than the pre-cruise rate.

### Day 2 / Day 3 — Nassau, Bahamas

![Welcome to Nassau gateway arch at the cruise port — your first sight on stepping off the ship](/ports/img/nassau/welcome-gateway.webp)

**Arrival:** Approximately 8:00 AM. Depart 5:00 – 6:00 PM. MSC Seaside docks at **Prince George Wharf** in downtown Nassau — a 2-minute walk to the **Festival Place** terminal, and a 5-minute walk to Bay Street (the main shopping/restaurant artery). No tender required.

**What Nassau actually is.** Capital of the Bahamas, a former British colonial city built around a deep natural harbor. The downtown is small, walkable, and dense with cruise tourism — at peak season three or four large ships are in port simultaneously, and the Bay Street strip absorbs 12,000+ day visitors. The historical depth is real (loyalist settlement, piracy era, Royal Bahamas Police Force band still in colonial dress) but the storefronts immediately around the cruise terminal lean heavily commercial. The good Nassau is a 10-minute walk from the terminal in most directions; the touristy Nassau is the first 200 yards.

**Free, walkable from the cruise terminal:**

- **Bay Street** — colonial-era main street; mix of straw markets, jewelry shops, and a few historic buildings (Christ Church Cathedral, Senate Square, Parliament Square with statues of Queen Victoria).
- **Queen's Staircase** — 66 limestone steps carved by enslaved Africans in the late 18th century to connect downtown to Fort Fincastle on the hill above. A short uphill walk; emotionally weighted; among the most affecting free sights in Nassau.
- **Fort Fincastle** ($3 entry) — small fort at the top of the staircase; views of the harbor and the cruise ships from above.
- **The Pompey Museum** at Vendue House — small museum on the Bahamian abolition movement and African heritage. Easy to miss; quietly excellent.
- **John Watling's Distillery** — a 1789 colonial estate at Buena Vista, ~10-minute walk uphill from the harbor. Free distillery tour, free rum tastings (one per visitor), photogenic grounds. The standout of the walking circuit.

**Paid options (decide ahead):**

- **Atlantis Resort day pass** ($100–195/person depending on package) — Aquaventure waterpark, aquarium walks, and beach access. Family-targeted; high cost. Atlantis is on Paradise Island, ~15 minutes by taxi over the bridge ($4/person each way, cash).
- **Pirates of Nassau Museum** ($14 adults / $7 kids) — touristy but a hit with kids; covers the Nassau pirate era (Blackbeard, Calico Jack, Anne Bonny).
- **Junkanoo Beach** — closest free public beach, 10-minute walk west of the terminal. Watch your belongings; bring your own snacks and water; vendors are aggressive but ignorable.
- **Cabbage Beach on Paradise Island** — better beach than Junkanoo; reachable by taxi over the bridge or by ferry from the Festival Place terminal. ~$10/person round-trip; the ferry runs every 30 minutes during cruise hours.
- **Independent excursions** (snorkel, sailboat tour, swimming with pigs day trips to Exuma) — generally $80–150/person from local operators just outside the terminal; book through the operator directly rather than the ship for 30–40% savings.

**For first-timers:** if you want one paid Nassau experience, it's either the **Atlantis day pass** (if you're traveling with kids or it's the only Atlantis you'll ever see) or a **half-day snorkel/sailboat trip** (if you're not committed to a resort day). For a quieter day, the John Watling's Distillery + Bay Street walking circuit + Queen's Staircase is free, real, and finishes by lunch.

**Worth a look on the way in:** the [Nassau port page](/ports/nassau.html), its [photo gallery](/ports/nassau.html#gallery), and the [Nassau logbook](/ports/nassau.html#logbook) — what other travelers actually did with their day, which excursions held up, which didn't. Build your day in the [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html); log it in the [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) on the way back.

**For accessibility:** Bay Street has sidewalks but they're narrow and the curbs are sometimes uneven. Queen's Staircase is stairs — not accessible. Cabbage Beach and Junkanoo Beach are accessible by road but the beach surface is sand. The Festival Place terminal itself is fully accessible. Taxis at the rank are not consistently wheelchair-accessible; arrange in advance with Guest Services if mobility is a concern.

**Pack for Nassau:** sun-protection (Bahamas sun in May is intense; SPF 30+ minimum), refillable water bottle, walking shoes, ~$60–100 USD cash for taxis and small purchases. **The Bahamian dollar (BSD) trades 1:1 with the US Dollar; USD is accepted everywhere.** Don't bother exchanging money.

### Day 2 / Day 3 — Ocean Cay MSC Marine Reserve

*(The day's defining visual is the lighthouse + ship view from this pack's cover — the marine reserve's land-and-sea aesthetic was designed around exactly that skyline.)*

**Arrival:** Approximately 9:00 AM. **Late departure 9:00 – 11:00 PM** (Ocean Cay days routinely run long; the late-night sunset lighthouse lighting is a signature MSC moment). On some 4-night sailings, the ship returns to Ocean Cay for an **overnight stay** with a Thursday-morning second day — verify your specific itinerary.

**What Ocean Cay actually is.** Ocean Cay MSC Marine Reserve opened **December 5, 2019** on a former industrial sand-dredging site in the Bimini chain, ~65 nautical miles from Miami. MSC's remediation effort converted the spoil islands into a 95-acre marine reserve with eight beaches, a marine conservation centre, the red-and-white striped lighthouse, and a single ship's worth of guests at a time (no second cruise ship shares the island). The before-and-after story is real: this was a barren industrial site fifteen years ago, and the coral, mangrove, and seagrass restoration work that MSC funded as part of the development is documented in MSC's own conservation reporting. As private islands go, Ocean Cay is more environmentally serious than most.

**The day's geography:**

The island runs roughly **north–south**, about 1.7 km long. The ship docks on the **east side**; the beaches face all four compass directions. Free **island shuttles** (open-air trams) loop the island every ~10 minutes during operating hours. Walking distances are short — even the farthest beach is a 20-minute walk from the ship.

The **eight beaches** (north to south, approximately):

- **North Beach** — quietest; long, open. Adults-only ambience by default but not enforced.
- **Marine Reserve Beach** — close to the conservation centre; smaller, calmer; good for snorkeling lessons.
- **Family Reef Beach** — calm water, beach toys, shaded family loungers. Where families gather.
- **South Beach** — long sandy stretch; the busiest beach; live music; the main BBQ buffet area.
- **Lighthouse Bay** — at the south tip, around the lighthouse; postcard photos.
- **Bimini Cay Beach** — smaller pocket; quieter; west-facing for sunset.
- **Wave Beach** — modest surf for the Bahamas (still gentle by ocean standards); body-surfing friendly.
- **Seakeepers Beach** — easternmost, ship-side; closest to the dock.

**What's included (free):**

- Beach access, all eight beaches
- Loungers (first-come, first-served) and beach umbrellas
- Open-air tram shuttle
- BBQ buffet lunch (typical Bahamian-American: jerk chicken, ribs, salads, conch fritters, key lime pie)
- Bottled water, lemonade, iced tea at the buffet
- Snorkel gear lending at the Marine Reserve Beach centre (snorkel + mask + fins; deposit on cruise card)
- Live steel drum and Bahamian music at South Beach
- The **Sunset Lighthouse Lighting ceremony** at the south point — choreographed light show on the lighthouse with music, around 7:30 – 8:00 PM. Free, no reservation, signature MSC moment.

**Paid options:**

- **Cabana rentals** ($300–700 for the day depending on beach and size — Lighthouse Cabanas at the top end). Includes a shaded private space, fresh towels, premium snacks delivery, a fruit plate, and bottled water service. Book through the MSC for Me app, **before boarding** — they sell out for sea-day favorite sailings.
- **Snorkel excursions** ($60–90/person) — guided, glass-bottom-boat-style tours of the marine reserve. Worth it for first-time snorkelers in the Bahamas; the marine reserve protection means more fish and coral than the average Caribbean stop.
- **Beach BBQ specialty packages** (specialty drinks, premium cocktails) — pay-as-you-go at the beach bars.
- **Wedding ceremonies** — Ocean Cay is a popular small-wedding location; ask Guest Services if you're curious about renewing vows.

**For first-timers:** if this is your first Ocean Cay day, **stay for the lighthouse lighting**. The ship sails late specifically so you can. Grab dinner at the included BBQ buffet around 6:00 PM, walk south to the lighthouse around 7:00 PM, watch the ceremony, then take a tram back to the ship. The whole day from breakfast (on board) to lighthouse-walk-back can be done without ever paying for anything beyond your cruise fare.

**For accessibility:** the island is **mostly flat** but the substrate is **sand** in beach areas — beach wheelchairs are available, free, from the Guest Services / Beach Concierge near the dock; reserve them in advance via the MSC for Me app if you can. Trams are wheelchair-accessible. The lighthouse pathway has a small slope but is paved.

**Pack for Ocean Cay:** swimsuit on under your clothes (cabins are far from the gangway by mid-day), sun-protection (sunscreen SPF 30+; reef-safe is **required** on the marine reserve), a wide-brim hat or cap, sunglasses, sandals or flip-flops, a refillable water bottle (fountains around the island), a beach towel from your cabin (the steward replaces them daily — bring one ashore), and a small daypack. There is **no Wi-Fi** on Ocean Cay; the ship's Wi-Fi doesn't reach reliably onto the beaches. Plan to be unreachable, which is the point.

### The "extra day" — Sea day or Ocean Cay return

On some 4-night sailings, the schedule includes a **sea day at sea** between the two port days; on others, the ship returns to **Ocean Cay for a second day** (sometimes with an overnight stay between). Verify your sailing's specific shape.

**If your day is a sea day:**

- The Waterfront Boardwalk is the best place to spend it. Coffee, a book, the slow water.
- The Solarium / quiet adult areas open earliest.
- Forest Aquaventure waterpark hits peak crowd around 11:00 AM – 2:00 PM.
- The Metropolitan Theater's headliner show is typically Wednesday or Thursday on a Mon-Fri sailing.
- The Twin Ziplines have shorter lines first thing (open at 9 AM).
- The Bridge of Sighs is best photographed mid-morning when sunlight hits the glass-floor square.

**If your day is a second Ocean Cay day:** treat it like Day 2/3 — but you now know the geography, so go to the beach you didn't see the first time. North Beach if you went to South Beach Day 2; the marine reserve snorkel lessons if you missed them.

### Day 5 — Friday — PortMiami (debarkation)

**The realistic timeline:**

- **6:00 – 8:00 AM:** Breakfast at the Marketplace Buffet or Biscayne Bay Buffet. Both run early on debarkation morning.
- **6:30 AM:** Bags must be in the hall the night before (MSC color-codes by tag).
- **8:00 – 10:00 AM:** Walk-off in waves by tag color. **Self-disembarkation** (you carry all bags off; first off the ship, around 7:00 – 7:30 AM) is the fastest if you have a flight to catch.
- **10:00 AM (latest):** All passengers off the ship.

**If you're catching a flight:**

- Uber/Lyft from PortMiami to MIA: ~$30–45, 20–30 minutes
- The drive is short; if your flight is mid-afternoon, you have time for a Cuban coffee at a Brickell café before heading to the airport.
- Carnival-port traffic on Fridays can stack at the terminal exit between 9:30 and 10:30 AM — leave on the early end if your flight is before noon.

**Killing time before an evening flight.** PortMiami terminals do not store bags — TSA prohibits cruise-terminal bag storage. Two workable options:

- **Third-party luggage storage** via Radical Storage or Bounce, $5–8/bag/day at host locations in Brickell and downtown Miami (10-min Uber from the port).
- **Hotel day-room** at a Brickell or downtown hotel, ~$60–90 if booked direct.

With four to six hours and bags handled, options in rough order of proximity:

- **Bayside Marketplace** is the closest walk-anywhere option (15 min on foot, or short Uber).
- **Wynwood Walls and district** (10-min Uber, ~$25–35 entry): a half-day of street-art photography.
- **South Beach** (15-min Uber): Lummus Park, Ocean Drive, beach walk in travel clothes.
- **Vizcaya Museum & Gardens** (15-min Uber, ~$25): the quietest of the four.

**MIA without an Uber.** Cruise Connections shared shuttle ($25–35/person, advance booking only) is the formal port-to-airport option. Public transit (Metromover + Metrorail) technically works but the bag transfers are rough. **Practical call: Uber/Lyft on a Friday afternoon, $20–30, 25 minutes, done.**

**Airport-arrival timing.** For a domestic flight after 5 PM, leave the port by 2 PM. For an international flight after 7 PM, leave by 3 PM. MIA TSA lines run 30–60 min during the Friday-afternoon cruise rush.

**Tipping at the end of the cruise:** MSC's **service charge** is added to your shipboard account daily. **A 2026 rate change to know about:** effective **May 11, 2026** for Caribbean sailings, MSC raised the daily Hotel Service Charge from $16 → **$17** per adult in standard staterooms, and from $20 → **$23** per adult in MSC Yacht Club suites. Children ages 2–11 pay roughly half the adult rate; under 2 free. Your May 18–22, 2026 sample sailing falls AFTER the change, so plan for the new rates. Bookings paid (or gratuities pre-purchased) before the May 11 effective date stay at the prior rate per MSC's own price-lock language. The service charge covers stateroom, dining-room, and bar tips. Additional cash tips for outstanding service are appreciated:

- Stateroom steward who went above and beyond: $20–40 cash, end of cruise
- Dining-room waiter for excellent service: $20–30 cash
- Bartender or barista you saw repeatedly: $10–20

If you tipped well in cash mid-cruise, you don't need to tip again at the end.

## Section 4 — Packing List (calibrated for Bahamas, 4 nights, May)

This list is tuned for: **Bahamas in May** — generally 78–86°F daytime, 70–75°F evenings, brief afternoon showers possible, water temperature ~79°F. **Two beach days** at Ocean Cay (potentially three if your sailing has the overnight). **One port day** at Nassau (walkable, urban). **MSC's relaxed-but-not-formal evening dress culture** (smart casual most nights; one "Gala Night" — jacket suggested, not required).

If you remember nothing else: **the Bahamas packing rule is sun and water**. Reef-safe sunscreen, water shoes for Ocean Cay reef areas, a hat that doesn't blow off, and one outfit you'd be photographed in.

### Documents and money

- Passport (preferred). For US citizens on a closed-loop Bahamas cruise, an original birth certificate + valid state ID is technically accepted by both MSC and the Bahamas, but a passport is the simpler path and eliminates Day-1 check-in friction.
- MSC cruise booking confirmation (printed and saved on phone).
- Printed or app-saved excursion confirmations.
- Credit card you trust (notify the issuer of travel; international fees waived if available).
- ~$150 USD cash in $5/$10/$20 bills for taxi tips, small purchases, and crew gratuities. **No need to bring Bahamian dollars** — USD is accepted at 1:1 everywhere in Nassau and on board.

### Carry-on (must arrive on the ship with you)

- All medications + 2-day buffer (NEVER in checked bags).
- Daily / scheduled / controlled-substance prescriptions in original labeled bottles.
- Swimsuit (pools open before checked bags arrive).
- Phone, charger, backup battery.
- Reading material for the pre-cabin window.
- Small refillable water bottle.
- Travel documents.

### For Ocean Cay specifically

- **Reef-safe sunscreen** — required on the marine reserve; oxybenzone-free and octinoxate-free. SPF 30+ minimum, SPF 50+ for kids.
- **Water shoes or sturdy sandals** — Ocean Cay's beaches are mostly fine sand but some snorkel areas have rocks at the entry; a water shoe makes the difference between a magical hour and a cut foot.
- **Snorkel mask and snorkel of your own** (optional — lending is free at the Marine Reserve Beach centre, but bringing your own ensures fit).
- **Beach bag** — large enough for two towels, sunscreen, water bottle, phone, book.
- **Quick-dry travel towel** (optional; cabin towels are heavier and the steward replaces them).

### For Nassau

- **Walking shoes** — Nassau has uneven sidewalks; a sneaker or sturdy sandal is more comfortable than flip-flops.
- **Light daypack** — small, hands-free; you'll be walking 2–4 miles in port.
- **Sunglasses** (polarized for harbor-water glare).
- **Hat or cap.**
- **Crossbody bag** or money belt if you're carrying more than $40 cash.

### For the ship — daytime

- 4 daily outfits — comfortable casual (shorts, t-shirts, sundresses).
- Lightweight long-sleeve shirts for sun protection at the pool.
- Comfortable walking shoes (broken in! not new!).
- Sandals or flip-flops for the pool deck.
- 2 swimsuits (so one's drying while you wear the other).

### For the ship — evening

- 3 evening outfits — MSC's dress code is **smart casual** most nights. Dressy jeans + a button-down or a sweater, a casual dress, dressy linen separates. NOT shorts and a t-shirt for dinner at the main dining room.
- 1 "Gala Night" outfit — typically Thursday on a Mon-Fri sailing. MSC's "Gala" is meaningfully more dressed-up than Royal Caribbean's "smart elegant" but not formal-formal. Cocktail dress or jacket-and-trousers is right.
- Light layer (sweater or jacket) for cold AC inside venues.

### Solo / quiet items

- A book or e-reader for ship time.
- A pen for the customs declaration form.
- A small lock for valuables (the cabin safe is sufficient for valuables; this is for the beach bag at Ocean Cay).

### Optional but useful

- **Magnetic hooks** (4–6) — Seaside's cabin walls are steel; stick hooks for jackets, hats, swimsuits to dry. Single best lifehack; see [cabin organization](/articles/cruise-cabin-organization.html).
- **A travel mug** (the coffee at Biscayne Bay buffet is fine but tastes better in your own cup).
- **Reusable rubber duck** for the cruise duck tradition; hide one on the ship for someone else to find. See [the cruise duck tradition article](/articles/cruise-duck-tradition.html).
- **A small umbrella** for an afternoon Bahamas rain squall (rare but possible in May; brief if so).

### Skip

- Hairdryer (cabin has one).
- Iron (not allowed; ship has limited self-service laundry).
- Heavy formal wear (smart casual + one Gala Night is the norm; MSC is less formal than Cunard or Holland America).
- Beach umbrella (Ocean Cay provides them free; Nassau's beach vendors rent them).
- Power strips with surge protection (banned by most cruise lines for safety; bring a USB-equipped charging cube instead).
- Currency exchange (USD is universally accepted; you don't need BSD).

## Section 7 — Seasickness on a Short Bahamas Cruise

Short Bahamas cruises out of Miami are about as calm as cruising gets. The Gulf Stream between Miami and the Bahamas is generally smooth in May; the open-water passage to Nassau or Ocean Cay is short (under 12 hours each way) and the ship's size (153,516 GT) filters most swell.

**The baseline:** if you've never been seasick on land or in a boat, you almost certainly won't be on this itinerary. Most first-time cruisers complete a 4-night Bahamas without noticing motion at all. The narrow exception is if your cabin is **forward** on a low deck and a tropical depression has pushed unusual swell into the Gulf Stream — this happens, but rarely in May.

### What to bring

- **Bonine** (meclizine) or **Dramamine Less Drowsy** — over-the-counter, take 1 hour before you expect motion. A small box of 4–8 tablets is plenty for a 4-night cruise.
- **Ginger candies or chews** — backup for mild queasiness.
- **Sea-Bands** — pressure-point wristbands. Mixed evidence; some people swear by them.

### What helps once you feel it

- Get on deck. The Waterfront Boardwalk gives you fresh air at sea level — almost no motion-sensitive cruiser stays nauseous on the Waterfront Boardwalk.
- Eat something dry. Crackers, dry toast, plain pretzels.
- Avoid alcohol. Caffeine in moderation is fine.
- Take meclizine the moment you suspect it's coming, not after it's full-on.

If you're highly motion-sensitive and booked a Bella cabin, you don't know your specific assignment in advance. The cure: book Fantastica or above to pick a cabin **mid-ship, mid-deck** — the most stable spot.

## Section 8 — Practical Logistics

### The MSC for Me app

Download it now (App Store / Google Play). Once your booking is final and you've checked in:

- **Daily program** — schedule of activities, dining hours, show times.
- **Cabin info** — your room, your dining, your reservations.
- **Deck plans** — ship layout, venue finder.
- **On-board messaging** — chat with members of your party without internet (uses ship's Wi-Fi network).
- **Pre-cruise purchases** — drinks packages, specialty dining, spa, excursions, Ocean Cay cabanas.

The app is functional, not great. Some features only work on the ship's Wi-Fi (not cellular). Plan accordingly.

### On-board internet

MSC's **Internet Packages** are tiered:

- **Browse & Social** — email, social media, light browsing. Slowest, cheapest.
- **Stream** — adds video streaming.
- **Premium** — multiple devices, faster speeds.

Pre-cruise pricing is generally cheaper than on-board purchase. For a 4-night cruise, **most travelers can survive without on-board Wi-Fi** — port days have cell service, sea days are short, the digital detox is part of the experience. If you do buy, buy for one device and share via the on-board chat in the app.

The [internet-at-sea](/internet-at-sea.html) page covers this in more detail across cruise lines.

### Cell service

- **In US (Miami):** your normal US carrier works.
- **In the Bahamas (Nassau, Ocean Cay):** Bahamas roaming applies. T-Mobile's free roaming covers Bahamas at slower speeds; AT&T and Verizon typically charge $10/day. Check your plan before sailing.
- **At sea:** no normal cell service. The ship may have a Cellular at Sea satellite system — rates are extortionate ($3+/minute). **Default behavior: airplane mode at sea.**

### Multi-language announcements

MSC's general announcements are made in **multiple languages** — typically Italian, English, German, French, and Spanish, in that order. Each announcement can take 60–90 seconds total. This is part of the European-line culture; it's not bad, just different from US lines. Some cabin TVs let you set your preferred announcement language; emergency announcements always run in all languages regardless.

### Tipping (everywhere on the trip)

- **On the ship:** MSC's service charge is added daily — for Caribbean sailings on or after May 11, 2026, $17/adult/day in standard staterooms ($23/adult/day in Yacht Club); children 2–11 roughly half the adult rate; under 2 free. This covers your stateroom, dining, and bar tips. Additional cash tips for outstanding service: stateroom steward $20–40, waiter $20–30.
- **In Nassau:** cash, in USD, in $5 and $10 denominations. ~15–20% on tour cost; $1–2 per taxi ride; $1 per drink at a Bay Street bar.
- **At Ocean Cay:** the BBQ buffet staff are on the ship's payroll — cash tipping is welcome but not expected. The shuttle drivers don't take tips.

### Money

- MSC uses your cruise card for all on-board charges; settle the account at disembarkation via credit card.
- Bring USD cash for Nassau and tips. ~$150 in $5/$10/$20 bills is enough for most travelers.
- ATMs exist in Nassau but charge fees ($3–5 per transaction). Bring cash from home if practical.
- Credit cards work almost everywhere in Nassau; carry a Visa or Mastercard backup.

### Safety in the ports

Nassau is generally safe in the cruise-tourism districts (Bay Street, downtown, immediate Paradise Island). The standard cautions apply: don't flash cash; stay in groups after dark; don't wander into residential neighborhoods at night; be aware that the Junkanoo Beach area sees pickpocketing at peak season. Atlantis and Cabbage Beach are well-policed and safe.

Ocean Cay is private island, controlled access — among the safest cruise destinations possible.

## Section 9 — Accessibility Notes

MSC Seaside has **52 accessible cabins** across categories and is generally well-equipped for mobility-limited travelers. Key notes:

- **Accessible cabins** available across all Experience tiers (Bella through Yacht Club). Book early.
- **Elevators** are abundant; the ship is laid out so most decks are reachable without stairs.
- **Main Dining Rooms** are fully accessible.
- **The Metropolitan Theater** has accessible seating sections.
- **The Waterfront Boardwalk** is fully accessible (level, paved, with ramped entries to bars/restaurants).
- **The Bridge of Sighs** is accessible by elevator from Deck 16; the glass-floor section itself is level.
- **The Twin Ziplines and Forest Aquaventure** have specific accessibility considerations; check with on-board staff.
- **Service animal accommodations** — confirm in advance with MSC's accessibility team.

For port days:

- **Miami:** PortMiami terminals are fully accessible. MIA airport is fully accessible.
- **Nassau:** Festival Place terminal is accessible. Bay Street has sidewalks but uneven curbs. Atlantis is fully accessible. Junkanoo Beach is accessible by road but the substrate is sand.
- **Ocean Cay:** the island is mostly flat with paved paths between beach areas. **Beach wheelchairs are available free** from the Beach Concierge near the dock; reserve in advance via the MSC for Me app. The trams are wheelchair-accessible.

## Section 10 — Solo Traveler Notes

A 4-night MSC Seaside Bahamas cruise is a particularly good solo entry point. The audience is more sample-the-cruise-life than party-mode; there's plenty of activity that doesn't require a buddy (Waterfront Boardwalk reading, the gym, the spa, the Top Sail Lounge if you're in Yacht Club, the Jungle Pool quiet pool deck).

## Section 11 — First Cruise Notes

If this is your first cruise of any kind (not just first MSC), a few things that won't be obvious:

**The ship is a small city.** 4,000+ passengers, 1,400 crew, 20 decks. You won't see all of it. Make peace with this.

**Embarkation is logistical, not magical.** Day 1 is paperwork, lines, and finding things. The good part starts on Day 2.

**The buffet is not the best food on the ship.** It's fine. The Main Dining Rooms are better — Italian-leaning, multi-course, real bread and pasta. Eat there at least 2 of the 4 nights.

**You're going to forget your cruise card the first day.** Everyone does. It's fine. Get a lanyard.

**The first sea day or Ocean Cay day, you'll feel disoriented.** That's normal. Pick 2 things from the daily program, do them, sit down, stare at water. You'll find the rhythm.

**The crew remembers you.** Your stateroom steward, your dining-room waiter — they learn your name, your drink preferences, your allergies. The relationship is real. It's worth being kind, and it's worth tipping in cash above the service charge if service warrants it.

**MSC is more European than American.** Italian wine at lunch is normal. Children play later in the evening than on a US line. Smoking is more visible (designated areas, but still). Pool deck behavior is slightly more reserved than Carnival or Royal. None of this is a problem; it's just the culture.

**You'll spend more on excursions than you think.** Build a "shore-day budget" before sailing. The [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html) helps prevent the $750-cabin-becomes-$2,000-trip surprise.

**You'll come home wanting to go back.** A 4-night Bahamas hop is a "test drive" for cruising. Most first-time cruisers do another within 18 months. Plan accordingly — don't book the next cruise during the first 48 hours back, but know it's coming.

For more first-cruise context across cruise lines, see the broader [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html).

A 4-night Bahamas hop on MSC Seaside is a particular kind of cruise: short enough that it's not a vacation in the heavy sense, long enough that you'll settle into the rhythm of a ship before it's over. The Bahamas in May is at its best — warm water, manageable heat, predictable sun.

A few things worth remembering as your sailing date approaches:

- **Fantastica is the sweet spot.** Bella saves a few dollars at the cost of cabin lottery; Aurea is good value if you'd buy drinks anyway. For most short-cruise first-timers, Fantastica is the right tier.
- **Stay for the lighthouse lighting at Ocean Cay.** The ship sails late so you can. It's the signature moment of this itinerary, and the BBQ buffet + walk to the lighthouse is free.
- **Eat in the Main Dining Rooms.** MSC's included Italian food covers the week. You don't need specialty dining to eat well.
- **Status-match before you sail.** If you've sailed any other line, MSC's Voyagers Club status match is free money — drink coupons, Wi-Fi minutes, on-board discounts. Submit 1–2 weeks before sailing.
- **The crew makes the trip.** Tip in cash, in envelopes, at the end. The service charge covers the floor; cash above that says thank-you in a language that crosses every language announcement.

If you want a one-line preflight reminder to yourself: **the goal is calm.** Pack early. Sleep before you fly. Get to PortMiami with margin. You're not racing the cruise; the cruise is the rest you wanted.

Soli Deo Gloria.
