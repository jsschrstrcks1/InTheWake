This document explains the FTC compliance rationale behind the bright lines locked in the v2.5 plan. It is not a legal document. ## Section 1 — Why this document exists

In the Wake's audience skews older (45–70 with a long tail in both directions, per the audience-profiles framework). A meaningful fraction of readers are bereaved, disabled, caregivers, or otherwise navigating decisions under emotional or physical strain. The Federal Trade Commission has, over the past several years, articulated heightened-scrutiny standards for content marketed to or consumed by older or vulnerable audiences. Those standards directly shape what we will and won't publish.

Our existing bright lines (locked in the v2.5 plan) were chosen partly for brand reasons and partly because the FTC standards make the alternatives untenable. This document captures the FTC reasoning, so when a partnership conversation surfaces a temptation to bend a bright line, the operator can re-read this and remember why the bright line is permanent.

## Section 2 — The four FTC documents that matter

The FTC has issued several documents over 2022–2024 that materially shape what a publisher in our position can and cannot do. The four most relevant:

### 2.1 — Health Products Compliance Guidance (December 2022)

Replaced the FTC's earlier dietary-supplement-focused guidance with a broader standard covering all health-related products, including over-the-counter remedies, supplements, monitoring devices, motion-sickness products, and adjacent categories.

Key requirements that shape our posture:

- **Substantiation must be specific.** Claims about a health product require competent and reliable scientific evidence — typically randomized controlled trials specifically designed to test the claim. General testimonial endorsement is not substantiation.
- **Disclaimers cannot save unsubstantiated claims.** "Results may vary" or "talk to your doctor" do not cure an unsubstantiated efficacy claim.
- **Heightened scrutiny when the audience is vulnerable.** Explicit reference to older consumers, caregivers, and consumers facing health concerns.

**Authoritative summary:** Cooley LLP, 2023-03-02 — *FTC Revises Health Products Compliance Guidance.* https://www.cooley.com/news/insight/2023/2023-03-02-ftc-revises-health-products-compliance-guidance

**Our response:** Permanent bright line — no medication-product affiliate links anywhere on the site. This includes seemingly-innocuous items where the FTC has signaled scrutiny: motion-sickness patches, Sea-Bands, sleep aids, melatonin, anti-nausea wristbands, magnesium "for cruisers," CBD products marketed for travel anxiety, and similar. The Sea-Bands affiliate link removed during Phase A of the v2.5 plan was an early instance of this rule applied retroactively; we do not re-enter the category.

### 2.2 — Endorsement Guides (2023 update)

Effective June 29, 2023. Updated the 2009 endorsement guides to address modern social media, AI-generated reviews, and platform-mediated endorsements.

Key requirements:

- **Disclosures must be clear and conspicuous.** Hidden affiliate disclosure footers fail; disclosure must be visible above the fold and adjacent to the endorsement.
- **Endorsements must reflect honest opinion based on actual experience.** Made-up or inflated endorsements are deceptive even if disclosed.
- **No astroturfing.** Paid reviews not labeled as such; "review networks" that pay for positive reviews; bot-generated review content. All deceptive.
- **Liability extends to the advertiser.** A brand that pays for an endorsement is liable for the deceptive content of that endorsement, not just the publisher who posts it.
- **Children and other vulnerable audiences receive specific protection.** Heightened standards when the audience is unable to evaluate the endorsement critically.

**Authoritative summary, one-year retrospective:** Hall Render, 2024-07-17 — *A Year Later: Revisiting FTC's Updated Endorsement Guides.* https://hallrender.com/2024/07/17/a-year-later-revisiting-ftcs-updated-endorsement-guides/

**Our response:** All four locked bright lines (no cruise-line affiliates, no shore-excursion commissions, no display ads, no sponsored content that reads as editorial) align with the substantiation-and-disclosure principles. The "editorially firewalled comparison page" model described in the public media kit (`admin/media-kit.md`) is the only paid-placement structure we'll consider; that model satisfies the disclosure requirement (clear and conspicuous label above the fold), the substantiation requirement (we test or first-source what we recommend), and the no-astroturf requirement (we write the review ourselves; no paid review-platform involvement).

### 2.3 — Protecting Older Consumers reports (2023, 2024)

Annual FTC reports to Congress on consumer protection issues affecting older consumers. The 2023 and 2024 reports specifically called out:

- Travel and timeshare scams targeting older consumers
- Healthcare-product deception aimed at older audiences
- The "vulnerable consumer" framework as a multiplier on standard FTC enforcement priorities

**Why this matters for us:** our audience is the population the reports describe. Conduct that would draw a wrist-slap on a younger general-audience publisher draws elevated scrutiny when older consumers are the affected audience.

### 2.4 — The general FTC Act, Section 5 (unfair or deceptive acts or practices)

The catch-all. Even where no specific guideline applies, "deceptive" content — content likely to mislead a reasonable consumer about a material fact — is a per-se violation. The FTC has consistently held that the "reasonable consumer" standard is calibrated to the audience the content actually reaches, not a hypothetical sophisticated reader.

For our older audience, the "reasonable consumer" is, on average, less likely to detect technical hedging language (e.g., "studies suggest," "may help with") and more likely to take a marketing claim at face value. The bar for what counts as deceptive is therefore lower for us than it would be on a publisher with a younger or more skeptical audience.

## Section 3 — How the four FTC documents map to our locked bright lines

| Bright line | FTC source | Why this bright line specifically |
|---|---|---|
| No cruise-line affiliate commissions | Endorsement Guides; Section 5 | Recommending a cruise line we earn a commission on creates an undisclosed (or insufficiently-disclosed-given-our-audience) conflict of interest. The cleanest fix is to take the commission off the table entirely. |
| No shore-excursion booking commissions | Endorsement Guides; Section 5 | Same logic. Excursion-booking commissions (Viator, GetYourGuide, etc.) influence which excursions we'd surface. |
| No display advertising | Endorsement Guides; Older Consumers reports | Display advertising on a vulnerable-consumer-skewing site invites scrutiny over what gets advertised, how the ads target, and whether the publisher exercises adequate gatekeeping over predatory or marginal advertisers. The brand promise ("No ads") is also stronger than any rate-card revenue would justify at our scale. |
| No medication-product affiliate links | Health Products Compliance Guidance | Direct application of the December 2022 guidance. Even seemingly-benign products in this category — motion-sickness wristbands, sleep aids, anti-nausea remedies — fall under heightened scrutiny when our audience is the buyer. |
| No advertising on grief, accessibility, or faith content | Older Consumers reports; Section 5 | Vulnerable-consumer multiplier at its strongest. Pastoral surfaces are the moments when readers are most susceptible to misleading claims. The "no ads on these surfaces, period" rule sidesteps the entire question. |
| No sponsored content that reads as editorial | Endorsement Guides | Direct application. The publisher and the advertiser are jointly liable for any blurred line between editorial voice and paid placement. The clear-and-conspicuous disclosure standard means a sponsored review must be labeled as such above the fold; if it can't be (because the placement format hides the disclosure), the placement is off-limits. |

## Section 4 — The Decision Tree

When a partnership conversation surfaces or a contributor proposes content with a commercial component, walk through this tree before committing:

1. **Is this a health product, a medical-adjacent product, or a financial product (insurance, payment, investment)?**
   - If health/medical-adjacent: **stop.** Permanent bright line. (Travel insurance is a financial product, not a medical product, and is allowable per the categories in `admin/media-kit.md`.)
   - If financial: continue.

2. **Does the placement involve an affiliate link, a commission, or a flat-fee sponsorship?**
   - If commission-based on a cruise-line or shore-excursion booking: **stop.** Permanent bright line.
   - If flat-fee sponsorship of a comparison page or review: continue with the editorial firewall.
   - If affiliate link in a non-cruise-non-excursion-non-medical category: continue with disclosure.

3. **Does the placement appear on a pastoral surface?**
   - If yes (`/solo/`, grief content, accessibility-as-disability content, faith content): **stop.** Permanent bright line.
   - If no: continue.

4. **Is the disclosure clear and conspicuous, above the fold, adjacent to the endorsement?**
   - If yes: continue.
   - If the placement format physically prevents this (e.g., a small native-ad widget): **stop.** Format-induced disclosure failures are the ad-placement category we don't run.

5. **Can we substantiate every claim about the product?**
   - "Substantiate" means: we have first-person experience, primary-source documentation, or verified third-party reporting. Vendor marketing copy is not substantiation.
   - If yes: continue.
   - If no: don't make the claim. Hedge to what's actually substantiated, or don't ship.

6. **Does the audience for this placement skew vulnerable?**
   - All of our audiences skew vulnerable to some degree (older base, accessibility audience, grief audience). Apply the vulnerable-consumer multiplier — i.e., assume the bar for "could mislead a reasonable consumer" is lower than the general-publishing standard.
   - When in doubt, don't ship.

7. **Does the placement language match our voice rules?**
   - No banned vocabulary (see `admin/CTA-STYLE-GUIDE.md`).
   - No urgency framing.
   - No fake-scarcity, fake-discount, or fake-expert tropes.
   - Brand-supplied copy gets rewritten into our voice before it ships, every time.

8. **Have we reviewed this with the brand?**
   - The brand bears legal liability under the Endorsement Guides for content we publish under their sponsorship. The brand's compliance team should sign off before publication. If they refuse to sign off, that's a signal something about the placement isn't compliant.

If you reach the bottom of the tree without a "stop" — proceed.

## Section 5 — Disclosure-language templates (for the day a paid placement happens)

When (if) a paid placement is published, the disclosure must appear above the fold and adjacent to the endorsement. Examples below.

### 5.1 — Sponsored review of a single brand

```
> **Sponsored review.** [Brand Name] paid for the placement of this review. The product was tested by us; we maintain editorial control over the content. Our affiliate-disclosure posture and bright lines are unchanged: [no cruise-line affiliates, no shore-excursion commissions, no display advertising, no medication-product placements](/affiliate-disclosure.html).
```

### 5.2 — Comparison page with multiple brands, one or more sponsored

```
> **Sponsored comparison.** [Brand A] paid for the lead position in this comparison. We tested all listed products independently; the comparison reflects our honest assessment regardless of which product paid for placement. See our [affiliate disclosure](/affiliate-disclosure.html) for the full editorial-firewall posture.
```

### 5.3 — Affiliate link inside an editorial article (non-medical, non-cruise, non-excursion category)

```
> Some links on this page are affiliate links — we earn a small commission if you buy through them, at no additional cost to you. We have not received product samples or any compensation for choosing what to link to; we link to what we use. See our [affiliate disclosure](/affiliate-disclosure.html) for the full posture.
```

### 5.4 — Where these disclosures must appear

- Immediately under the page H1 or article title — above the fold on both desktop and mobile.
- Repeat-near-the-link if the article is long and the disclosure block scrolls out of view.
- In the JSON-LD structured data (`reviewBody` or similar) flagging the sponsored relationship, so AI grounding context picks it up correctly.
