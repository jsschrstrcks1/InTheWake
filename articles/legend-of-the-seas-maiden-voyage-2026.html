Legend of the Seas Sails: The Maiden-Season Calculus

Royal Caribbean's third Icon-class ship took her first passengers on July 4 from Rome — about 250,000 gross tons, now the largest cruise ship afloat by a narrow margin. Her debut date moved twice before she sailed. What booking a ship's first months actually gets you, and what it costs you.

by Ken Baker | July 6, 2026

Answer first: Legend of the Seas, Royal Caribbean's third Icon-class ship, welcomed her first passengers on July 4, 2026 in Civitavecchia and departed on a seven-night Western Mediterranean maiden voyage returning July 11. At about 250,000 gross tons — some outlets report 250,800 — she edges past her sisters Icon of the Seas and Star of the Seas as the largest cruise ship afloat. She sails Rome and Barcelona round trips through October, then repositions to Fort Lauderdale in November for the Caribbean. Her maiden voyage date moved twice — from August 2 to July 11 to July 4 — which is the fact this article is actually about. A maiden season buys you the newest hardware at sea. It also buys you the schedule risk that comes with hardware that new.

The debut

On the Fourth of July, the newest and largest cruise ship in the world took her first paying passengers at Civitavecchia, the port that serves Rome. Delivered by the Meyer Turku shipyard in Finland in June, Legend of the Seas crossed the Mediterranean over the following weeks — Málaga, then Civitavecchia — before boarding day. The maiden itinerary: seven nights, Western Mediterranean, returning July 11.

Published reports agree on Barcelona, Palma de Mallorca, and Marseille among the calls, and disagree on the rest — one lists Florence's port, another Naples and Capri. For a specific sailing, Royal Caribbean's own itinerary page is the record. This kind of small discrepancy across cruise media is normal for a new ship; it is also a preview of the article's larger point.

The ship, briefly

The third Icon-class ship measures about 250,000 gross tons; several outlets put the precise figure at 250,800, a touch over her sisters. That makes her the largest cruise ship in the world by a margin a passenger will never perceive. The three Icons share the same bones — the neighborhood layout, the aqua park, the seven-pool top deck, over 40 dining and bar venues, capacity north of 5,000 guests at double occupancy. If you have walked Icon of the Seas, you know this ship. For the full comparison of what these tonnage numbers mean and don't mean, the site's ship-size explainer does that work; the short version is that "largest" is a marketing fact, not a passenger experience.

After the Mediterranean summer — Rome and Barcelona departures through October — she crosses to Fort Lauderdale and starts Caribbean itineraries in November. For most American cruisers, that November reposition is when this ship becomes bookable without a transatlantic flight.

The maiden-season calculus

Here is the trade, stated plainly, because the brochures will not state it.

What a maiden season gets you. The newest ship at sea, before the crowds have worn the newness off it. A crew at maximum attentiveness — first seasons run under corporate scrutiny, and it shows in the service. The genuine event of it: first-season passengers on a ship this large are a small club, and some people value that honestly.

What it costs you. A premium fare — new hardware prices at a markup that the same cabins will not carry a year later. Shakedown quirks: on new ships, venues sometimes open late, entertainment schedules shift, systems get tuned in service. And schedule risk, which this ship has already demonstrated twice. Royal Caribbean first announced an August 2 maiden voyage. Then it moved to July 11. Then to July 4. Each move was announced as good news — the ship was ready early — and for the cruise line it was. For a traveler who had booked nonrefundable flights to Rome around the August 2 sailing, the calculus looked different. Early delivery moves dates forward; late delivery moves them back; either way, the maiden season is the one part of a cruise calendar where the dates themselves are soft.

The honest guidance follows from the trade. If your plans bend — flexible flights, no fixed school calendar, a tolerance for the itinerary shifting under you — a maiden season is a real event and this is a remarkable ship to see new. If your plans do not bend, the same ship runs the same Caribbean loops from Fort Lauderdale next year, at a lower fare, with the kinks worked out. The ship does not get smaller. Only the price does.

What is not in this report

I was not aboard the maiden voyage; this article is a synthesis of the published record. Royal Caribbean's announcements and the cruise trade press are the sources for the dates and specifications, and they disagree on details as small as one port call and 800 gross tons — disagreements noted above rather than resolved. What the first passengers actually found aboard — what opened on time, what didn't, how the crew handled week one — will come from their accounts, not from anything available to this article on July 6.

FAQ

When did Legend of the Seas start sailing?

First passengers boarded July 4, 2026 at Civitavecchia (Rome) for a seven-night Western Mediterranean maiden voyage returning July 11. The date moved twice: from the originally announced August 2, to July 11, then to July 4. Meyer Turku delivered the ship in June 2026.

Is she the largest cruise ship in the world?

As of July 2026, yes — by a narrow margin. About 250,000 gross tons (reported by some outlets at 250,800), a touch over Icon of the Seas and Star of the Seas. The difference is real on paper and imperceptible on board; all three Icon-class sisters share the same layout and carry over 5,000 guests at double occupancy.

Where will she sail?

Seven-night Western Mediterranean round trips from Rome and Barcelona through October 2026. Reported calls include Barcelona, Palma de Mallorca, and Marseille, with published itineraries differing on the remaining ports — check Royal Caribbean's itinerary page for a specific sailing. From November 2026: Caribbean cruises from Fort Lauderdale.

Should you book a maiden season?

If your plans can bend, it is a genuine event: newest hardware, attentive crew, first-season standing. If they cannot, note that this ship's own debut date moved twice before she sailed, that maiden fares carry a premium, and that new ships work out kinks in service. The same ship runs the Caribbean from Fort Lauderdale a year from now, cheaper.
