I'm a Solo Cruise Specialist, and I don't just book solo cruises — I sail them. After years of waiting for someone to say "yes" to traveling with me, I stopped waiting, and it changed my life. So when a first-timer asks me how to do this, here's what I actually tell them.

1. Solo isn't single

This is the first thing I clear up, every time. Traveling solo doesn't mean you're single — plenty of the cruisers I work with are happily married. It just means you're going on your own terms. We're a solo community, not a singles scene. No dating, no pressure. Your trip, your way.

2. The single supplement — and how to soften it

Yes, sailing alone can carry a "single supplement," because cruise fares assume two people in a cabin. But it varies enormously by cruise line, ship, and sailing date — and a good agent's whole job is to find the ones that treat solo travelers fairly. Some ships have dedicated solo studio cabins with no supplement at all. Others run promotions that waive or reduce it. You rarely have to pay full double if you know where to look.

3. Pick a solo-friendly ship — and research it before you book

Not every ship is built with solo travelers in mind. The good ones have studio cabins, solo gatherings, and lounges designed for meeting people. Before I ever put a client on a ship, I dig into its deck plans, cabin types, and what travelers actually say about it. One resource I lean on for that homework is Cruising in the Wake's ship guides — it's a clear, honest place to compare ships and cabins so you board knowing exactly what you're getting.

4. You're never actually alone (unless you want to be)

This is the part people don't expect. Yes, you get your own cabin and total freedom to do your own thing. But on our hosted group sailings — trips I organize just for our solo community — there are familiar faces for dinner, shore days, or a drink whenever you feel like company. You decide each day how social you want to be.

5. Dining solo is easier than you think

The dining room intimidates a lot of first-timers, but it shouldn't. Ask for a shared table and you'll meet people; ask for a table for one and you'll get a quiet view. Specialty restaurants are wonderful solo, too — bring a book, sit at the bar, take your time. No one is watching, I promise.

6. Start with a sailing that's easy to love

Your first solo cruise doesn't need to be a two-week expedition. A shorter, well-run sailing on a solo-friendly ship is the best way to fall in love with this. Get one under your belt and you'll wonder why you waited.
