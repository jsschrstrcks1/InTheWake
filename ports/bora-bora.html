Bora Bora

The Pearl of the Pacific

Tender Port

Ships anchor offshore and passengers take small boats (tenders) to reach Vaitape.

Last reviewed: December 2025

Captain's Logbook

I had seen thousands of photographs of Bora Bora before my ship dropped anchor in Pofai Bay. None prepared me for what my eyes witnessed that morning. Mount Otemanu filled the frame from my balcony – an extinct volcanic spire rising 2,385 feet from a lagoon so impossibly turquoise it looked digitally enhanced. The colors exist on a spectrum that cameras simply cannot capture: blues that shift from aquamarine to sapphire to emerald depending on depth and light, greens so vivid they almost hurt to look at. I understood immediately why they call this place the "Pearl of the Pacific" – it genuinely defies description.

Polynesians have called this island home for thousands of years, naming it Pora Pora ("firstborn") in their navigation mythology – the sacred first child of their ancestral homeland. When European explorers arrived in 1722, they mispronounced the name; the error stuck. The history I didn't expect to find was military: during World War II, the Americans transformed paradise into a fortress with 5,000 GIs stationed here, building airstrips, fuel depots, and coastal defenses to protect South Pacific supply lines. When I visited Faanui Bay, the relics still stood – cannons rusting in the jungle, concrete bunkers reclaimed by flowering vines – surprising reminders that this paradise once bristled with military purpose.

The tender ride from ship to shore became an excursion in itself. My small boat crossed water so clear I could watch coral gardens pass thirty feet below while Mount Otemanu's sacred peak grew larger against the sky. I had worried about tendering – sometimes it feels rushed, impersonal – but here the five-minute journey across that impossibly blue lagoon felt like a gift. I wasn't just arriving at a destination; I was being gradually immersed in something extraordinary. Vaitape, the main village, has a relaxed charm: perhaps 4,000 residents, a handful of shops, a post office popular for its stamps, and tour operators lining the waterfront. This isn't a place for shopping or nightlife; it's a gateway to water.

The moment that stopped my world came at the Lagoonarium – a natural aquarium formed by nets in the lagoon itself. I slipped into water that felt like bathwater, warm and so clear visibility seemed infinite. Within minutes, a black-tip reef shark glided beneath me, close enough to touch, utterly disinterested in my presence. Eagle rays swept past like underwater birds. Schools of tropical fish parted around me as if I were invisible. The silence underwater was profound. I forgot I was a tourist, forgot my ship, forgot schedules. I was simply floating in liquid light, surrounded by creatures who tolerated my presence with ancient indifference. However beautiful the surface world appeared, underneath it was even more spectacular.

Matira Beach earned its reputation as one of Earth's finest stretches of sand. The southern tip of the island curves into a crescent of powder-white beach where the lagoon turns impossibly shallow – I waded out fifty yards with water barely reaching my knees, the warmth enveloping me while Mount Otemanu framed itself perfectly against clouds and sky. Unlike beaches overwhelmed by development, Matira retains a feeling of unspoiled beauty. I rented a bicycle and circled the island's 32-kilometer ring road, passing through villages, WWII remnants, roadside fruit stands selling papayas the size of footballs, and viewpoints where sacred mountain and shimmering lagoon composed themselves into perfect frames. Although overwater bungalows cluster on the eastern shore (inaccessible without resort reservations), the island itself felt accessible, welcoming, genuinely warm in both temperature and spirit.

Bora Bora is expensive – among the most expensive destinations I've encountered in my travels. A simple lunch ran me $45; excursions typically cost $150 or more per person. The currency here is the CFP Franc, though many vendors accept US dollars at unfavorable rates. I learned to budget carefully and discovered that some of the best experiences – simply floating in that warm lagoon water, watching the light change on Mount Otemanu as clouds drifted past – cost nothing at all. The most vivid moment wasn't the shark encounter or the snorkeling; it was sitting on Matira Beach at sunset, watching colors I had no names for paint themselves across sky and water, feeling profoundly grateful that such places still exist.

But certain experiences justify any price, and floating in that lagoon, watching sharks and rays circle beneath me while sacred Otemanu pierced the equatorial sky, I understood what "once in a lifetime" actually means. Some places exceed their legends. Bora Bora is one of them. The photographs I took don't capture what I saw; they can't. But the memory of that water, that mountain, that profound underwater silence – those will stay with me forever. I left the island changed, carrying something ineffable that photographs cannot convey but that my heart will remember always. When people ask me about Bora Bora now, I tell them: go. Whatever it costs, go. You'll never be the same.

Cruise Port

Bora Bora has no dock – the barrier reef and shallow lagoon make direct berthing impossible. Ships anchor in protected waters of Pofai Bay, and tender boats ferry passengers to Vaitape village on the island's western shore. The tender ride takes 5-10 minutes and offers gorgeous views of Mount Otemanu growing larger as you approach. Tender tickets are typically distributed based on excursion bookings and mobility needs. Shore excursion passengers board early; independent travelers follow. The tender pier at Vaitape sits in the heart of the small village with shops, restaurants, and tour operators within walking distance. An information booth near the pier provides maps and guidance. Currency exchange and an ATM are available nearby. The return tender schedule is posted at the pier – final tender times are strictly enforced as ships must clear the shallow anchorage before nightfall.

Getting Around

Bora Bora's 32-kilometer ring road circles the island and connects all major attractions. Most visitors explore by organized tour, rental car, scooter, or bicycle. Car rentals are available near the tender pier in Vaitape – expect to pay $70-100 for a full day. Scooters rent for less but require experience on narrow roads. Bicycle rentals offer a leisurely way to explore at $20-30 per day. 4x4 island tours provide guided exploration including stops at WWII sites, viewpoints, and local villages. Le Truck – local open-air buses – circulate occasionally but schedules are unreliable for cruise visitors with limited time. Taxis exist but are expensive; negotiate fares in advance.

Accessibility note: Vaitape village is relatively flat with some wheelchair access near the pier area. However, beaches and nature sites typically have sand and uneven terrain. Most boat-based excursions require ability to board small vessels – typically stepping down from pier to boat deck. Visitors with mobility concerns should discuss specific needs when booking excursions well in advance. Some higher-end resort beaches offer better accessibility with boardwalks, accessible restrooms, and beach wheelchair rentals. The tender boats have crew assistance but involve stepping down into smaller craft from the ship's gangway. Mobility scooters can be difficult to manage on tenders. Travelers with accessibility needs should contact the cruise line's accessibility department before booking excursions at this tender-only port.

Beach Guide

Matira Beach: Consistently rated one of the world's finest beaches, this crescent of powder-white sand curves along Bora Bora's southern tip. The lagoon here is extraordinarily shallow – you can wade out fifty yards with water barely reaching your knees. Public access exists along the beach, though the best facilities (loungers, food service, restrooms) belong to the hotels lining the shore. Water temperature averages 79-84°F (26-29°C) year-round. The gentle slope makes this ideal for families and non-swimmers. Afternoon light creates perfect photography conditions with Mount Otemanu as backdrop.

Motu Beaches: The small islands (motus) ringing the lagoon offer pristine, often deserted beaches accessible only by boat. Many excursions include motu stops with fresh fruit service, snorkeling gear, and shade structures on white sand that squeaks underfoot. These feel like private island experiences – the embodiment of South Pacific dreams.

Hotel Beach Access: Several resorts offer day passes ($150-300+) including beach access, pool use, lunch, and sometimes spa credits. For those seeking overwater bungalow ambiance without overnight rates, this provides a taste of ultimate luxury. Contact resorts directly or book through shore excursion providers.

Shore Excursions

Lagoonarium Snorkeling: The signature Bora Bora experience. This natural aquarium formed by nets in the lagoon allows swimming with blacktip reef sharks, eagle rays, sea turtles, and tropical fish in crystal-clear water. Guides supervise all encounters; the sharks are not aggressive toward humans. Ship excursions offer guaranteed return to the vessel and typically include equipment, but independent operators often provide smaller groups and more personal attention. Book ahead during peak season as this sells out quickly.

Shark and Ray Feeding: Boat tours visit designated feeding sites where stingrays and reef sharks gather in shallow water. You can stand waist-deep as rays brush past seeking handouts – exhilarating rather than frightening. Guides control food distribution and supervise visitor safety. Most tours combine this with snorkeling stops and motu beach visits. Expect 3-4 hours duration.

4x4 Island Tour: Explore Bora Bora's interior and wartime past via off-road vehicle. Stops include Faanui Bay's rusting cannons and bunkers from the 1940s American presence, panoramic viewpoints, village visits, and sometimes fruit tastings or cultural demonstrations. Tours typically last 2-3 hours and work well combined with water activities. Less physically demanding than water excursions; good for non-swimmers or those interested in wartime context.

Glass-Bottom Boat: For those who prefer staying dry, glass-bottom boat tours reveal the underwater world without getting wet. Most combine coral garden viewing with scenic cruising around the lagoon's iconic landmarks. Family-friendly option that accommodates all mobility levels.

Jet Ski Tours: Circumnavigate the island at speed, stopping at viewpoints and swimming spots. Most tours require previous jet ski experience. Exhilarating way to cover the entire lagoon in 2-3 hours. Sun exposure is intense – sun protection is essential.

Independent vs. Ship Excursions: Ship excursions guarantee return before departure – critically important given Bora Bora's tender-only access where missing the last tender means being stranded. Independent operators often offer better prices, smaller groups, and more flexible timing but put the responsibility entirely on you to return before final tender. Given the complications of tendering and tight departure windows, many visitors prefer ship-organized tours despite higher costs – the peace of mind is worth the premium. If booking independently, confirm pickup location, exact duration, and return timing carefully. Ask specifically what happens if weather delays occur. Always know your ship's final tender time and plan to return at least 30 minutes before that deadline. Local operators understand cruise schedules and most will get you back reliably, but the consequences of miscommunication are severe. Book ahead for peak season visits when popular tours fill quickly.

Local Cuisine

Poisson Cru: French Polynesia's signature dish – raw fish marinated in lime juice and coconut milk, similar to ceviche but richer. Available at most restaurants; quality varies, but good poisson cru is transcendent.

Vaitape Restaurants: Several restaurants near the tender pier offer Polynesian-French fusion cuisine. Expect to pay $40-60 for lunch with drinks. Aloe Café and Bloody Mary's are well-known options – the latter famous for its sand floor and celebrity guest book.

Beach BBQ: Many motu excursions include grilled fish, fresh fruit, and coconut on private island beaches. These feel quintessentially Polynesian and often represent the best value dining despite being included in tour prices.

Budget Tips: Vaitape has a few food trucks and small snack shops with lower prices. Fresh fruit from roadside stands is affordable and delicious. Consider eating lunch aboard ship and spending port time on activities rather than expensive restaurants.

Travel Notices

Currency: CFP Franc (XPF), tied to the Euro. USD sometimes accepted but at poor exchange rates. ATM available in Vaitape near the tender pier.

Sun Protection: Equatorial sun is extremely intense. Reef-safe sunscreen is mandatory for water activities – non-reef-safe sunscreens damage the coral ecosystems that make Bora Bora special.

Tender Operations: Weather can affect tender operations. If seas are rough, tendering may be delayed or cancelled – have backup plans aboard ship.

Cost Awareness: Everything is expensive. Budget accordingly or book ship excursions for predictable pricing.

Depth Soundings

Underwater Visibility: The lagoon offers visibility of 100+ feet on calm days. The water clarity is legendary – you can see coral formations and fish from the surface as clearly as through glass. This isn't marketing hyperbole; Bora Bora's lagoon genuinely offers some of the clearest water in the Pacific.

Marine Life: Expect blacktip reef sharks (harmless to humans), stingrays, eagle rays, sea turtles, moray eels, and hundreds of tropical fish species. Larger creatures like manta rays appear seasonally. The Lagoonarium concentrates wildlife for reliable encounters, but snorkeling anywhere in the lagoon reveals abundant life.

Coral Health: Despite tourism pressure, Bora Bora's reefs remain reasonably healthy thanks to French Polynesia's protected status. However, climate change and coral bleaching pose ongoing threats. Using reef-safe sunscreen isn't just recommended – it's an ethical obligation for anyone who wants these reefs to survive for future visitors.

Water Temperature: Bathwater warm year-round, ranging from 79°F (26°C) in winter months to 84°F (29°C) in summer. No wetsuit needed for snorkeling. The warmth makes extended water time comfortable but also means dehydration sneaks up on you – drink water between activities.

Practical Information

Best Time to Visit: May through October (dry season) offers the most reliable weather. November through April brings more rain but also warmer water and fewer crowds.

Time Zone: Tahiti Time (TAHT), UTC-10. No daylight saving time.

Language: French and Tahitian are official languages. English is widely understood in tourist areas.

Tipping: Not traditionally expected in French Polynesia but increasingly common in tourist-facing businesses.

WiFi: Available at some cafés in Vaitape. Quality varies. Resort WiFi requires guest access.

What to Bring: Reef-safe sunscreen, hat, sunglasses, snorkel mask (personal gear ensures good fit), water shoes for coral areas, waterproof camera, and more cash than you think you'll need.

Frequently Asked Questions
Do ships dock at Bora Bora?

No – there is no dock at Bora Bora. The barrier reef and shallow lagoon make direct berthing impossible. Ships anchor in the protected lagoon waters and tender passengers to Vaitape village. The tender ride (5-10 minutes) offers gorgeous views of sacred Mount Otemanu as you approach.

What is the Lagoonarium?

The Lagoonarium is a natural aquarium in the lagoon itself – a netted section where you can snorkel safely with eagle rays, blacktip reef sharks, sea turtles, and tropical fish. It's one of the most reliable ways to encounter marine life up close in crystal-clear water.

Can I visit Matira Beach?

Yes – Matira Beach on the southern tip is publicly accessible and consistently rated one of the world's best beaches. The water is incredibly shallow and warm, ideal for wading. Hotels line the shore with better facilities, but the beach itself is open to all.

Is snorkeling with sharks safe?

Yes – the black-tip reef sharks and lemon sharks at Bora Bora's feeding sites are not aggressive toward humans. Guides supervise all encounters. It's exhilarating rather than frightening.

Can I visit an overwater bungalow resort?

Some resorts offer day passes including lunch, pool, and beach access. Contact resorts directly or through shore excursions. Expect to pay $200+ per person for this luxury experience.

How expensive is Bora Bora really?

Very expensive – among the costliest destinations on Earth. Budget $40-60 for lunch, $150+ for excursions, $10+ for drinks. The CFP franc is the currency. Many visitors book ship excursions to control costs and guarantee return times.

Quick Answer

Bora Bora – the "Pearl of the Pacific" – is French Polynesia's crown jewel. An extinct volcanic island with sacred Mount Otemanu (2,385 ft) rising from a lagoon so beautiful it defies photography. Ships anchor in barrier reef-protected waters and tender to Vaitape village. The experience is all about the water: Lagoonarium snorkeling, Matira Beach, swimming with sharks and rays, kayaking through impossible blues. Home to 10,000 residents with a fascinating WWII past. Expensive but genuinely once-in-a-lifetime.
