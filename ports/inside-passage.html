Inside Passage

Alaska's Scenic Waterway

Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA)

🚢

Scenic Cruising: The Inside Passage is a waterway, not a port. Ships transit this 1,000-mile protected route between islands, glaciers, and forested mountains. This guide covers the sailing experience.

From the Logbook

We sailed through a world of islands — over a thousand of them, forest-clad and fjord-cut, stretching from Puget Sound to the land of ice. The Inside Passage is less a route than a maze, a protected labyrinth where the Pacific's fury is broken by land masses so thoroughly that our ship glided on water smooth as glass. I stood on the observation deck as we entered the passage, my hands wrapped around a cup of hot coffee, and watched eagles circle above dark green forests. The smell was pine and salt and cold, and I breathed it in until my lungs ached with the freshness of it.

The first day of scenic cruising was a revelation. I had expected beauty, but not this kind — not the intimate, constantly changing panorama of islands sliding past, channels narrowing until I could almost touch the trees on either side, then opening into vast fjords where glaciers hung blue and ancient on distant mountainsides. I heard the splash of a seal diving from a rock, the cry of an eagle overhead, and once the deep exhalation of a whale surfacing somewhere off the starboard bow. My camera stayed in my pocket for most of the morning. Some experiences resist being reduced to photographs.

At 3 a.m., I stood on deck watching the light. This far north in June, the sun barely sets — it just dips toward the horizon and hesitates, painting everything gold and violet for hours that feel stolen from time. A humpback breached off the port side, hung impossibly in mid-air, and crashed back in a fountain of spray. I was too stunned to photograph it. Tears came to my eyes — not from sadness but from the overwhelming beauty of witnessing something so wild and free and perfectly indifferent to my presence. Some moments are for witnessing, not capturing. That whale did not breach for me, yet I will carry the image forever.

However, the Inside Passage is not all postcard perfection. Rain came on the second day — cold, persistent rain that turned the mountains into gray shadows and made the deck slippery beneath my feet. I felt the chill through my waterproof layers and retreated to the observation lounge, where I watched raindrops streak down the windows and tasted the hot chocolate the steward brought without being asked. Despite the weather, the passage remained beautiful — perhaps even more so, because the rain gave it an honest roughness that sunny photographs never capture. The forests looked darker, the water more mysterious, and the occasional break in the clouds sent shafts of light across the fjord like something from a painting.

On the glacier day at Tracy Arm, I stood on the bow as the ship eased between icebergs into a narrow fjord. The cold hit me like a wall — my nose went numb within minutes, and I felt the rough wool of my gloves against my skin as I gripped the railing. The Sawyer Glacier filled the end of the fjord, a wall of blue ice hundreds of feet tall. Then it calved. A section the size of a building simply fell away in slow motion, and the thunderous crack reached us seconds later, followed by a wave that rocked the ship gently. I whispered something — I do not remember what — and felt my wife squeeze my hand beside me. The glacier did not care that we were watching. It had been doing this for ten thousand years. Yet witnessing it changed us.

That evening, surrounded by the quiet conversation of fellow passengers who had seen the same calving event, I reflected on what the Inside Passage teaches. A retired fisherman from Oregon told me he had sailed these waters commercially for thirty years and never tired of them. "Every transit is different," he said, his weathered hands cupped around a mug of tea. "The light changes, the wildlife changes, the weather changes. You never see the same passage twice." I understood what he meant. Despite having read every guidebook and watched every documentary, nothing had prepared me for the scale and the silence and the sheer aliveness of this waterway.

Looking back, I learned that the journey itself is the destination. The ports along the way — Ketchikan, Juneau, Skagway — are wonderful, but they are punctuation marks in a sentence written by the passage itself. The real gift was the time between ports: the slow sailing, the patient watching, the willingness to stand in the rain because something wild might happen at any moment. The lesson is simple but easy to forget — slow down, pay attention, and trust that nature will reward those who wait. I closed my journal and watched the last light fade over mountains that have stood here since before humans could name them, grateful for every cold, beautiful, imperfect mile we had traveled through this extraordinary waterway.

Cruise Port and Terminal

The Inside Passage has no cruise port or terminal because it is a scenic waterway, not a destination where ships dock. The passage is a 1,000-mile network of protected channels, fjords, and straits stretching from Seattle or Vancouver to the ports of Southeast Alaska. Ships transit the passage as part of their itinerary, sailing continuously through the protected waters without stopping between designated port calls. There is no pier, no tender operation, and no shore access during the scenic cruising portions. Passengers experience the Inside Passage entirely from the ship — from observation decks, balcony cabins, and the forward-facing lounges that fill quickly during scenic sections. Wheelchair accessibility is excellent aboard cruise ships transiting the passage, as all public viewing areas are accessible via elevator. The cost of the Inside Passage experience is included in Alaska cruise fares, which typically range from $800 to $5,000 per person for a 7-day round-trip itinerary depending on cruise line and cabin category.

Getting Around

Getting around during Inside Passage scenic cruising means navigating your ship rather than any external destination. There are no taxis, buses, rental cars, or walking routes — you are aboard a cruise ship transiting a waterway. The entire experience takes place on the vessel, and your "getting around" consists of moving between viewing locations on the ship to catch the best scenery.

The forward observation lounge is the most popular viewing spot and fills early on scenic cruising days. Arrive at least 30 minutes before scheduled glacier approaches. Upper outdoor decks provide the best vantage for whale watching and wildlife spotting. Balcony cabins offer private viewing — arguably the best investment for an Alaska cruise, as wildlife appears without warning and having your own outdoor space means you never miss a moment. Balcony cabin upgrades typically cost $200-$800 more than interior cabins per person for a 7-day Alaska itinerary.

For passengers with mobility limitations, elevators connect all public decks, and wheelchair-accessible viewing areas are available on the observation deck and in the main lounge. The ship provides blankets on the upper deck free of charge during glacier approaches, as temperatures can drop significantly near tidewater glaciers. Moving around the ship during the Inside Passage is generally comfortable — the protected waters mean far less motion than open ocean sailing, and most passengers can walk the decks without difficulty. There is no additional fare or fee for accessing any viewing area aboard the ship.

Excursions

Inside Passage excursions are unlike typical port excursions because the scenic cruising portions have no shore access. However, cruise lines offer structured onboard activities during transit days, and the ports along the passage (Ketchikan, Juneau, Skagway) have extensive ship excursion programs. This section covers both the onboard scenic cruising experience and the independent exploration options available at ports along the route.

Ship Excursion: Glacier Bay Scenic Cruising

The premier Inside Passage ship excursion is the full-day Glacier Bay cruise, included in your cruise fare at no additional cost. National Park Service rangers board the ship and provide live commentary as the vessel navigates 65 miles into the bay over 8-10 hours. You will see Margerie Glacier and Grand Pacific Glacier up close. Glacier calving — watching massive chunks of ice break away and crash into the sea — is the highlight. The ranger program includes educational talks throughout the day. This excursion costs $0 extra beyond the cruise fare. Not all ships have Glacier Bay permits, so book ahead on itineraries that include this stop, as permit-bearing sailings sell out months in advance. An Alaska cruise with Glacier Bay access costs approximately $1,200-$5,000 per person for a 7-day itinerary.

Independent Exploration at Ports

At ports along the Inside Passage, independent exploration is straightforward and often more rewarding than organized tours. In Ketchikan, walking the historic Creek Street district is free, and the Totem Heritage Center costs $6 per adult. In Juneau, the Mendenhall Glacier Visitor Center is accessible by city bus for $2 each way, and entrance costs $5 per person. In Skagway, the historic boardwalk district is entirely walkable from the cruise pier at no cost. For passengers who prefer independent exploration, the guaranteed return to the ship is your own responsibility — watch the all-aboard time carefully. Most ports provide walking maps at the pier for free.

Wildlife Watching from the Ship

The Inside Passage itself is one of the world's great wildlife viewing corridors. From the ship, you can spot humpback whales ($0 — just watch), orcas, bald eagles, bears on distant shorelines, sea lions, and harbor seals on ice floes. Binoculars are essential and cost $100-$300 if purchased before the trip. The ship's naturalist (available on many Alaska sailings) provides guided commentary from the observation deck at no additional cost. Photography workshops are sometimes offered for $30-$50 per session. Waterproof camera covers cost $20-$35 and are worth the investment for deck photography. The best wildlife viewing occurs during early morning and late evening hours, when many passengers are at dinner. Book ahead for specialty dining if you want to eat early and be on deck during prime viewing time.

Port Excursion Highlights

Along the passage, port excursions range from $50 helicopter glacier landings in Juneau to $200 floatplane bear-viewing flights in Ketchikan. Whale watching tours cost $150-$200 per person at most ports. The White Pass scenic railway in Skagway costs $130 per person and is one of the most popular ship excursion options in Alaska. For passengers with walking difficulty, many excursions offer accessible alternatives including wheelchair-friendly bus tours for $60-$80.

Depth Soundings

The Inside Passage is one of the most honest scenic cruising experiences in the industry. The beauty is real, the wildlife is wild, and the glaciers are genuinely awe-inspiring. However, it is important to set realistic expectations. Rain is common — Ketchikan receives over 150 inches annually — and overcast skies can obscure mountain views for entire days. The postcard-perfect sunny Alaska day happens, but not every day. Experienced Alaska cruisers know to dress for rain and celebrate sunshine as a bonus rather than an expectation.

The financial cost of an Inside Passage cruise is moderate by industry standards. Budget $800-$2,000 per person for interior cabins on mainstream lines, or $2,000- $5,000 for balcony cabins that offer the best scenic viewing value. Despite the premium, balcony cabins are worth the cost in Alaska — you will see more wildlife from your private balcony than from any scheduled viewing event. Port excursions add $200-$600 per person to the overall cost, and some of the best experiences (walking Creek Street in Ketchikan, watching glaciers calve from the ship) are entirely free. The Inside Passage rewards patience and attention more than spending, and the passengers who get the most from it are those who simply stand on deck, watch, and wait for nature to reveal itself.

FAQs
Is the Inside Passage rough sailing?

Generally very calm. The protected waters behind the island chain mean far less swell than open ocean. Brief open-water sections like Queen Charlotte Strait can be rougher, but they are usually manageable for most passengers. The Inside Passage is one of the smoothest cruise routes in the world, making it excellent for passengers who are sensitive to motion or have mobility concerns.

Will I see glaciers calving in the Inside Passage?

At Glacier Bay or Tracy Arm, very likely. Ships position for extended viewing, sometimes spending several hours near glacier faces. Calving is unpredictable but happens frequently — you may see several calving events during a single approach. The thunderous crack of ice breaking away from the glacier face, followed by the splash and resulting wave, is one of the most memorable experiences in Alaska cruising.

Should I book a balcony or inside cabin for an Alaska cruise?

Balcony cabins are strongly recommended for Alaska and worth the premium of $200-$800 per person. Wildlife appears unexpectedly — whales, eagles, bears on shore — and glacier viewing from your private balcony is magical. Inside cabins work on a budget, but you will miss spontaneous wildlife moments that happen throughout the day and night. At 3 a.m. in June, the midnight sun light show is best enjoyed from your own balcony.

What is the best month to cruise the Inside Passage?

June and July offer the longest daylight hours and warmest temperatures, making them ideal for wildlife viewing and glacier excursions. May and September have fewer crowds, lower prices, and the chance of seeing northern lights in September. All months from May through September provide good wildlife viewing, though whale sightings peak in June and July when humpbacks are most active in the feeding grounds.

Do I need binoculars for the Inside Passage?

Yes, binoculars are strongly recommended. Good quality binoculars cost $100-$300 and are one of the best investments for an Alaska cruise. Much wildlife is visible from the ship but at a distance — bears on shorelines, eagles perched in trees, and whale spouts on the horizon all benefit from magnification. Some ships loan binoculars from the observation deck, but availability is limited.
