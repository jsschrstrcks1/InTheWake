Honolulu: Doing It All in One Day

We docked at Pier 2 right in downtown Honolulu – the name means "protected bay" in Hawaiian, and standing on deck watching the morning light spill across the water, you understand why the ancients chose this gentle harbor. The ukuleles from Aloha Tower marketplace were already playing their bright welcome as we stepped ashore. This city has been the capital of the Hawaiian Islands since 1845, when King Kamehameha III moved the seat of his kingdom here from Lahaina. But the history runs deeper still – back in 1795, his father Kamehameha I arrived in this same harbor with nearly one thousand war canoes and ten thousand warriors, an armada that secured his rule over the entire island chain in the fierce Battle of Nuʻuanu. The peaceful bay we sailed into carries centuries of arrival and departure in its protected waters.

We took an early Uber to Pearl Harbor – twenty minutes through the awakening city – and arrived just after opening. The Arizona memorial boat leaves every fifteen minutes and we walked straight on. Standing over the sunken battleship with the oil still bubbling up from her ruptured tanks felt exactly as heavy and necessary as it should. The USS Arizona went down in just nine minutes on the morning of December 7th, 1941, taking 1,177 souls with her. She still holds 1,102 of those men in her iron hull – a tomb and a testament resting in the harbor that King Kamehameha himself once ordered harvested for oysters to please foreign traders who valued pearls. The stillness above that shattered deck is profound. We stood quietly, then walked across the harbor to the USS Missouri – the massive battleship where Japan signed the unconditional surrender on September 2nd, 1945. The docent called them "the bookends of World War II in the Pacific," and that phrase stayed with me all day. The Arizona marks where it began; the Missouri marks where it ended. Both ships rest here, in the same harbor, carrying the full weight of that history between them.

Back in Waikiki by eleven – Waikiki means "spouting fresh water," named for the springs that once fed this shoreline – we had plenty of time to walk the beach, grab a shave ice at Waiola, and hike Diamond Head. The Hawaiians called that extinct volcanic cone Lēʻahi, "the point of the tuna fish," for its sharp ridgeline cutting into the sky. The trail is fully paved now, 1.6 miles round-trip, hot but very doable. We were at the summit in forty-five minutes and the panoramic sweep of the island spreading out below us – the city, the harbor, the endless Pacific – made every step worth it. Lunch was proper island food: kalua pork plate with laulau and a side of ahi poke, washed down with a cold Longboard lager at Duke's while we watched surfers ride waves under the shadow of Lēʻahi. For dessert we shared haupia – a coconut milk pudding that tastes like the ocean breeze feels.

We spent the afternoon wandering Historic Downtown. The bronze King Kamehameha statue stands tall in front of the old judiciary building – he's the warrior-king who united all the Hawaiian Islands, first by conquest and then by diplomacy, leaving a kingdom his descendants would rule for nearly a century. Across the street sits Kawaiahaʻo Church, built from coral blocks in 1842, still holding services in Hawaiian and English every Sunday. Then we walked to ʻIolani Palace, the only former royal residence on American soil. King Kalākaua built it in 1882 after circumnavigating the globe, bringing back ideas from the great courts of Europe. The palace had electricity, telephones, and indoor plumbing before the White House did – a technological marvel dressed in what architects called "American Florentine" style, the only building of its kind in the world. You can still see the throne room where Hawaiian sovereignty ended when the monarchy was overthrown in 1893, just eleven years after the palace rose. The beauty and the sorrow stand side by side in those elegant rooms. Afternoon traffic back to the ship was light and we were onboard with hours to spare. The pros: everything is genuinely close and the public bus system works perfectly. The cons: Waikiki is busy, but the beach is long and the vibe stays remarkably relaxed.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing silently on the Arizona memorial while a light trade-wind rain started and a rainbow appeared over Pearl Harbor – the past and the present sharing the exact same sky, the old Hawaiian name for this place suddenly making perfect sense.

Cruise Terminal & Docking

Cruise ships typically berth at the historic Aloha Tower complex in downtown Honolulu. Norwegian Cruise Line vessels use Pier 2, located approximately 400 yards to the south. The terminal sits just 15 minutes from Honolulu International Airport and offers convenient access to the Waikiki resort district.

Getting Around Honolulu

Pier 2/11 is downtown – Uber/taxi to Waikiki 10–15 min, to Pearl Harbor 20–30 min depending on traffic. TheBus #20 or #42 are reliable and cheap alternatives.

The Red Line Trolley operates from 9:21 AM to 5:21 PM with departures every 40 minutes. Day passes cost $32 and can be purchased at the DFS Galleria or Ala Moana Center. The city's public bus system provides direct service and is particularly useful for visiting Chinatown. Taxis to Pearl Harbor typically cost $25-30, often less expensive than organized shore excursions from the ship.

Top Attractions

Pearl Harbor and the USS Arizona Memorial remain the most poignant attractions—tickets are distributed first-come, first-served, so arriving early is essential. The Arizona sank in just nine minutes during the December 7th, 1941 Japanese surprise attack, taking 1,177 seamen down with her; 1,102 of those souls remain entombed within her hull. The harbor itself carries deep Hawaiian history – King Kamehameha once ordered oyster harvesting here to obtain pearls for foreign traders. Today the harbor holds what historians call "the bookends of World War II in the Pacific": the USS Arizona marks where the war began on December 7th, 1941, and the USS Missouri – where Japan signed the unconditional surrender documents on September 2nd, 1945 – marks where it ended. The USS Bowfin submarine and Pacific Aviation Museum round out the military history experience.

Waikiki Beach – the name means "spouting fresh water" in Hawaiian – offers the classic Hawaiian beach experience: world-famous resort playground, legendary surfing, and pau hana (happy hour) overlooking Diamond Head's extinct volcanic crater. The Hawaiians called Diamond Head "Lēʻahi," meaning "point of the tuna fish," for its distinctive profile. The hike to the summit rewards you with panoramic views of the entire island. Historic Downtown holds deep cultural treasures: the bronze King Kamehameha statue honors the warrior-king who united the Hawaiian Islands in 1810 after arriving in this harbor in 1795 with nearly one thousand war canoes and ten thousand warriors, securing victory in the fierce Battle of Nuʻuanu. Kawaiahaʻo Church preserves Hawaiian spiritual heritage, and ʻIolani Palace stands as the only former royal residence on American soil. King Kalākaua built the palace in 1882 after circumnavigating the globe, creating an architectural masterpiece in the "American Florentine" style – the only building of this style in the world. Remarkably, the palace had electricity, telephones, and indoor plumbing before the White House. The monarchy was overthrown in 1893, just eleven years after the palace was completed.

The Bishop Museum offers the finest collection of Hawaiian natural history and Pacific Island culture under one roof. If you have time and transportation, the Polynesian Cultural Center (about 90 minutes north) presents living exhibits of Hawaii, Samoa, Fiji, Tahiti, and other Pacific Island cultures through dance, music, and traditional crafts. Honolulu's Chinatown district has thrived for over two centuries and features authentic markets, herbal shops, and family-run restaurants serving dim sum and noodle soups that locals line up for. Honolulu itself – meaning "protected bay" – has served as the capital of the Hawaiian kingdom since 1845, when King Kamehameha III moved the seat of government here from Lahaina.

Shopping

Ala Moana Center ranks among the world's largest open-air shopping destinations, featuring more than 200 stores. Complimentary shuttle buses run to Walmart and Hilo Hatties for bargain hunters. For a more authentic local experience, Chinatown's markets sell fresh herbs, tropical produce, and traditional flower leis.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Traffic into Waikiki can slow after 10 a.m. – an early start keeps the entire day relaxed and enjoyable.

Currency & Payments: The currency is US dollars, and credit cards are accepted throughout the island. Hawaii's sales tax ranges from 4-6%.

Tipping: Standard tipping practices apply: 15-20% at restaurants, 10-15% for taxi drivers, and $1-2 per bag for bellhops.

Emergency Services: In case of emergencies, dial 911.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Honolulu worth it?
A: The most "do-able" Hawaiian port day.

Q: Best thing?
A: Pearl Harbor first, then Waikiki + Diamond Head.

Q: How long for Pearl Harbor + Diamond Head?
A: 7–8 hours total with zero rush.

Q: Walk from port?
A: To Chinatown/Aloha Tower yes; everything else needs transport.
