Bordeaux: My Wine Capital

The ship docks 45 minutes from the city center, but the shuttle drops you at the most beautiful urban waterfront in Europe – the Garonne reflecting 18th-century façades like a mirror. We walked the entire Miroir d'Eau (the world's largest reflecting pool) barefoot while kids splashed and the Place de la Bourse shimmered behind us.

What strikes you first is the sheer scale of beauty — in 2007 UNESCO designated 1,810 hectares of Bordeaux as a World Heritage Site, making it the most extensive urban heritage site on Earth. That's half the entire city. I counted more than 350 buildings with bronze plaques declaring them national Historical Monuments, from Romanesque bell towers to neoclassical hôtels particuliers. The Celts founded this place in the 3rd century BC, but what you see today was shaped during the 18th-century Age of Enlightenment, when merchants made fortunes shipping wine to England and built palaces along the quays. In 1453 Charles VII won the city back from the English at the Battle of Castillon – ending the Hundred Years' War and giving French kings control of the vineyards. The wine never stopped flowing after that.

Speaking of wine — this region is staggering. Over 6,000 wineries stretch across 270,000 acres, divided into six great regions: Médoc, Graves and Sauternes, Blaye and Bourg, Saint-Emilion and Pomerol and Fronsac, and Entre-Deux-Mers. Médoc alone is the world's leading red wine producer, with more than 16,000 hectares and eight appellations. I took a half-day excursion to Château Margaux – the neoclassical mansion built in 1802 looks like a Greek temple rising from vineyards – and then to Mouton-Rothschild, where the wine cellar smells like centuries of oak and ambition. Château Haut-Brion has been making wine since 1525. These names aren't just labels – they're living history you can taste.

Cité du Vin is worth every penny – interactive exhibits, smells of oak and blackcurrant, and a tasting on the 8th floor with 360° views over the river. The building itself is shaped like a wine decanter, all golden curves and glass catching the light. We had lunch at Le Quatrième Mur – Philippe Etchebest's brasserie opposite the opera – duck breast with perfect crispy skin and a glass of Saint-Émilion that tasted like liquid velvet. In the afternoon we took the tram to Saint-Émilion village (45 min) – medieval streets surrounded by actual grand cru vineyards. The pros: wine heaven with zero pretension. The cons: hot in summer, but the river breeze saves you.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing on the Belvédère at Cité du Vin with a glass of 2015 Château Figeac watching the sun set over the Garonne while the entire golden city turned pink.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Bordeaux stone reflects heat – light clothing and plenty of water make wine tasting even more pleasant.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Bordeaux worth it?
A: The most elegant wine city on Earth.

Q: Best thing?
A: Cité du Vin + riverfront wander.

Q: How long for Cité du Vin?
A: 3–4 hours including tasting.

Q: Walk from port?
A: No – shuttle required.
