Guam: Where America's Day Begins

My Visit to Guam

I stepped off the gangway at Apra Harbor just as the morning sun cleared the ridge above the naval station, and the humid air hit me like a warm towel pressed against my face. The smell of frangipani mixed with diesel exhaust from the container yard, and I could hear the low hum of a forklift somewhere beyond the terminal fence. We had arrived at Guam — "Where America's Day Begins," the westernmost point of US soil in the Pacific — and my wife and I had exactly nine hours to see what this island had been keeping from us for years of planning itineraries that always seemed to route somewhere else.

Our first stop was the War in the Pacific National Historical Park at Asan Beach, and I was not prepared for what it did to me. The drive from the port took only fifteen minutes, but the transition felt like crossing into a different century. The turquoise lagoon looked deceptively peaceful, sunlight dancing on ripples that hid nothing of the coral reef below. However, the moment I walked past the first Japanese coastal defense gun — still aimed seaward, its barrel corroded green and pocked with rust — the beauty of the place took on a weight I hadn't expected. I stood in the shade of a palm tree beside a weathered concrete pillbox, reading the brass plaque listing the names of Marines who had fought and died on this beach in July 1944. The sound of waves breaking on the reef was the only noise. My heart swelled with something I could not name — gratitude, perhaps, or grief for young men who never got to grow old in the country they defended. I whispered a quiet prayer for them and for the Chamorro families who endured three years of occupation before the liberation came.

We drove north to Two Lovers Point through lush green hills blanketed in tropical jungle, the road winding past small villages and the occasional roadside barbecue stand selling chicken and red rice for $5 a plate. The legend of Puntan Dos Amantes says two ancient Chamorro lovers leapt from the 400-foot cliff rather than be separated by their feuding families. Standing at the viewpoint, looking down at the reef-laced water so far below, I felt the vertigo in my stomach and the wind tugging at my hat. The silence up there was enormous. Despite the small crowd of tourists ringing the brass bell for good luck — the admission fee is $3 per person — my wife and I found a quiet spot at the railing and just watched the ocean stretch toward the Philippines without saying anything for several minutes. Sometimes the most important conversations happen without words.

Back in Tumon Bay by midday, we found the beaches exactly as advertised: powdery white sand, water so clear I could see my toes at chest depth, and a gentle warmth that never quite became oppressive. I rented a snorkel set from a beachside shack for $10 and spent an hour drifting over the reef inside the bay, watching parrotfish gnaw at coral heads and schools of silver jacks turning in formation like a single shimmering organism. The taste of salt on my lips and the cool pressure of water against my chest felt like the purest kind of freedom. Yet even here, the war's presence lingered — I noticed a small concrete bunker half-buried in the sand at the far end of the beach, its gun slit staring blankly at the lagoon where families now splashed and laughed.

We spent our afternoon at the Chamorro Village in Hagatna, where we wandered among stalls selling carved latte stone replicas, woven coconut baskets, and fiery bottles of homemade donne sauce. Although the famous Wednesday night gathering was not running during our visit, the daytime vendors were welcoming and unhurried. An older woman named Rosa sold me a small wooden carving of a proa, the ancient Chamorro sailing canoe, for $15 and told me her grandmother had survived the occupation by hiding in the jungle caves above Agat. Her voice was steady, but her eyes told a story that words could not. I tasted kelaguen at a tiny stall — spicy Chamorro chicken salad with lemon, coconut, and scorching hot peppers — and my mouth burned in the best possible way while we sat on a bench beneath a breadfruit tree and watched children playing in the plaza.

On the drive back to the port, we stopped at a viewpoint above Cetti Bay where the jungle dropped steeply to a dark blue cove far below. The road south of Hagatna winds through terrain so thick with vegetation that the sunlight barely reaches the asphalt. I could smell wet earth and rotting leaves, and the bird calls from the canopy were sharp and unfamiliar. Still, even in this wildness there were reminders — a rusted artillery piece on a concrete pad beside the road, a small memorial cross tucked into the bushes, the remains of a Japanese communications bunker slowly being swallowed by roots and vines.

Tears came to my eyes unexpectedly as we passed the Asan Bay Overlook on the way back, where a simple stone monument marks the spot where the first wave of Marines came ashore under heavy fire. I had read about this battle in books, but standing where it happened — feeling the same heat, hearing the same waves, seeing the same ridge where Japanese gun emplacements had poured fire onto the landing craft — changed something in me. I learned that history is not truly history until you stand in the place where it happened and let it press against your skin. Looking back, I realized that Guam had given me something no beach resort ever could: the understanding that beauty and sorrow can occupy the same ground, that the same ocean that brings tourists on cruise ships once carried young men to war, and that the Chamorro people who wave and smile at us from their roadside stalls carry a resilience that I can only hope to understand.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Standing before the ancient latte stones at the Plaza de Espana in Hagatna, massive coral pillars shaped by Chamorro hands over a thousand years ago — still standing after typhoons, invasions, and empires, silent witnesses to 4,000 years of unbroken island memory. I touched the rough surface of one pillar and felt the cool, pitted coral beneath my fingertips, and for the first time in a long while, I understood what permanence really means.

The Cruise Port

Ships dock at the Port of Guam in Apra Harbor on the western shore, one of the finest natural deepwater harbors in the Pacific. The terminal sits about 20 minutes south of Tumon Bay's resort district and 15 minutes from the capital of Hagatna. The harbor itself is an active US naval base — you will see destroyers and submarines sharing the anchorage, a quiet reminder that Guam remains one of America's most strategically important territories. Taxis and tour buses meet the ships at the pier. Many cruise visitors opt to rent cars for maximum flexibility exploring the island's scattered historical sites and remote southern beaches. The terminal area has basic amenities including restrooms and a small waiting area, but most services are in Tumon or Hagatna. Wheelchair accessible pathways lead from the ship to the taxi staging area.

Getting Around

Guam has no public transit system worth planning around — taxis, rental cars, and tour buses are your main options. Taxi fares are fixed by law: around $25-30 from the port to Tumon Bay, and $15-20 to Hagatna. Rental cars are widely available at $45-65 per day and roads are in good condition; driving is on the right side, signs are in English, and distances are manageable since the island is only 30 miles long and 9 miles wide at its widest point.

Many cruise visitors book island tours through their ship, which handle transportation and hit the major sites efficiently. If you want to explore independently, a rental car gives you the freedom to linger at quieter beaches and historical sites off the beaten path. Uber and Lyft also operate on Guam, though availability can be limited outside the Tumon area. For visitors with mobility concerns, most major attractions including Two Lovers Point and the War in the Pacific Visitor Center have accessible parking and paved pathways. The Tumon Bay resort strip is flat and walkable, with sidewalks connecting most hotels, shops, and restaurants along the main road. Budget approximately $60-80 per person for a full-day taxi tour with multiple stops.

Beaches

Tumon Bay is the crown jewel of Guam's beaches — a crescent of powdery white sand backed by resort hotels and protected by a fringing reef that keeps the water calm and clear. Snorkeling along the reef edges reveals parrotfish, sea cucumbers, and clusters of brain coral in water warm enough to stay in for hours. Ypao Beach Park, at the southern end of Tumon, offers free public access, showers, picnic shelters, and lifeguards on duty. Gun Beach to the north is quieter and named for the Japanese coastal defense guns still visible in the cliffside.

Excursions & Activities

Guam offers a rare blend of pristine Pacific beauty, deep indigenous culture, and the profound weight of 20th-century history. Whether you book a ship excursion or go independent, the island rewards exploration. If you choose to explore independently, book ahead for popular activities like wreck diving, as spaces fill quickly on cruise ship days. For those concerned about timing, a ship excursion provides a guaranteed return to the pier before departure.

War in the Pacific National Historical Park

This is not a single site — it is seven distinct units spread across Guam preserving the American liberation of 1944. The Asan Beach Unit features the main Visitor Center (free admission), beach landing sites, and hilltop gun emplacements. Walking the coastal trail you will pass Japanese pillboxes, American artillery positions, and interpretive signs explaining the brutal 21-day battle. It is sobering, necessary, and beautifully maintained. Plan at least 2-3 hours if you want to absorb the history properly. The Visitor Center is wheelchair accessible with paved paths to the main memorial.

Two Lovers Point (Puntan Dos Amantes)

The clifftop viewpoint rises 400 feet above the Philippine Sea and offers panoramic views of Tumon Bay and the northern coastline. Admission is $3 per person. There is a small gift shop, a brass bell you can ring for good luck, and viewing platforms with safety railings. Go in the late afternoon when the light turns golden and the tour buses have cleared out. The drive from the port is about 20 minutes and taxis charge approximately $25 each way.

Tumon Bay Water Sports

This crescent-shaped beach is Guam's main tourist strip — resorts, restaurants, duty-free shopping, and turquoise water that looks digital until you wade in. The bay is protected by a reef, so the swimming is gentle and safe. Snorkeling gear rents for $10-15 at beachfront shacks, and dive shops offer everything from beginner courses ($95-130) to advanced wreck dives on sunken Japanese freighters. If you only have an afternoon, Tumon delivers the classic tropical beach experience without complications.

Latte Stone Park & Chamorro Village

In Hagatna you will find ancient latte stones — massive coral pillars topped with hemispherical capstones, the architectural signature of the Chamorro people who have lived here for over 4,000 years. These are not replicas; they are the real foundation stones of ancient houses and meeting halls, relocated and preserved. Nearby, the Chamorro Village comes alive Wednesday nights for the weekly gathering — local food, live music, and handicrafts. Even during daytime visits, the stalls offer carved latte stone souvenirs ($10-25) and bottles of locally made hot sauce.

Diving the WWII Wrecks

Guam's underwater world rivals its topside attractions. The SMS Cormoran (a WWI German wreck) and the Tokai Maru (a WWII Japanese freighter) lie together in Apra Harbor — two wars, two ships, one dive site. A two-tank dive costs around $120-150. Visibility averages 80-100 feet, water temperature stays near 80 degrees year-round, and the marine life is abundant. If you are certified, this is an extraordinary dive. If you are not, shore snorkeling at Piti Bomb Holes Marine Preserve offers colorful coral and tropical fish in crystal-clear shallows.

History

Guam's history stretches back over 4,000 years to the first Chamorro settlers who arrived by outrigger canoe from Southeast Asia. The latte stones scattered across the island date from approximately 900-1600 AD and represent one of the most distinctive architectural achievements in the Pacific. Ferdinand Magellan landed at Umatac Bay in 1521, making Guam one of the earliest European contact points in the Pacific. Spain governed the island for over three centuries until the Spanish-American War of 1898 transferred control to the United States.

Japan seized Guam in December 1941, just hours after the attack on Pearl Harbor, and occupied the island for nearly three years. The US liberation in July 1944 — the Battle of Guam — was one of the bloodiest campaigns in the Pacific theater, with over 18,000 American and Japanese casualties. Today, war memorials and preserved fortifications across the island ensure that sacrifice is not forgotten.

Cultural Highlights

Chamorro culture is the heartbeat of Guam, a living connection to 4,000 years of Pacific island heritage. The Chamorro language, closely related to Filipino and Indonesian languages, is still spoken alongside English, and visitors will hear "Hafa Adai" (hello) everywhere they go. The island's cuisine, music, dance, and family-centered values reflect both indigenous roots and the layered influences of Spanish colonial rule, American governance, and Japanese occupation.

Latte stones — the iconic pillared foundations of ancient Chamorro buildings — have become the symbol of Guam itself. The Guam Museum in Hagatna offers exhibits on Chamorro navigation, weaving, and oral history, and admission is $5 for adults. Respect at heritage sites is expected and appreciated.

Shopping

Guam's duty-free status makes it a significant shopping destination, particularly for Japanese and Korean tourists who stock up on designer goods, cosmetics, and electronics at prices well below their home countries. The DFS Galleria in Tumon is the flagship duty-free store, offering luxury brands in an air-conditioned mall setting. Tumon Sands Plaza and the Micronesia Mall provide broader retail options from budget to mid-range.

For locally made goods, the Chamorro Village stalls sell carved wooden items, coconut oil products, boonie pepper hot sauce, and woven baskets. Prices for handmade souvenirs typically range from $8-30. American chain stores are also present — Kmart, Ross, and Costco — for visitors who need practical items or want to stock up on American products not easily found elsewhere in the Pacific.

Food & Dining

Chamorro cuisine blends indigenous, Spanish, Filipino, and American influences into something uniquely Guamanian. Red rice (tinaktak) gets its color and flavor from achote seeds. Kelaguen is a spicy salad made with grilled chicken, seafood, or venison, mixed with lemon juice, coconut, and hot peppers — refreshing, tangy, and addictive. BBQ short ribs appear on nearly every menu, often marinated in soy, garlic, and sugar, then grilled over charcoal.

For dessert, try latiya, a custard cake topped with cinnamon and sugar, or apigigi, coconut and tapioca steamed in banana leaves. Local beer options include the easy-drinking Guam Brewery lagers, while fresh coconut water and buko juice (young coconut) provide natural refreshment in the tropical heat. A typical Chamorro lunch plate costs $8-12 at local restaurants.

Special Notices

Typhoon Season: June through November is typhoon season, with peak risk in August-October. Cruise lines monitor conditions closely and may reroute if necessary. Travel insurance is strongly recommended for cruises during these months.

Sun Safety: Guam sits at 13 degrees north latitude — the sun is intense year-round. Apply reef-safe sunscreen frequently, wear a hat, and carry water. Heat exhaustion is a real risk for visitors walking outdoor sites like the War in the Pacific park.

Respect at Memorial Sites: WWII sites and Chamorro heritage locations are not merely attractions — they are places of remembrance and cultural significance. Speak quietly, stay on marked paths, and do not remove artifacts or disturb the sites.

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Guam's tropical climate means heat and humidity year-round. Bring sunscreen, a hat, and plenty of water. Sudden rain showers are common, but they are brief and warm. Respect is expected at WWII memorials and Chamorro cultural sites — these are not just tourist attractions, they are sacred ground to the people who live here.

Currency & Payments: US dollar. Credit cards widely accepted at hotels, restaurants, and shops. No passport required for US citizens, but bring government-issued ID. ATMs are plentiful in Tumon and Hagatna.

Tipping: Standard US practice applies: 15-20% at restaurants, $1-2 per bag for hotel porters, 10-15% for taxi drivers.

Language: English and Chamorro are official languages; English is universally spoken. A few Chamorro phrases are appreciated: "Hafa Adai" (hello), "Si Yu'os ma'ase" (thank you).

Duty-Free Shopping: Guam's duty-free status makes it a shopping destination for designer goods, electronics, and cosmetics. The DFS Galleria and Tumon Sands Plaza offer the biggest selections. Expect to save 20-40% compared to mainland US retail prices on luxury items.

Emergency Services: Dial 911. Guam Memorial Hospital is in Tamuning, about 10 minutes from Tumon Bay. US health insurance generally works here since Guam is a US territory.

Practical Information

Time Zone: Chamorro Standard Time (ChST), UTC+10. Guam is a full day ahead of the US mainland.

Electricity: Standard US outlets (120V, 60Hz). No adapter needed for American devices.

Cell Service: Major US carriers (AT&T, T-Mobile) provide coverage. International visitors should check roaming rates.

Water: Tap water is safe to drink throughout the island.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Is Guam a US territory?
A: Yes. Guam is an organized, unincorporated territory of the United States. US citizens do not need a passport, the currency is US dollars, and English is widely spoken alongside Chamorro. You can use your US driver's license and health insurance here.

Q: What is the best WWII site to visit?
A: War in the Pacific National Historical Park at Asan Beach is the most comprehensive option. The Visitor Center is free and provides excellent context. Plan 2-3 hours to see the main sites including the beach landing areas and hilltop gun emplacements. The site is accessible for visitors with limited mobility, with paved paths to the main memorial areas.

Q: Can you see Two Lovers Point from the ship?
A: Not clearly. It is about 20 minutes north of the port — take a taxi ($25 each way) and plan 45 minutes for the viewpoint and photos. Admission is $3 per person.

Q: Is the diving really world-class?
A: Absolutely. Crystal-clear water with 80-100 foot visibility, WWII wrecks in Apra Harbor, vibrant reefs, and warm temperatures year-round. A two-tank dive runs $120-150. Even non-divers can enjoy outstanding snorkeling at protected reef areas.

Q: What are latte stones?
A: Ancient Chamorro architectural pillars — massive coral columns topped with hemispherical capstones, used as building foundations over 1,000 years ago. They are the iconic symbol of Guam's indigenous heritage and can be seen at Latte Stone Park in Hagatna.

Q: When is the best time to visit Chamorro Village?
A: Wednesday night for the weekly gathering — food, music, and local culture in one spot. Daytime visits are also worthwhile for handicraft shopping and quiet browsing, though the atmosphere is much quieter without the evening festivities.

Q: Is Guam wheelchair accessible?
A: Major attractions including Two Lovers Point viewing platform, the War in the Pacific Visitor Center, Tumon Bay hotels, and the DFS Galleria are wheelchair accessible. Beach wheelchair access varies by location — Ypao Beach Park in Tumon has the best accessible facilities. Taxis can accommodate folding wheelchairs with advance notice.
