Grenada

Grenada: My Spice Island Paradise

The sail-in through the Carenage horseshoe harbor with colorful houses stacked up the hills is one of the prettiest in the Caribbean. St. George's, Grenada's capital, has been called "the most appealing capital in the entire Caribbean" – and I can't argue. Those French colonial red tile roofs climbing the hillside, the turquoise water, the scent of nutmeg in the air... it's intoxicating.

This is the true Spice Island – Grenada produces a staggering one-third of the world's nutmeg supply, along with cloves, cinnamon, mace, ginger, bay leaves, and turmeric. The island wears its nickname proudly, and you'll smell why the moment you step ashore.

My perfect day: off the ship and straight to Grand Anse Beach (quick 15-min taxi) – two miles of perfect white sand and calm water that ranks in the world's top ten beaches for good reason. You can also catch a water taxi from the port if you prefer a scenic approach.

Lunch at Umbrellas Beach Bar – best fish roti on the island, and their rum punch is famous among visitors. Afternoon is the Concord Falls hike and chocolate tour at Belmont Estate (nutmeg ice cream is life). For history buffs, Fort George offers panoramic harbor views from its perch above St. George's – the French built it in 1705, and it's still commanding. Fort Frederick, begun by the French and completed by the British in 1783, sprawls across four levels and offers even more spectacular vistas.

The island's story is written in layers: French colonization in the 1600s (tragically wiping out the indigenous Caribs), British capture in 1762, and hard-won independence in 1974. You can read this history in the architecture, the place names, the forts that still stand sentinel.

Hurricanes Ivan and Emily devastated Grenada's nutmeg industry in 2004-2005, but the island has shown remarkable resilience. The trees are maturing again, production is recovering, and the world's second-largest nutmeg processing plant continues to anchor the economy. The spice must flow.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Swimming under the waterfall at Concord while a local kid cannonballed in beside me – pure joy.

Getting Around Grenada

Melville Street Cruise Terminal – 10-min walk to town or wander through St. George's red-tiled streets to absorb the French colonial charm. Taxis to Grand Anse are great value, or catch a water taxi for a more scenic journey.

  • Grand Anse Beach: 2-mile stretch of white sand, short taxi or water taxi ride
  • Fort George: Built by French 1705, panoramic harbor views over St. George's
  • Fort Frederick: Four-level fortress, French/British construction 1779-1783
  • Concord Falls: 30-min drive, combine with spice plantation tour
  • Belmont Estate: Chocolate and spice tour, see nutmeg processing
  • Nutmeg Processing Station: World's second-largest facility (Gouyave)

Depth Soundings Ashore

Practical tips before you step off the ship.

Grenada's spice-scented air and winding roads are the island's way of giving you a free aromatherapy tour with panoramic views! The island produces one-third of the world's nutmeg – you'll find it in everything from ice cream to rum punch. Don't miss the nutmeg processing stations where you can see the entire operation from harvest to export.

The island recovered beautifully from devastating hurricanes in 2004-2005 that wiped out 90% of the nutmeg trees. Today's maturing groves are a testament to Grenadian resilience, and the spice industry is thriving again.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Best beach?
A: Grand Anse – two miles of white sand, top-10 world for good reason. Water taxi or quick taxi from port.

Q: Why "Spice Island"?
A: Grenada produces 1/3 of the world's nutmeg, plus cloves, cinnamon, mace, ginger, bay leaves, and turmeric. The entire island smells divine.

Q: Nutmeg ice cream?
A: House of Chocolate in St. George's – absolutely try it!

Q: Best historical sites?
A: Fort George (French 1705) for harbor views, Fort Frederick (1783) for the four-level fortress experience.

Q: Safe?
A: Very – friendliest island in the Caribbean.
