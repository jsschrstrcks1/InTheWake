Drake Passage

The Price of Antarctica

Wikimedia Commons (CC BY-SA)

🌊

Sea Crossing: The Drake Passage is not a destination but the 600-mile ocean crossing between South America and Antarctica. This guide helps you prepare for what many consider the world's roughest seas.

From the Logbook

The captain's announcement came at dinner: "We're entering the Drake. Seas building to 6 meters overnight. Secure loose items." I felt my stomach tighten even before the first swell hit. Within hours, the ship was climbing walls of water and sliding down the other side, a rhythm that continued for 36 hours. The dining room emptied. The expedition staff, cheerfully immune, delivered lectures on penguin biology to whoever could stay upright. I was not among the upright.

My cabin became my entire world. I lay in the dark, listening to the groaning of the hull and the crash of waves against the bow. The smell of the sea came through the ventilation — salt and cold and something metallic. Every few minutes, the ship would shudder as it crested a wave, pause at the summit, and then slide forward into the trough with a stomach-dropping lurch. I heard glasses rattling in the bar above, and once a distant cheer from passengers who had apparently made peace with the motion. Yet I could not move without the world tilting. However, even in my misery, I felt something unexpected: gratitude. The Drake was teaching me that I was not in charge.

By the second day, the seas had moderated enough for me to venture to the observation lounge. I wrapped my hands around a cup of hot tea and watched the Southern Ocean through windows streaked with spray. The water was gray-green, immense, and empty — no land in any direction, just water and sky meeting at a horizon that tilted and swayed. Then I saw my first wandering albatross.

On the return crossing, the Drake was glass — barely a ripple, the horizon so flat and endless I could see the curvature of the Earth. A wandering albatross glided past the observation deck, wingspan wider than a car is long, riding the wind without a single flap. I watched it for twenty minutes, tears forming in my eyes at the effortless grace of this creature that lives its entire life over the most hostile ocean on the planet. Something shifted inside me — the Drake's bargain became clear: sometimes a monster, sometimes a mirror, always a teacher of humility.

That evening, I sat in the lecture hall listening to the expedition leader describe how early explorers crossed these same waters in wooden ships with no stabilizers, no weather forecasts, and no guarantee of survival. The cost they paid was measured not in dollars but in lives. Our crossing, despite its discomfort, was a luxury they could not have imagined. I touched the rough fabric of the lecture chair, felt the warmth of the heated room, tasted the ginger candy I had been living on, and understood for the first time how thin the line is between adventure and survival. Despite the seasickness, despite the fear, I would not trade this crossing for the fly-cruise shortcut. The Drake is the price of Antarctica, and paying it makes the ice feel earned.

The next morning I woke to stillness. The ship had stopped its relentless rolling, and through my cabin window I saw something that took my breath away — the first iceberg, a pale blue wedge floating in water so dark it looked like ink. I dressed quickly and went to the observation deck, where a crowd had already gathered in quiet awe. We had crossed. The Drake was behind us, and Antarctica lay ahead, impossibly white against a steel-gray sky. I noticed my hands were shaking, though not from the cold. Around me, I heard the soft sound of cameras clicking, but also something rarer — silence, the kind that falls when people see something they have dreamed of their entire lives. A woman beside me reached for her husband's hand. Nobody spoke. We simply stood together at the edge of the known world, humbled and grateful, each carrying our own quiet prayer of thanks for safe passage.

Looking back, I learned that the Drake Passage is not an obstacle to be endured but a threshold to be crossed with respect. It teaches patience, it teaches that the sea does not care about our schedules or our comfort, and it teaches that the most meaningful experiences are often the most difficult ones. The real gift was not arriving at Antarctica — it was discovering that I could surrender control, trust the ship, and find wonder even in the waves that tried to break me. I sat that final evening with my journal open, attempting to capture what I had felt during those two days at sea. But words fall short when describing the Drake. It is not a story to be told — it is a crossing to be made. I wrote one line and closed the book: "The ocean does not negotiate." That was enough. It said everything I needed to remember.

Cruise Port and Terminal

The Drake Passage has no cruise port, no terminal, and no land of any kind. It is 600 miles of open Southern Ocean between Cape Horn at the southern tip of South America and the South Shetland Islands near Antarctica. Ships enter the Drake from Ushuaia, Argentina (the most common departure point) or occasionally from Punta Arenas, Chile. There is no dock, no tender operation, and no shore access during the crossing. Passengers with mobility limitations or wheelchair users can navigate the ship's interior throughout the crossing, though movement on deck may be restricted during rough seas. The cost of crossing the Drake is included in expedition cruise fares, which typically range from $6,000 to $25,000 per person depending on cabin class and itinerary length. There is no additional port fee or crossing surcharge. The ship's medical center carries seasickness medication including scopolamine patches (approximately $15-$30 per patch) and meclizine tablets for passengers who did not bring their own supply.

Getting Around

Getting around during the Drake Passage crossing means navigating your expedition ship rather than any external destination. There are no taxis, buses, or walking routes — you are at sea for 36-48 hours with nothing but ocean in every direction. The entire experience takes place aboard the ship, and your mobility depends entirely on sea conditions.

During a Drake Shake (rough crossing with 6-12 meter swells), moving through the ship requires caution. Handrails line every corridor, and the crew secures ropes along key passageways for passengers to grip. Walking on exterior decks may be prohibited during severe weather. Elevators continue to operate, and wheelchair-accessible routes remain available throughout the interior. The observation lounge, library, and lecture hall are typically the most comfortable spaces during rough conditions, as they tend to be located mid-ship where motion is minimized. Stairwells can be challenging when the ship rolls, so take your time and keep one hand on the rail at all times.

During a Drake Lake (calm crossing), the experience is entirely different. Passengers can walk freely on exterior decks, and many spend hours on the observation deck watching for wildlife. The ship's expedition team sets up spotting scopes on the upper deck, available to all passengers at no cost. There is no fare or fee for moving around the ship — everything is included in your expedition cruise price. The key advice for getting around during the Drake crossing is simple: move slowly, hold on, and accept that the ocean sets the pace.

Excursions

The Drake Passage has no shore excursions because there is no shore. However, expedition cruise lines fill the 36-48 hour crossing with structured onboard programming that functions as the excursion experience. These ship excursion activities are included in your cruise fare at no additional cost and are designed to prepare passengers for the Antarctic experience ahead.

Ship Excursion: Expedition Lectures

The primary onboard activity during the Drake crossing is the expedition lecture series. Expert naturalists, marine biologists, and historians deliver presentations on Antarctic wildlife, glaciology, polar history, and photography techniques. These talks typically run 2-3 times per day in the ship's lecture hall. Attendance is free, and the quality is exceptional — many expedition leaders have decades of polar experience. Topics include penguin species identification (cost: free), whale behavior patterns, the history of Shackleton's Endurance expedition, and ice photography workshops. These are essentially independent learning opportunities within the ship's program, and passengers choose which sessions to attend based on personal interest.

Wildlife Watching from Deck

When conditions permit, the observation deck becomes a wildlife viewing platform. The expedition team sets up spotting scopes (free to use) and provides guided commentary on seabirds — particularly albatross, giant petrels, and storm petrels. Binoculars are essential and cost $100-$300 if purchased before the trip. The ship's gift shop may stock basic pairs for $40-$60. Camera equipment rental is not available aboard most expedition ships, so book ahead and bring your own gear. A waterproof camera cover costs $20-$35 and is worth the investment for deck photography during spray-heavy conditions. Wildlife identification cards are provided free of charge and cost nothing to keep.

Independent Activities

Independent time during the Drake crossing is plentiful. The ship's library stocks polar exploration literature (free to borrow), and the lounge areas provide quiet spaces for reading, journaling, or simply watching the ocean. Some passengers use the crossing to rest before the active Antarctic itinerary ahead. The spa offers massage services during calm conditions, typically priced at $80-$150 per session. The gym remains open though equipment use can be challenging in rough seas. Board games and puzzles are available in the lounge at no cost. The guaranteed return to your departure port is built into every expedition itinerary — the ship crosses the Drake twice, once going and once returning, with the return crossing often calmer than the outbound journey. Passengers with walking difficulty or wheelchair users have full access to all interior public spaces and lecture facilities throughout the crossing.

Depth Soundings

The Drake Passage is the most honest passage in cruising. There is no way to dress it up or market it as something pleasant — it is 600 miles of open ocean that can be brutally rough, and every passenger pays the price in some form. However, this honesty is precisely what makes the Drake valuable. Unlike port stops that can be curated and controlled, the Drake is raw nature, indifferent to your comfort or schedule.

The financial cost is significant — expedition cruises through the Drake range from $6,000 to $25,000 per person, and the fly-cruise alternative that skips the crossing costs $2,000- $4,000 more. Yet many experienced polar travelers consider the crossing essential to the Antarctic experience. The value is not in the comfort but in the transformation: arriving at Antarctica after two days of open ocean feels earned rather than delivered. Despite the discomfort and despite the seasickness, most passengers look back on the Drake crossing as one of the most memorable parts of their expedition. The lesson the Drake teaches is timeless — that the most meaningful destinations are often reached through the most difficult passages, and the difficulty is not a defect but a feature.

FAQs
Will I definitely get seasick crossing the Drake Passage?

Not necessarily. Many people cross without problems, especially with preventive medication such as scopolamine patches or meclizine. Those prone to motion sickness should prepare carefully and start medication before entering the Drake. Even rough crossings are survivable, and the ship's medical center can provide additional support. Mid-ship, lower-deck cabins experience the least motion.

Can I fly to Antarctica instead of crossing the Drake?

Yes — "fly-cruise" options fly from Punta Arenas to King George Island, skipping the Drake entirely. This costs approximately $2,000-$4,000 more per person than traditional sailing routes. You spend less time at sea, but some consider the Drake crossing essential to the full Antarctic experience and a rite of passage for polar travelers.

Is the Drake Passage dangerous for modern ships?

Modern expedition ships are specifically designed for Southern Ocean conditions. The Drake is uncomfortable, not dangerous. Ships have stabilizers, reinforced hulls, experienced ice-pilot crews, and conservative navigation practices. Captains can adjust course and speed to minimize discomfort, and the bridge team monitors weather forecasts continuously.

How long does it take to cross the Drake Passage?

Typically 36-48 hours each way, depending on sea conditions, weather patterns, and ship speed. The crossing is approximately 600 miles from Cape Horn to the South Shetland Islands. Most expedition itineraries allocate two full sea days for each crossing, with lectures and activities filling the onboard time.

What wildlife can I see in the Drake Passage?

The Drake is home to wandering albatross (wingspan over 3 meters), black-browed albatross, cape petrels, giant petrels, and storm petrels. Whale sightings are possible on calmer crossings. The Antarctic Convergence zone, crossed approximately midway through the passage, brings a visible change in water temperature, color, and wildlife species.
