Is the Drink Package Worth It? Using the Calculator to Find Your Real Break-Even

The "5 to 7 drinks per day" rule everyone repeats is wrong for the Royal Caribbean Deluxe package in 2026. Three worked examples through the In the Wake calculator — light, moderate, heavy — showing where a la carte still wins and where Deluxe actually starts to pay off.

by Ken Baker | May 17, 2026

Answer first: At Royal Caribbean's 2026 fleet-median Deluxe price of about $77 per person per day plus 18% gratuity (effective $91), you need roughly 6 to 7 cocktail-equivalents per person per day to break even — about 11 for a couple sharing. The widely quoted "5 to 7 drinks per day" rule under-counts because it usually ignores gratuity and assumes only $14 cocktails. Run through the In the Wake calculator at honest consumption levels, a la carte beats Deluxe in every typical case. The package's real break-even is higher than the marketing wants you to believe.

  • You're booking a Royal Caribbean cruise and trying to decide whether to pre-buy Deluxe, Refreshment, or Soda — or skip the package entirely.
  • You're traveling with a spouse who drinks much more (or less) than you do and trying to figure out the stateroom math.
  • You're Crown & Anchor Diamond+ or higher and want to know whether your free-drink vouchers already cover what you'd actually order.

Of every cost decision on a Royal Caribbean cruise, the drink package is the one with the most conflicting advice online. Some sites tell you it's a no-brainer above three drinks a day. Others say five drinks. Others six. The numbers float around because most of them ignore gratuities, mix up package tiers, or quietly assume you drink only the most expensive items on the menu.

The In the Wake drink calculator handles the math the way it actually works. It accepts your specific cruise length, sea-day mix, group size, and drink-by-drink consumption — sodas, coffees, mocktails, beers, wines, cocktails — and compares your a la carte total against each package option, with 18% gratuity baked in. The result is a side-by-side chart. This article walks through three worked examples — light, moderate, heavy — and explains what each one means.

The "5 to 7 Drinks" Rule Is Wrong

Two reasons the conventional break-even number under-counts.

It usually ignores gratuity. Royal Caribbean's Deluxe package median price is about $77 per person per day in 2026 — but at the bar, the 18% gratuity adds about $14 a day to that. The effective cost is closer to $91. At an average cocktail price of $13 plus 18% (effective $15.34), you need 5.9 cocktails per person per day to match the package — not 5 to 6 you'd see quoted from the pre-gratuity math.

It assumes only cocktails. The 5-to-6 number works if every drink you order is a $13 cocktail. In real life, most cruisers mix in beers ($7.50), wines ($11), specialty coffees ($4.50), and sodas ($3.50–$3.95 by the glass in 2026). Each lower-priced drink moves the break-even up. Mix in a beer and a soda alongside three cocktails, and your effective drink-cost average drops below $10 — meaning you'd need closer to 9 or 10 drinks per day for the package to match.

This is the kind of thing a calculator handles in two seconds and a spreadsheet handles in twenty minutes. Let's run the actual numbers.

Example 1 — Light Drinker

Most first-time cruisers fall into this band whether they realize it or not. Two adults, 7-night sailing, three sea days. Each adult averages one cocktail and one soda per day. Honest, modest, what most couples actually do on a vacation that involves daytime activities and a real dinner.

A la carte total: $290. Deluxe would cost $1,560 — more than five times what you'd actually drink. Even the Soda package at $460 is significantly more than a la carte. Skip every package and pay per drink.

This is the configuration most cruisers underestimate themselves into. A couple hears "the package is worth it if you have more than four drinks a day" and convinces themselves they'll hit the number. Then they sail, do shore excursions, eat real dinners, and finish the trip with 14 cocktails between them on the bar tab. Total cost: under $300. The Deluxe package would have cost them an extra $1,200 to drink the same amount.

Example 2 — Moderate Drinker

The pool-day couple. Both adults order three cocktails, one beer, one wine, one specialty coffee, and one soda per day. That's 7 drinks per person per day, 14 for the couple — well above the conventional 5-to-7 break-even number you'd find quoted on most cruise blogs.

A la carte total: $1,050. Deluxe: $2,325. A la carte still wins by more than $1,200 — even though this couple is drinking 14 items between them per day, double what some sources call the break-even.

This is the result that surprises people most. "I drink a lot on cruises" is true for many of us, but most of us are still well below where Deluxe pays off. The mix matters: every beer at $7.50 instead of a $13 cocktail saves $5.50, and that compounds across 14 drinks a day.

Example 3 — Heavy Drinker

The all-in pool-bar couple. Both adults order five cocktails, two beers, two sodas, and one specialty coffee per day. Ten items per person, 20 across the couple. This is well past what most cruisers actually do.

A la carte total: $1,520. Deluxe: $2,800. A la carte still wins by $1,280 even at 20 drinks per day across the couple.

This is the part that breaks most people's expectations. You can drink more than enough to feel like you've earned the package and still lose money on it. The reason is the cost mix — at 5 cocktails plus 2 beers plus 2 sodas, the average drink price falls to about $9.50, which means you'd need way more total drinks to match $91 per person per day after gratuity.

Where Deluxe Actually Pays Off

To break even on Deluxe in the configuration above, the couple needs roughly $2,800 in a la carte drinks across 7 days — about $200 per day for the pair, which works out to about 13 cocktails per day for the group (or 6.5 per person) at average pricing. Or it could be 16 to 18 mixed drinks per person if you lean on beers and wines instead of cocktails.

That's a lot of drinking. Deluxe makes financial sense for:

  • Bachelor / bachelorette / heavy-party sailings where the group genuinely drinks 12+ items per person across the day.
  • Solo travelers in a single-occupancy cabin who drink at a high rate (the stateroom rule doesn't bite them since they're alone).
  • Couples on short 3- to 4-night cruises where the package's daily fixed cost is lower in absolute terms, sea-day percentage is higher, and the "vacation pace" pushes intake up.
  • Travelers who genuinely value the convenience — handing over a SeaPass card without thinking about price — and are willing to overpay for that experience.

It does not make financial sense for the typical cruiser who has a couple of drinks by the pool, wine with dinner, and a coffee in the morning. The calculator shows that plainly when you run your real numbers.

Crown & Anchor Vouchers Change Everything

Royal Caribbean's Crown & Anchor loyalty program awards free-drink vouchers at higher tiers. Diamond gets four per day, Diamond Plus gets five, Pinnacle gets six. Each voucher is valid for one drink up to $14 — so a Diamond Plus member effectively has $70 per day in pre-paid drinks before adding the bartender tip.

The drink calculator has a voucher field. Once you enter your tier's allotment, the package break-even shifts substantially. If you're Diamond Plus and only drink the five vouchers' worth per day, your actual demand for the Deluxe package is zero — you're already covered. Many higher-tier loyalty members find pay-as-you-go (or rather, pay-with-vouchers) beats every package option.

One nuance: vouchers don't cover non-alcoholic drinks for non-alcoholic guests, and they don't apply at Starbucks-branded outlets or the cabin minibar. If you also drink coffees and sodas in volume, the Refreshment or Soda package can still earn its keep alongside Crown & Anchor benefits.

Refreshment and Soda Packages

The non-alcoholic packages get less attention than Deluxe but they have a clearer break-even because the price gap is smaller.

The Soda package at about $12.99 per day plus 18% gratuity (effective $15.33) pays off at roughly 4 fountain drinks per day at $3 each plus tip — easy if you're a soda-with-meals person or have kids who go through Coca-Cola Freestyle cups, harder if you're an iced-tea-and-water type. The Soda package retained the Coca-Cola Freestyle souvenir cup that the Deluxe and Refreshment packages dropped in March 2026, which makes it the better choice for soda-heavy users.

The Refreshment package at about $38 per day plus gratuity (effective $45) needs 6 to 8 premium non-alcoholic items per day — mocktails at $6.50, specialty coffees at $4.50, fresh juices at $6, bottled waters at $2.95. That's achievable for hydration-focused or sober-curious travelers but rarely worth it for someone who'd otherwise drink the free dining-room iced tea, lemonade, and tap water.

For grief and recovery travelers — for whom packages can take the social weight off ordering — Refreshment also matters as a psychological tool, not just a financial one. The cost of "always having something pleasant in your hand without thinking about price" sometimes justifies what the spreadsheet doesn't.

The All-Adults Stateroom Rule

Since August 2025, Royal Caribbean enforces a stateroom-wide purchase rule on the Deluxe Beverage Package: if any guest 21 or older in a stateroom buys it, every other guest 21 or older in that same stateroom must buy it too. There are no medical, religious, or sobriety exemptions. Minors get the option of separate Bottomless Bubbles or Refreshment without triggering the rule.

This is the part that breaks the math for mixed-drinker couples. If one of you drinks heavily and the other almost never drinks, the heavy drinker can't buy Deluxe alone — both must pay. The calculator handles this by letting you set adults separately, so you can model the actual stateroom cost rather than the one-person break-even.

The practical workaround is to skip Deluxe and pay per drink for the heavy drinker, or for both to choose Refreshment (which has no all-adults rule) if the lighter drinker would use it. The non-alcoholic-only path keeps options open without forcing a $700 add-on onto someone who won't drink it.

Actionable Takeaways

Run Your Real Numbers Before You Pre-Buy

The Royal Caribbean Cruise Planner pushes Deluxe pre-cruise pricing as "20% off onboard." That's true, but only relevant if you'd actually buy it onboard, which most people who run the math wouldn't. Spend two minutes in the drink calculator with your real consumption before clicking buy.

Use the Quick Presets to Sanity-Check Yourself

The "Light Drinker" / "Moderate" / "Party" presets are calibrated against what real cruisers actually drink in those bands. If you've labeled yourself "Moderate" but you're still over-buying Deluxe on your own input, the preset will catch the inconsistency.

If You're Diamond+ or Higher, Check Voucher Coverage First

Diamond Plus members have 5 free drinks per day per person up to $14 each. That's $70 a day. For many cruisers at this level, the loyalty allowance already covers their day and the package is pure overpayment.

Mixed-Drinker Couples: Look at Refreshment, Not Deluxe

The stateroom rule makes Deluxe expensive when only one of you drinks. Refreshment has no stateroom rule and covers most non-alcoholic needs. The lighter drinker takes Refreshment; the heavier drinker pays per cocktail. The total is almost always cheaper than two Deluxe packages.

Soda Package Is Still the Cup-Friendly Option

After March 2026 Royal Caribbean removed Coca-Cola Freestyle souvenir cup access from Deluxe and Refreshment — but kept it on the Soda package. If the souvenir cup actually matters to you, the Soda package is the only path that still includes it without a $4.99 add-on.

Skip the Package Entirely on Short Cruises

On a 3- or 4-night sailing, the fixed daily cost of any package is high relative to how much you'll realistically drink. Short cruises have less sea time, more port time, and less drinking on average. Pay-as-you-go is almost always cheaper on a 4-night Bahamas run.

FAQ

How many drinks per day to break even on Deluxe?

At Royal Caribbean's 2026 fleet-median Deluxe price (about $77 plus 18% gratuity, effective $91 per person per day), about 6 to 7 cocktail-equivalents per person per day — roughly 11 for a couple sharing. The widely quoted "5 to 7" number usually ignores gratuity and assumes only $14 cocktails. Mix in beers and wines and the break-even climbs further.

Why does the conventional 5-to-7-drink rule miss?

It quotes package price without gratuity, then assumes you only order $14 cocktails. The 18% gratuity adds about $14 per person per day at the Deluxe median, and a real mix of beers, wines, sodas, and coffees lowers your average drink cost. Both shifts move the break-even up.

Does the Refreshment package pay off for non-drinkers?

Only at heavy non-alcoholic consumption. About $38 plus gratuity (effective $45) needs 6 to 8 premium non-alcoholic items per day — coffees, mocktails, juices, bottled water — to break even. Light non-alcoholic users do better with the Soda package or pay-as-you-go.

How does the calculator handle port days?

Packages cover onboard only, so port days dilute value. The calculator takes cruise days and sea days as separate inputs and applies a sea/port variability percentage (default +20% on sea days). This is closer to how real cruisers drink than a flat daily assumption.

What about Crown & Anchor vouchers?

Diamond Plus gets five free drinks per day up to $14 each. That's roughly $70 a day in pre-paid drinks. Once vouchers cover your demand, the package break-even rises substantially. Many Diamond Plus and Pinnacle members skip packages because their loyalty allowance already covers their day.

Do all adults in a stateroom have to buy Deluxe if one does?

Since August 2025, yes. Every guest 21 and over in the same stateroom must buy Deluxe if any one of them does, with no exemptions. Non-alcoholic packages have no such rule, which is why Refreshment is the standard workaround for mixed-drinker couples.

Where to Start

The shortest path to an answer is to open the calculator, set your cruise length and sea days, enter the drinks you actually order on a typical vacation day (be honest, not optimistic), and look at the chart. Either a la carte wins by a clear margin (most cruisers), one package wins by a clear margin (volume drinkers, Refreshment-heavy non-drinkers), or you're at the break-even line — in which case the package is rarely worth the lock-in.
