Indian Ocean

From the Logbook

Sri Lanka smells like cinnamon and incense. The moment I stepped off the ship in Colombo, I was breathing air perfumed by spice markets and temple offerings, a fragrance that's been drifting through this harbor for two thousand years. This is where Buddhism planted deep roots, where elephants still matter more than highways, where tea plantations carpet misty mountains like green velvet, and where the world's most tragic recent conflicts somehow produced the world's kindest people. The salt air mixed with sweet temple smoke as our gangway touched the pier.

Colombo itself is a study in beautiful contradictions—colonial mansions painted in fading pastels lean against glass towers, Buddhist monks in saffron robes scroll through smartphones, and the Galle Face Green oceanfront promenade hosts families flying kites beside young couples sharing spicy street food while the Indian Ocean crashes against the seawall. I walked along that promenade at sunset, the taste of isso wade (prawn fritters) still lingering on my tongue, watching children chase the last light of day. The city has been shaped by Portuguese, Dutch, and British occupiers, but it remains unmistakably Sri Lankan—warm, chaotic, spiritual, and utterly sincere.

My first morning I hired a driver for the journey to Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage, about two and a half hours into the green interior. The drive itself was an education—rice paddies giving way to rubber plantations, Buddhist temples appearing around every bend, roadside stands selling king coconuts and tropical fruit I couldn't name. When we arrived at Pinnawala, I understood immediately why this place has become legendary. Sixty elephants—many rescued from injury or abandonment—live here under government care, and twice daily they walk down to the Ma Oya River to bathe.

I positioned myself at the riverbank just before the 10am bathing and watched as the herd emerged from the trees. Mothers shepherded babies with their trunks, teenagers splashed each other with obvious playfulness, elders stood in the current with eyes half-closed in contentment. The sound of their trumpeting echoed off the hills. A young elephant not much taller than my waist waded right past where I stood, close enough that I could smell his earthy musk and hear his breath. My eyes filled with unexpected tears—not sadness but something closer to awe. For twenty minutes, I forgot about cruise schedules and itineraries and simply witnessed grace on an elephantine scale. Some moments don't fit in photographs.

Back in Colombo, I made my way to Gangaramaya Temple, the city's most prominent Buddhist complex. Fifteen minutes from the port, this sprawling sacred space defies every architectural rule—Sri Lankan stupas sit beside Thai chedis, Chinese pagodas neighbor Indian gopurams, and the museum displays everything from antique cars to thousands of Buddha statues donated by devotees worldwide. It's chaotic, eclectic, and somehow deeply peaceful. I removed my shoes at the entrance and felt the cool stone beneath my bare feet. The scent of lotus flowers and burning oil lamps filled my lungs as I walked through courtyard after courtyard, each revealing new wonders.

A monk noticed me admiring the temple's diverse architecture and approached to explain. The temple's architectural diversity, he told me, reflects Buddhism's central truth: all paths lead to the same awakening. I'm not qualified to judge the theology, but the kindness in his eyes was unmistakable. He blessed me before I left, pressing his palms together and murmuring words I couldn't understand but somehow felt. That blessing stays with me still.

The Pettah Market consumed my final afternoon—a chaotic, colorful bazaar district where narrow streets overflow with stalls selling spices, Ceylon tea, gems, textiles, electronics, and produce. The spice section smelled like heaven itself—cinnamon native to this very island, cardamom, cloves, curry powders in every shade of yellow and red. I bought far more than I needed, unable to resist the aromatic abundance. A vendor handed me a stick of fresh cinnamon bark and told me to smell—nothing I'd purchased in American grocery stores came close to that intensity.

Sri Lanka has known terrible suffering in recent decades—civil war, the tsunami of 2004, economic crisis. Yet everywhere I went, people greeted me with genuine warmth. My driver refused to let me pay for the coconut water he bought me. The temple monk blessed a complete stranger. The spice vendor threw in extra cinnamon "for your family." This generosity amid hardship humbled me profoundly. I left Colombo understanding something about resilience that no textbook could teach.

The moment that stays with me: That young elephant at Pinnawala, close enough to touch, water dripping from his ears, looking at me with an ancient intelligence that predates human civilization by millions of years. My heart swelled with gratitude that such creatures still exist, still live protected and cared for, still demonstrate that gentleness and power can coexist. For the first time in months, I felt completely present in a moment, completely alive to wonder. Sri Lanka gave me that gift.

Reflections are my own; I'm charting soundings in another's wake. Colombo cruise visit, winter 2024.

The Cruise Port

Cruise ships dock at Colombo Port in the main harbor, a working commercial facility that handles both cargo and passengers. The cruise terminal offers basic amenities—restrooms, currency exchange, tourist information desk, and a small selection of vendors. Taxis and tuk-tuks gather at the terminal exit, and ship-organized excursions depart from designated areas near the gangway.

Downtown Colombo lies about 15-20 minutes by taxi from the cruise terminal. The port area itself is industrial and not particularly scenic—you'll want to head into the city proper for sightseeing. Gangaramaya Temple is approximately the same distance. Day trips to Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage require 2.5 hours each way, making early departure essential. The terminal handles modern cruise ships without issue, and wheelchair access to taxis is available though facilities vary.

Getting Around

Colombo requires transport from the cruise terminal to reach meaningful attractions. Walking is impractical—the city lacks consistent sidewalks, traffic is chaotic, and tropical heat drains energy quickly. Plan your transport before leaving the ship, especially for day trips to Pinnawala or tea country.

By taxi: The most practical option from the port. Negotiate fare before departure or use metered radio cabs—apps like PickMe and Uber work in Colombo. Port to Gangaramaya Temple runs approximately 500-800 LKR ($2-3). For non-metered cabs, always agree on price before getting in. Air-conditioned taxis provide essential relief from heat and humidity.

By tuk-tuk: Three-wheeled vehicles are ubiquitous and cheaper than taxis. Always negotiate firmly—initial quotes typically run 2-3x fair price. Figure 400-600 LKR for short trips around the city. An adventurous option if you're comfortable with Colombo's traffic chaos, but not air-conditioned. Best for short hops between nearby attractions.

Car with driver: Essential for day trips to Pinnawala, Kandy, or tea estates. Arranged through ship excursion desk or local tour operators. Expect $60-100 USD for a full day including English-speaking driver. The driver navigates routes, provides local context, handles parking, and serves as informal guide. Highly recommended for destinations beyond Colombo city.

Mobility considerations: Colombo presents significant challenges for mobility-limited visitors. Sidewalks are inconsistent, curb cuts rare, and traffic crossings require agility. Temples like Gangaramaya involve uneven surfaces and stairs. Air-conditioned taxis or cars with drivers offer the most comfortable option for low-stamina visitors. The Pinnawala elephant bathing viewing areas can be reached by vehicle, though the riverside paths are unpaved. Ship excursions can accommodate wheelchairs with advance notice but options are limited.

Excursions

Colombo rewards both city exploration and ambitious day trips. Book ship excursions for guaranteed return times to long-distance destinations like Pinnawala—missing the ship from Sri Lanka's interior would be catastrophic. Independent exploration works well for city attractions within taxi distance.

Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage

Conservation center for orphaned and injured elephants—2.5 hours from Colombo through scenic countryside. Watch 60+ elephants bathe in Ma Oya River at 10am and 2pm bathing times. See baby elephants, feeding sessions, and daily care routines. Ship excursion packages run $70-100 USD including transport, guide, and lunch. Independent visitors can hire car with driver ($60-80 round trip) plus entry fee (~$25 USD). Full-day commitment but deeply rewarding. Morning bathing less crowded. Book ahead through ship excursion desk for guaranteed return times—this is essential given the distance involved.

Colombo City Tour with Gangaramaya Temple

Half-day exploration of Colombo's highlights—Gangaramaya Temple (Buddhist complex with diverse architecture and museum), Galle Face Green oceanfront promenade, Independence Square, and Old Dutch Hospital shopping precinct. Ship excursion versions run $40-60 for 4-5 hours. Independent visitors can taxi between sites for approximately $20-30 total. Gangaramaya entry ~300 LKR. Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered) and remove shoes at temples. Moderate stamina required—walking within each site. Book ship excursion if you prefer structured touring, or go independent for flexibility.

Pettah Market Walking Tour

Immersive exploration of Colombo's chaotic bazaar district—narrow streets packed with stalls selling spices, Ceylon tea, gems, textiles, and produce. Ship excursions typically combine this with other Colombo attractions. Independent visitors can taxi to Pettah (~300-500 LKR from port) and explore freely. Free to wander. Watch belongings carefully. Bargaining expected at all stalls. The spice section alone justifies the trip. Moderate stamina required—walking on crowded, uneven surfaces in heat. No booking needed for independent visits.

Kandy and Temple of the Tooth

Sacred Temple of the Tooth Relic houses Buddha's tooth—one of Buddhism's holiest shrines. UNESCO-protected site in Kandy, Sri Lanka's cultural capital. However, Kandy is 3+ hours each way from Colombo, making this extremely rushed on typical port days. Ship excursions run $90-150 USD for full-day marathon. Only feasible if your ship docks before 7am and departs after 7pm (12+ hours in port). Consider Pinnawala instead for typical port times—equally memorable and more manageable. Book through ship excursion desk only—guaranteed return is critical given the distance.

Ceylon Tea Experience

Visit working tea estates and factories in Sri Lanka's hill country—watch tea picking, processing, grading, and tasting. Learn why Ceylon tea achieved worldwide renown. Note that tea plantations are 2-3 hours from Colombo in the central highlands. Full-day ship excursions combine tea estate visit with other attractions, running $80-120 USD. Only feasible with long port days. Independent options require car with driver ($80-100 for full day). Alternative: excellent tea available at shops near port and in Pettah Market without the journey. Book ship excursion if tea country is a priority—guaranteed return essential.

Depth Soundings

Colombo's harbor has welcomed traders for over two thousand years. Arab merchants arrived in the 7th century, establishing trading posts that made the island's cinnamon famous across the medieval world. Portuguese sailors following Vasco da Gama's route landed in 1505 and spent the next century and a half extracting spices and converting locals to Christianity. The Dutch seized control in 1656, building the fort whose remnants still stand near the port. British forces arrived in 1796 and held the island—which they called Ceylon—until independence in 1948.

The British legacy runs deep. Cricket remains a national obsession. English serves as the link language between Sinhalese and Tamil speakers. The famous Ceylon tea industry was a British creation—Scottish and English planters established the hill country estates that still produce some of the world's finest teas. Even the railway that connects Colombo to the mountains follows routes the British surveyed a century ago.

Sri Lanka's recent decades have been marked by tragedy and resilience. The civil war between the government and Tamil Tigers lasted from 1983 to 2009, claiming over 100,000 lives. The 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami killed 35,000 Sri Lankans in a single morning. Economic crisis brought hardship in 2022. Yet through all of this, Sri Lankan hospitality has somehow survived—even strengthened. The warmth visitors experience today has been tested by suffering and found genuine. Buddhism's emphasis on compassion and impermanence seems to have inoculated the culture against lasting bitterness. Colombo smiles because it has learned that sorrow, like joy, eventually passes.

Practical Notes
  • Currency: Sri Lankan Rupee (LKR). US Dollars accepted at tourist shops. ATMs at terminal and throughout city
  • Language: Sinhala and Tamil official, English widely spoken in tourist areas
  • Weather: Tropical year-round. Best January-March. Southwest monsoon May-September
  • Prices: Very affordable. Tuk-tuk rides 400-600 LKR, restaurant meals 500-1500 LKR, temple entry 300-600 LKR
  • Tipping: 10% in restaurants. Round up for taxi/tuk-tuk. Small tips appreciated at temples
  • Safety: Generally safe for tourists. Watch bags in markets and crowded areas
  • WiFi: Available at restaurants and cafes. Ship WiFi may reach terminal area

Frequently Asked Questions
Where do cruise ships dock in Colombo?

Ships dock at Colombo Port in the main harbor. The terminal has basic facilities with taxis readily available outside. Downtown Colombo and Gangaramaya Temple are about 15-20 minutes away by taxi.

Can I visit elephants from Colombo on a port day?

Yes—Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage is about 2.5 hours from Colombo. Watch 60+ elephants bathe in the river at 10am and 2pm. Ship excursions run $70-100 including transport. Book ahead through ship excursion desk for guaranteed return times.

Is Kandy feasible as a day trip?

Kandy is 3+ hours each way, making it extremely rushed for typical port days. Only attempt if your ship is in port 12+ hours. Pinnawala Elephant Orphanage offers an equally memorable Sri Lankan experience with half the travel time.

What's the best temple to visit in Colombo?

Gangaramaya Temple (15-20 minutes from port) offers the most comprehensive experience—diverse architecture, extensive museum, active worship, peaceful lake pavilion. Dress modestly (shoulders and knees covered), remove shoes, and allow 1-2 hours.

What should I buy in Colombo?

Ceylon tea is the classic purchase—premium quality at excellent prices. Also consider cinnamon (native to Sri Lanka), spices from Pettah Market, Ayurvedic products, and handmade batik textiles. Gems available from reputable dealers with certificates.
