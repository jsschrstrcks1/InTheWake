## Welcome

This pack exists to make one specific cruise easier: a 7-night Alaska round-trip from Seattle on **Norwegian Bliss**, July 11 – 18, 2026, calling at **Sitka, Juneau, Icy Strait Point, Ketchikan, and Victoria BC**. It's a **hosted solo cruisers group cruise** organized by **Kristie McClung Escoe**.

It pulls together the ship-specific tips, port-specific advice, packing notes calibrated for an Alaska summer, the group's pre-cruise and onboard rhythm Kristie has set, and the budget math you'd otherwise assemble from a dozen browser tabs.

It is calm by design. There is no urgency, no upsell, no countdown timer. The site that produced this pack is independent — no cruise-line affiliations, no shore-excursion commissions, no display ads.

**Includes an offline-capable Emergency Contacts section** — a fillable family handoff card, cruise-line and embassy numbers tailored to this itinerary, available offline after first load via [In the Wake](/index.html)'s PWA. Designed to share with one trusted person ashore before sailing.

**This pack is written for first-time Alaska cruisers and solo cruisers** in Kristie's group. NCL Bliss is one of the strongest solo-cabin ships afloat (no single supplement on Studios, dedicated Studio Lounge, built-in solo cohort), and Alaska in July is the iconic Inside Passage week — long daylight, green mountains, glaciers within reach, the small towns of southeast Alaska in their summer high season.

If this is also your very first cruise of any kind, the [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html) is a useful read alongside this pack. If you've sailed before and are wondering what's different about NCL specifically (Freestyle Dining, Studio Lounge culture, no formal nights), Section 1 below covers it.

## Section 1 — Your Ship: Norwegian Bliss {#section-1}

![Norwegian Bliss anchored off Portland, Dorset — the Robert Wyland humpback whale mural visible on the bow, the Aqua Park waterslides on the upper deck. Photo by Andrew Bone via Flickr, CC BY 2.0](/assets/ships/Norwegian_Bliss_Portland_Port_2020.webp)

### A purpose-built Alaska ship

[Norwegian Bliss](/ships/norwegian/norwegian-bliss.html) was delivered in April 2018 by Meyer Werft at Papenburg, Germany — the third ship of NCL's **Breakaway Plus class** (after Norwegian Escape 2015 and Norwegian Joy 2017). She was **purpose-designed for Alaska**: NCL built her with wraparound observation decks, the largest indoor-outdoor Observation Lounge in their fleet (Deck 15 forward), and the line's first hull mural by marine-life artist **Robert Wyland** — a 270-foot humpback whale that wraps the bow and starboard side. By your July 2026 sailing she's eight years into service — well past any new-ship issues, her crew rhythms long settled.

Her direct sisters are Norwegian Joy (2017) and Norwegian Encore (2019). Norwegian Escape (2015) is the same hull but technically the lead ship of the original Breakaway Plus class, sometimes counted separately. If you've sailed Joy or Encore, Bliss feels familiar; if you've sailed older Breakaway-class ships (Breakaway, Getaway), Bliss is the bigger, more refined version.

### What's distinctive about Bliss

She's a ship designed around **outdoor observation** — which matches Alaska perfectly. The signature features:

- **Observation Lounge** (Deck 15 forward) — NCL's largest. Floor-to-ceiling windows wrapping the bow, indoor seating, a coffee bar, books, board games. Open all day; the quietest place on the ship in the early morning. This is where most Alaska cruisers spend their best hours.
- **Sea View Bar** (Deck 17) — open-air bar with panoramic views; less crowded than the main pool deck. *(This is where Kristie is holding the group's sailaway — see [Section 9](#section-9).)*
- **Aqua Park** with the Ocean Loops Aqua Coaster — NCL's first looping water slide; free; lines build by mid-morning.
- **Galaxy Pavilion** — interactive arcade and VR experiences (paid extra).
- **The Cavern Club** — Beatles tribute venue; full original-set songs by a resident band.
- **Headliners Comedy Club** — late-night comedy in a small-room setting.
- **The Haven** — NCL's ship-within-a-ship luxury enclave with private restaurant, courtyard, pool, and lounge.

What's NOT on Bliss (because she's Breakaway Plus, not Prima Plus): no Ocean Boulevard 360 wraparound (that's a Prima-class feature), no infinity pools. The closest visual equivalent is the Waterfront on Deck 8 — a partial outdoor promenade with bars and seating.

### Cabin categories — the solo calculus

NCL's signature solo offering is at its strongest on Bliss: dedicated **Studio cabins** with **no single supplement**, [**Studio Lounge**](/restaurants/ncl/studio-lounge.html) **access** (free coffee, social space, scheduled hosted hours for solo cruisers), and a built-in cohort of fellow solos. Studio cabins are roughly 100 sq ft and windowless — compact and efficient.

**Decision rule for the July 11 sailing (group rates):**

- **Solo Studio Cabin ($1,269)** — the flyer-advertised rate. Best value for solo cruisers who'll spend daylight hours off the ship or in public spaces. Studio Lounge is your social anchor.
- **Oceanview / Balcony / Mini-Suite / Suite** — verify category and pricing with Kristie. Most other categories don't waive the single supplement, so the solo-traveler value drops sharply above Studio.
- **The Haven** — NCL's premium enclave. Real if budget allows, but transformative; the Haven cabins are unconnected to the Studio Lounge culture, so you'd be opting out of the built-in solo community.

For Alaska specifically, the **balcony argument** is real: the ship spends hours threading the Inside Passage; a private outdoor space is genuinely valuable. But the Studio cabin + Observation Lounge combo covers the same need with less spend.

For ship-size context, the [Ship Size Atlas](/tools/ship-size-atlas.html) lets you compare Bliss to other ships. Follow her live position throughout your week via the [Ship Logbook](/tools/ship-tracker.html) — a small comfort if family ashore wants to know where you are.

### Dining

Bliss runs NCL's **Freestyle Dining** model — no fixed dining-room assignment, no traditional set seating; you eat where and when you want within the included venues, with specialty restaurants available at additional cost.

**Included main dining:**
- [**Manhattan Room**](/restaurants/ncl/the-manhattan-room.html) (Deck 7 aft) — main dining room with a small dance floor and live music most evenings; the most atmospheric of the included options.
- [**Taste**](/restaurants/ncl/grand-pacific.html) and **Savor** (Deck 6) — paired main dining rooms with identical menus; slightly more efficient on busy nights.
- [**Garden Café**](/restaurants/ncl/garden-cafe.html) (Deck 16) — buffet; long hours; an upper-deck view that's especially good in Alaska.
- [**The Local Bar & Grill**](/restaurants/ncl/the-local.html) — pub-style, 24-hour favorites.

**Specialty restaurants (paid extra):**

- [**Cagney's Steakhouse**](/restaurants/ncl/cagneys-steakhouse.html) — NCL's signature steakhouse. *(Kristie's group dines here Tuesday 7/14, 5:30 PM — see [Section 9](#section-9).)*
- [**Le Bistro**](/restaurants/ncl/le-bistro.html) — French. *(Kristie's group dines here Wednesday 7/15, 5:45 PM.)*
- [**Teppanyaki**](/restaurants/ncl/hasuki.html) — Japanese teppanyaki room. *(Kristie's group dines here Sunday 7/12, 5:00 PM.)*
- [**Q Texas Smokehouse**](/restaurants/ncl/cocos.html) — barbecue.
- [**Los Lobos**](/restaurants/ncl/cocos.html) — Mexican.
- [**Food Republic**](/restaurants/ncl/grand-pacific.html) — small-plate concept; Pan-Asian.
- [**Ocean Blue**](/restaurants/ncl/grand-pacific.html) — seafood; the priciest specialty.
- [**Onda by Scarpetta**](/restaurants/ncl/onda-by-scarpetta.html) is on the Prima class, not Bliss — for Italian on Bliss, **La Cucina** is the included-or-light-cover option ([la-cucina](/restaurants/ncl/la-cucina.html)).

A **Specialty Dining Package** (3 or 5 meals) prepaid in the Cruise Planner is good value if you'd dine specialty 3+ times anyway. Since Kristie has booked three group specialty nights (Teppanyaki, Cagney's, Le Bistro), a 3-meal package may already cover the group dinners if you're attending all three. Run the math in the [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html).

### Drinks

NCL's drink-package math: the Premium Beverage Package is commonly included in the **"Free at Sea"** promotional bundle when booking. If your group fare included Free at Sea, drinks may already be covered — verify with Kristie. Otherwise the package runs ~$109/day per person + 20% gratuity at pre-cruise rates; breaks even at ~5–6 drinks per day. ### Day 1 — Saturday, July 11 — Seattle (embarkation)

![Seattle skyline at twilight from Kerry Park](/ports/img/seattle/seattle-hero.webp)

**Terminal:** **Bell Street Cruise Terminal at Pier 66** — 2225 Alaskan Way, Seattle. Different terminal from the Pier 91 used by RCL/Princess/HAL; Pier 66 is the working downtown terminal, an easy walk from Pike Place Market, the Seattle Aquarium, and the Great Wheel. About 30–40 minutes from Sea-Tac (SEA) by Uber/Lyft.

**Embark window:** 11:30 AM – 3:00 PM. Ship departs ~4:00 PM. **You must be back at the terminal by 2:00 PM** to complete embarkation processing.

**The Saturday morning routine Kristie laid out:**

NCL allows **early luggage drop-off from 8:00 AM** at the terminal — so you can drop bags, then enjoy a few hours of Seattle before boarding.

- **Option 1 (longer Pike Place):** Drop luggage 8–9 AM → walk 10 minutes to **Biscuit Bitch** in Belltown for breakfast (cult-favorite breakfast biscuits) → walk to **Pike Place Market** to explore → back to the terminal by 2 PM.
- **Option 2 (Pike Place first):** Drop luggage 8–9 AM → walk straight to **Pike Place Market** for breakfast (vendors open ~9 AM) → continue exploring waterfront, the Seattle Aquarium, or the museums district → back by 2 PM.

Either option works. Pike Place is at its quietest before 10 AM; the flying-fish performance gets going around 11.

**The realistic Day 1 timeline:**

- **8:00 AM – 9:00 AM:** Early luggage drop-off if you're using it.
- **9:00 AM – 1:00 PM:** Seattle morning (Option 1 or 2 above).
- **1:30 – 2:00 PM:** Return to Pier 66 with margin.
- **2:00 PM:** All-aboard begins for late-embarkation guests; lines at security may build.
- **2:30 PM:** Find your cabin (cabins typically open by 1 PM; if you boarded earlier, they're ready now). Meet your stateroom steward.
- **3:30 PM:** Mandatory muster drill — NCL's drill is partly app-based (watch the safety video in the NCL app) plus an in-person check-in at your assigned station.
- **4:00 PM:** **Sailaway at the [Sea View Bar, Deck 17](/restaurants/ncl/swirls-wine-bar.html).** *Kristie is here.* Less-crowded than the main pool deck; panoramic views; she'll be passing out the week's printed info. If you want sailaway outside first, come find her at Sea View afterward.
- **5:00 PM:** **Group dinner reservation** — check with Kristie for the venue. Open to anyone who wants to join.
- **Later evening:** NCL hosts a **solo-group meet-and-greet** sometime on Day 1 — Kristie will share the time once NCL confirms it.

**What to pack in your carry-on for Day 1:** swimsuit (pools open before checked bags arrive), light jacket (Seattle evenings can be 60°F even in July), all medications, change of clothes, passport (preferred — Victoria BC on Day 7 is a foreign port), phone charger, refillable water bottle, your printed group itinerary if Kristie sent one. Your checked bags may not arrive until 7–9 PM.

**Money/card:** Your NCL keycard is your ID, room key, and onboard charge. You'll receive it at check-in. Currency aboard: US Dollar.

### Day 3 — Juneau, Alaska

![Juneau seen from Mount Roberts, with cruise ships docked along the waterfront](/ports/img/juneau/juneau-hero.webp)

**Arrival typically:** Mid-morning. **Depart typically:** Evening (Juneau is a long port day for most Alaska sailings — verify your specific times).

**Honest read on Juneau.** Alaska's capital — and the only U.S. state capital that cannot be reached by road. Reachable only by ship, plane, or float plane. Population ~32,000 year-round; ~17,000 daily cruise passengers in peak summer. Downtown is built into a near-vertical mountainside; the Mount Roberts Tramway is the iconic up-the-mountain ride.

**Free, walkable from the cruise dock:**

- **Downtown Juneau** — South Franklin Street's bars, the Red Dog Saloon (touristy but historic), state-museum-quality Sealaska Heritage Institute (free; among the best Alaska Native cultural exhibits in the state), Alaska State Capitol (free 30-minute tours weekdays).
- **Last Chance Mining Museum** — short walk uphill, free, gold-mining history.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Mendenhall Glacier visit** — the glacier is 13 miles from downtown. **Blue Bus / Mighty Great Trips** ([mmtoursofjuneau.com](https://mmtoursofjuneau.com/glacier-express/)) runs the cheapest direct shuttle at ~$25–35 round-trip; ship excursion ~$50–80. Visitor center, Nugget Falls walk, glacier viewpoints; 1.5–2 hours is enough.
- **Mount Roberts Tramway** — 1,800-foot vertical tram up Mount Roberts; ~$45 round-trip independent at the dock. Easy hiking trails at the top.
- **Whale watching** — humpback season peaks June–September. **Harv & Marv's** ([harvandmarvs.com](https://www.harvandmarvs.com/)) runs out of Auke Bay; among the highest-quality wildlife excursions on this itinerary. ~$130–180/person.
- **Helicopter to Juneau Icefield + glacier landing** — $600–900; once-in-a-lifetime if budget allows.

**For first-timers in Juneau:** Mendenhall Glacier (independent bus) for the budget-conscious, or whale watching for the wildlife-focused. A combined Mendenhall + whale-watching package (~$220) is a strong full day.

**For each port day**, the [Port Day Planner](/tools/port-day-planner.html) builds an hour-by-hour timeline from your priorities; the [Port Logbook](/tools/port-tracker.html) lets you log your day for future reference.

### Day 4 — Icy Strait Point, Alaska

![Huna Tlingit cultural performance at Icy Strait Point — the contemporary expression of the village's pre-cruise heritage](/ports/img/icy-strait-point/icy-strait-point-huna-totem.webp)

**Arrival typically:** Mid-morning. **Depart typically:** Evening. **Tender port** — the ship anchors offshore, tenders run to the dock area.

**Honest read on Icy Strait Point.** A modest cruise destination on Chichagof Island, adjacent to the village of **Hoonah** (population ~750), the largest Tlingit settlement in Alaska. The cruise port was built around a restored 1912 salmon cannery, with the Huna Tlingit tribal corporation as the principal owner and operator. **The world's longest zip line** (ZipRider — 5,330 feet, vertical drop ~1,300 ft) is the iconic Icy Strait Point excursion. Unlike Sitka or Juneau, this is a port purpose-built for cruise visits — no large downtown to wander.

**Free or low-cost, walkable:**

- **Cannery Museum** — restored 1912 cannery building; cultural and industrial history. Free.
- **Hoonah Village** walk — 1.5-mile flat walk into the village; small museum, Russian Orthodox church, the Hoonah Indian Association heritage building.
- **Beach walks** — the gravel/cobble shoreline; quiet; eagle-watching common.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **ZipRider** — $179 for the world's longest zip line. Three minutes of flight; once-in-a-lifetime. Reserve ahead — the line is real on a Bliss-sized ship.
- **Whale watching** — Icy Strait is one of the best whale-watching waters in southeast Alaska; humpback feeding grounds. ~$150–180/person.
- **Bear-watching tour** — Chichagof Island has one of the highest brown-bear populations in the world. Tours go inland or by skiff to bear-frequented salmon streams. ~$150–250.
- **Cultural performance** at the Tribal Dance & Cultural Legends show (typically ~$30).

**For first-timers in Icy Strait Point:** the ZipRider if you want the iconic experience, or whale watching for the wildlife. The ship-built nature of the port means there's less "wander town" than at Sitka/Juneau — plan an excursion or settle in for a peaceful beach-and-cannery afternoon.

### Day 5 — Sitka, Alaska

![Sitka harbor in a Library of Congress archive photograph — the town's Russian-Tlingit heritage shows in any era](/ports/img/sitka/sitka-hero.webp)

**Arrival typically:** Mid-morning. **Depart typically:** Evening. **Tender port** — the ship anchors; tenders run between ship and the small downtown waterfront. Plan for a 20–30 minute end-to-end tender process at peak.

**Honest read on Sitka.** The most historically dense of the Alaska panhandle ports. Capital of Russian America from 1808 until the 1867 sale of Alaska to the United States; the only Russian Orthodox cathedral in North America (St. Michael's, rebuilt after a 1966 fire); deep Tlingit history that shaped its first centuries before Russian contact. Most Alaska itineraries skip Sitka in favor of Ketchikan; **this itinerary covers both** — take advantage. The walkable downtown is small (under a mile end-to-end) and the [Sitka National Historical Park](https://www.nps.gov/sitk/index.htm) totem trail is among the most affecting easy walks in southeast Alaska.

**Free, walkable from the tender pier:**

- **St. Michael's Cathedral** — the rebuilt Russian Orthodox cathedral (small donation suggested for entry).
- **Sitka National Historical Park** — totem-pole trail through old-growth forest, 30-minute walk from downtown. Free entry. The Tlingit and Russian-era exhibits at the visitor center are quietly excellent.
- **Sheldon Jackson Museum** — Alaska's oldest museum; one of the best ethnographic collections of Alaska Native material culture in the state.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Sea otter and wildlife boat tour** (~$120–180/person) — Sitka has the densest sea-otter population in southeast Alaska. Worth it if wildlife is your trip priority.
- **Fortress of the Bear** sanctuary tour (~$50–80) — rescued bears in semi-natural habitat. Ethically run; not a roadside zoo.
- **Russian Bishop's House tour** (NPS, ~$5).
- **Independent transit:** the local Visitor Transit Bus runs a $9 day pass between downtown, the park, Fortress of the Bear, and the tender area.

**Pack for Sitka:** rain jacket (Sitka rains 220+ days per year — your day might be one), waterproof or treated walking shoes, fleece layer, $40–60 cash USD for transit and tips.

### Day 6 — Ketchikan, Alaska

![Ketchikan waterfront in a Fisher Studio archive photograph — the town's salmon-canning heritage in sepia](/ports/img/ketchikan/ketchikan-1.webp)

**Arrival typically:** Mid-morning. **Depart typically:** Afternoon. Ketchikan's cruise pier is downtown — a 2-minute walk to Creek Street and the central tourist district.

**Honest read on Ketchikan.** The "salmon capital of the world" and the southernmost major town in Alaska. Population ~8,200. The town clings to a narrow shelf between mountains and the Tongass Narrows — main street is right at the water. Wood-plank Creek Street (a stilted boardwalk over Ketchikan Creek) was the historical red-light district; today it's preserved as a shopping/walking area with a salmon-spawning creek running beneath. The world's largest collection of standing **totem poles** is split between Saxman Native Village (south) and Totem Bight State Historical Park (north) — both worth seeing.

**Free, walkable from the cruise dock:**

- **Creek Street** — the iconic boardwalk; restaurants, shops, the historic Dolly's House Museum (~$10). Salmon visible in the creek in season.
- **Downtown Ketchikan** — short, walkable, the totem-pole displays in the main square.
- **Totem Heritage Center** — short walk; $6 entry; nationally significant totem collection.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Saxman Native Village** (~$45–65) — Tlingit cultural village 2.5 miles south of downtown; totem park + carving studio + dance performance.
- **Totem Bight State Park** (~$10 cab each way) — totem park 10 miles north; less crowded than Saxman; quieter walking.
- **Misty Fjords flightseeing** (~$300–400) — small floatplane to the fjords inland. The iconic Ketchikan splurge.
- **Lumberjack Show** (~$45) — exactly what it sounds like; touristy and well-rated. Right by the dock.

**For first-timers in Ketchikan:** Creek Street + downtown + Saxman OR Totem Bight is a strong half-day. Misty Fjords flightseeing is the splurge if budget allows.

### Day 7 — Victoria, British Columbia

![Victoria's Inner Harbour at night — Parliament Buildings illuminated](/ports/img/victoria-bc/victoria-bc-1.webp)

**Arrival typically:** Late afternoon (often 6:00 PM). **Depart typically:** Just before midnight. Victoria is the **Jones Act port** — a foreign-port call required by the Passenger Vessel Services Act on US-only itineraries. The ship docks at the **Ogden Point** breakwater, ~3 km from downtown. Free shuttle buses or a $10 round-trip cab/rideshare run between Ogden Point and the Inner Harbour.

**Honest read on Victoria.** Provincial capital of British Columbia; unapologetically charming small city — Edwardian architecture, the Empress Hotel afternoon tea, the Royal BC Museum, the famous **Butchart Gardens** 30 minutes outside town. The evening port-call duration limits realistic options, but Victoria isn't trying to compete with Alaska — it's the wind-down stop.

**Free, walkable from downtown drop-off:**

- **Inner Harbour** — iconic Parliament Buildings (lit at night), the Empress Hotel exterior, harbor walking path, totem poles at Thunderbird Park.
- **Beacon Hill Park** — 30-minute walk from harbor.
- **Bastion Square / Old Town** — walkable, restaurants, evening atmosphere.

**Paid (decide ahead):**

- **Butchart Gardens** ($35 entry + transport ~$60 total) — world-famous gardens. Evening lighting is theatrical; the gardens stay open until 10:00 PM in summer. Confirm sailing time before committing.
- **Royal BC Museum** ($30) — closes 5–6 PM, so only works if you arrive earlier than 6 PM.
- **Hop on Hop off bus tour** (~$45 for a 90-minute orientation if you have time).

**For first-timers in Victoria:** the trip's gentle decompression. A walk through Inner Harbour, dinner at a real Canadian pub, a short stroll back, and you're done. Save Butchart Gardens for a longer Victoria-specific trip.

**Currency:** Canadian dollars are local; USD widely accepted near the harbor. Credit/debit cards universal — you don't need to exchange.

### Day 8 — Saturday, July 18 — Seattle (debarkation)

**The realistic timeline:**

- **6:00 – 8:00 AM:** Breakfast at Garden Café or Manhattan Room.

## Section 4 — Packing List (calibrated for Alaska, 7 nights, July) {#section-4}

This list is tuned for: **Alaska summer (July)** — generally 55–70°F daytime, 50–60°F evenings, frequent rain, occasional 75°F afternoons. **Five port days** mostly walking + outdoor activity. **One sea day with significant deck-observation time.** **NCL's smart-casual evening dress culture** (no formal nights; one optional "Norwegian's Night Out" is a slightly dressier evening but jacket not required).

If you remember nothing else: **the Alaska packing rule is layering.** A thermal, a fleece, a waterproof shell — you'll wear all three on the same morning some days.

### Documents and money

- Passport (preferred). Victoria BC on Day 7 is a foreign port; passport is the cleaner path. Birth certificate + state ID is technically accepted but a passport eliminates Day-1 check-in friction.
- NCL booking confirmation and check-in QR (printed and saved on phone).
- Kristie's group contact card / printed schedule (she's distributing at sailaway).
- Credit card you trust (notify the issuer of travel; international fees waived if available).
- ~$200 USD cash in $5/$10/$20 bills for tips, excursion guides, taxis, small purchases. **No CAD needed** for Victoria — USD is accepted.

### Carry-on (must arrive on the ship with you)

- All medications + 3-day buffer (NEVER in checked bags).
- Daily / scheduled / controlled-substance prescriptions in original labeled bottles.
- Swimsuit (the heated Aqua Park pools open before bags arrive).
- Phone, charger, backup battery.
- Reading material for the pre-cabin window.
- Small refillable water bottle.
- Travel documents.

### Outerwear (the layering system)

- **Base layer:** thermal long-sleeve top + thermal long underwear (for cold mornings on deck — synthetic or merino, not cotton).
- **Mid layer:** fleece pullover or down sweater. Worn most port days.
- **Outer shell:** waterproof and windproof rain jacket. Not a fashion jacket — a real shell. **The most important single item.**
- Hat (warm beanie, not just a baseball cap).
- Light gloves.
- Scarf or buff for cold mornings on Observation Lounge / Sea View Bar.

### For the port days

- **Sturdy walking shoes** — broken in. The Sitka totem trail, Saxman Native Village, Mount Roberts Tramway top trails all involve walking distance.
- **Daypack** (hiking-style, ~15–20L).
- **Binoculars** (8x42 or 10x42) for whale-watching and wildlife.
- **Sun hat / cap** (rare clear day at altitude burns fast).
- **Sunglasses** (polarized for water glare).
- **Sunscreen** (SPF 30+; reef-safe if going on whale-watching tours).

### For the ship — daytime

- 7 daily outfits — comfortable casual; jeans or cargo pants are fine.
- 5–6 t-shirts or long-sleeve casual tops.
- Lightweight long-sleeve shirts for sun protection.
- Comfortable walking shoes (in addition to your port-day pair).
- Sandals or flip-flops for the Aqua Park.

### For the ship — evening

- 5–6 evening outfits — NCL's dress code is **smart casual** all week.
- 1 nicer outfit for the group specialty-dining nights (Cagney's, Le Bistro especially). Dressy jeans + a button-down, a casual dress, dressy linen.
- Light layer (sweater) for cold AC inside venues.
- **No formal nights on NCL** — leave the tuxedo at home.

### Solo / quiet items

- A book or e-reader for the Observation Lounge.
- A pen for the customs declaration form.
- A small lock for valuables (the cabin safe is sufficient; this is for the daypack at ports).

### Optional but useful

- **Magnetic hooks** (4–6) — Bliss's cabin walls are steel; stick hooks for jackets, hats, wet swimsuits. See [cabin organization](/articles/cruise-cabin-organization.html).
- **A travel mug** for Observation Lounge coffee.
- **Reusable rubber duck** — the [cruise duck tradition](/articles/cruise-duck-tradition.html); hide one on the ship for someone else to find.
- **A small folding stool or stadium cushion** for extended deck observation in the Inside Passage.

### Skip

- Hairdryer (cabin has one).
- Iron (not allowed).
- Heavy formal wear (no formal nights).
- Beach umbrellas (Alaska beaches don't need them).
- Power strips with surge protection (banned by most cruise lines for safety; bring a USB-equipped charging cube).
- Currency exchange (USD is fine for Victoria).

## Section 7 — Seasickness on an Alaska Cruise {#section-7}

Alaska is calmer than people fear. The Inside Passage is — by design — protected water. Most of the route is sheltered from open-ocean swell. Where you'll feel motion: the **open-water transit between the Strait of Juan de Fuca and the entrance to the Inside Passage** (Day 1 evening through Day 2 morning, briefly), and the **Strait of Georgia** (Day 7 evening, briefly). Both are short and rarely rough.

**The honest read:** if you've been seasick on a Caribbean cruise, you'll likely be fine on Alaska. If you've never been seasick, you almost certainly won't be on this itinerary. The ship is large enough (168,028 GT) that even moderately rough water is filtered substantially.

### What to bring

- **Bonine** (meclizine) or **Dramamine Less Drowsy** — over-the-counter, take 1 hour before expected motion.
- **Sea-Bands** — pressure-point wristbands.
- **Ginger candies or chews** — backup for mild queasiness.

### What helps once you feel it

- **Get on deck.** The Observation Lounge has horizon access in any weather.
- **Eat something dry.** Crackers, dry toast, pretzels.
- **Avoid alcohol** (alcohol exacerbates motion sickness).
- **Take meclizine** at the first hint, not after it's full-on.

If you're motion-sensitive and booked a Studio cabin (interior, no window), spend daylight hours on the Observation Lounge or Sea View Bar — horizon access fixes the problem.

## Section 8 — Practical Logistics {#section-8}

### The NCL app

Download it now (App Store / Google Play). Once your booking is final and you've checked in:

- **Daily program** — schedule of activities, dining hours, show times.
- **Cruise Planner** — pre-cruise booking of drinks, specialty dining, spa, excursions.
- **On-board messaging** — chat with members of your party (Kristie's group, if she sets up a channel) without internet.

The app is functional, not great. Some features only work on the ship's Wi-Fi (not cellular). Plan accordingly.

### Onboard internet

NCL's **Internet** is tiered:

- **Wi-Fi Plus** — basic; web, email, social media.
- **Wi-Fi Premium** — streaming; multiple devices; faster.

Pre-cruise pricing is generally cheaper than onboard purchase. For a 7-night cruise, buy only if you genuinely need it — port days have cell service, sea days are short. The [internet-at-sea](/internet-at-sea.html) page covers this in more depth.

### Cell service

- **In US ports (Seattle, Juneau, Icy Strait Point, Sitka, Ketchikan):** your US carrier works.
- **In Canada (Victoria):** roaming applies. T-Mobile's free roaming covers Canada at slower speeds; AT&T and Verizon charge $10/day.
- **At sea:** no normal cell service. Default to airplane mode.

### Tipping

- **On the ship:** NCL's service charge added daily ($20/person standard, $25 in Haven). Covers stateroom, dining, bar. Additional cash tips for outstanding service: steward $30–50; specialty dining server who handled the group's three nights: $20–40.
- **In ports:** cash, USD, $5/$10 bills. 10–15% on tour cost; $1–2 per taxi; $1 per drink.

For broader cruise tipping context, see [Cruise Tipping in 2026](/articles/cruise-tipping-2026.html).

### Money

- NCL uses your keycard for onboard charges; settle at debarkation via credit card.
- Bring USD cash for ports and tips (~$200 in small bills).
- ATMs exist in all five Alaska ports + Victoria; bring cash from home if practical.

### Safety in the Alaska ports

Alaska's cruise-port towns are among the safest stops in any cruise itinerary. Crime rates are low; "tourism crime" is mostly opportunistic theft, easily avoided. The bigger risks are weather (slip-and-fall on wet boardwalks; getting cold and wet) and wildlife (don't approach bears; if a bear is on a Ketchikan trail, walk slowly the other way; never run). The [disability-at-sea](/disability-at-sea.html) page covers accessible-transit considerations.

## Section 11 — First Cruise Notes {#section-11}

If this is your first cruise of any kind (not just first NCL or first Alaska), a few things that won't be obvious:

**The ship is a small city.** 4,000+ passengers, 1,700 crew, 20 decks. You won't see all of it. Make peace with this.

**Embarkation is logistical, not magical.** Day 1 is paperwork, lines, and finding things. The good part starts on Day 2.

**The buffet (Garden Café) is not the best food on the ship.** It's fine. The Manhattan Room dinner is genuinely good. Eat there at least 2 of the 4 nights you're not doing group specialty dining.

**You're going to forget your keycard the first day.** Everyone does. Get a lanyard.

**The first sea day, you'll feel disoriented.** Normal. Pick 2 things from the daily program, do them, sit down, stare at water. You'll find the rhythm.

**The crew remembers you.** Your stateroom steward, the bartender at Sea View Bar — they learn your name, your drink preferences, your allergies. The relationship is real. It's worth being kind, and worth tipping in cash above the auto-gratuity if service warrants it.

**NCL is more casual than the other major US lines.** No formal nights. The Freestyle Dining model means you set your own schedule.

**The Observation Lounge is where you'll spend your best Alaska hours.** Make peace with this too. Coffee, a book, the Inside Passage moving past.

**You'll spend more on excursions than you think.** Build a shore-day budget before sailing. The [cruise-budget-calculator](/tools/cruise-budget-calculator.html) helps prevent the surprise.

**You'll come home wanting to go back.** Most first-time Alaska cruisers do another within 2 years. Plan accordingly.

For more first-cruise context across cruise lines, see the broader [first-cruise guide](/first-cruise.html).
