Seasickness — What Actually Works

Cruise Seasickness: What Actually Works (and What Doesn't)

Most people don't get seasick on a big modern ship — and if you do, it usually passes within a day. Here's what genuinely helps, what the evidence says to skip, and how to stop worrying about it before you sail.

by Ken Baker | June 18, 2026

The short version: On a big modern cruise ship, most people never feel seriously seasick. If you do, it usually settles within a day or two as your body adjusts. The things that actually help are mostly free: book a cabin midship and low in the ship, keep the horizon in view, get fresh air, go easy on alcohol, and don't bury your eyes in a book or phone while the floor is moving. If you know you're prone to it, talk to your doctor about medication before you sail — taken in advance, it works far better than anything you reach for once you already feel green.

A note before we start: This is general travel information, not medical advice. Medications, supplements, and existing conditions interact in ways only your own doctor or pharmacist can sort out — especially if you're older, pregnant, traveling with children, or already taking something. When this page talks about medicine, treat it as a prompt to ask them, not an answer in itself.

Who This Is For

Anyone the worry has gotten to: the first-timer who's half-decided the sea will undo them, the caregiver booking for an elderly parent, the traveler weighing whether a patch is worth the side effects, and the veteran cruiser who hit one rough crossing and hasn't forgotten it. The fear is common, it's reasonable, and for most people it turns out to be bigger than the reality.

I'll say where I'm speaking from. I've sailed a Quantum-class ship across the Pacific, including a stretch of the Tasman Sea that handed us something close to thirty-foot seas, and I came through it comfortable. That's not a promise you will — sensitivity is personal, and I'm not built to get sick easily. But it's the honest backdrop to the calm-it-down tone of this page: the ocean is a big thing, the ship is built for it, and the worry usually outruns the day.

Why a Big Ship Moves Less Than You Fear

A modern cruise ship is not a fishing boat. It's tens of thousands of tons, hundreds of feet long, and built with stabilizers — fins that extend from the hull underwater and actively work against the roll. Add to that the simple fact that most cruise itineraries are designed around sheltered, calmer water: a Caribbean loop or an Alaska Inside Passage week spends much of its time where the sea is doing very little.

That's why the people who board braced for misery usually find the floor steadier than they expected. The motion you'll actually notice is concentrated in two places: the first night, before your body has adjusted, and any stretch of open, exposed ocean — a Pacific crossing, a winter Atlantic repositioning, the run across the Gulf of Alaska. A calm route in calm season is a different animal from a crossing, and it's worth knowing which one you've booked.

And here's the most reassuring fact in all of this, straight from the CDC's guidance for travelers: your own body adapting — habituation — is "the most effective countermeasure — even more than any medication." Most people who feel off the first evening feel fine by the second. You are likely to get your sea legs whether you do anything or not.

Pick a Cabin That Helps

If you're prone to motion sickness, cabin location is the one big decision you make before you ever board, and it's free. The principle is simple physics: a ship pivots around its center, so the middle of the ship moves least, and the lower you are, the gentler the swing. The ends and the heights move most.

So aim for midship, on a lower passenger deck. Avoid the bow — the forward end — which the CDC specifically names as the spot to stay away from, and avoid the highest cabin decks, which sway the most in a seaway. A midship balcony three or four decks up will ride noticeably easier than a forward cabin near the top.

Two free tools on this site help you do exactly this: the deck-by-deck cabin strategy in our Anthem of the Seas deck plans guide walks through the midship sweet spot on a real ship, and the Stateroom Check flags noise and location issues for a specific cabin number before you book it.

The Free Things That Actually Work

Once you're aboard, the most effective steps cost nothing. These are the measures the CDC's evidence actually backs:

  • Keep the horizon in view. Looking at the distant, steady horizon gives your eyes and inner ear the same story to tell your brain — and motion sickness is, at heart, a disagreement between those two. Get up on an open deck where you can see it. (The catch the CDC names: a horizon view "is often not possible" when you're below deck, which is part of why a cabin with a window or balcony helps.)
  • Don't read or stare at a screen while it's rough. Reading and close visual scanning make it worse — your eyes lock onto a still page while your body feels the roll. Save the book and the phone for calm water.
  • Get fresh air. A stuffy interior space tends to amplify the feeling; open air settles it. When the queasiness creeps in, the rail of an open deck is a better place than your cabin.
  • Go easy on alcohol — and don't smoke. The CDC is blunt that both "increase motion sickness." The welcome-aboard cocktails on a rocky first night are working against you.
  • Eat lightly, but do eat. An empty stomach and a heavy, greasy one are both worse than something plain and modest. Small, bland, frequent beats a big rich dinner on a rough evening.
  • Let your body adjust. The single most effective thing, per the evidence, is simply staying with it while your balance system catches up. The misery, if it comes, is usually front-loaded and short.

Ginger, Wristbands, and What the Evidence Really Says

This is where I'll be straight with you rather than tell you what's easy to sell. Ginger, acupressure wristbands, magnets, and vitamin-B6 remedies are everywhere in cruise advice, and plenty of people swear by them. But the science behind them is thin. The CDC describes the supporting evidence for ginger and similar supplements as "weak and contradictory," and says that in laboratory trials, "acupressure bands or similar treatments are no more effective than placebo."

That doesn't mean don't try them. They're cheap, they're harmless, and if a wristband or a few ginger chews settles your nerves, the calmer head is worth something on its own. What it means is this: don't make them your whole plan if you genuinely know you're prone to seasickness. Treat them as a comfort, not a defense — and have a real strategy (cabin choice, the free measures above, and medication if your doctor agrees) underneath them.

Medication — the Talk-to-Your-Doctor Part

If you've been seasick before, or you're sailing a route you know is exposed, medication is worth a conversation with your doctor or pharmacist before the trip. A few honest points to bring to that conversation, all from the CDC's guidance:

  • Take it before, not after. "Medication is most effective when taken before exposure, rather than after the onset of symptoms." Once you're already sick, you've missed the best window. Plan to dose ahead of a rough stretch, not in the middle of it.
  • The common options. For shorter exposure, antihistamines such as meclizine or dimenhydrinate are typical; for stronger motion, promethazine; and for longer stretches — over about six hours of moderate-to-intense motion — a prescription scopolamine patch. Which one fits you is exactly the doctor's call.
  • Drowsiness is the trade-off. The CDC notes the effective antihistamines sedate you — and the non-drowsy ones "appear to be less effective." The patch is "slightly less sedating" than the pills; promethazine is "the most sedating." Factor that into your day, especially if you're driving to the port or want to be present, not foggy.
  • Trial it at home first. The CDC's own advice is to "take a trial dose of medication at home before departure to find what works best." You don't want to discover how a drug hits you for the first time on day one of a cruise.
  • Mind the interactions. This is the part only your pharmacist can do — checking a new medication against everything you already take. Don't skip it.

Kids, Older Travelers, and Pregnancy

The general picture changes for some travelers, and these are precisely the cases to take to a professional rather than a website:

  • Children. The scopolamine patch is not for kids — the CDC warns it "can cause dangerous adverse effects such as hallucinations and mental confusion in children and should be avoided." Children's dosing of anything is a pediatrician's question, not a guess.
  • Older travelers. Sedating antihistamines can hit harder with age — more drowsiness, more confusion, more fall risk — and they interact with a lot of common prescriptions. Worth a careful word with the doctor who manages those prescriptions.
  • Pregnancy. What's safe in pregnancy is a specific medical question with real stakes; don't self-select an over-the-counter remedy. Ask your OB before you sail.
  • Anyone who's anxious about it. A good share of pre-cruise seasickness worry is anxiety as much as biology, and the two feed each other. Knowing you have a plan — the right cabin, a doctor's recommendation in your bag, the simple measures ready — often does more for the dread than any single remedy.

When to See the Ship's Doctor

Ordinary seasickness is miserable but self-limiting — it passes. Every large cruise ship has a medical center with a doctor and nurses aboard, and there's no shame in going. Go if you can't keep fluids down and it's been many hours (dehydration is the real risk with prolonged vomiting), if the symptoms are severe or simply not easing the way "give it a day" suggests they should, or if something feels off in a way that doesn't match plain motion sickness. They can give stronger anti-nausea treatment, including by injection, when the over-the-counter path isn't holding. Worth knowing: shipboard medical visits are billed and generally aren't covered by ordinary health insurance, which is one more reason travel insurance earns its place.

One Last Thing

Don't let this be the reason you stay ashore. The fear of being sick at sea keeps more people off the water than the sickness itself ever would. The odds are genuinely in your favor on a big ship in a normal season, the worst of it — if it comes at all — is usually the first night, and your own body is quietly working the whole time to make peace with the motion. Pick a good cabin, keep the horizon in front of you, ask your doctor if you're worried, and go. The wake behind the ship is one of the better views you'll get to keep.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do most people get seasick on a cruise?

No. Most people on a big modern ship never feel seriously seasick — the size, the stabilizers, and the largely sheltered itineraries see to that. Of those who do feel it, most settle within a day or two as the body adjusts.

What is the best cabin to avoid seasickness?

Midship and low. The middle of the ship on a lower passenger deck moves the least. Avoid the bow (the forward end) and the highest decks, which swing the most. Our deck-plan guide and Stateroom Check help you find that spot.

Does ginger or an acupressure wristband work?

The evidence is weak. The CDC calls the support for ginger and similar supplements "weak and contradictory," and says acupressure bands are no more effective than placebo in lab trials. They're cheap and harmless to try, but don't rely on them alone.

What is the best medication for seasickness?

That's a question for your doctor or pharmacist. Broadly, antihistamines like meclizine or dimenhydrinate suit shorter trips and a prescription scopolamine patch suits longer, rougher stretches; all work best taken before symptoms start, and the effective ones cause drowsiness. Try a dose at home first and check for interactions.

How long does cruise seasickness last?

Usually a day or two, easing as your body adapts. The first night and any open-water stretch are the hardest. Per the CDC, that natural adjustment — habituation — is the most effective countermeasure of all, more than any medication.

Can you still get seasick on a big cruise ship?

Yes, it's possible, but much less likely than on a small boat. Sea conditions on your route and your own sensitivity matter most. A calm Caribbean week feels very different from an ocean crossing.
