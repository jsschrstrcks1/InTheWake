Scenic Cruising

Ships cruise through Glacier Bay without docking. All viewing is from the ship's decks and balconies.

Glacier Bay, Alaska

Region: Alaska  |  Season: May – September  |  Type: Scenic Cruising (no landing)

From the Pier

  • There Is No Pier
    This is a national park. Only two cruise ships per day are permitted. You're one of them
    🚢 —
  • Margerie Glacier
    250-foot ice face, a mile wide, actively calving — the main event
    🧊 8–10 hr in
  • Lamplugh Glacier
    Bright blue ice — one of the most photogenic in the park
    🧊 mid-bay
  • Johns Hopkins Glacier
    In its own inlet — harbor seals pup on the ice. Sometimes closed for seal protection
    🦭 restricted
  • ~7 Tidewater Glaciers
    Margerie, Grand Pacific, Johns Hopkins, Lamplugh, McBride, Reid, and Riggs reach the sea in the main bay (sources count 7–11 depending on classification). Your ship approaches several over 8–10 hours of sailing.
    🧊 all day

Glacier Bay is scenic cruising only — 8–10 hours in the park. NPS rangers and a Huna Tlingit cultural interpreter board your ship near the bay entrance and stay aboard the full transit. Margerie Glacier is the anchoring highlight; your captain holds position for extended viewing. Watch for humpback whales, sea otters, harbor seals on drifting ice, and brown bears patrolling the shoreline. Big-ship lines (Princess, Holland America, Royal Caribbean, Norwegian, Celebrity) do not launch kayaks here — ship-launched kayaking is a small-ship expedition activity only (UnCruise, Lindblad, some luxury lines with marina platforms). Be on deck all day.

Captain's Logbook

I woke at 5:30 a.m. without an alarm — pure excitement — and grabbed coffee before heading to the bow deck. We were just entering the mouth of Glacier Bay, sliding into a roughly 3.3-million-acre national park and preserve that shelters more than a thousand glaciers. Two National Park Service rangers had boarded via pilot boat and were already setting up at the forward deck, pointing out wildlife and sharing stories over the ship's PA system. Their narration would continue all day, and it transformed everything from passive viewing to active learning. A Huna Tlingit cultural interpreter joined them and told stories of how his ancestors — the Xunaa Tlingit — lived in this landscape for thousands of years, were displaced when Little Ice Age glaciers advanced over their villages in the 1700s, and have since returned to co-manage cultural programs with the park.

We spotted our first humpback whales within the first hour — a mother and calf surfacing together, their breath hanging in the cold air like smoke signals. Over the course of the day, I counted at least fifteen separate whales, including a spectacular bubble-net feeding event where a group of six humpbacks worked together to corral fish before lunging through the surface with mouths agape. The rangers explained that Glacier Bay is among Alaska's most reliable whale watching areas — the nutrient-rich waters attract massive concentrations of prey. I also watched orcas hunting in a pod, Steller sea lions hauled out on rocky points, harbor porpoises racing alongside the ship, and sea otters floating on their backs in kelp beds.

But the glaciers are why we came. The ship cruised past Lamplugh Glacier first — glowing turquoise in the morning light, one of about seven major tidewater glaciers in the bay. Then Johns Hopkins Glacier, though we could only approach partway — the inlet is closed to large ships during harbor seal pupping season (May 1 through June 30), and even outside that window ships turn around near Jaw Point about seven miles from the glacier face. The ranger explained what we were seeing: around 1750, at the Little Ice Age peak, this entire bay was choked with a single glacier more than 4,000 feet thick. When George Vancouver sailed past in 1794, he charted only a slight indentation fronted by a sheer wall of ice. By 1879 John Muir could paddle thirty miles or more into what had become an actual bay. Cumulative retreat today is about sixty-five miles — one of the fastest documented large-glacier retreats on Earth.

Then came the main event: Margerie Glacier. The ship approached slowly, engines barely turning, until we were perhaps a quarter mile from a wall of ice 250 feet tall above the waterline (with another 100 feet of ice hidden below) and about a mile wide. The captain announced he would rotate the ship 360 degrees so every cabin and deck position would get unobstructed views. For nearly an hour we drifted, watching the blue-white face for movement. I heard the glacier before I saw it calve — a crack like rifle fire, then a deep rumble, and suddenly a house-sized chunk of ice separated from the face and crashed into the water with a boom that shook the ship. Everyone on deck gasped simultaneously. I saw three major calving events during our time at the face, each one sending waves radiating outward and small icebergs spinning.

Harbor seals dotted the floating ice throughout the bay — mothers nursing pups on flat bergs that drifted past the bow. Brown bears appeared on distant shores, digging for clams at low tide. Bald eagles patrolled from snags. Mountain goats perched on impossible cliff faces. The wildlife density was extraordinary, and the rangers helped us spot creatures I never would have noticed on my own. However, the real magic was simply the silence when the captain cut the engines — no sound except cracking ice, raven calls, and the distant thunder of glaciers calving into the sea.

Lunch was room service on our balcony as we drifted through Johns Hopkins Inlet, glaciers visible in three directions. The ship felt small against the scale of everything — mountains rising directly from the water, ice flowing down valleys that have been carved over millennia. I finally understood why Alaska cruisers say Glacier Bay is the highlight of the entire trip. It's not just scenery; it's witnessing geological time, watching ice do what ice has done for eons while knowing that this particular bay didn't even exist 250 years ago.

The Moment That Stays With Me: Engine cut, total silence except for the glacier growling for ten straight minutes, then a section the size of a city block let go with a roar that shook the entire ship while two thousand people on deck gasped as one — and I forgot to take photos because I was too busy crying.

The pros: the most spectacular scenery imaginable, expert ranger narration that makes everything richer, close-range wildlife viewing, and a day you'll remember for the rest of your life.

The cons: weather can close in — fog or rain can limit visibility, though even moody weather adds drama. The day is long and exhausting in the best way. You'll take 500 photos and wish you'd taken 500 more.

Tips: Wake up early and claim a spot on the bow by 6 a.m. — bring coffee, binoculars, a warm jacket, and a camera with a charged battery. Order room service for breakfast and lunch so you can stay on deck. Move around throughout the day; different spots offer different perspectives. And put down the camera sometimes to just watch with your own eyes.

What Glacier Bay taught me has little to do with glaciers. Standing on the bow as house-sized chunks of ice crashed into the sea, I realized I was witnessing geological time — processes that began when humans were still figuring out fire. The ranger explained that 250 years ago, where our ship floated, ice stood 4,000 feet thick. I understood then why they limit access to just two ships per day. Some places are too magnificent for crowds, too fragile for carelessness, too important for commerce to determine their fate. I learned that the best things in life sometimes require limits, and that watching a glacier calve teaches patience no vacation can hurry.

Cruise Port

Glacier Bay is unique among Alaska "ports" because you never leave the ship. This is pure scenic cruising — the entire day spent gliding through a 65-mile fjord surrounded by tidewater glaciers, mountains, and exceptional wildlife. Instead of docking at a pier, your ship becomes your viewing platform for one of the most spectacular natural environments on Earth.

Access is strictly limited to protect the bay's fragile ecosystem and its harbor seal populations. Under NPS regulations, no more than two large cruise ships may enter per day, with a seasonal cap of roughly 153 "use days" in June through August plus smaller quotas in May and September. Not every Alaska cruise includes Glacier Bay — check your itinerary carefully. Ships that do typically spend 8–10 hours in the bay, entering around 6 a.m. and leaving in the late afternoon. Most sailings with Glacier Bay run from late April through early October, with July and August as the peak visitation months.

National Park Service interpretive rangers, often accompanied by a Huna Tlingit cultural interpreter, board your ship via the NPS transfer vessel near the bay entrance and stay aboard for the full transit. They narrate throughout the day over the ship's PA system, set up at viewing stations on deck, lead indoor talks in lounges, and run Junior Ranger activities. This shipboard ranger program is written into Glacier Bay's cruise concession contracts — bringing rangers aboard is not optional. The combined ranger and cultural interpreter presence transforms the day from passive viewing into active learning about glaciology, wildlife biology, and the Huna Tlingit (Xunaa Tlingit) homeland history of the bay.

The day typically includes cruising past several tidewater glaciers before the main event: 45 minutes to an hour stationed in front of Margerie Glacier while the captain slowly rotates the ship 360 degrees so every cabin and deck position gets unobstructed views of the glacier face. This is when calving events are most likely to be witnessed up close. Johns Hopkins Inlet is typically closed to large ships from May 1 through June 30 during harbor seal pupping season, and even outside that window ships turn around near Jaw Point about seven miles from the Johns Hopkins face to minimize disturbance to the colony.

Getting Around

Since you never leave the ship, "getting around" in Glacier Bay means knowing where to position yourself for the best views. Prime spots fill early, so plan ahead and be willing to move throughout the day as the ship repositions.

Top Deck / Open Air

  • Forward Bow: The best unobstructed forward views, especially during the approach to glaciers. Fills very early — stake out your spot by 6 a.m. with coffee and a warm jacket. Rangers often set up here.
  • Top Deck / Sun Deck: Higher vantage point with 360-degree views. Less crowded than the bow, accessible for wheelchair users, but can be windier and colder.
  • Helicopter Pad: If your ship opens the helipad, this offers unobstructed views in all directions. Check with guest services for accessibility.

Comfortable Indoor Options

  • Private Balcony: If you have one, your balcony offers comfort, privacy, and protection from wind. a good base for all-day viewing, and room service keeps you fed between glaciers.
  • Observation Lounge: Most ships have a forward-facing lounge with floor-to-ceiling windows. Climate-controlled, with seating and bar service. Fills quickly on Glacier Bay day.
  • Promenade Deck: Walking areas on lower decks offer protected viewing. Good for mobility-limited passengers who can't access higher decks.

Tip: Don't camp in one spot all day. Move around as the ship repositions — the best angle changes constantly. During the 360-degree rotation at Margerie Glacier, every position on the ship will have a turn at the best view.

Ship Programs & Activities

Since Glacier Bay is scenic cruising without a port stop, "excursions" are actually onboard programs and activities designed to enhance your viewing experience. Some cruise lines offer unique experiences available only on Glacier Bay day.

Ranger Programs (Free)

NPS interpretive rangers board every cruise ship permitted into the bay and provide narration throughout the day (included free with your cruise — no booking required). They set up at stations on deck to answer questions, help spot wildlife, and explain the geology and ecology of the park. Attend any ranger presentations scheduled in the ship's theater or lounge — these typically cover glaciology, wildlife, and the remarkable story of this landscape's transformation over 250 years. A Huna Tlingit cultural interpreter shares Xunaa Tlingit perspectives on this ancestral homeland, including the story of the Little Ice Age displacement and the modern tribal partnership with NPS that produced Xunaa Shuká Hít — the Huna Tribal House dedicated in 2016 at Bartlett Cove.

Kayaking Inside Glacier Bay

Ship-launched kayaking inside Glacier Bay itself is a small-ship expedition experience, not a mainstream cruise offering. Large lines (Princess, Holland America, Royal Caribbean, Norwegian, Celebrity) treat Glacier Bay as a no-disembarkation scenic day — they do not launch kayaks or put passengers ashore in the park. Small-ship expedition operators (UnCruise Adventures, Lindblad Expeditions–National Geographic, American Cruise Lines) and some ultra-luxury lines with marina platforms (Seabourn, Regent Seven Seas, Silversea) do advertise kayak and Zodiac tours launched from the vessel in Glacier Bay and adjacent fjords on specific itineraries. If paddling among icebergs is on your wish list, shop small ships, not big ones, and confirm exact activities directly with the operator for your sailing date.

Camera Workshops

Many ships offer special photography sessions or workshops on Glacier Bay day ($50–$100), with professional guidance on capturing glaciers, wildlife, and dramatic landscapes. Even without a formal program, this is a day to charge all batteries, clear memory cards, and consider bringing a tripod for stability during long zoom shots of calving glaciers.

Independent Viewing Strategy

The best "excursion" is free: spending the day moving around the ship with binoculars, camera, and layers. Book a balcony stateroom if budget allows ($200–$500 more than inside cabins) — it serves as your private viewing platform all day. Order room service for meals — gratuity runs $5–$15 per delivery — so you can stay on deck through the best viewing hours. Bring a thermos for hot drinks on deck — the ship's coffee bar charges $4–$6 per specialty drink. The ship rotates 360 degrees at Margerie Glacier, so every position eventually gets prime views.

Booking Tips

NPS ranger programs are free and don't require booking — they happen automatically on every permitted ship. For the best experience on a large cruise ship, book ahead for a balcony stateroom when you reserve your cruise. There is no independent tour option here: because there is no landing, you cannot compare a ship excursion against an independent operator the way you would in Juneau or Ketchikan. If you want actual kayaking inside Glacier Bay, compare small-ship expedition itineraries (UnCruise, Lindblad, American Cruise Lines) — these are a different product category from mainstream Alaska cruises and should be researched and booked separately.

Weather & What to Bring

Glacier Bay can be cold even in summer — summer highs average 55–62°F (13–17°C) with lows 45–50°F (7–10°C), and katabatic winds off tidewater glaciers routinely make deck temperatures feel 10–20°F colder. Pack warm layers, a waterproof-and-windproof outer shell, hat, and gloves for extended outdoor viewing. Weather changes rapidly; fog and rain are common but add drama to the scenery. Even overcast days offer spectacular views, and glaciers photograph beautifully in soft diffuse light.

Essential Gear

  • Binoculars: Essential for spotting wildlife and glacier detail. If you don't own a pair, some ships offer rentals.
  • Camera with zoom lens: Bring extra batteries (cold drains them fast) and cleared memory cards. A 200mm+ lens helps capture distant wildlife and glacier detail.
  • Warm layers: Dress in layers you can add or remove. Windproof outer layer is critical.
  • Thermos: Fill with hot coffee or cocoa for deck viewing.
  • Sunscreen and sunglasses: Ice and water reflect UV intensely, even on cloudy days.

Useful Details

  • WiFi/Cell: No cell service in Glacier Bay. Ship WiFi may be limited or unavailable during cruising.
  • Meals: Consider ordering room service for breakfast and lunch to avoid missing viewing time. The buffet is quick, but seated service venues require you to leave the views.
  • Ship announcements: Listen for PA announcements about wildlife sightings and glacier approaches. Some ships have dedicated Glacier Bay commentary channels.
  • Accessibility: Most ships have accessible viewing areas. Check with guest services for locations and elevator access to top decks.

Wildlife Viewing Tips

  • Whales: Watch for spouts (misty breath) and dark dorsal fins. Humpbacks often breach or slap their tails.
  • Bears: Scan shorelines, especially at low tide when bears dig for clams.
  • Mountain goats: Look for white dots on steep cliff faces — they're surprisingly common.
  • Sea otters: Float on their backs in kelp beds, often in groups called rafts.
  • Seals: Rest on floating ice throughout the bay, especially near glaciers.

Pro Tip: This is an all-day experience — pace yourself. Take breaks in your cabin or the observation lounge, but be on deck for the approach to Margerie Glacier (typically mid-morning to early afternoon).

Frequently Asked Questions
Q: When is the best time of year to visit Glacier Bay?

Late June through early August offers the most reliable weather. Average summer highs run 55–62°F (13–17°C) with lows 45–50°F (7–10°C), daylight peaks at about 18 hours 20 minutes on the June solstice, and wildlife activity is at its peak. May is typically the driest month and often has spectacular snow-capped peaks, while September sees fewer crowds but rising rainfall as Gulf of Alaska storm tracks intensify.

Q: What should I pack for Glacier Bay weather?

Pack warm layers including fleece, a waterproof-and-windproof outer shell, hat, gloves, and non-slip footwear — katabatic winds draining off the tidewater glaciers make a 58°F deck feel like 40°F in minutes. Bring binoculars, extra camera batteries (cold drains them fast), a thermos for hot drinks, polarized sunglasses, and SPF 30+ sunscreen since UV reflects hard off ice and water even under overcast.

Q: Does Glacier Bay have a rainy season?

Bartlett Cove (park headquarters) receives roughly 70 inches of precipitation a year. April, May, and June are typically the driest months; September and October are the wettest as autumn Gulf of Alaska storms intensify. Rain or drizzle occurs on a majority of days even in midsummer — expect moisture on your deck time and plan for it rather than against it.

Q: How cold does it get on deck while viewing glaciers?

Ambient summer air at the waterline is usually 45–60°F (7–16°C), but the "feels like" on deck runs 10–20°F colder when katabatic winds drain off tidewater glaciers. NPS explicitly warns that hypothermia is a year-round risk in Glacier Bay because of the combination of cool air, wind, and damp clothing. Dress in layers, protect your extremities, and take warming breaks indoors every 30–45 minutes.

Q: What weather hazards should I be aware of in Glacier Bay?

The three real risks are hypothermia from prolonged exposure on wet, windy decks; katabatic gusts off the glacier faces (some NPS monitoring sites have recorded gusts over 70 mph); and sunburn from upward-reflected UV off ice and water, which hits foreheads, ears, and the underside of the nose even on overcast days. Dangerous calving waves are a kayaker-scale concern, not a cruise-ship concern.

Q: Can fog affect glacier viewing in Glacier Bay?

Yes. Marine fog can roll in quickly and obscure glacier faces for hours, and persistent low cloud is common in late summer and early fall. Banks often break up or lift by afternoon in a typical marine pattern, so patience is usually rewarded. Even fogged-in viewing has its own atmosphere, and rangers continue narrating wildlife and park history when visibility is limited.

Q: Is there a hurricane or storm season that affects Glacier Bay cruises?

Alaska is not in a hurricane zone. The main weather concern is Gulf of Alaska extratropical storms — these build through autumn and winter and can deliver gale-force winds and heavy rain during the shoulder of the cruise season (late August through September). Summer itineraries rarely encounter severe storms inside Glacier Bay itself, though transit to and from the bay can be rougher.

Q: Will rain ruin my Glacier Bay experience?

No — rain is an expected part of the day, not a ruined day. Most precipitation arrives as light drizzle or passing showers rather than daylong downpours. Many photographers prefer overcast or misty conditions because the diffuse light brings out the blue of glacier ice without harsh shadows. Pack a waterproof jacket, embrace the weather, and you will still see glaciers calve.

Q: Is Glacier Bay worth it on an Alaska cruise?

Yes. Glacier Bay is consistently rated the single most memorable day of an Alaska cruise: ~7 major tidewater glaciers visible along the route, actively calving ice, NPS ranger narration, a Huna Tlingit cultural interpreter aboard, and wildlife density (humpback whales, harbor seals, sea otters, brown bears, mountain goats) that is difficult to match anywhere else in Southeast Alaska. Itineraries that include Glacier Bay typically cost more — and are worth the premium.

Q: Do passengers get off the ship in Glacier Bay?

No — Glacier Bay is scenic cruising only. There is no port, pier, or landing for large cruise ships. You experience the entire day from decks, balconies, and indoor viewing areas. Only small-ship expedition operators (UnCruise, Lindblad Expeditions–National Geographic, and some luxury lines with marina platforms) run ship-launched kayaking or Zodiac tours inside the park; mainstream lines like Princess, Holland America, Royal Caribbean, Norwegian, and Celebrity do not put passengers ashore here.

Q: How long does the ship stay in front of Margerie Glacier?

Typically 45 minutes to 1 hour. The captain slowly rotates the ship 360 degrees so every cabin and deck position gets unobstructed views of the 250-foot ice face (with another ~100 feet of ice hidden below the waterline, and about one mile across). This is the most likely window for a major calving event and the emotional highlight of the day for most passengers.

Q: What is the best spot on the ship to view Glacier Bay?

The forward bow offers the best approach views but fills early — arrive by 6 a.m. with coffee and layers. A private balcony (if you have one) provides comfort and all-day viewing with room service to keep you fed. The top deck or observation lounge offers 360-degree views and is typically more wheelchair accessible. Move around throughout the day as the ship repositions; during the 360-degree rotation at Margerie, every position eventually gets the prime view.

Q: Will I see glaciers calving (breaking off into the water)?

Very likely. Margerie Glacier calves frequently throughout the day — you often hear it before you see it, a crack like rifle fire followed by a rumbling roar. Historically Margerie was stable or slightly advancing, but recent NPS and NASA data show thinning since the late 1990s. Watch the face continuously during your time there.

Quick Answer: Glacier Bay is the crown jewel of Alaska cruising — a 65-mile fjord with about seven major tidewater glaciers, NPS rangers and a Huna Tlingit interpreter narrating from the bow, and the 250-foot Margerie Glacier wall calving thunder into the sea.
